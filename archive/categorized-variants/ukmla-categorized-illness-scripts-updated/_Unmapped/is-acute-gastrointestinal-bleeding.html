Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Acute gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding refers to the rapid loss of blood from the digestive tract. It can originate from any site along the gastrointestinal tract, typically classified into upper GI bleeding (above the ligament of Treitz) and lower GI bleeding (below the ligament of Treitz). Causes range from peptic ulcers to diverticulosis, with severity varying from occult bleeding to massive haemorrhage leading to shock.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • In the UK, acute upper GI bleeding affects 50-150 per 100,000 people annually. It is more common in hospital settings, particularly in patients over 60. Worldwide, it is one of the leading causes of hospital admissions due to gastrointestinal issues. Lower GI bleeding is less common but increases in frequency with age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • GI bleeding occurs when there is a disruption in the integrity of the GI mucosa or vessels. Upper GI bleeding often results from ulcers, varices, or erosive gastritis, while lower GI bleeding is frequently due to diverticular disease, angiodysplasia, or haemorrhoids. Blood loss leads to decreased intravascular volume, hypoperfusion, and if untreated, can cause shock.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Acute GI bleeding can present suddenly, often with haematemesis (vomiting blood) or melaena (black, tarry stools) for upper GI bleeds, or with haematochezia (bright red blood per rectum) for lower GI bleeds. Without intervention, progression can be rapid, leading to haemodynamic instability and shock within hours. Early intervention is crucial to stabilise the patient and control bleeding.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting of blood, either fresh or "coffee-ground," is a hallmark of upper GI bleeding. This occurs due to bleeding into the stomach and regurgitation of blood.
  • Melaena - Black, tarry stools result from digestion of blood as it passes through the GI tract, typically seen in upper GI bleeding.
  • Haematochezia - Passage of fresh red blood per rectum, usually from lower GI bleeding but can occur in brisk upper GI bleeding.
  • Dizziness and syncope - These symptoms arise from hypovolaemia and resultant hypotension.
  • Abdominal pain - Typically associated with peptic ulcer disease or inflammatory causes of GI bleeding.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - A compensatory mechanism in response to hypovolaemia and blood loss.
  • Hypotension - Seen in more severe blood loss due to decreased circulating blood volume.
  • Pallor - Indicative of anaemia from blood loss, often seen in significant or chronic bleeding.
  • Abdominal tenderness - May be present if there is a localised cause of bleeding, such as an ulcer or diverticulitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Shock - Indicates severe hypovolaemia and poor perfusion, requiring immediate resuscitation.
  • Persistent hypotension despite fluids - Suggests ongoing or uncontrolled bleeding.
  • Large-volume haematemesis or haematochezia - May indicate a major arterial bleed, which can quickly lead to haemodynamic collapse.
  • Altered mental status - Could indicate hypoperfusion of the brain due to significant blood loss or hypoxia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Occult bleeding, presenting with iron deficiency anaemia without overt GI symptoms. This can be seen in slow, chronic blood loss due to small ulcers or tumours.
  • Painless haematochezia in the elderly, often from diverticular bleeding, may be mistaken for haemorrhoidal bleeding.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Anaemia and low haemoglobin indicate blood loss. Raised urea to creatinine ratio suggests upper GI bleeding due to blood digestion in the stomach.
  • Endoscopy (OGD or Colonoscopy) - Direct visualisation of the GI tract to locate the source of bleeding and facilitate treatment such as cautery or clipping.
  • CT angiography - Useful for identifying active bleeding in patients where endoscopy is inconclusive, especially in lower GI bleeds.
  • Lactate levels - Elevated lactate can suggest tissue hypoperfusion and shock in severe blood loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a sudden onset of haematemesis and melaena over the past 12 hours. He is pale, tachycardic, and hypotensive, with no significant abdominal pain. He reports a history of NSAID use for chronic back pain.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - Typically presents with epigastric pain and may cause haematemesis or melaena, but often without haematochezia.
  • Diverticular Bleeding - Often painless lower GI bleeding, presenting with haematochezia rather than haematemesis or melaena.
  • Oesophageal Varices - Seen in patients with liver cirrhosis, presenting with massive upper GI bleeding and haematemesis.
  • Colorectal cancer - Chronic, occult blood loss leading to anaemia and sometimes visible bleeding, but often asymptomatic until late stages.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Resuscitation - Immediate IV fluid resuscitation or blood transfusion to restore circulating volume and treat shock.
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) - IV PPIs are used in upper GI bleeding to reduce gastric acid secretion and stabilise clots.
  • Endoscopic therapy - Endoscopic intervention, such as banding, clipping, or cautery, is used to control the source of bleeding.
  • Surgery - Required in cases where endoscopic management fails or in the event of perforation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Results from severe blood loss and may lead to multi-organ failure if untreated.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Chronic blood loss can result in anaemia, particularly in occult bleeding.
  • Death - Severe GI bleeding, especially if not promptly treated, carries a significant risk of mortality.
