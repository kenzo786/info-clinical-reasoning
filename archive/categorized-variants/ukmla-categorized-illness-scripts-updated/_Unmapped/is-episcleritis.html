Episcleritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Episcleritis is a benign, self-limiting inflammation of the episclera, the thin vascular layer between the conjunctiva and the sclera. It typically presents as acute redness in one or both eyes, often without significant pain or visual impairment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 6â€“8 cases per 10,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less commonly seen in secondary care unless recurrent or associated with systemic disease.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher in regions with prevalent systemic autoimmune diseases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Inflammation in episcleritis is mediated by immune activation, leading to vasodilation and infiltration of inflammatory cells in the episcleral tissue.
  • Often idiopathic but can be associated with systemic autoimmune conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Acute onset with episodes typically lasting 1â€“2 weeks.
  • May recur, especially in cases with underlying systemic conditions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Redness in one or both eyes - Due to superficial episcleral vessel dilatation.
  • Mild discomfort - Often described as a gritty sensation rather than pain; inflammation causes irritation of surrounding tissues.
  • Tearing - Reflex tearing occurs as a response to ocular surface irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Diffuse or sectoral redness - Episcleral vessels are dilated and mobile when manipulated with a cotton swab.
  • No discharge - Distinguishing it from conjunctivitis.
  • Normal vision - No impact on visual acuity differentiates it from scleritis or uveitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Severe pain - May indicate scleritis or other deeper ocular inflammation.
  • Photophobia or vision loss - Suggests possible uveitis or keratitis.
  • Recurrent episodes - Raises concern for underlying systemic diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis or systemic vasculitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral episcleritis - Though uncommon, bilateral presentation might be seen in systemic diseases.
  • Chronic episcleritis - Prolonged course over weeks may occur, particularly with autoimmune associations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Slit-lamp examination - Identifies inflammation confined to the episcleral layer.
  • Phenylephrine test - Blanching of episcleral vessels with phenylephrine confirms episcleral vessel involvement.
  • Blood tests - ESR, CRP, and autoimmune panel (ANA, RF) may be indicated if systemic disease is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with 3 days of redness in her right eye, mild discomfort but no pain, tearing, and no visual changes. Symptoms started acutely and are confined to one sector of the eye.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Scleritis - More severe pain, associated with systemic disease, deeper inflammation.
  • Conjunctivitis - Discharge and diffuse conjunctival involvement distinguish it.
  • Keratitis - Associated with corneal involvement, pain, and photophobia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care - Artificial tears for symptomatic relief.
  • Topical NSAIDs - For inflammation control in persistent cases.
  • Systemic investigation - Refer for further assessment if recurrent or associated with systemic disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Generally, episcleritis resolves without complications.
  • Recurrent episodes may lead to further investigations for systemic disease.
