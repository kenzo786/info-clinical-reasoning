Anxiety
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Anxiety is a common mental health disorder characterised by excessive worry, fear, or apprehension about future events or situations, often disproportionate to the actual risk. It may manifest as generalised anxiety or in specific contexts, such as social situations (social anxiety disorder), panic attacks (panic disorder), or phobias.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anxiety Disorder"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Genetic predisposition, childhood adversity, chronic stress, personality traits (e.g., neuroticism), substance misuse, and medical conditions such as hyperthyroidism or cardiovascular disease increase the risk of developing anxiety disorders.
  • Prevention: Strategies include stress management techniques (e.g., mindfulness, cognitive behavioural strategies), regular physical activity, adequate sleep, limiting caffeine and alcohol intake, and early intervention for at-risk individuals with cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Anxiety is thought to arise from dysregulation of neurotransmitters such as serotonin, norepinephrine, and gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA). Overactivation of the amygdala and under-regulation by the prefrontal cortex lead to heightened threat perception. Chronic stress and maladaptive coping mechanisms can exacerbate these circuits, resulting in persistent anxiety. Genetic predisposition, environmental stressors, and trauma are contributing factors.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of intense worry or fear, often triggered by stress or a specific event. Duration may be hours to days.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent anxiety lasting for months to years. Symptoms fluctuate but may progressively worsen without treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Excessive worry: A pervasive sense of apprehension about future events. Pathophysiologically, this is linked to overactivity in the amygdala.
  • Restlessness: A feeling of being "on edge" is common due to heightened autonomic arousal.
  • Fatigue: Prolonged activation of the body's stress response can lead to physical and mental exhaustion.
  • Difficulties concentrating: Cognitive functions are impaired due to the brainâ€™s preoccupation with perceived threats.
  • Muscle tension: Chronic stress leads to sustained contraction of muscles, especially in the neck and shoulders.
  • Sleep disturbances: Difficulty falling or staying asleep due to hyperarousal.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia: Increased heart rate due to sympathetic nervous system activation during anxiety episodes.
  • Hyperventilation: Shallow, rapid breathing as part of the body's "fight or flight" response.
  • Palpitations: Awareness of an irregular heartbeat, often related to increased catecholamine release.
  • Diaphoresis (sweating): Profuse sweating as a result of increased sympathetic activity.
  • Trembling or shaking: Muscle tremors caused by autonomic arousal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Suicidal ideation: An urgent red flag that may indicate severe anxiety or co-existing depression. It signifies a significant risk of self-harm or suicide and requires immediate intervention.
  • Substance misuse: May indicate maladaptive coping with anxiety and is a red flag for potential addiction or overdose complications.
  • Agitation with confusion: May suggest a panic disorder with severe autonomic dysfunction, potentially leading to hyperventilation syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Somatic symptoms: Anxiety may present primarily with physical complaints, such as chronic pain or gastrointestinal disturbances, without an obvious emotional trigger.
  • Blunted affect: Some individuals may not report excessive worry, but instead display emotional numbness or detachment, making the diagnosis more challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • GAD-7 questionnaire: A self-reported questionnaire used to assess the severity of anxiety symptoms. Scores above 10 indicate moderate to severe anxiety.
  • Thyroid function tests: Hyperthyroidism can mimic anxiety symptoms; elevated free T4 and suppressed TSH levels would suggest thyroid dysfunction.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Performed to rule out cardiac causes of palpitations and tachycardia; anxiety-related ECG changes are often minimal.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of excessive worry about work, difficulties concentrating, and muscle tension. She reports sleep disturbances and occasional palpitations, with no identifiable physical cause.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Depression: Overlap with anxiety, but depression often includes low mood, anhedonia, and feelings of hopelessness.
  • Panic disorder: Sudden, intense panic attacks, typically shorter in duration, with an acute onset of symptoms like dyspnoea and chest pain.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Symptoms such as tremors, tachycardia, and weight loss can mimic anxiety but with abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Substance-induced anxiety: Stimulants, caffeine, and recreational drugs can provoke anxiety-like symptoms, confirmed by a history of use.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): First-line psychological treatment in the UK, targeting maladaptive thoughts and behaviours.
  • Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs): Sertraline or escitalopram are first-line pharmacological treatments, helping to balance serotonin levels.
  • Pregabalin: Considered if SSRIs are ineffective or not tolerated, helping reduce anxiety through GABA modulation.
  • Beta-blockers: Used symptomatically to manage physical symptoms such as palpitations and tremors.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Depression: Co-occurs in up to 50% of patients with anxiety, with shared pathophysiological features including dysregulation of serotonin and chronic stress exposure.
  • Substance misuse: Anxiety can lead to maladaptive coping mechanisms, such as alcohol or drug abuse, increasing the risk of addiction.
  • Cardiovascular complications: Chronic anxiety may contribute to hypertension and an increased risk of coronary artery disease due to persistent sympathetic activation.
