Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Repetitive strain injury (RSI) refers to a group of musculoskeletal disorders that arise from repetitive tasks, forceful exertions, or sustained postures. It typically affects the muscles, tendons, and nerves of the upper limbs, neck, and back. RSI is also known as work-related upper limb disorder (WRULD).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: ~2% of GP visits involve RSI complaints.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Exact data limited, but occupational health studies suggest RSI is a significant cause of lost workdays.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Up to 20% of individuals in office or manual labour jobs may experience symptoms of RSI.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Repetitive mechanical loading or static postures result in microtrauma to muscles and tendons.
  • Inflammatory mediators are released, leading to localised pain and swelling.
  • Chronic overuse can cause fibrosis, reduced elasticity of tissues, and nerve entrapment.
  • Prolonged nerve compression may lead to neuropathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset of mild discomfort during or after activity.
  • Subacute phase: Pain becomes more persistent and starts interfering with daily activities.
  • Chronic phase: Long-standing pain with potential loss of function and visible musculoskeletal changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Pain - Localised or diffuse, worsens with activity. Caused by tissue microtrauma and inflammatory mediators.
  • Tingling or numbness - Suggestive of nerve involvement, often due to compression or entrapment.
  • Weakness - Functional impairment from chronic muscle strain or nerve involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over affected area - Indicates localised inflammation or microtrauma.
  • Reduced range of motion - Due to pain or fibrosis limiting flexibility.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Severe or worsening pain unresponsive to rest - May indicate a structural injury such as tendon rupture.
  • Signs of infection (e.g., erythema, fever) - Suggests septic arthritis or tenosynovitis.
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., muscle wasting, significant weakness) - Suggests severe nerve entrapment or neuropathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Generalised pain without clear localisation - May mimic fibromyalgia or other chronic pain syndromes.
  • Symptoms confined to non-dominant side - Less typical for RSI, could suggest another systemic condition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - May reveal tendon inflammation or tears.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Useful for assessing nerve involvement in chronic cases.
  • X-rays - To exclude fractures or other structural abnormalities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old office worker presents with 6 months of wrist pain, tingling in the hand, and difficulty typing, worsened with prolonged use and unresponsive to over-the-counter analgesics.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome - Localised tingling and numbness, often worse at night.
  • Tendonitis - More localised pain over affected tendon.
  • Fibromyalgia - Generalised pain and fatigue, non-localised.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Rest and ergonomic adjustments - Modify work environment to reduce strain.
  • Physiotherapy - Exercises to strengthen muscles and improve posture.
  • Analgesia - NSAIDs for inflammation and pain relief.
  • Steroid injections - For refractory cases involving inflammation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI)"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Due to persistent inflammation or nerve damage.
  • Functional impairment - Reduced ability to perform occupational tasks.
