Pregnancy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy is the physiological state during which a fertilised egg (zygote) implants and develops within the uterus, eventually leading to the birth of an offspring. It lasts approximately 40 weeks from the last menstrual period (LMP) and involves significant anatomical, physiological, and hormonal changes in the body.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, there were approximately 640,000 live births in 2021, equating to a pregnancy rate of around 60 per 1,000 women aged 15-44 years. Around 20% of pregnancies result in miscarriage or stillbirth. Worldwide, there are approximately 140 million live births annually, with variation in fertility rates across regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy begins with fertilisation of an ovum by sperm in the fallopian tube, forming a zygote. The zygote undergoes mitotic divisions, forming a blastocyst, which implants in the endometrium around 6-7 days post-ovulation. Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) is produced by the syncytiotrophoblast to support the corpus luteum, maintaining progesterone secretion. This prevents menstruation and supports the early pregnancy. Placental development begins around week 3, and the placenta takes over hormone production from the corpus luteum by the end of the first trimester. Physiological changes such as increased blood volume, cardiac output, and renal function support fetal development.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy is divided into three trimesters:
  • 1st trimester (0-12 weeks): Fertilisation, implantation, and early organogenesis occur. Common symptoms include nausea, vomiting, and fatigue.
  • 2nd trimester (13-26 weeks): Organ development continues, and symptoms like quickening (first fetal movements) appear. Nausea generally subsides.
  • 3rd trimester (27-40 weeks): Fetal growth accelerates, and symptoms such as back pain, Braxton Hicks contractions, and oedema are common. Labour usually occurs between 37-42 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Amenorrhoea - The absence of menstruation due to sustained high levels of progesterone inhibiting the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis.
  • Nausea and vomiting ("morning sickness") - Linked to rising hCG levels, particularly in the first trimester. This can vary in severity and timing.
  • Breast tenderness - Caused by hormonal changes, primarily elevated oestrogen and progesterone, which increase breast glandular tissue growth.
  • Fatigue - Related to increased metabolic demands and hormonal fluctuations (e.g. progesterone).
  • Frequent urination - Due to increased renal blood flow and later pressure on the bladder from the enlarging uterus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Positive pregnancy test - Detection of hCG in urine or serum. Levels double approximately every 48 hours in early pregnancy.
  • Uterine enlargement - The uterus begins to grow after implantation, palpable at 12 weeks at the pubic symphysis and reaching the umbilicus by 20 weeks.
  • Chadwick's sign - Bluish discolouration of the cervix due to increased blood flow to the pelvic organs.
  • Goodell's sign - Softening of the cervix, usually occurring around 4-6 weeks gestation due to hormonal effects.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Could indicate ectopic pregnancy or placental abruption, both of which can be life-threatening for the mother and fetus.
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding - Suggests miscarriage, placental abruption, or placenta previa, all of which require urgent intervention.
  • Severe headache and visual disturbances - May indicate pre-eclampsia, a condition characterised by hypertension and end-organ damage, particularly after 20 weeks.
  • Absent fetal movements - May indicate intrauterine fetal death or fetal distress, requiring immediate evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Silent miscarriage - Occurs when the fetus has stopped developing, but no signs like heavy bleeding or severe pain are present, leading to a missed diagnosis if not identified by routine ultrasound.
  • Ectopic pregnancy with mild pain - An ectopic pregnancy may present with atypically mild symptoms, delaying diagnosis and increasing risk of tubal rupture.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Urine pregnancy test - Detects hCG levels in urine. Expected to be positive within 10-12 days post-conception.
  • Serum hCG - Quantitative test used to confirm pregnancy, especially in cases of suspected ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage. Rising levels (doubling every 48 hours) are indicative of a viable intrauterine pregnancy.
  • Ultrasound scan - Transvaginal ultrasound is used to confirm intrauterine pregnancy as early as 5-6 weeks gestation. Fetal heartbeat can typically be seen from around 6-7 weeks.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents with 6 weeks of amenorrhoea, nausea, and breast tenderness. Urine pregnancy test is positive. Physical examination reveals a soft, enlarged uterus consistent with early pregnancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Often presents with abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, but absence of intrauterine pregnancy on ultrasound helps differentiate it from normal pregnancy.
  • Miscarriage - Presents with vaginal bleeding and cramping. Ultrasound may show an empty gestational sac or non-viable embryo.
  • Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) - Symptoms like breast tenderness and fatigue may overlap, but the absence of a positive pregnancy test differentiates it from pregnancy.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Pre-eclampsia - Affects about 5% of pregnancies, characterised by hypertension, proteinuria, and organ dysfunction. If untreated, can progress to eclampsia, which involves seizures and is life-threatening.
  • Gestational diabetes - Occurs in 3-5% of pregnancies, leading to increased risk of macrosomia, birth complications, and maternal type 2 diabetes later in life.
  • Preterm labour - Birth before 37 weeks can lead to complications such as respiratory distress syndrome and developmental delays in the neonate.
