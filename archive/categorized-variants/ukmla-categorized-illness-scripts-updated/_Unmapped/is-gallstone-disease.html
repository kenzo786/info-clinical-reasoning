Gallstone Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Gallstone disease is a condition characterised by the formation of solid particles (gallstones) in the gallbladder, typically composed of cholesterol or bilirubin. It can lead to complications if stones obstruct the biliary tract, potentially causing cholecystitis, choledocholithiasis, or pancreatitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Gallstone disease affects approximately 10-15% of adults, though many are asymptomatic.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Gallstones are a common finding in patients presenting with upper abdominal pain and account for 30-50% of cases of acute pancreatitis.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Varies significantly, with higher rates in Western countries. Prevalence ranges from 5-20% of adults globally, more common in women and individuals over 40.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Cholesterol Gallstones: Result from cholesterol supersaturation in bile, where excess cholesterol forms crystals that aggregate into stones, often due to poor gallbladder motility.
  • Pigment Gallstones: Formed from excess bilirubin and are common in conditions causing increased bilirubin turnover, such as haemolytic anaemias.
  • Obstruction and Inflammation: Stones can obstruct the cystic or common bile duct, leading to gallbladder inflammation (cholecystitis), infection (cholangitis), or pancreatitis if the pancreatic duct is involved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic phase: Many individuals remain asymptomatic, sometimes indefinitely.
  • Symptomatic phase: Characterised by episodes of biliary colic, which are typically episodic and self-limiting but can last 1-5 hours.
  • Complications: Progression to acute cholecystitis, cholangitis, or pancreatitis may occur with prolonged obstruction and inflammation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Right upper quadrant (RUQ) pain: Typically intense, intermittent, lasting 1-5 hours, and linked to gallbladder contraction against a stone; often postprandial, especially after fatty meals.
  • Nausea and vomiting: Secondary to pain and local inflammation.
  • Jaundice: Occurs if stones obstruct the common bile duct, leading to bilirubin buildup in the blood.
  • Fever and chills: Indicative of infection or inflammation, especially in cholecystitis or cholangitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Murphyâ€™s sign: Sharp RUQ pain on palpation during inhalation; indicates gallbladder inflammation, often cholecystitis.
  • Jaundice: Yellowing of skin and sclera due to increased bilirubin levels, suggestive of biliary obstruction.
  • Tachycardia and hypotension: May accompany severe pain or infection, such as in cholangitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent RUQ pain with fever: Can indicate acute cholecystitis, cholangitis, or pancreatitis, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Jaundice and fever: Raises concern for ascending cholangitis, which can lead to sepsis.
  • Shock or altered mental status: Suggestive of severe cholangitis or gallstone pancreatitis with systemic inflammatory response.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Epigastric pain radiating to the back: May mimic pancreatitis; occurs if gallstones impact the common duct.
  • Silent jaundice: Jaundice without pain can occur with biliary obstruction; common in elderly or diabetic patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: First-line, shows gallstones as hyperechoic foci with posterior shadowing and may detect biliary sludge.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs): Elevated bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase indicate biliary obstruction; ALT may be raised.
  • MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography): Non-invasive, highly accurate imaging for bile duct stones.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with intermittent postprandial RUQ pain, occasional nausea, and jaundice, persisting over several weeks and worsened by fatty foods.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease: Epigastric burning pain, often relieved by antacids; lacks typical gallstone disease triggers.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, elevated serum lipase/amylase.
  • Acute hepatitis: Jaundice with malaise and right-sided discomfort, abnormal liver function tests but no biliary colic history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Symptomatic management: NSAIDs for pain; fasting during acute episodes to reduce gallbladder contractions.
  • Cholecystectomy: Laparoscopic removal is the definitive treatment for symptomatic gallstones, often advised in recurrent cases.
  • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): For removal of stones in the common bile duct, especially in cholangitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gallstone Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute cholecystitis: Inflammation of the gallbladder, commonly requiring hospitalisation and possible surgery.
  • Cholangitis: Life-threatening infection of the bile ducts, often leading to sepsis if untreated.
  • Gallstone pancreatitis: Inflammation of the pancreas due to stone obstruction; associated with significant morbidity.
