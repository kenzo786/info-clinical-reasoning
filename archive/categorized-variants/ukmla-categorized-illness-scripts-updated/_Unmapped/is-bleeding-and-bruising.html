Bleeding & Bruising
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Bleeding and bruising refer to a spectrum of symptoms caused by abnormalities in haemostasis, including excessive bleeding, spontaneous bruising, or prolonged bleeding. These symptoms may be due to vascular defects, platelet disorders, coagulation factor deficiencies, or combined mechanisms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Easy bruising - Due to capillary fragility or platelet dysfunction, often associated with petechiae or ecchymoses in platelet disorders.
  • Prolonged bleeding from minor cuts - Suggestive of coagulation factor deficiencies like haemophilia, where the clotting cascade is impaired.
  • Spontaneous bleeding - Often seen in severe thrombocytopenia or coagulation factor deficiencies, occurring without obvious trauma.
  • Menorrhagia - Frequently observed in women with von Willebrand disease due to deficient platelet adhesion.
  • Bleeding into joints (haemarthrosis) - Characteristic of haemophilia, resulting from repeated joint trauma and insufficient clot formation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Petechiae - Small red or purple spots indicating capillary bleeding, often seen in thrombocytopenia.
  • Ecchymoses - Larger, confluent bruises indicating extravasation of blood into subcutaneous tissue, common in clotting factor deficiencies.
  • Haemarthrosis - Swollen, tender joints due to blood accumulation, pathognomonic for severe haemophilia.
  • Mucosal bleeding - Epistaxis or gum bleeding suggests platelet dysfunction or von Willebrand disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Intracranial bleeding - Life-threatening complication of severe thrombocytopenia or anticoagulant overdose, presenting with headache, altered consciousness, or focal neurological deficits.
  • Massive gastrointestinal bleeding - Suggestive of advanced liver disease, anticoagulant toxicity, or gastric malignancy; presents with haematemesis, melaena, or hypotension.
  • Retroperitoneal bleeding - Can occur with anticoagulation or severe trauma, presenting with back pain, hypotension, and pallor.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Isolated epistaxis - May be the sole presenting symptom in mild von Willebrand disease.
  • Prolonged post-surgical bleeding - May occur in subclinical platelet or clotting factor disorders, such as mild haemophilia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Identifies thrombocytopenia; low platelets indicate possible ITP or bone marrow suppression.
  • Prothrombin time (PT) and activated partial thromboplastin time (APTT) - Prolonged in coagulation factor deficiencies (e.g., haemophilia A and B).
  • Bleeding time - Prolonged in platelet dysfunction, but rarely used now due to limited specificity.
  • Von Willebrand factor antigen - Reduced in von Willebrand disease, confirming the diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of easy bruising, heavy menstrual bleeding, and prolonged bleeding after minor cuts. Physical examination reveals scattered ecchymoses and petechiae. FBC shows thrombocytopenia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) - Distinguished by isolated thrombocytopenia without systemic illness.
  • Haemophilia - Prolonged APTT, normal platelet count; presents with haemarthrosis.
  • Von Willebrand disease - Mild prolongation of APTT, reduced von Willebrand factor antigen.
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) - Prolonged PT/APTT, thrombocytopenia, schistocytes on blood film.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • ITP - Corticosteroids for initial treatment; IV immunoglobulin for severe cases.
  • Haemophilia - Factor VIII or IX replacement therapy for bleeding episodes.
  • Von Willebrand disease - Desmopressin or von Willebrand factor concentrates.
  • DIC - Treat underlying cause, supportive care with platelets or fresh frozen plasma.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bleeding & Bruising"][/sc]
  • Chronic joint damage - Seen in haemophilia due to recurrent haemarthrosis.
  • Intracranial haemorrhage - High risk in severe bleeding disorders, potentially fatal.
  • Anaemia - Common in chronic or severe bleeding episodes, requiring transfusion in severe cases.
