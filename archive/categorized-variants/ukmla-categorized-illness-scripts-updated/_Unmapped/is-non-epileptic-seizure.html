Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Non-epileptic seizures (NES) are episodes that resemble epileptic seizures but are not associated with abnormal electrical discharges in the brain. They are considered a manifestation of psychological or emotional distress and often occur in individuals with a history of trauma or psychiatric conditions. NES are classified as psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES) and are part of the broader category of functional neurological disorders (FND).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 2-33 per 100,000 people.
  • UK hospital prevalence: NES accounts for 20-30% of cases referred to seizure clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar prevalence rates globally, with variations based on cultural and healthcare factors.
  • More common in women than men (approximately 3:1 ratio), with onset typically in young adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • NES is thought to result from a complex interplay of psychological stressors and abnormal brain functioning. Emotional stress, particularly stemming from trauma (e.g., abuse, neglect), can manifest as physical symptoms due to altered brain pathways. In NES, areas of the brain related to emotional regulation, such as the limbic system, interact abnormally with motor control areas, leading to seizure-like movements.
  • No abnormal electrical activity occurs during NES, unlike true epileptic seizures, and the condition is often associated with dissociation, conversion disorder, or somatisation disorder.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically during stressful or emotionally challenging situations; often in late adolescence or early adulthood.
  • Course: NES episodes can occur intermittently over years, often increasing in frequency if psychological distress remains unresolved.
  • Duration: Individual episodes last longer than epileptic seizures, often up to several minutes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Seizure-like episodes - Sudden, involuntary movements that may resemble epileptic seizures but lack the typical postictal phase. Pathophysiology relates to dissociative or conversion disorders.
  • Eye closure during episodes - Unlike in true epileptic seizures where the eyes are typically open, patients with NES often have their eyes closed tightly.
  • Absence of tongue biting or incontinence - These features, more common in epileptic seizures, are usually absent in NES.
  • Emotional triggers - Episodes often occur during or after periods of psychological stress or trauma, reflecting the underlying emotional distress.
  • Dissociation - Some patients report feeling detached from their surroundings or body, often as a defence mechanism to cope with trauma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Asynchronous limb movements - Movements during NES are often out of sync, unlike the coordinated rhythmic movements of epileptic seizures, reflecting the non-neurological basis of the episode.
  • Preserved awareness - During NES, patients may retain partial awareness, differentiating it from epileptic seizures where consciousness is often impaired.
  • Lack of postictal confusion - Patients recover quickly without the confusion or lethargy typical of the postictal state seen in epileptic seizures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Severe trauma history - Suggests a strong psychological component, possibly requiring mental health intervention to prevent worsening of NES.
  • Sudden onset in adulthood - May suggest an alternative diagnosis, such as a structural brain lesion or tumour, if not previously identified.
  • Progressively worsening episodes - Could indicate co-existing epilepsy or a different neurological condition, necessitating further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Intermittent NES without psychological distress - May occur in patients without clear trauma or psychological stress, leading to misdiagnosis. Pathophysiology could involve subconscious factors or unrecognised trauma.
  • Co-occurring epileptic and non-epileptic seizures - Some patients may have both types of seizures, making diagnosis more difficult. Misinterpretation of symptoms may lead to overmedication or undertreatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Video EEG monitoring - NES can be differentiated from epileptic seizures by the absence of abnormal electrical activity during episodes. EEG shows normal patterns despite seizure-like movements.
  • Psychiatric evaluation - Assessment for underlying trauma, dissociation, or psychiatric disorders such as anxiety or depression is essential for diagnosis.
  • Neuroimaging (MRI/CT) - Used to rule out structural brain abnormalities that could mimic NES, such as brain tumours or vascular malformations.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old woman presents with episodes of jerking movements lasting 5-10 minutes, with her eyes closed, occurring after arguments or stressful events. She remains conscious throughout and has no postictal confusion. EEG is normal, and psychiatric evaluation reveals a history of childhood trauma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy - Differentiated by abnormal EEG findings, tongue biting, and postictal confusion.
  • Syncope - Characterised by a loss of consciousness, often with a preceding prodrome (e.g., lightheadedness), and a shorter duration of motor symptoms.
  • Panic disorder - May involve hyperventilation and feelings of impending doom, but lacks the motor features of NES.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Psychotherapy (CBT) - Cognitive behavioural therapy is the mainstay of treatment, addressing underlying psychological stressors.
  • Psychiatric referral - Referral for treatment of co-morbid psychiatric conditions such as depression, anxiety, or PTSD.
  • Patient education - Explaining the diagnosis and removing the stigma can help in reducing the frequency of episodes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Misdiagnosis as epilepsy - Leads to unnecessary use of anti-seizure medications with potential side effects and social implications.
  • Chronic disability - Recurrent episodes can lead to significant social and occupational impairment if left untreated.
  • Co-existing psychiatric disorders - Anxiety, depression, and PTSD may worsen if not concurrently managed.
