Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS) is a serious, life-threatening complication of diabetes mellitus, characterised by profound hyperglycaemia (typically >30 mmol/L), hyperosmolality (effective osmolality >320 mOsm/kg), and severe dehydration without significant ketoacidosis (ketones absent or minimally elevated).
  • HHS is more commonly seen in type 2 diabetes and often triggered by infection, illness, or inadequate insulin therapy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare but significant, primarily in elderly individuals with type 2 diabetes.
  • UK hospital prevalence: HHS accounts for approximately 1% of diabetes-related hospital admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: HHS is less common than diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), with higher incidence in regions with limited access to diabetes management resources.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Reduced insulin activity leads to hyperglycaemia and osmotic diuresis.
  • Dehydration occurs as a result of excessive water loss through osmotic diuresis, exacerbated by inadequate fluid intake.
  • Hyperosmolality results from high plasma glucose, pulling water from intracellular to extracellular spaces, causing cellular dehydration.
  • Ketogenesis is minimal due to residual insulin activity, distinguishing HHS from DKA.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Develops over days to weeks, slower than diabetic ketoacidosis.
  • Early stages involve progressive hyperglycaemia and dehydration.
  • Late stages include severe dehydration, altered consciousness, and potentially life-threatening complications such as thromboembolism.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Polyuria - Caused by osmotic diuresis due to severe hyperglycaemia.
  • Polydipsia - Intense thirst due to dehydration and hyperosmolality.
  • Fatigue and weakness - Results from dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and inadequate glucose utilisation.
  • Confusion or altered mental state - Related to hyperosmolality and potential cerebral dehydration.
  • Weight loss - Secondary to fluid depletion and catabolic processes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor due to fluid loss.
  • Hypotension and tachycardia - Secondary to volume depletion.
  • Neurological deficits - Focal deficits or seizures due to hyperosmolality.
  • Reduced level of consciousness - Advanced cases may present with stupor or coma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status - Suggests severe hyperosmolality or cerebral oedema.
  • Severe hypotension - May indicate profound dehydration or shock.
  • Signs of thromboembolism - Chest pain or limb swelling may suggest complications such as pulmonary embolism or deep vein thrombosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Minimal hyperglycaemia - Rare in patients with concurrent renal dysfunction.
  • Absence of altered mental state - Can occur in early stages or mild cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Plasma glucose - Typically >30 mmol/L, confirms hyperglycaemia.
  • Serum osmolality - >320 mOsm/kg; elevated due to hyperglycaemia and dehydration.
  • Serum ketones - Absent or minimally elevated, distinguishing HHS from DKA.
  • Electrolytes - Sodium may appear falsely low due to glucose dilution.
  • Blood gases - No significant acidosis (pH >7.3).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • An 80-year-old male with poorly controlled type 2 diabetes, presenting with a 1-week history of increasing fatigue, confusion, polyuria, and dehydration. Investigations reveal glucose >30 mmol/L and serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg without significant ketosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - Distinguished by significant ketosis and metabolic acidosis.
  • Sepsis - May mimic altered consciousness and dehydration but typically involves infection signs.
  • Stroke - Neurological symptoms dominate; glucose and osmolality findings help differentiate.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • IV fluids: Initial resuscitation with isotonic saline to address dehydration.
  • Insulin: Low-dose infusion to gradually correct hyperglycaemia while monitoring potassium.
  • Electrolyte replacement: Monitor and correct potassium and sodium levels.
  • Monitor for complications: Regular assessments for thromboembolism and neurological changes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Thromboembolic events - Common due to hypercoagulable state in dehydration.
  • Seizures - Secondary to severe hyperosmolality and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Renal failure - Prolonged dehydration may lead to acute kidney injury.
