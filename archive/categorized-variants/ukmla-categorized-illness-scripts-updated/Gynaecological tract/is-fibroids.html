Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids, also known as uterine leiomyomas, are benign smooth muscle tumours of the uterus. They are non-cancerous growths that can vary in size, location, and number, and may be found within the uterine wall, projecting into the uterine cavity, or bulging towards the outer surface of the uterus. They are hormone-dependent, often influenced by oestrogen and progesterone.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 1 in 3 women will develop fibroids at some point during their reproductive years.
  • Prevalence in UK hospital settings: Fibroids are one of the most common reasons for gynaecological surgeries such as hysterectomy and myomectomy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Global prevalence is estimated at 20-70% depending on the population studied and diagnostic criteria.
  • Higher prevalence is observed in women of African descent, typically with larger and more symptomatic fibroids.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids arise from the smooth muscle layer (myometrium) of the uterus. They are monoclonal tumours, meaning they arise from a single smooth muscle cell that undergoes genetic mutations. The growth of fibroids is influenced by oestrogen and progesterone, which stimulate the proliferation of smooth muscle cells and extracellular matrix deposition.
  • Genetic alterations: Specific mutations in the MED12 gene and HMGA2 gene are commonly associated with fibroid development.
  • Oestrogen and progesterone promote the growth and maintenance of fibroids, explaining why fibroids often shrink after menopause when hormonal levels decline.
  • They may grow slowly or rapidly, depending on factors such as hormonal fluctuations, pregnancy, and genetic predisposition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids usually develop during reproductive years and may remain asymptomatic or grow over time. They tend to stabilise or regress after menopause due to reduced hormone levels.
  • In some cases, rapid growth during pregnancy is common, likely due to increased hormone levels.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids may rarely present with acute pain due to degeneration or torsion of a pedunculated fibroid.
  • Chronic, unexplained anaemia may be the only clue in cases where menorrhagia is not overtly reported.

  • Medical management â€“ Includes hormonal therapies such as the levonorgestrel intrauterine system (LNG-IUS), combined oral contraceptives, or GnRH agonists to shrink fibroids and manage bleeding.
