Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
Pulmonary Embolism Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE) is the blockage of one or more pulmonary arteries by a thrombus (blood clot) that has usually travelled from a deep vein in the legs or pelvis (deep vein thrombosis, DVT). This obstruction compromises the lung's ability to oxygenate blood effectively, potentially leading to hypoxia and right heart strain.
Pulmonary Embolism Epidemiology, Risk Factors & Prevention [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Embolism Risk Factors: Recent surgery, prolonged immobility (e.g., long-haul travel, hospitalisation), malignancy, pregnancy, oestrogen use (oral contraceptives, HRT), thrombophilia (e.g., Factor V Leiden mutation), smoking, and obesity increase the risk of venous thromboembolism.
  • Prevention: Early mobilisation, compression stockings, and pharmacological thromboprophylaxis (low molecular weight heparin, DOACs) in high-risk patients reduce PE risk. Lifestyle measures such as weight management, smoking cessation, and regular movement during travel also help prevent PE.
Pulmonary Embolism Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • PE usually originates from a thrombus in the deep veins of the lower extremities or pelvis. Upon detachment, the thrombus embolises, travelling through the venous system to the right side of the heart and eventually becoming lodged in a pulmonary artery.
  • The obstruction leads to increased pulmonary vascular resistance, right ventricular strain, and impaired gas exchange. The extent of the impact depends on the size of the embolus and the affected pulmonary artery branches.
  • Hypoxaemia results from impaired perfusion in ventilated lung regions (ventilation-perfusion mismatch), while right heart strain can lead to heart failure if untreated.
Pulmonary Embolism Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • PE can present acutely, typically over hours to days, with sudden onset symptoms such as shortness of breath and chest pain.
  • In subacute or chronic PE, symptoms may develop more gradually over weeks to months, often in cases of recurrent small emboli leading to progressive pulmonary hypertension.
Pulmonary Embolism Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Occurs due to impaired gas exchange and reduced oxygenation. It is the most common symptom, present in over 80% of cases.
  • Pleuritic chest pain - Caused by inflammation of the pleura due to ischaemia of the lung parenchyma. This pain is sharp and worsens with breathing or coughing.
  • Haemoptysis - May occur if the infarcted lung tissue causes capillary leakage into the alveoli, although it is less common.
  • Syncope - Sudden loss of consciousness may result from reduced cardiac output due to right heart strain or a massive PE that compromises circulation.
  • Calf or thigh pain/swelling - This suggests DVT as the source of the embolism.
Pulmonary Embolism Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - A compensatory mechanism to improve oxygenation due to hypoxaemia.
  • Tachycardia - Results from sympathetic activation due to hypoxia and right ventricular strain.
  • Hypoxia (low oxygen saturation) - Reflects the impaired oxygen exchange in the lungs.
  • Jugular venous distension - Indicates increased pressure in the right side of the heart due to strain or failure.
  • Low-grade fever - Can occur in response to inflammation from lung tissue ischaemia.
Pulmonary Embolism Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Massive PE (hypotension, shock, or cardiac arrest) - Suggests large central emboli obstructing a significant portion of the pulmonary vasculature, causing cardiovascular collapse.
  • Syncope or sudden death - Can occur if PE causes immediate and profound cardiac failure or arrhythmias due to hypoxia.
  • Signs of right heart failure - Peripheral oedema, ascites, or hepatomegaly indicate advanced right ventricular dysfunction, a severe consequence of untreated or recurrent PE.
Pulmonary Embolism Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Isolated dyspnoea without chest pain - Can occur in smaller or subacute PEs where pleuritic pain does not develop.
  • Minimal symptoms in elderly or immobile patients - These patients may present with vague complaints of fatigue or weakness rather than classic symptoms of PE.
Pulmonary Embolism Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA) - The gold standard for PE diagnosis, showing filling defects in pulmonary arteries. It directly visualises the emboli.
  • D-dimer test - Elevated in the presence of thrombus formation, but non-specific. Used to rule out PE in low-probability cases.
  • ECG - May show sinus tachycardia, right heart strain patterns (e.g., S1Q3T3, right bundle branch block), although non-diagnostic.
  • Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) - Can show hypoxaemia and respiratory alkalosis (due to hyperventilation).
  • Venous Doppler Ultrasound - Used to detect DVT as the source of embolism.
Pulmonary Embolism Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with sudden onset pleuritic chest pain and shortness of breath. She has a history of recent long-haul travel. On examination, she is tachycardic and hypoxic.
Pulmonary Embolism Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - Associated with fever, productive cough, and consolidation on CXR. Lacks pleuritic chest pain and the rapid onset of PE.
  • Pneumothorax - Sudden chest pain and dyspnoea, but typically accompanied by hyper-resonance and absent breath sounds on the affected side.
  • Acute Myocardial Infarction (MI) - Chest pain in MI is typically central and crushing, often associated with ST changes on ECG.
Pulmonary Embolism Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Anticoagulation - Immediate treatment with low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), followed by long-term anticoagulation for 3-6 months.
  • Thrombolysis - Indicated in massive PE with haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension or shock). Alteplase is commonly used.
  • IVC Filter - Considered in patients who cannot be anticoagulated or have recurrent PEs despite treatment.
Pulmonary Embolism Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) - Results from unresolved emboli leading to progressive right heart failure.
  • Right heart failure - Occurs if right ventricular strain becomes severe and is untreated.
  • Recurrent PE - Increases the risk of further episodes and can lead to cumulative right ventricular dysfunction.
