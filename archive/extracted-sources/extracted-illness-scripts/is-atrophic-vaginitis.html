Atrophic Vaginitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Atrophic vaginitis, also known as genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM), is a chronic condition characterised by thinning, drying, and inflammation of the vaginal walls due to oestrogen deficiency. It is commonly seen in postmenopausal women and may involve urinary symptoms due to similar changes in the lower urinary tract.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, atrophic vaginitis is estimated to affect up to 50% of postmenopausal women, though only about 20-25% seek medical help.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence mirrors these figures, with increasing awareness leading to more diagnoses.
  • Hospital-based data in the UK shows a significant number of cases being referred from gynaecology and urology outpatient departments.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Oestrogen deficiency following menopause leads to reduced glycogen in the vaginal epithelium, causing decreased lactobacilli levels.
  • Lower lactobacilli result in increased vaginal pH, disrupting the normal microflora.
  • Thinning of the vaginal epithelium and loss of elasticity occur due to reduced collagen and vascularisation.
  • Decreased blood flow leads to dryness and impaired lubrication during sexual activity.
  • Similar changes in the lower urinary tract contribute to urinary symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset, typically months to years after menopause.
  • Symptoms progressively worsen without treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginal dryness - Results from reduced glandular secretions due to oestrogen deficiency.
  • Dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse occurs due to loss of elasticity and lubrication.
  • Pruritus or irritation - Caused by epithelial thinning and increased pH-related irritation.
  • Postcoital spotting - Due to fragile vaginal mucosa and minor trauma during intercourse.
  • Urinary symptoms - Dysuria, urgency, and increased frequency due to atrophic changes in the urinary tract.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Pale, thin vaginal mucosa - Loss of oestrogen leads to atrophic changes.
  • Decreased vaginal elasticity - Reduced collagen and hydration in tissues.
  • Vaginal stenosis - Seen in more advanced or untreated cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - May indicate endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy, requiring further investigation.
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections - Can be secondary to atrophic changes predisposing to infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pelvic pain without obvious cause - Due to underlying inflammation and atrophy.
  • Isolated urinary symptoms without vaginal complaints - Particularly in cases where urinary atrophy dominates.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginal pH testing - pH >5.0 is suggestive of atrophic vaginitis.
  • Microscopy - Shows reduced lactobacilli and increased parabasal epithelial cells.
  • Pelvic ultrasound - To rule out other causes of symptoms like postmenopausal bleeding.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with progressive vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, and urinary urgency over the past 6 months, with no postmenopausal bleeding.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Lichen sclerosus - Usually presents with white plaques and severe pruritus.
  • Vulvovaginal candidiasis - Characterised by discharge and itching rather than dryness.
  • Urinary tract infection - Dysuria and frequency are acute rather than chronic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Topical oestrogen therapy - First-line treatment to restore vaginal epithelium integrity.
  • Lubricants and moisturisers - To alleviate symptoms of dryness.
  • Systemic hormone replacement therapy (HRT) - For women with concurrent menopausal symptoms affecting quality of life.
  • Non-hormonal options - For patients contraindicated for oestrogen therapy, such as ospemifene (a selective oestrogen receptor modulator).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atrophic Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections - Due to changes in the urogenital epithelium.
  • Sexual dysfunction - Secondary to dyspareunia and associated psychological distress.
  • Urogenital prolapse - Chronic atrophic changes can predispose to pelvic floor weakness.
