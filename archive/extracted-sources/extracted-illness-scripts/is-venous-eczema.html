Venous Eczema
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Venous eczema, also known as stasis dermatitis or gravitational eczema, is a chronic inflammatory skin condition that occurs in the lower extremities due to venous insufficiency. It is characterised by erythema, scaling, and pruritus in the areas surrounding varicose veins, especially around the ankles. The condition is often associated with chronic venous hypertension, leading to fluid accumulation and inflammation of the skin.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Venous eczema affects approximately 1% of the general population and is more common in the elderly. In the UK, it is estimated to affect about 3-5% of the general practice population over the age of 50. The condition accounts for around 70% of leg ulcer presentations in UK hospitals. Worldwide prevalence mirrors that in Western nations, particularly where sedentary lifestyles and obesity are more common.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Venous eczema occurs as a result of chronic venous insufficiency, where the valves of the deep veins in the legs fail to function properly. This leads to blood pooling in the lower extremities, resulting in increased venous pressure. The elevated pressure causes fluid to leak into the surrounding tissues (oedema), leading to inflammation and skin barrier dysfunction. The persistent inflammation triggers an immune response, resulting in the characteristic erythema, pruritus, and scaling of venous eczema. Chronic inflammation can lead to fibrosis and skin thickening if left untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Initial erythema, pruritus, and mild oedema around the ankles or lower legs.
  • Subacute Phase: Ongoing inflammation with increasing scaling, hyperpigmentation, and possible development of vesicles or blisters.
  • Chronic Phase: Thickened skin (lichenification), fibrosis, and development of venous ulcers. Without proper treatment, recurrent cycles of inflammation may occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Pruritus (itching) - Due to inflammation of the skin caused by chronic venous stasis and irritation from oedema.
  • Pain or discomfort - Often due to oedema and inflammation of the lower limbs, with increased sensitivity to pressure.
  • Swelling (oedema) - Caused by fluid leakage from veins under increased pressure due to venous insufficiency.
  • Heaviness in the legs - This occurs due to prolonged venous stasis and oedema, causing discomfort, especially after prolonged standing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Erythema - Redness of the skin caused by inflammation and increased blood flow due to venous congestion.
  • Scaling - Flaky or peeling skin due to chronic inflammation and impaired skin barrier function.
  • Hyperpigmentation - Darkening of the skin due to hemosiderin deposition from red blood cell leakage as venous pressure damages capillaries.
  • Varicose veins - Visible, dilated superficial veins caused by increased venous pressure and valve failure.
  • Venous ulcers - Open sores that occur in chronic cases due to breakdown of the skin from prolonged inflammation and venous stasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Rapidly spreading erythema and warmth - May indicate secondary bacterial infection (cellulitis), which can lead to sepsis if untreated.
  • Sudden severe leg pain with swelling - Could signify deep vein thrombosis (DVT), a serious complication of venous insufficiency.
  • Venous ulcers with necrotic tissue - Indicate severe and advanced disease, with a risk of chronic wound infection and osteomyelitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Non-pruritic lesions - In some cases, venous eczema may present without the typical itching, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Localised vesicles - Small fluid-filled blisters that can be mistaken for other dermatological conditions like contact dermatitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Duplex ultrasound - Used to assess venous insufficiency by visualising blood flow in the veins. Abnormal flow or reflux indicates valve dysfunction.
  • Ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI) - Used to rule out arterial disease. A normal ABPI (0.9â€“1.2) is expected in venous eczema, but an abnormal result (<0.8) suggests arterial involvement.
  • Skin biopsy - Rarely needed, but may be performed if the diagnosis is unclear. Histology will show spongiosis and chronic inflammatory changes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female presents with bilateral lower leg swelling, erythema, and itching. She reports gradual onset over several months and associated heaviness in her legs, with visible varicose veins and no history of trauma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis - Differs by typically having a history of exposure to an allergen or irritant, and lesions are usually localised to the point of contact.
  • Cellulitis - Acute onset with marked tenderness, erythema, and systemic symptoms (fever). In contrast, venous eczema has a chronic course.
  • Arterial ulcers - Usually found on the toes or pressure points, and are associated with a reduced ABPI, unlike venous ulcers in venous eczema.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Compression therapy - The first-line treatment using graduated compression stockings to reduce venous pressure and oedema.
  • Topical corticosteroids - To reduce inflammation and pruritus. Potent corticosteroids like betamethasone are commonly used in the UK.
  • Emollients - Regular use of moisturisers to maintain skin barrier function and prevent further irritation.
  • Venous surgery - In cases of severe venous insufficiency, surgical interventions such as vein ablation or sclerotherapy may be considered.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Venous Eczema"][/sc]
  • Venous ulcers - Occur in up to 30% of patients with chronic venous eczema, particularly in those with untreated or recurrent disease.
  • Cellulitis - Bacterial infection of the skin that can spread quickly and lead to systemic infection if not treated promptly.
  • Lipodermatosclerosis - A form of chronic skin inflammation and fibrosis leading to hard, painful areas in the lower legs.
