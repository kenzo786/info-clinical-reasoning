Pyelonephritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis is an acute, bacterial infection of the renal pelvis and kidney, commonly resulting from ascending urinary tract infection (UTI) that has spread from the bladder to the upper urinary tract. It involves inflammation of the kidney tissues and collecting system, often accompanied by systemic symptoms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 12 per 10,000 women and 3 per 10,000 men annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Pyelonephritis accounts for 2â€“3% of all emergency admissions for UTI-related cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated annual incidence is 25-30 cases per 10,000 women and 7-10 per 10,000 men.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Most cases of pyelonephritis are caused by ascending infection, where uropathogenic bacteria (e.g., *E. coli*) enter the bladder and migrate to the kidneys. Factors like vesicoureteral reflux, obstruction, or immune suppression can facilitate this progression.
  • Bacterial adherence to urothelial cells triggers local inflammation and cytokine release, causing tissue oedema and inflammatory cell infiltration, leading to kidney damage if untreated.
  • Severe cases may cause renal abscesses or bacteraemia due to systemic spread.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over hours to a few days, with fever, flank pain, and urinary symptoms.
  • Resolution phase: With appropriate treatment, symptoms generally resolve within 7â€“14 days.
  • Complicated cases: In cases of delayed treatment, infection may progress, leading to abscess formation or systemic complications requiring longer treatment courses.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Fever - Systemic inflammatory response due to bacterial infection, often accompanied by chills.
  • Flank pain - Localised inflammation of renal parenchyma causes referred pain to the flank.
  • Dysuria and frequency - Indicates concurrent lower UTI, as infection often ascends from the bladder.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Systemic response to severe infection or pyrexia.
  • Malaise and fatigue - General signs of systemic infection and inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Costovertebral angle tenderness (CVA tenderness) - Localised tenderness due to inflammation of the kidney capsule.
  • Fever >38Â°C - Indicator of systemic infection, often with rapid onset in pyelonephritis.
  • Tachycardia - Common in response to fever and infection-induced dehydration.
  • Hypotension (in severe cases) - May occur if the infection is severe and associated with sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension - Suggestive of sepsis or septic shock; indicates urgent need for fluid resuscitation and antibiotics.
  • Persistent high fever despite antibiotics - May indicate abscess formation or resistant organisms.
  • Severe flank pain unresponsive to treatment - May signify renal abscess or obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - May present with confusion, weakness, or functional decline rather than classic symptoms.
  • Immunocompromised patients - More likely to have milder symptoms or afebrile presentations despite severe infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Leucocytes and nitrites commonly found due to infection. White cell casts may suggest upper tract involvement.
  • Urine culture - Confirms pathogen, typically *E. coli*. Essential to tailor antibiotic therapy.
  • Blood tests - Elevated CRP, ESR, and white cell count indicate systemic infection.
  • Imaging (Ultrasound or CT) - Indicated in complicated cases or if abscess or obstruction is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • 32-year-old female with acute onset of high fever, right flank pain, dysuria, and nausea, progressing over 24 hours with CVA tenderness on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute cystitis - Lacks systemic symptoms (fever, flank pain) but presents with dysuria and frequency.
  • Renal colic - Severe flank pain without fever; haematuria often present without infection.
  • Appendicitis - Fever and right lower quadrant pain but without dysuria; location of pain differs.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - May have fever and abdominal pain but is associated with vaginal discharge rather than dysuria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Empiric antibiotics like ciprofloxacin or co-amoxiclav, then tailored based on culture results (NICE guidelines).
  • IV fluids - For dehydration and to support renal perfusion in severe cases.
  • Pain management - Paracetamol or NSAIDs as needed, provided renal function allows.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Renal abscess - May develop if infection localises and progresses despite treatment.
  • Sepsis and septic shock - Resulting from bacteria spreading into the bloodstream; potentially life-threatening.
  • Chronic pyelonephritis - Recurrent infections may lead to renal scarring, hypertension, and chronic kidney disease.
