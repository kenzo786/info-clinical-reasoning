Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • An osteoporotic vertebral fracture is a type of compression fracture affecting the vertebral body due to weakened bone from osteoporosis. These fractures most commonly occur in the thoracic and lumbar spine and are frequently associated with minimal or no trauma. They contribute significantly to pain, disability, and increased morbidity in older adults.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Osteoporotic vertebral fractures are common, with a prevalence of about 12% in individuals over 50 years of age.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospitalised patients over 65 with vertebral fractures due to osteoporosis account for around 10% of admissions for fragility fractures.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, approximately 200 million people suffer from osteoporosis, with vertebral fractures being the most common osteoporotic fracture type.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Bone Density Reduction: Osteoporosis leads to decreased bone density and deterioration of bone microarchitecture, weakening the vertebrae and increasing susceptibility to fractures.
  • Mechanical Load: Vertebrae bear significant mechanical load, particularly in the lower thoracic and upper lumbar regions, making these areas more prone to compression fractures.
  • Vertebral Collapse: Under stress, weakened vertebrae may collapse, resulting in a compression fracture. This can be asymptomatic or cause pain if the bone collapses further or impacts surrounding structures.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Pain typically starts abruptly with a fracture and may persist for weeks to months, especially with further compression.
  • Subacute to Chronic Phase: Persistent pain, loss of height, and potential spinal deformities may develop over months to years if untreated, affecting quality of life.
  • Progression: Risk of subsequent fractures and spinal deformity increases if osteoporosis remains unmanaged.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Back Pain: Localised back pain is the most common symptom, typically in the mid to lower back, due to collapse and instability of the fractured vertebra.
  • Height Loss: Loss of height occurs with progressive vertebral collapse, causing shortening of the spine.
  • Kyphosis: Forward bending of the spine, or kyphosis, is due to multiple fractures leading to vertebral wedging and deformity.
  • Asymptomatic: Many cases are asymptomatic and are discovered incidentally, as pain may not always accompany vertebral collapse.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Localised Tenderness: Tenderness over the affected vertebrae can indicate a recent or healing fracture.
  • Kyphosis (Dowagerâ€™s Hump): A visible curve in the upper back from vertebral wedging in chronic cases, affecting posture and balance.
  • Loss of Height: Observable shortening of the stature due to cumulative vertebral collapse, often measured by patient history and physical assessment.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Severe, Sudden-Onset Pain: May indicate an acute vertebral fracture. Severe pain warrants urgent imaging to exclude further complications.
  • Neurological Deficits: Weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction suggests spinal cord compression, necessitating immediate assessment.
  • Unexplained Weight Loss and Night Pain: Concerning for underlying malignancy or infection, which may mimic vertebral fractures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Upper Cervical Pain: Rare but possible, vertebral fractures higher in the cervical spine may initially be mistaken for other neck pain causes.
  • No Pain with Deformity: Vertebral fractures may present with noticeable kyphosis but no reported pain, leading to missed diagnoses, especially in elderly patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • X-ray (Thoracolumbar Spine): Shows vertebral collapse or wedging. Loss of vertebral height >20% confirms fracture.

  • MRI: Useful to differentiate acute fractures (oedema present) from chronic ones.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old female presents with sudden onset mid-thoracic back pain following a minor fall, with a known history of osteoporosis, height loss, and mild kyphosis on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Spinal Metastasis: Often accompanied by night pain, weight loss, and neurological symptoms.
  • Discitis/Osteomyelitis: Presents with fever, elevated inflammatory markers, and systemic symptoms, unlike the typically localised pain in vertebral fractures.
  • Muscular Strain: Usually lacks height loss or kyphosis, with pain limited to movement rather than compression.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Pain Management: NSAIDs or acetaminophen for mild pain, with opioids for severe pain. Gradual reduction of analgesics as pain subsides.
  • Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation: Essential for bone health, preventing further fractures.
  • Bisphosphonates: First-line for osteoporosis management in reducing further fracture risk (e.g., alendronic acid).
  • Kyphoplasty/Vertebroplasty: Considered for severe pain unresponsive to conservative management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain: Common due to persistent vertebral instability and further height loss.
  • Spinal Deformity: Severe kyphosis may develop, leading to breathing issues and decreased mobility.
  • Increased Fracture Risk: Osteoporosis-related fractures can lead to a cycle of further fragility fractures.
