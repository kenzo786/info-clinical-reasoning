Hyperglycaemic Crisis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Hyperglycaemic crises are acute complications of diabetes mellitus, characterised by severe hyperglycaemia. The two main types are Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) and Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS). Both are life-threatening conditions associated with significant dehydration, metabolic derangement, and altered consciousness.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): Insulin deficiency and counter-regulatory hormone excess (glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine) lead to hyperglycaemia, lipolysis, and production of ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate). Ketonaemia causes metabolic acidosis.
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Insulin deficiency, usually less severe than in DKA, prevents ketosis but results in extreme hyperglycaemia. Hyperosmolality and dehydration develop from osmotic diuresis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • DKA: Onset is rapid, typically over hours to 1-2 days, triggered by infection, insulin omission, or new-onset diabetes.
  • HHS: Develops more insidiously over several days to weeks, often in response to an acute illness or poor glycaemic control.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Polyuria - Osmotic diuresis from hyperglycaemia increases urine output, leading to significant fluid loss.
  • Polydipsia - Resulting dehydration triggers excessive thirst.
  • Fatigue - Due to hyperglycaemia, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Abdominal pain (common in DKA) - Related to acidosis and electrolyte disturbances (especially in children and young adults).
  • Altered mental state - Ranges from confusion to coma, more common in HHS due to hyperosmolality.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Frequently seen in DKA due to metabolic acidosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, and hypotension from fluid losses.
  • Kussmaul breathing (in DKA) - Deep, laboured breathing is a compensatory mechanism for metabolic acidosis.
  • Fruity breath odour (DKA) - Caused by exhalation of acetone, a byproduct of ketone metabolism.
  • Altered mental status - Ranging from confusion to stupor or coma, especially in HHS due to severe hyperosmolality.
  • Tachycardia - Compensation for hypovolaemia and acidosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration and hypotension - Indicates profound fluid loss, may progress to shock.
  • Coma - Suggests severe hyperosmolality or cerebral oedema, especially in HHS.
  • Refractory acidosis - May indicate impending respiratory failure or multi-organ dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • DKA without significant hyperglycaemia (euglycaemic DKA) - Occurs in patients using SGLT2 inhibitors or in pregnancy.
  • HHS with mild hyperglycaemia - Seen in some elderly patients with impaired thirst sensation and access to water.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Blood glucose - Elevated in both DKA (usually >11 mmol/L) and HHS (>30 mmol/L).
  • Serum ketones - High in DKA (beta-hydroxybutyrate >3 mmol/L); absent or minimal in HHS.
  • Arterial blood gases - Metabolic acidosis in DKA (pH <7.3, HCO3 <15 mmol/L); typically normal or only mildly abnormal in HHS.
  • Serum osmolality - Markedly elevated in HHS (>320 mOsm/kg).
  • Electrolytes - Sodium may be falsely low due to hyperglycaemia; potassium levels vary, and careful monitoring is crucial.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old female with type 1 diabetes presents with 2 days of polyuria, polydipsia, nausea, and abdominal pain, progressing to confusion. Blood glucose is 25 mmol/L, ketones are elevated, and she is found to have Kussmaul respirations with a pH of 7.1.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Lactic acidosis - Distinguished by history of shock or hypoxia, no significant hyperglycaemia, and elevated lactate levels.
  • Alcoholic ketoacidosis - Typically seen in chronic alcoholics, with a history of alcohol binge, without marked hyperglycaemia or dehydration.
  • Sepsis - Overlapping symptoms of altered mental status and dehydration, but with underlying infection and no significant hyperglycaemia or ketosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Fluid resuscitation - 0.9% saline is typically given to correct dehydration and hypotension.
  • Insulin therapy - IV insulin infusion for DKA (fixed rate of 0.1 units/kg/hour) until ketones clear and acidosis resolves. Insulin initiation is delayed in HHS until adequate fluid replacement is achieved.
  • Electrolyte monitoring and replacement - Potassium is crucial, with supplementation required if levels fall below 5 mmol/L.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema - Especially in younger patients with DKA, results from rapid osmotic shifts during treatment.
  • Thromboembolism - Increased risk in HHS due to dehydration and hyperosmolality.
  • Acute kidney injury - Due to severe dehydration and poor perfusion.
