Dry Skin (Xerosis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Dry skin, or xerosis, is characterised by rough, scaly, and flaky skin resulting from reduced water content in the epidermis. It commonly affects exposed areas, such as the hands, legs, and face, and is often associated with environmental factors, systemic diseases, or ageing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Estimated in up to 20% of visits due to dermatological complaints, especially in older adults.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Common in geriatric and dermatology units, with prevalence rates up to 50% in elderly hospitalised patients.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Affects all populations, though higher prevalence in regions with low humidity and colder climates.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Dry skin occurs due to a reduction in stratum corneum hydration, often from reduced natural moisturising factors (NMFs) like amino acids and urea, increased trans-epidermal water loss (TEWL), and an impaired lipid barrier.
  • Environmental factors such as low humidity, cold weather, or hot showers exacerbate this process by stripping the skin of lipids and moisture, further weakening the barrier.
  • With age, the skinâ€™s capacity to retain moisture decreases due to reduced sebaceous gland activity and fewer NMFs.
  • Systemic conditions, like hypothyroidism or renal disease, can also reduce skin hydration and impair the barrier, leading to xerosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Following exposure to harsh environmental conditions or irritants, skin may become temporarily dry but may recover with moisturisation.
  • Chronic: Persistent xerosis may develop with prolonged exposure to low humidity, repeated hot water contact, or in individuals with chronic illnesses.
  • Seasonal: Symptoms often worsen in winter (winter xerosis) due to cold, dry air, with improvement during more humid seasons.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Itching (Pruritus) - Commonly reported as skin dries and loses protective lipids, which increases TEWL and irritates nerve endings.
  • Tightness - Patients often feel a sense of tightness in areas of dry skin due to reduced elasticity as hydration decreases.
  • Rough or Scaly Skin - Occurs as a direct result of the disrupted barrier and loss of moisture, leading to uneven skin texture.
  • Flaking - Dead skin cells shed more visibly due to the compromised barrier, particularly noticeable in the extremities.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Visible Scaling - Fine to coarse scaling is common and indicates disrupted barrier function and dehydration of the stratum corneum.
  • Cracked Skin - In severe cases, especially on hands and feet, cracks may appear due to structural weakness from dehydration and loss of elasticity.
  • Erythema (Redness) - Often present due to low-level inflammation caused by barrier impairment and irritants.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Severe Itching or Intractable Itch - May suggest an underlying systemic condition (e.g., renal or hepatic dysfunction) requiring further investigation.
  • Extensive Cracks with Bleeding - Increases the risk of secondary bacterial infections; needs close monitoring and possibly antimicrobial treatment.
  • Thickened, Hyperkeratotic Skin - Suggests chronicity and possible association with other dermatological conditions, such as eczema or psoriasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Dry Skin Without Visible Scaling - In older adults, xerosis may present with a fine texture rather than typical scaling due to thinner skin.
  • Localised Intense Itching without Erythema - Can occur in elderly patients as "senile pruritus," often with minimal other signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Clinical Examination - Primary diagnosis based on visual assessment and tactile evaluation of skin texture, with no specific test for simple xerosis.
  • Skin Hydration Measurement - Corneometry may be used in research to measure stratum corneum hydration, showing reduced water content in affected areas.
  • Blood Tests (if Red Flags) - Serum creatinine, liver function tests, and thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) may be considered to identify underlying systemic conditions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old female presents with chronic, pruritic, scaly skin on the lower legs, worsening during winter, associated with a sense of skin tightness and occasional cracking.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Atopic Dermatitis - Differs with episodic intense itching, erythematous patches, and flexural distribution; often a personal or family history of atopy.
  • Psoriasis - Characterised by well-defined plaques with silvery scales, often on extensor surfaces and scalp, and may involve nail changes.
  • Ichthyosis Vulgaris - Genetic condition with widespread dry, fish-like scales; begins in childhood and lacks seasonal variation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dry Skin (Xerosis)"][/sc]
  • Emollients - First-line treatment; apply daily to restore barrier function (e.g., E45 cream, Cetraben).
  • Topical Steroids - For associated inflammation or itching, a mild steroid cream may be prescribed in short courses.
  • Humidifiers and Avoidance of Irritants - Use to reduce TEWL, and avoid hot showers and soaps that strip natural oils.
