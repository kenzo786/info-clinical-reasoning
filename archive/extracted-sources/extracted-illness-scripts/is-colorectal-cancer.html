Colorectal Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer is a malignant neoplasm that arises from the lining of the colon or rectum. It typically develops from adenomatous polyps, which undergo dysplasia and progress to invasive cancer over time. Colorectal cancer includes both colon and rectal cancers and is characterised by unregulated cellular growth that can lead to local invasion, metastasis, and systemic disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Colorectal cancer is the fourth most common cancer in the UK, with over 42,000 cases diagnosed annually. It is most common in individuals over 50 years old, and the lifetime risk is approximately 1 in 15 for men and 1 in 18 for women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 16,000 deaths from colorectal cancer occur in the UK each year. It represents around 10% of all cancer deaths.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Colorectal cancer is the third most common cancer globally, with over 1.9 million new cases and 935,000 deaths annually (GLOBOCAN 2020).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer typically begins as benign adenomatous polyps, which accumulate mutations in key oncogenes (e.g., KRAS) and tumour suppressor genes (e.g., APC, TP53). These mutations lead to the progression from normal mucosa to dysplasia and, ultimately, invasive carcinoma.
  • Early-stage cancer is often confined to the mucosa and submucosa, but as the tumour grows, it invades deeper into the bowel wall, potentially breaching the muscularis propria. This can lead to local invasion into adjacent organs, lymphatic spread, and haematogenous metastasis, particularly to the liver and lungs.
  • Microsatellite instability (MSI) and chromosomal instability are two common pathways in colorectal cancer development. MSI is associated with Lynch syndrome, while chromosomal instability is more common in sporadic cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: The development of colorectal cancer is insidious and often asymptomatic. The transformation from adenomatous polyp to carcinoma can take 10-15 years.
  • Intermediate Phase: Symptoms such as changes in bowel habits, rectal bleeding, and abdominal discomfort may arise as the tumour grows.
  • Late Phase: Advanced disease can present with weight loss, anaemia, bowel obstruction, or metastasis to organs like the liver or lungs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Rectal bleeding - Caused by mucosal disruption and ulceration of the tumour. Bleeding can be occult or overt.
  • Change in bowel habits - Alternating constipation and diarrhoea occur due to tumour obstruction or irritation of the bowel wall.
  • Abdominal pain - Often caused by tumour growth leading to partial obstruction or local invasion.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Indicative of advanced disease or systemic malignancy.
  • Fatigue - Often a result of chronic blood loss and iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients are diagnosed incidentally through screening, particularly in the early stages.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Due to iron deficiency anaemia from chronic blood loss.
  • Abdominal mass - A palpable mass may indicate locally advanced or metastatic disease.
  • Hepatomegaly - Can be a sign of liver metastasis.
  • Obstructive bowel signs - Including distension and hyperactive bowel sounds, suggestive of a mechanical obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained rectal bleeding - Especially in those over 50 years, indicates possible malignancy.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - In older adults, this raises concern for chronic blood loss due to colorectal cancer.
  • Change in bowel habits lasting more than six weeks - Especially alternating diarrhoea and constipation or new onset constipation, suggests obstructive pathology.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Indicates systemic involvement or metastasis.
  • Abdominal mass or pain - A mass could indicate advanced local disease, while pain may signify bowel obstruction or peritoneal involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Right-sided colorectal cancer - May present only with fatigue and anaemia without overt gastrointestinal symptoms, due to slow occult bleeding.
  • Rectal cancer - Can present with tenesmus or a feeling of incomplete evacuation rather than overt bleeding.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Faecal immunochemical test (FIT) - Detects blood in stool. Used for screening and diagnostic purposes. Positive results suggest occult bleeding, potentially from colorectal cancer.
  • Colonoscopy - Gold standard for diagnosis. Direct visualisation and biopsy of suspicious lesions. Polyps or tumours appear as irregular masses or ulcerated lesions.
  • CT colonography - Used in patients who cannot undergo colonoscopy. Can detect masses or polyps, particularly larger tumours.
  • CT scan (chest, abdomen, pelvis) - Used to assess metastatic spread, particularly to the liver and lungs.
  • Cancer staging (TNM system) - Based on depth of invasion (T), lymph node involvement (N), and distant metastasis (M).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of rectal bleeding, altered bowel habits (constipation), and unintentional weight loss. He reports increasing fatigue and has a palpable mass in the left lower abdomen.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids - Unlike colorectal cancer, bleeding is often associated with defecation, and the blood is bright red. No systemic symptoms like weight loss.
  • Diverticular disease - Can present with bleeding or changes in bowel habits but lacks the systemic symptoms of cancer such as weight loss or anaemia.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease - Patients often have chronic diarrhoea with abdominal pain, but the onset is typically in younger individuals.
  • Ischaemic colitis - Typically presents with acute abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, often following an episode of hypotension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Surgery - Primary treatment for localised disease. Options include colectomy or anterior resection, depending on tumour location.
  • Chemotherapy - Adjuvant therapy for stage III and some stage II cancers. Common regimens include FOLFOX (5-fluorouracil, leucovorin, oxaliplatin).
  • Radiotherapy - Mainly used for rectal cancer to shrink tumours preoperatively or for palliation.
  • Targeted therapy - Bevacizumab or cetuximab may be used for metastatic disease in selected patients.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction - Due to tumour growth causing luminal narrowing.
  • Perforation - May occur if the tumour erodes through the bowel wall.
  • Liver metastasis - The most common site of distant spread due to venous drainage of the colon through the portal circulation.
  • Prognosis - Overall 5-year survival for colorectal cancer in the UK is approximately 60%. Early detection significantly improves survival rates.
