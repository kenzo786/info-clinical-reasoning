Appendicitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is an acute inflammation of the vermiform appendix, commonly caused by luminal obstruction. It presents as abdominal pain, typically starting periumbilically and migrating to the right lower quadrant. If untreated, it may progress to perforation, peritonitis, or sepsis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Appendicitis is a common cause of abdominal pain in primary care, representing about 5% of acute abdominal cases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Appendicitis accounts for roughly 10% of emergency abdominal surgeries, with an incidence rate of 100-150 cases per 100,000 people annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Appendicitis has a global incidence of approximately 86-100 cases per 100,000 people per year, more prevalent in developed countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the Appendix: The initial trigger is often blockage of the appendix lumen by faecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, or other debris.
  • Increased Intraluminal Pressure: Obstruction causes mucus and fluid accumulation, leading to rising pressure within the appendix.
  • Ischaemia and Bacterial Overgrowth: Rising pressure compromises blood flow, resulting in ischaemia and promoting bacterial invasion and inflammation.
  • Necrosis and Perforation: Without treatment, ischaemic necrosis can lead to perforation, spilling infectious material into the peritoneum and causing peritonitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase (0-12 hours): Vague, poorly localised periumbilical pain due to referred visceral pain from appendix inflammation.
  • Classic Progression (12-24 hours): Pain migrates to the right iliac fossa (McBurneyâ€™s point), becoming sharper due to parietal peritoneal irritation.
  • Complications Phase (24+ hours if untreated): Perforation risk increases, potentially leading to peritonitis or abscess formation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Periumbilical Pain: Caused by visceral stretching of the appendix, leading to referred pain near the umbilicus due to shared sensory nerves.
  • Right Lower Quadrant Pain: Pain migrates to the right iliac fossa as inflammation extends to the parietal peritoneum, localising the pain.
  • Anorexia: Mediated by inflammatory cytokines affecting the hypothalamus, leading to reduced appetite.
  • Nausea and Vomiting: Likely due to visceral stimulation and the bodyâ€™s inflammatory response.
  • Low-grade Fever: Reflects systemic inflammation; high fever suggests potential perforation or abscess formation.

  • Rebound Tenderness: Indicates peritoneal irritation; sudden release of pressure increases pain.
  • Guarding: Reflex contraction of abdominal muscles due to parietal peritoneal irritation.
  • Rovsingâ€™s Sign: Pain in the right lower quadrant when palpating the left side, suggesting local peritoneal inflammation.
  • Psoas Sign: Pain with right thigh extension, indicating retrocaecal appendix irritation involving the psoas muscle.
  • Obturator Sign: Pain with internal rotation of the right hip, seen in a pelvic appendix causing obturator muscle irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • High Fever (>38.5Â°C): May indicate abscess formation or generalised peritonitis.
  • Generalised Abdominal Pain: Suggests possible perforation with resulting peritonitis.
  • Rigidity: Diffuse, involuntary muscle guarding across the abdomen, indicating peritonitis.
  • Tachycardia and Hypotension: Signs of sepsis, which can develop with perforation and infection spread.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Retrocaecal Appendix: May present with flank or back pain instead of typical right iliac fossa pain.
  • Pelvic Appendix: Can cause suprapubic pain, dysuria, or tenesmus due to proximity to the bladder and rectum.
  • Elderly Patients: Less pronounced pain and signs; higher risk of perforation due to delayed diagnosis.
  • Children: Non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor appetite, and diffuse abdominal pain, which can delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Raised white cell count (WCC) and neutrophilia indicate an inflammatory response.
  • C-reactive Protein (CRP): Elevated levels suggest acute inflammation.
  • Ultrasound: Preferred in children and pregnant women; may show an enlarged, non-compressible appendix.
  • CT Scan: Gold standard for adults, showing an inflamed appendix and possible periappendiceal fat stranding.
  • Urinalysis: To exclude urinary causes of abdominal pain; mild pyuria can be present with pelvic appendicitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old male with 18 hours of worsening periumbilical pain, migrating to the right iliac fossa, associated with anorexia and low-grade fever.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis: Diffuse, non-localising pain with diarrhoea; lacks localised tenderness at McBurney's point.
  • Mesenteric Adenitis: Right lower quadrant pain in children but often with viral prodrome; lacks rebound tenderness.
  • Ovarian Torsion: Acute lower abdominal pain in females; ultrasound confirms with Doppler showing lack of ovarian blood flow.
  • Renal Colic: Flank pain radiating to the groin; urinalysis shows haematuria.
  • Diverticulitis: Left lower quadrant pain more common in older adults; confirmed via imaging with inflammation in the sigmoid colon.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Surgical Management: Laparoscopic appendicectomy is the standard treatment in the UK.
  • Antibiotics: Preoperative antibiotics to cover gram-negative and anaerobic bacteria (e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam); postoperative antibiotics in cases of perforation.
  • Conservative Management: May be considered in selected cases with antibiotics alone, though recurrence is common.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Occurs in 20-30% of untreated cases; risk rises after 24 hours, leading to peritonitis or abscess formation.
  • Abscess Formation: Localised collection of pus, more common in delayed or conservatively managed cases.
  • Peritonitis: Generalised infection of the peritoneal cavity, can lead to sepsis and multi-organ failure if untreated.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response due to perforation, requiring aggressive resuscitation and intravenous antibiotics.
