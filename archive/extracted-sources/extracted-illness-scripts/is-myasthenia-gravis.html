Myasthenia Gravis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Myasthenia gravis (MG) is an autoimmune neuromuscular disorder characterised by fluctuating skeletal muscle weakness and fatigability, particularly affecting muscles that control voluntary movements such as the eyes, face, and limbs. It is caused by autoantibodies that target acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, impairing signal transmission between nerves and muscles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 15â€“20 per 100,000 people.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Myasthenia gravis accounts for about 0.5% of neurology consultations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 20 per 100,000 people.
  • MG is more common in women under 40 and men over 60.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • 1. Autoantibodies (mainly IgG) target acetylcholine receptors (AChR) or associated proteins at the neuromuscular junction.
  • 2. These antibodies block, alter, or destroy the AChRs, reducing the efficiency of neuromuscular transmission.
  • 3. Reduced AChR function leads to impaired muscle contraction and increased muscle fatigue upon exertion.
  • 4. In some cases, antibodies may target muscle-specific kinase (MuSK), disrupting the organisation of the neuromuscular junction.
  • 5. The thymus gland is implicated in the pathogenesis, often abnormal in MG patients, sometimes with thymomas (tumours).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Subtle muscle weakness often begins with ocular muscles (ptosis, diplopia).
  • Progressive phase: Weakness spreads to bulbar, limb, and respiratory muscles over weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: Fluctuating symptoms with exacerbations and remissions. In severe cases, respiratory failure (myasthenic crisis) may occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Ocular symptoms (ptosis, diplopia) - Commonest early symptom due to weakness of the extraocular muscles. The autoantibodies target receptors, leading to muscle fatigue.
  • Bulbar weakness (dysarthria, dysphagia) - Difficulty with speech and swallowing due to impaired transmission at the neuromuscular junction of the muscles involved in speech and swallowing.
  • Limb weakness - Fluctuating weakness, typically worse with prolonged activity, reflecting impaired muscle contractility from reduced acetylcholine receptor activity.
  • Respiratory weakness - Shortness of breath or respiratory failure in severe cases (myasthenic crisis), reflecting involvement of the respiratory muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Ptosis - Drooping of the upper eyelids due to fatigued levator palpebrae muscles.
  • Diplopia - Double vision due to weakness of extraocular muscles, leading to misalignment of the eyes.
  • Dysarthria - Slurred speech caused by weakness of the muscles controlling speech.
  • Generalised muscle weakness - Symmetrical weakness that worsens with use, particularly of proximal limb muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure (Myasthenic crisis) - Sudden onset of respiratory weakness leading to potential life-threatening respiratory failure, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe dysphagia - Inability to swallow, risking aspiration pneumonia, dehydration, and malnutrition.
  • Bulbar weakness with rapid progression - May indicate impending crisis or a severe form of the disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Purely ocular symptoms persisting for years (ocular MG), which may never generalise to other muscle groups.
  • Asymmetric limb weakness, which may be confused with other neuromuscular disorders like motor neurone disease.
  • Weakness predominantly in the neck extensors or wrist flexors ("dropped head syndrome"), which can be misdiagnosed as a localised muscle problem.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Serological tests: Positive anti-AChR antibodies in ~85% of generalised MG cases. Anti-MuSK antibodies may be found in seronegative cases.
  • Electromyography (EMG): Repetitive nerve stimulation reveals a decremental response in muscle action potential due to impaired neurotransmission.
  • Edrophonium test: Temporary improvement of muscle weakness after administration of edrophonium (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor), though now less commonly used.
  • CT or MRI of the chest: To assess for thymoma, which is associated with MG in some cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with fluctuating eyelid drooping and double vision, worsening by the evening. She also reports mild difficulty swallowing and occasional generalised weakness.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS): Weakness improves with activity, unlike MG. LEMS often associated with small-cell lung cancer.
  • Botulism: Flaccid paralysis with descending pattern, pupils affected, and history of food contamination in cases of botulism.
  • Stroke: Sudden onset, often unilateral weakness, and associated with sensory loss or aphasia.
  • Motor neurone disease (MND): Presents with muscle atrophy and fasciculations, with no fluctuations or significant ocular involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (pyridostigmine): First-line symptomatic treatment to improve neuromuscular transmission.
  • Corticosteroids (prednisolone): For moderate to severe cases, aiming to reduce autoantibody production.
  • Immunosuppressants (azathioprine, mycophenolate): Used in steroid-sparing roles for long-term disease control.
  • Thymectomy: Recommended in patients with thymoma and considered in generalised MG for long-term benefit.
  • Plasmapheresis or intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG): Used in myasthenic crisis or for rapid symptom control.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Myasthenic crisis: Life-threatening respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation. Occurs in up to 20% of MG patients.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Due to bulbar weakness leading to difficulty swallowing and aspiration of food or liquids.
  • Long-term steroid use complications: Osteoporosis, diabetes, and hypertension.
  • Thymoma: Occurs in 10â€“15% of patients, potentially malignant and requiring surgical removal.
