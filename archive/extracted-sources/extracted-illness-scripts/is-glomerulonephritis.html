Glomerulonephritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Glomerulonephritis is a group of diseases characterised by inflammation of the glomeruli within the kidney. This inflammation disrupts the kidneyâ€™s filtration process, leading to proteinuria, haematuria, and reduced kidney function, potentially progressing to chronic kidney disease (CKD).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Glomerulonephritis is relatively rare in primary care settings, with an estimated incidence of 0.6-1 per 10,000.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Approximately 5-10% of kidney biopsies reveal glomerulonephritis, a leading cause of end-stage kidney disease (ESKD).
  • Worldwide Prevalence: The prevalence of glomerulonephritis varies widely depending on geographical and genetic factors, with some types more common in Asia and others in Europe and North America.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Immune Complex Deposition: Antigen-antibody complexes can deposit in the glomeruli, triggering complement activation and inflammation, leading to damage to the glomerular capillary walls.
  • Complement Activation: In some forms (e.g., membranoproliferative GN), excessive complement activation damages endothelial and epithelial cells.
  • Cellular Infiltration: Inflammatory cells (e.g., neutrophils, macrophages) infiltrate the glomeruli, worsening damage and increasing glomerular permeability.
  • Scarring and Fibrosis: Prolonged inflammation can cause fibrosis and scarring, leading to irreversible kidney damage and CKD.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Days to weeks. Symptoms develop rapidly, often with haematuria, proteinuria, and oedema.
  • Subacute Phase: Weeks to months. Progression may slow with treatment but can lead to partial renal recovery or persistent mild symptoms.
  • Chronic Phase: Months to years. Chronic glomerulonephritis progresses with slow loss of kidney function, potentially leading to CKD.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Haematuria (microscopic or macroscopic) - Blood in the urine due to damaged glomerular capillaries allowing erythrocytes to pass into urine.
  • Proteinuria - Increased permeability of the glomerular membrane allows protein to leak into the urine.
  • Oedema - Salt and water retention and hypoalbuminaemia lead to fluid accumulation, commonly in the lower extremities and periorbital areas.
  • Fatigue - Due to anaemia and uraemia as kidney function declines, reducing erythropoietin production.
  • Hypertension - Sodium retention and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) contribute to raised blood pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Indicates sodium and water retention and RAAS activation, often accompanying glomerular inflammation.
  • Peripheral Oedema - Commonly in ankles and feet, caused by fluid retention and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Oliguria - Reduced urine output due to impaired filtration and acute kidney injury.
  • Red Cell Casts in Urine - Suggests glomerular origin of haematuria, specific to glomerulonephritis.
  • Pallor - Due to anaemia, commonly secondary to reduced erythropoietin production in chronic glomerulonephritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Severe Hypertension - Indicates potential for hypertensive emergency, end-organ damage, and need for urgent management.
  • Severe Oedema (Pulmonary/Oedema) - Suggests fluid overload with risk of respiratory compromise; urgent diuresis may be needed.
  • Rapid Decline in Urine Output - May signal acute kidney injury requiring immediate intervention to prevent ESKD.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated Proteinuria - Mild cases may present only with proteinuria, which can be missed without further testing.
  • Normotensive Presentation - Some patients may not exhibit hypertension despite glomerular disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis: Shows proteinuria, haematuria, and red cell casts. Proteinuria occurs due to glomerular permeability, while haematuria and red cell casts confirm glomerular bleeding.
  • Serum Urea and Creatinine: Elevated in cases of impaired filtration and kidney injury.
  • Renal Ultrasound: Used to assess kidney size and rule out other structural causes.
  • Renal Biopsy: Histological examination is key to definitive diagnosis, revealing immune complex deposits or cellular proliferation patterns.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male presents with a one-week history of haematuria, oedema, and new-onset hypertension, suggestive of glomerulonephritis, confirmed on urinalysis and renal biopsy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • IgA Nephropathy: Often presents with episodic haematuria post-URTI, unlike persistent symptoms in GN.
  • Post-Streptococcal GN: Typically follows streptococcal infection with delayed onset (1-3 weeks).
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE): May involve multi-systemic symptoms, including skin rash and joint pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • ACE Inhibitors or ARBs: Used to manage hypertension and reduce proteinuria.
  • Corticosteroids: Considered in cases with significant inflammation or immune activity, particularly in rapidly progressive forms.
  • Immunosuppressive Therapy: Cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate mofetil may be indicated in severe cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD): Progressive decline in renal function may lead to ESKD, requiring dialysis or transplant.
  • Hypertensive Crisis: Due to uncontrolled blood pressure, risking organ damage.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Patients with CKD and hypertension are at elevated risk of CVD.
