Orthostatic Hypotension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Orthostatic hypotension (OH) is defined as a sustained reduction in systolic blood pressure of at least 20 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure of at least 10 mmHg within three minutes of standing. It reflects an inadequate cardiovascular or autonomic response to upright posture, leading to dizziness, lightheadedness, or fainting when standing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Estimated to affect about 5-20% of older adults (aged over 65).
  • Prevalence in UK hospital population: More common in hospitalised older adults, with rates up to 50% reported in frail or bedridden patients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar trends globally, affecting 15-30% of adults over 65, with higher prevalence in those with chronic conditions such as diabetes or Parkinsonâ€™s disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Orthostatic hypotension occurs when the autonomic nervous system fails to compensate for the sudden pooling of blood in the lower extremities upon standing. Normally, standing triggers vasoconstriction and increased heart rate to maintain blood pressure. In OH, impaired autonomic function, hypovolaemia, or medication effects cause inadequate vascular response, leading to a fall in blood pressure and cerebral hypoperfusion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • OH can present acutely or develop gradually. Acute OH may occur due to hypovolaemia or new medications, while chronic OH develops in patients with progressive autonomic failure or long-term comorbidities like diabetes. Symptoms tend to fluctuate depending on factors like dehydration, time of day, or physical activity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness - Resulting from cerebral hypoperfusion due to the drop in blood pressure when standing.
  • Blurred vision - Caused by reduced blood flow to the optic nerve and retina.
  • Weakness or fatigue - Inadequate perfusion of tissues during postural changes can lead to feelings of fatigue.
  • Syncope (fainting) - If blood pressure drops significantly, cerebral perfusion is reduced, leading to transient loss of consciousness.
  • Chest pain or palpitations - Occurs in some cases as the heart attempts to compensate for the sudden drop in blood pressure by increasing heart rate.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Sudden or severe syncope - May indicate a more serious underlying condition such as cardiac arrhythmia or structural heart disease.
  • Chest pain - Could signal myocardial ischaemia due to reduced coronary perfusion during hypotensive episodes.
  • Neurological deficits - New focal deficits may suggest cerebrovascular events or neurodegenerative disorders like multiple system atrophy (MSA).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Postprandial hypotension - Occurs after meals due to blood pooling in the gastrointestinal tract; common in older adults and those with autonomic dysfunction.
  • Orthostatic intolerance without hypotension - Patients may experience symptoms without meeting the diagnostic criteria for OH, due to impaired baroreceptor sensitivity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Orthostatic BP measurements - Measure blood pressure and heart rate after lying down for 5 minutes, then again at 1 and 3 minutes of standing. A significant drop in BP with or without compensatory tachycardia confirms the diagnosis.
  • ECG - To rule out arrhythmias or cardiac causes of syncope.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (to check for anaemia), electrolytes, and glucose levels to rule out dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, or hypoglycaemia as potential causes.
  • Autonomic function testing - Includes tests such as tilt-table testing to assess autonomic nervous system function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • An 80-year-old woman presents with several months of dizziness and lightheadedness when standing, worsened after meals. She also reports episodes of blurred vision and weakness, with one instance of fainting. Orthostatic BP measurements confirm a systolic BP drop of 25 mmHg after standing for 2 minutes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope - Characterised by transient loss of consciousness due to vagally mediated hypotension and bradycardia, triggered by stress or pain. Differs from OH as vasovagal syncope is usually situational and not related to posture.
  • Dehydration - Presents with hypotension, tachycardia, and signs of volume depletion, but BP drops can occur in any position, not necessarily only orthostatically.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - May present with syncope or dizziness, but often have abnormal ECG findings and are not directly related to postural changes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Non-pharmacological management - Includes increasing fluid and salt intake, wearing compression stockings, and advising on gradual positional changes to minimise hypotension episodes.
  • Medications - In cases where lifestyle modifications are insufficient, medications such as fludrocortisone (a mineralocorticoid that increases blood volume) or midodrine (an alpha-agonist that increases vascular tone) may be used.
  • Management of underlying conditions - Treating conditions like diabetes or heart failure that contribute to OH is critical for long-term management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Orthostatic Hypotension"][/sc]
  • Recurrent falls - Frequent episodes of hypotension can lead to falls and fractures, especially in elderly patients.
  • Cerebral hypoperfusion - Prolonged or severe hypotensive episodes can result in cognitive impairment or ischaemic stroke.
  • Myocardial ischaemia - In patients with coronary artery disease, episodes of hypotension may trigger angina or myocardial infarction due to reduced coronary perfusion.
