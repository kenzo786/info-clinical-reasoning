Optic Neuritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis is an acute or subacute inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve, often associated with autoimmune or infectious conditions. It frequently results in vision loss and pain, particularly on eye movement.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • UK Prevalence: 1â€“5 per 100,000 general population annually.
  • Hospital Prevalence: Optic neuritis accounts for up to 20% of neuro-ophthalmic consultations.
  • Worldwide: Incidence varies, more common in Caucasian populations, and frequently seen in regions with higher prevalence of multiple sclerosis (MS).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis involves inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve, mediated by immune cells and cytokines. This leads to disrupted conduction of visual signals.
  • Axonal injury may occur, leading to potential permanent visual loss if inflammation persists.
  • Frequently linked to multiple sclerosis or other systemic autoimmune diseases like neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorders (NMOSD).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms develop over hours to days, peaking within one week.
  • Recovery Phase: Begins within 2â€“4 weeks, often with partial or complete resolution of vision loss over 6â€“12 months.
  • Chronic Phase: Some patients may experience residual deficits or recurrent episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Vision Loss: Usually unilateral, painless or painful. Pathophysiology involves conduction block due to demyelination.
  • Pain with Eye Movement: Present in 90% of cases, due to inflammation of the optic nerve sheath.
  • Photopsias: Perception of flashing lights caused by irritation of the optic nerve.
  • Reduced Colour Vision: Commonly affects red hues, attributed to impaired cone cell signalling.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD): Due to asymmetric optic nerve dysfunction, observed via swinging flashlight test.
  • Swollen Optic Disc (Papillitis): Seen in 30% of cases, caused by optic nerve inflammation visible on fundoscopy.
  • Normal Fundus (Retrobulbar Neuritis): Optic disc appears normal despite functional deficits, indicating retrobulbar location of inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Vision Loss: May suggest NMOSD or other systemic causes rather than isolated optic neuritis.
  • Severe Persistent Vision Loss: Could indicate alternative diagnoses such as ischaemic optic neuropathy.
  • Progression Beyond Two Weeks: Atypical for optic neuritis, warrants further investigation for infiltrative or neoplastic causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Presentation: May be missed in systemic autoimmune disorders like lupus.
  • Painless Presentation: Less common, potentially indicating other causes such as toxic or nutritional optic neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • OCT: Reveals retinal nerve fibre layer thinning, indicating axonal loss.
  • MRI Brain and Orbits: Enhancing optic nerve segments confirm inflammation, while white matter lesions may suggest MS.
  • Visual Evoked Potentials: Prolonged P100 latency confirms slowed conduction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old female presents with acute, unilateral, painful vision loss over three days, associated with reduced colour perception. She has a history of mild paraesthesia a year ago.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Multiple Sclerosis: Distinguished by history of other neurological symptoms and MRI findings.
  • Neuromyelitis Optica: Often bilateral and associated with longitudinal spinal cord lesions.
  • Ischaemic Optic Neuropathy: Presents in older adults with vascular risk factors, often painless.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • High-dose IV Steroids: Methylprednisolone 1 g/day for 3â€“5 days, as per NICE guidelines, accelerates recovery.
  • Plasma Exchange: Considered in severe or steroid-refractory cases.
  • MS Modifying Therapy: If diagnosed, initiate disease-modifying treatments such as interferons or monoclonal antibodies.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic Visual Deficits: Due to irreversible axonal damage.
  • Recurrent Optic Neuritis: Associated with underlying systemic diseases like MS or NMOSD.
  • Progression to MS: Approximately 50% of patients develop MS within 15 years.
