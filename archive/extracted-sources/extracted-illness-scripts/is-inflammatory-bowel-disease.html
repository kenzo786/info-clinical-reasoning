Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) is a group of chronic, relapsing-remitting inflammatory conditions of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, primarily comprising Crohnâ€™s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC). It is characterised by immune-mediated inflammation leading to tissue damage, with UC limited to the colon and rectum, while CD can affect any part of the GI tract from mouth to anus.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of IBD is approximately 0.5%, with an estimated 300,000 people affected. Ulcerative colitis is more common than Crohnâ€™s disease.
  • Hospital prevalence of IBD is around 100-200 per 100,000 hospitalisations annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence varies, with higher rates in developed countries, particularly in Europe and North America, with increasing incidence in developing regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Genetic predisposition: Mutations in genes related to immune function (e.g., NOD2, IL23R) lead to an abnormal immune response.
  • Environmental triggers: Smoking, diet, and microbial dysbiosis contribute to immune system dysregulation.
  • Immune dysregulation: An overactive immune response results in the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-alpha, IL-12), leading to tissue injury.
  • Barrier dysfunction: Damage to the intestinal epithelium compromises its barrier function, allowing bacterial translocation, which perpetuates inflammation.
  • Tissue damage: Chronic inflammation results in fibrosis, strictures, and fistula formation in Crohnâ€™s disease, while ulcerative colitis leads to mucosal ulceration and bleeding.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Symptoms may develop insidiously over weeks to months, with intermittent periods of remission.
  • Relapsing-remitting course: Both Crohnâ€™s disease and ulcerative colitis follow an unpredictable course with flares of active inflammation interspersed with symptom-free intervals.
  • Chronic phase: In long-standing disease, complications such as strictures, fistulas, and an increased risk of colorectal cancer may arise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea: Chronic, often bloody in ulcerative colitis, due to mucosal inflammation and ulceration. In Crohnâ€™s disease, it may be non-bloody due to ileal involvement.
  • Abdominal pain: Crampy and localised to the right lower quadrant in Crohnâ€™s disease or left lower quadrant in ulcerative colitis, caused by intestinal inflammation.
  • Weight loss: Due to malabsorption in Crohnâ€™s disease or anorexia during active flares.
  • Fatigue: Chronic inflammation and anaemia contribute to systemic fatigue.
  • Urgency and tenesmus: Common in ulcerative colitis due to rectal involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal tenderness: Localised to affected areas, such as the right lower quadrant in Crohnâ€™s disease or diffuse tenderness in ulcerative colitis.
  • Fever: Indicates active inflammation or infection, especially during flares.
  • Perianal disease (Crohnâ€™s disease): Fistulas, abscesses, and skin tags are common in Crohnâ€™s disease due to transmural inflammation.
  • Pallor: Suggests anaemia from chronic blood loss or nutritional deficiencies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate toxic megacolon or perforation, which are life-threatening complications.
  • Severe bleeding: Suggests significant mucosal damage and may require emergency intervention.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Significant weight loss may indicate malnutrition, cancer, or severe Crohnâ€™s disease.
  • Persistent fever: Indicates uncontrolled inflammation, abscess, or sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Extraintestinal manifestations: Arthritis, uveitis, and erythema nodosum may present before bowel symptoms, often linked to systemic inflammation.
  • Subclinical disease: Some patients may only present with mild anaemia or elevated inflammatory markers without overt GI symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Anaemia (microcytic or normocytic), leucocytosis, and thrombocytosis due to chronic inflammation and bleeding.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): Raised in active inflammation.
  • Faecal calprotectin: Elevated due to neutrophil migration into the intestinal mucosa, a sensitive marker of intestinal inflammation.
  • Colonoscopy with biopsy: Gold standard for diagnosis, revealing mucosal inflammation, crypt abscesses in ulcerative colitis, or granulomas in Crohnâ€™s disease.
  • Magnetic resonance enterography (MRE): Useful for assessing small bowel involvement and identifying strictures or fistulas in Crohnâ€™s disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with 6 months of intermittent bloody diarrhoea, weight loss, and crampy abdominal pain in the lower left quadrant. Blood tests show anaemia and elevated inflammatory markers. Colonoscopy confirms ulcerative colitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS): Unlike IBD, IBS does not involve inflammation and is characterised by alternating bowel habits without bleeding or elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Coeliac disease: Gluten sensitivity causing malabsorption, with diarrhoea and weight loss but without the chronic inflammation seen in IBD.
  • Infectious colitis: Acute onset of bloody diarrhoea, often with a history of travel or contaminated food, and resolves with appropriate antibiotics.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Aminosalicylates (e.g., mesalazine): First-line for mild-to-moderate ulcerative colitis, reducing inflammation locally in the bowel.
  • Corticosteroids: Used for acute flares to suppress inflammation but not suitable for long-term use due to side effects.
  • Immunomodulators (e.g., azathioprine): Used for maintenance in both UC and Crohnâ€™s disease to prevent relapse.
  • Biologic therapies (e.g., infliximab): Target TNF-alpha and are used in refractory or severe cases of both UC and Crohnâ€™s disease.
  • Surgery: Required in cases of complications such as strictures, fistulas, or refractory disease, with colectomy potentially curative in ulcerative colitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Toxic megacolon: Acute colonic distension leading to perforation if untreated.
  • Colorectal cancer: Long-standing inflammation increases the risk of dysplasia, particularly in ulcerative colitis.
  • Fistulas and strictures: Common in Crohnâ€™s disease due to transmural inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Malnutrition: Due to malabsorption, especially in Crohnâ€™s disease.
