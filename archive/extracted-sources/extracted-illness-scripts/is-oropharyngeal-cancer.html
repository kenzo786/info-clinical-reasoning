Oropharyngeal Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Oropharyngeal cancer is a malignancy arising in the oropharynx, the middle part of the throat that includes the back of the tongue, tonsils, soft palate, and the walls of the pharynx. It is typically a squamous cell carcinoma but can also include other histological types.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • In the UK, oropharyngeal cancer has an incidence of approximately 7 per 100,000 annually, with a higher prevalence in men than women. Hospital-based prevalence is higher, especially in regions with known high-risk factors.
  • Worldwide, incidence rates vary significantly, with high prevalence in regions with elevated rates of HPV infection or tobacco use, such as Southeast Asia and Eastern Europe.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Oropharyngeal cancers develop from the squamous epithelium lining the oropharynx. Genetic mutations and molecular changes, often triggered by carcinogens like tobacco, alcohol, or human papillomavirus (HPV), lead to uncontrolled cellular proliferation and tumour formation.
  • HPV-positive cancers frequently result from HPV type 16 infection, which causes overexpression of oncogenes E6 and E7, leading to inactivation of tumour suppressor proteins such as p53 and Rb.
  • HPV-negative cancers are more commonly associated with chronic exposure to tobacco and alcohol, leading to DNA damage, inflammation, and epithelial dysplasia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with mild symptoms such as throat discomfort or mild dysphagia.
  • Intermediate phase: Tumour growth leads to more prominent symptoms, including pain, persistent sore throat, and more noticeable swallowing difficulties.
  • Advanced phase: Metastasis to local lymph nodes or distant organs may occur, with symptoms including neck mass, weight loss, and possible airway obstruction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Persistent sore throat - Due to local inflammation and tissue invasion by the tumour.
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) - Caused by tumour growth obstructing the oropharyngeal passage and affecting surrounding muscles.
  • Otalgia (ear pain) - Referred pain due to tumour invasion into cranial nerves.
  • Hoarseness - Occurs if the tumour affects the vocal cords or nerve supply to the larynx.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Advanced cancer often leads to reduced appetite and metabolic changes that contribute to weight loss.
  • Neck mass - Due to regional lymph node metastasis, commonly found in advanced cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Neck lymphadenopathy - Enlarged, firm cervical lymph nodes due to metastasis.
  • Visible oropharyngeal mass or ulceration - Mass or irregular ulcerations on physical examination indicate tumour growth.
  • Trismus (difficulty opening the mouth) - Seen in advanced cases with local tumour infiltration into nearby muscles.
  • Halitosis - Due to necrotic tissue and secondary bacterial infection within the tumour.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May indicate advanced disease and potential metastasis, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Persistent hoarseness or dysphagia - Often indicates tumour involvement of the larynx or pharynx and needs further evaluation.
  • Neck mass - Particularly in patients without infection, a neck mass is a concerning sign of regional metastasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic at presentation - Particularly common in HPV-related oropharyngeal cancers, which may be discovered incidentally.
  • Otalgia without throat pain - Referred pain due to cranial nerve involvement without primary throat symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Biopsy of lesion - Confirms diagnosis through histopathological examination showing squamous cell carcinoma or other subtypes.
  • Imaging (CT, MRI, PET) - Used to assess local and distant spread; CT/MRI for locoregional spread, PET for distant metastasis.
  • HPV testing - Important for prognosis, as HPV-positive cancers have a better response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a three-month history of sore throat, difficulty swallowing, and a palpable neck mass. History of smoking and alcohol use.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Tonsillitis - Often presents with acute onset sore throat, fever, and no neck mass.
  • Lymphoma - Can present with a neck mass but typically without dysphagia or otalgia.
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - May cause throat discomfort but lacks a visible mass and lymphadenopathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection - Primary treatment in early stages, depending on tumour location and operability.
  • Radiotherapy - Standard treatment, especially in HPV-positive cancers and advanced local disease.
  • Chemotherapy - Often used alongside radiotherapy for advanced stages.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oropharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Common in advanced disease; metastasis to lymph nodes and distant organs, reducing survival rates.
  • Airway obstruction - Tumour growth can compromise the airway, requiring emergency intervention.
  • Quality of life impacts - Including dysphagia, speech difficulties, and emotional distress post-treatment.
