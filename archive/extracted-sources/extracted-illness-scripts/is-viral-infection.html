Viral Infection
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • A viral infection is an illness caused by a virus, a type of infectious agent that invades host cells to replicate. It can affect various organ systems, including the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and central nervous systems. Viruses are typically self-limiting but can occasionally lead to severe complications, especially in immunocompromised individuals or those with chronic diseases.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Viral infections are extremely common worldwide. In the UK, they account for a large proportion of GP consultations, with the common cold, influenza, and other respiratory viruses being frequent complaints. Globally, viral infections contribute to a significant burden of disease, with seasonal influenza affecting 5-10% of the population annually and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) and enteroviruses being common causes of childhood illness.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • 1. Viruses enter the body via mucous membranes or direct inoculation (e.g., bites). Common routes of entry include the respiratory and gastrointestinal tracts.
  • 2. The virus attaches to specific receptors on host cells and enters them, where it uses the host's machinery to replicate.
  • 3. Viral replication often leads to cell death, which can trigger the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, causing systemic symptoms such as fever and malaise.
  • 4. The immune response, both innate (e.g., interferons) and adaptive (e.g., T-cell-mediated), is crucial in controlling viral replication and eventually eliminating the virus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • 1. **Incubation period**: Ranges from hours to days depending on the virus (e.g., 1-4 days for influenza, 2-14 days for COVID-19).
  • 2. **Prodromal phase**: Nonspecific symptoms such as malaise, headache, and mild fever may develop.
  • 3. **Acute phase**: Characterised by peak symptoms, such as fever, cough, fatigue, and myalgia. This phase typically lasts 3-7 days.
  • 4. **Resolution**: Symptoms resolve gradually as the immune system clears the virus, though fatigue may persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Fever - Caused by the release of pyrogens as part of the immune response. Pyrogens act on the hypothalamus, raising body temperature.
  • Cough - A reflex initiated by viral irritation or inflammation in the respiratory tract.
  • Sore throat - Inflammation of the pharyngeal mucosa due to viral infection.
  • Fatigue - Likely due to systemic inflammation and energy diversion towards immune activity.
  • Myalgia - Muscle pain caused by systemic inflammatory cytokines like IL-6.
  • Runny nose - Due to increased secretion of mucus in response to viral infection of the nasal epithelium.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Fever - Raised body temperature detected on examination, correlating with systemic immune response.
  • Pharyngeal erythema - Redness of the throat due to local inflammation.
  • Nasal congestion - Swelling of the nasal mucosa from inflammation, causing blockage of the nasal passages.
  • Rhonchi or wheezing - Airflow obstruction due to bronchial inflammation in lower respiratory tract infections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • High fever (>39Â°C) - May indicate severe infection or complications such as bacterial superinfection.
  • Shortness of breath - Sign of lower respiratory tract involvement, potentially indicating pneumonia or severe bronchiolitis.
  • Altered mental status - Could suggest encephalitis, especially in viral infections like herpes simplex virus.
  • Chest pain - May indicate viral myocarditis or pericarditis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases, especially in young, healthy individuals or with certain viruses like COVID-19.
  • Non-specific symptoms like mild gastrointestinal discomfort, particularly in elderly or immunocompromised patients.
  • Isolated neurological symptoms such as confusion or headache, which could suggest viral encephalitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Viral PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) - Detects viral RNA or DNA in respiratory secretions or blood. Used for common respiratory viruses like influenza, RSV, or SARS-CoV-2.
  • Serology - Detects specific antibodies (IgM/IgG) against viral pathogens. Helpful in confirming past infections or immunity status (e.g., hepatitis, HIV).
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Often shows lymphocytosis in viral infections, indicating viral immune response.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Typically mildly elevated in viral infections, though markedly raised CRP may indicate bacterial superinfection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old previously healthy male presents with a 3-day history of fever, sore throat, and fatigue. He reports nasal congestion and a non-productive cough. Examination reveals a mild fever and pharyngeal erythema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Bacterial Pharyngitis - Presents with higher fever and tonsillar exudates compared to viral infections, often requires antibiotic therapy.
  • Allergic Rhinitis - More likely to cause sneezing and itchy eyes, without fever or systemic symptoms.
  • Pneumonia - Associated with productive cough, dyspnoea, and focal lung findings like crepitations or consolidation on chest X-ray.
  • Influenza - Similar symptoms, but often presents with more abrupt onset of fever, myalgia, and headache.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Most viral infections are self-limiting and require supportive care. Management includes:
    • Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever and pain relief.
    • Rest and hydration.
    • Antiviral medications for specific viral infections (e.g., oseltamivir for influenza, acyclovir for herpes simplex virus).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Viral Infection"][/sc]
  • Bacterial superinfection - Secondary bacterial infections (e.g., pneumonia, sinusitis) can occur, requiring antibiotics.
  • Myocarditis - Viral infection of the heart muscle, potentially leading to heart failure or arrhythmias.
  • Encephalitis - Viral spread to the central nervous system, leading to confusion, seizures, or focal neurological deficits.
  • Most viral infections resolve without complications in otherwise healthy individuals, with full recovery expected within weeks.
