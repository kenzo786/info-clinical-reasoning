Cholecystitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis is inflammation of the gallbladder, most commonly due to obstruction of the cystic duct by gallstones (calculous cholecystitis) but can also occur in the absence of stones (acalculous cholecystitis). It often presents as acute right upper quadrant pain, fever, and leucocytosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Cholecystitis has a low prevalence in general practice settings, but cholelithiasis (gallstones) affects around 10-15% of adults, with 1-3% developing cholecystitis annually.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Among patients hospitalised with abdominal pain, acute cholecystitis accounts for about 10-15% of cases.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Cholecystitis is common in regions with a high prevalence of gallstones, with up to 20% of adults in Western countries affected by gallstones, and 1-3% of those progressing to cholecystitis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis typically begins with an obstruction of the cystic duct, often by a gallstone, leading to stasis of bile.
  • Bile stasis promotes bacterial overgrowth, mainly by Gram-negative bacteria like Escherichia coli and Klebsiella, initiating inflammation of the gallbladder mucosa.
  • Inflammation progresses to involve the entire gallbladder wall, causing oedema, distension, and potentially ischaemia, which can lead to gangrene or perforation if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Obstruction of the cystic duct causes acute symptoms (within hours to a day), such as pain and tenderness.
  • Progression: Over 1-3 days, inflammation may increase, with fever and worsening pain, leading to potential complications (e.g., abscess or gangrene).
  • Resolution/Complication Phase: With treatment, inflammation typically resolves within a few days; without treatment, severe complications like perforation or sepsis can occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) Pain: Commonest presenting symptom, resulting from distension and inflammation of the gallbladder, often radiating to the right shoulder.
  • Nausea and Vomiting: Often accompany RUQ pain due to irritation of the gastrointestinal tract by inflammation.
  • Fever and Chills: Fever occurs as a systemic response to infection and inflammation, indicating more severe inflammation.
  • Anorexia: Loss of appetite is common due to systemic inflammatory response and discomfort.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Murphyâ€™s Sign: Tenderness and sudden inspiratory arrest on palpation of the RUQ, indicative of gallbladder inflammation and contact with the inflamed peritoneum.
  • Fever: Typically low-grade, resulting from the systemic inflammatory response; high fevers may suggest complications like abscess or gangrene.
  • Tachycardia: Common in response to pain, fever, or possible systemic inflammation.
  • Jaundice: Occasionally present if bile duct obstruction or liver involvement occurs, due to increased bilirubin levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Severe, Persistent RUQ Pain: May indicate impending complications such as gangrene or perforation.
  • High Fever (>39Â°C): Suggests potential abscess formation or systemic infection.
  • Hypotension or Septic Shock: Could indicate biliary sepsis, often a result of bacterial spread, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Peritonitis Signs (e.g., Rebound Tenderness): Indicative of gallbladder perforation or rupture, an emergency requiring surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Elderly Patients: May present with vague symptoms like malaise or confusion due to reduced inflammatory response, often leading to diagnostic delays.
  • Diabetics: More prone to severe infections (emphysematous cholecystitis), often with subtler symptoms.
  • Pain in Atypical Locations: Left-sided or epigastric pain, especially in individuals with anatomic variants or obesity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: First-line imaging; findings include gallbladder wall thickening (>3 mm), pericholecystic fluid, and presence of gallstones, indicating inflammation and possible obstruction.
  • Blood Tests: Elevated white cell count, C-reactive protein (CRP), and liver function tests (LFTs) may show mild transaminitis or bilirubin elevation if the bile duct is obstructed.
  • Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP): Used if ductal obstruction or choledocholithiasis is suspected; shows the biliary tree in detail.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • A 50-year-old woman presents with a 24-hour history of severe right upper quadrant pain radiating to the shoulder, nausea, fever, and tenderness on deep palpation of the RUQ. Murphy's sign is positive.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Often epigastric pain, worse with meals; lacks RUQ tenderness and Murphyâ€™s sign.
  • Pancreatitis: Epigastric pain radiating to the back; elevated amylase/lipase distinguishes it.
  • Acute Hepatitis: Diffuse abdominal pain with jaundice and elevated LFTs; less localised RUQ tenderness.
  • Biliary Colic: Pain similar to cholecystitis but without fever or leucocytosis; typically resolves after a few hours.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive Care: IV fluids, analgesics, and antiemetics for symptomatic relief.
  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., co-amoxiclav or ceftriaxone + metronidazole) are recommended to cover Gram-negative and anaerobic bacteria.
  • Cholecystectomy: Surgery within 72 hours is recommended for fit patients to remove the source of infection and prevent recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Gangrene and Perforation: Seen in 10-15% of untreated cases due to prolonged ischaemia, leading to peritonitis.
  • Empyema: Abscess formation within the gallbladder from untreated infection, leading to risk of sepsis.
  • Biliary Sepsis: Systemic infection due to bacterial translocation; potentially fatal if untreated.
