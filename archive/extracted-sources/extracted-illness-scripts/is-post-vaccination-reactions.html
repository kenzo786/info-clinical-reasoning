Post-Vaccination Reactions
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Post-vaccination reactions refer to the spectrum of immune and non-immune responses occurring after the administration of a vaccine. These reactions can range from mild, self-limiting symptoms such as local injection site pain and fever to rare, severe adverse events such as anaphylaxis or myocarditis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 10â€“20% of patients report mild post-vaccination symptoms such as soreness or fever.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Severe adverse reactions requiring hospitalisation are rare, occurring in less than 0.01% of vaccine recipients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, minor adverse events occur in 15â€“30% of vaccine recipients, with severe reactions being exceptionally rare.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Vaccines stimulate the immune system to produce a protective response without causing the disease. This involves activation of innate immune cells and subsequent adaptive immune responses.
  • Common reactions, such as fever or soreness, are mediated by inflammatory cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-Î±.
  • Rare adverse events, such as myocarditis, may involve immune cross-reactivity or dysregulated immune responses.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Immediate phase: Reactions such as syncope or anaphylaxis occur within minutes to hours of vaccination.
  • Early phase: Common symptoms like fever, fatigue, and soreness typically appear within 24â€“48 hours and resolve within 2â€“3 days.
  • Delayed phase: Rare events like myocarditis or Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome may develop days to weeks post-vaccination.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Injection site pain - Due to localised inflammation and cytokine release at the site of injection.
  • Fever - A systemic response to cytokine release as part of the immune activation process.
  • Fatigue - Associated with systemic inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6.
  • Headache - May be related to cytokine-mediated inflammation or dehydration post-vaccination.
  • Muscle aches - Reflect systemic inflammatory response and local immune activation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Localised redness and swelling - Indicates inflammation due to immune activation.
  • Mild lymphadenopathy - Reflects immune response and lymphatic involvement.
  • Elevated temperature - Suggests systemic inflammatory cytokine release.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Difficulty breathing - May indicate anaphylaxis, a medical emergency due to histamine release.
  • Chest pain - Could suggest myocarditis or pericarditis; inflammation of the heart tissues.
  • Severe weakness or paralysis - May signify Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome, a rare post-vaccination autoimmune condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic course - Common, particularly with mild local or systemic reactions.
  • Delayed hypersensitivity reactions - Skin rashes or other immune-mediated effects days after vaccination.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show elevated white cell count if inflammation is present.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated in systemic inflammatory responses.
  • Troponin levels - Raised in myocarditis cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presenting 24 hours post-vaccination with fever, headache, and fatigue, resolving spontaneously within 48 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Viral infection - Differs by prolonged symptoms and additional findings like sore throat.
  • Allergic reaction - Presents immediately with urticaria and wheezing.
  • Autoimmune conditions - Rarely, mimics vaccination reactions but typically has a more chronic course.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Symptomatic management - Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever and pain.
  • Antihistamines - For mild allergic reactions.
  • Emergency management - Adrenaline and supportive care for anaphylaxis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Occurs in 1 in a million doses; caused by severe IgE-mediated reaction.
  • Myocarditis - Occurs in approximately 1 in 50,000 young males, associated with immune cross-reactivity.
