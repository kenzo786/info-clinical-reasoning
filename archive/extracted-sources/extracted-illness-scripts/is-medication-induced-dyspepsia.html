Medication-Induced Dyspepsia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced dyspepsia is discomfort or pain in the upper abdomen caused by medications that irritate the gastrointestinal (GI) mucosa, reduce protective GI mechanisms, or increase acid secretion. It is commonly associated with NSAIDs, aspirin, corticosteroids, and certain antibiotics.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK General Practice: Estimated at 30-40% of patients on NSAIDs or aspirin.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Around 20% of hospitalised patients on chronic NSAIDs or multiple medications may experience dyspeptic symptoms.
  • Global Prevalence: Variable depending on NSAID or high-risk medication usage; estimated at 10-20% in general populations on these medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Direct Mucosal Injury: NSAIDs and similar drugs directly damage the GI lining, leading to mucosal irritation and erosion, which may result in pain and dyspepsia.
  • Prostaglandin Inhibition: NSAIDs inhibit COX enzymes, reducing protective prostaglandins, which leads to decreased mucus and bicarbonate production, making the stomach lining vulnerable to acid damage.
  • Increased Acid Secretion: Certain medications, like corticosteroids, may increase gastric acid production, exacerbating irritation.
  • Reduced GI Motility: Some medications, such as opioids, reduce gastric motility, leading to delayed emptying and symptoms of bloating and discomfort.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Onset: Dyspepsia may begin within days to weeks of starting the medication.
  • Chronic Phase: Symptoms persist or worsen with continued use; may remit if the medication is ceased.
  • Resolution: Symptoms generally improve within 1-2 weeks after discontinuing the offending medication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Epigastric Pain - Central, upper abdominal pain due to mucosal irritation and inflammation.
  • Bloating - Accumulation of gas in the stomach, often from delayed gastric emptying due to reduced GI motility.
  • Early Satiety - Caused by delayed stomach emptying and increased gastric distention.
  • Nausea - Triggered by irritation of the gastric lining, often worsened by the acidity and reduced protective factors.
  • Heartburn - Reflux symptoms may occur due to relaxation of the lower oesophageal sphincter or increased acid production.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Epigastric Tenderness - Localised pain on palpation due to mucosal inflammation.
  • Abdominal Distension - Visual abdominal fullness, sometimes associated with bloating from slowed GI motility.
  • Rebound Tenderness - Suggestive of potential deeper GI involvement or complications, like ulceration.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting blood may indicate significant GI bleeding, likely from an ulcer.
  • Melena - Black, tarry stools suggest upper GI bleeding, possibly from erosions or ulcers.
  • Weight Loss - Unintended weight loss can indicate poor nutritional intake or malignancy, which can be associated with medication-related GI injury in older patients.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing may suggest an oesophageal ulcer or stricture.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Silent Dyspepsia - Patients may experience subclinical GI mucosal damage without overt symptoms, especially in elderly patients on chronic NSAID therapy.
  • Non-Epigastric Pain - Discomfort may present as back pain or chest discomfort, leading to misdiagnosis as musculoskeletal or cardiac in origin.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • FBC (Full Blood Count) - May show anaemia if there is occult bleeding.
  • Urea Breath Test - Checks for H. pylori, which can exacerbate dyspepsia and is often associated with NSAID use.
  • Upper GI Endoscopy - Visualisation of GI mucosa for erosions, ulcerations, and signs of bleeding.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female with chronic NSAID use presents with a 3-week history of epigastric pain, bloating, and early satiety. No weight loss or red flags are reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - Shares similar epigastric pain but often has more definitive ulcerative lesions on endoscopy.
  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) - Heartburn and acid regurgitation are more prominent in GORD.
  • Gallbladder Disease - Pain often localised to right upper quadrant and triggered by fatty meals.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation of Offending Medication - Switch to a safer alternative or reduce the dosage if possible.
  • Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) - Omeprazole or lansoprazole are first-line options to reduce acid production and relieve symptoms.
  • H. pylori Eradication - If positive, use triple therapy, including a PPI and antibiotics.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • GI Bleeding - Most common in patients on chronic NSAIDs; can lead to significant anaemia or haemorrhage.
  • Peptic Ulceration - NSAIDs and similar drugs increase ulcer risk due to reduced mucosal protection.
  • Gastric Perforation - Severe mucosal damage may progress to perforation, leading to peritonitis, which is life-threatening.
