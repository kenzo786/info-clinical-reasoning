Cancer Cachexia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Cancer cachexia is a multifactorial syndrome characterised by severe weight loss, muscle wasting, and loss of adipose tissue despite adequate caloric intake. It is driven by metabolic disturbances associated with malignancies and results in functional impairment. Unlike simple starvation, cancer cachexia involves systemic inflammation and metabolic dysregulation that cannot be reversed by nutritional supplementation alone.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Cancer cachexia is rare in the general population but common in advanced cancer patients, with prevalence rates of 15-20% in primary care oncology patients.
  • Prevalence in UK hospital settings: It affects up to 50-80% of patients with advanced malignancies, particularly those with gastrointestinal, pancreatic, lung, or head and neck cancers.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Cancer cachexia affects 50-80% of cancer patients globally, leading to death in 20-30% of cases. It is more prevalent in low- and middle-income countries where late-stage cancer is common.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Cancer cachexia is driven by a complex interaction between tumour-derived factors and host responses. Tumours secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-Î±, IL-6) and proteolysis-inducing factors, leading to systemic inflammation and breakdown of skeletal muscle and adipose tissue. Increased muscle protein degradation via the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway and reduced protein synthesis result in muscle wasting. Simultaneously, fat loss occurs due to lipolysis driven by catecholamines and other tumour factors. These metabolic abnormalities are compounded by anorexia, insulin resistance, and alterations in energy expenditure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Cancer cachexia typically develops over months and is progressive. Early stages may involve subtle weight loss and fatigue, which can progress to severe muscle wasting, profound weakness, and functional decline in advanced stages. It often parallels the progression of the underlying malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss - Most common and defining symptom, occurring due to increased catabolism of fat and muscle driven by systemic inflammation.
  • Muscle weakness - Results from loss of muscle mass and decreased protein synthesis, leading to functional decline.
  • Loss of appetite (anorexia) - Driven by tumour-induced changes in metabolic signalling and inflammatory cytokines that suppress appetite.
  • Fatigue - Caused by muscle wasting, metabolic dysregulation, and systemic inflammation, leading to reduced energy availability.
  • Early satiety - Occurs due to impaired gastric emptying and reduced gastrointestinal motility associated with cancer and systemic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Muscle wasting - Visible loss of muscle mass, especially in the temporal, clavicular, and thigh regions, due to increased proteolysis and reduced protein synthesis.
  • Weight loss - Significant, rapid weight loss despite normal or even increased caloric intake, caused by tumour-induced catabolic processes.
  • Pallor - May result from underlying malignancy-related anaemia or overall poor nutritional status.
  • Tachycardia - Often seen as a result of anaemia or systemic inflammation.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Rapid, unexplained weight loss - Suggests advanced cancer with systemic involvement and warrants urgent evaluation.
  • Severe muscle wasting - Can indicate late-stage cachexia and is associated with poor prognosis.
  • Anorexia and early satiety - May signify advanced cachexia or gastrointestinal involvement of the malignancy.
  • Refractory fatigue - Could indicate systemic progression or poor nutritional status, leading to severe functional decline.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Absence of weight loss - Some patients may maintain weight due to oedema or tumour mass, masking the underlying muscle wasting.
  • Normoalbuminaemia - Patients with cachexia may have normal serum albumin levels despite significant malnutrition, making diagnosis more challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Serum albumin - Often low due to malnutrition or inflammation, reflecting the severity of the catabolic state.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated due to systemic inflammation associated with tumour and cachexia.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show anaemia, reflecting underlying malignancy, inflammation, or malnutrition.
  • CT scan - Can demonstrate muscle wasting, especially in advanced cases, and assess tumour burden.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • An elderly male with advanced lung cancer presenting with progressive weight loss, muscle wasting, fatigue, and anorexia over 6 months, refractory to nutritional support.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Can cause muscle wasting and weight loss, but usually without systemic inflammation and more pronounced respiratory symptoms.
  • Congestive heart failure (CHF) - May cause weight loss and muscle wasting, but typically presents with oedema, dyspnoea, and cardiac findings.
  • Chronic liver disease - Can lead to muscle wasting and anorexia, but more often presents with jaundice, ascites, and coagulopathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Nutritional support - Enteral or parenteral feeding is provided, though often insufficient to reverse cachexia alone.
  • Anti-inflammatory agents - Corticosteroids or non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) can reduce systemic inflammation and improve appetite.
  • Progestogens (e.g., megestrol acetate) - Used to stimulate appetite and induce weight gain in cancer cachexia patients.
  • Exercise and rehabilitation - Physical therapy to improve muscle function and quality of life.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Severe functional impairment - Progressive muscle wasting leads to weakness and immobility, significantly reducing quality of life.
  • Increased mortality - Cachexia is a direct cause of death in 20-30% of cancer patients.
  • Recurrent infections - Immunosuppression and malnutrition increase the risk of infections, complicating the patient's overall prognosis.
