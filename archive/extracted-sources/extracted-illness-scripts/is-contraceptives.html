Contraceptives
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Contraceptives refer to methods or devices used to prevent pregnancy. These can be hormonal (e.g., oral contraceptive pills, injectable methods, implants, patches), barrier methods (e.g., condoms), intrauterine devices (IUDs), or behavioural and permanent methods. Hormonal contraceptives function primarily by preventing ovulation, thickening cervical mucus, or altering the uterine lining.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Approximately 70% of women in the UK aged 16-49 use some form of contraception, with around 28% using oral contraceptive pills.
  • Worldwide: Usage varies widely; approximately 64% of women of reproductive age use some contraceptive method, with hormonal contraceptives being highly popular in developed regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Combined hormonal contraceptives (CHCs) inhibit the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, suppressing follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinising hormone (LH) to prevent ovulation.
  • Progestin in hormonal methods thickens cervical mucus, creating a barrier to sperm. Additionally, it thins the endometrial lining, reducing the likelihood of implantation if fertilisation occurs.
  • Non-hormonal methods like IUDs create a sterile inflammatory response in the endometrium, which is toxic to sperm and egg cells, preventing fertilisation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Immediate onset: Barrier methods such as condoms act immediately upon use.
  • Delayed onset: Hormonal contraceptives may take 7-14 days to reach full efficacy, depending on the method and timing within the menstrual cycle.
  • Long-term use: Some methods (e.g., IUDs, implants) are effective for years, with immediate cessation upon removal. Other hormonal methods require a transition period to restore ovulation post-discontinuation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Menstrual changes: Hormonal methods often cause irregular or lighter periods due to endometrial thinning.
  • Mood changes: Can be seen with hormonal methods due to modulation of central nervous system receptors.
  • Weight gain: Some users report weight gain with progestin-only methods, possibly due to fluid retention.
  • Headaches: Oestrogen in combined methods can affect vascular tone and sensitivity, leading to migraines or headaches in some individuals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Acne improvement or worsening: Changes in skin oil production are modulated by hormonal contraceptives, particularly with progestin types.
  • Blood pressure changes: Some individuals on combined hormonal contraceptives may have elevated blood pressure due to oestrogen effects on the vasculature.
  • Breast tenderness: Related to hormonal effects on breast tissue sensitivity, particularly with oestrogen-containing methods.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: Could signify complications like ectopic pregnancy or severe progestin-related effects.
  • Severe headache or vision changes: May indicate increased risk of stroke, especially in users of oestrogen-containing contraceptives who have additional risk factors.
  • Unilateral leg swelling and pain: Suggests potential deep vein thrombosis (DVT), particularly in oestrogen-containing contraceptive users.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Persistent amenorrhea post-cessation: Although rare, amenorrhea can persist after discontinuing long-term contraceptive use due to prolonged hormonal suppression.
  • Severe mood disturbances: Rarely, individuals may experience significant mood or depressive symptoms that persist, often seen in those with prior mood disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy test: To rule out pregnancy if contraception failure is suspected, or in case of amenorrhea.
  • Blood pressure measurement: Recommended before initiation and periodically for those on combined hormonal methods.
  • Liver function tests: In individuals with history or risk of liver disease, as oestrogen-containing contraceptives may affect hepatic function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents with intermittent headaches and light, irregular periods after 3 months of starting combined oral contraceptives, with a history of well-controlled hypertension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): Irregular periods and weight changes, but typically not linked to contraceptive use.
  • Pregnancy: Similar symptoms, but confirmable with a positive pregnancy test and absence of contraceptive use or contraceptive failure.
  • Primary hypertension: If blood pressure elevation occurs independently of contraceptive use.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Combined Oral Contraceptive Pill: Standard first-line option; choice of type depends on patient tolerance and risk factors.
  • Progestin-Only Contraceptives: For those with contraindications to oestrogen, such as hypertension or migraine.
  • IUD or Implant: Long-acting reversible options suitable for individuals seeking low-maintenance methods.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Contraceptives"][/sc]
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE): Particularly with oestrogen-containing methods, with increased risk in smokers and those with a family history.
  • Hypertension: Seen more commonly in users of combined hormonal methods due to oestrogen effects on vasculature.
  • Osteoporosis: Extended amenorrhea from certain long-term contraceptives may impact bone health.
