Testicular Torsion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Testicular torsion is a urological emergency in which the spermatic cord twists, leading to obstruction of the testicular blood supply. This condition can cause ischaemia and potential testicular necrosis if not promptly treated, typically within 6 hours.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • In the UK, testicular torsion is relatively uncommon, with an incidence of about 1 in 4,000 males under age 25 annually.
  • Higher prevalence is noted in adolescents and young men (12â€“18 years), with the condition also occurring in neonates and young children, albeit more rarely.
  • Worldwide, testicular torsion is a leading cause of acute scrotum in younger males.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • The condition arises when the spermatic cord twists, obstructing venous drainage from the testicle. Venous congestion leads to arterial ischaemia, causing infarction if left untreated.
  • The primary risk factor is a congenital anomaly called the "bell-clapper deformity," where the testicle has increased mobility within the scrotum, predisposing it to twist.
  • As ischaemia progresses, cellular damage and necrosis ensue, potentially leading to loss of the affected testicle if revascularisation is not restored quickly.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms usually develop suddenly, with intense pain peaking within hours. Intervention is critical within the first 6 hours to preserve testicular viability.
  • Delayed presentation: Some cases may present after 6 hours, but the risk of irreversible damage increases significantly, often leading to a non-viable testicle by 12â€“24 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Acute, severe scrotal pain - Pain is sudden in onset and severe due to rapid ischaemia from cord twisting.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Common due to severe pain stimulating the vagal response.
  • Abdominal pain - Often referred pain due to the shared sensory innervation with the testes.
  • Sudden testicular swelling - Due to venous congestion and resultant oedema from vascular compromise.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Tender, high-riding testis - Testicle may be pulled up within the scrotum from cord twisting.
  • Absent cremasteric reflex - Loss of this reflex is typical, due to impaired nerve function from ischaemia.
  • Scrotal erythema and swelling - Inflammatory response secondary to compromised blood flow.
  • Testicular firmness - Indicative of progressive ischaemia and oedema within the testis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Severe scrotal pain with nausea/vomiting - Suggests acute torsion and impending ischaemia; urgent evaluation is required.
  • Absent cremasteric reflex - Indicates nerve compromise and is highly suspicious for torsion, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Rapid scrotal swelling and erythema - Can signify worsening ischaemia and inflammatory reaction; critical to address quickly.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Mild, intermittent pain - Some patients may have intermittent torsion and detorsion episodes, leading to subtler symptoms but increased risk of complete torsion.
  • Presentation without pain - Rare but possible in cases of missed or silent torsion, more common in neonates.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Doppler Ultrasound - Usually shows decreased or absent blood flow to the affected testis, confirming torsion. Limited by testicular position and ischaemia stage.
  • Urinalysis - Typically normal but can help exclude infection-related causes.
  • Clinical examination - Gold standard for high suspicion; surgery is often warranted without delay for definitive diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • A 16-year-old male presents with sudden, severe left-sided scrotal pain and nausea. Examination reveals a high-riding, tender testicle with absent cremasteric reflex.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Epididymitis - Typically has gradual onset, fever, and positive urinalysis; Doppler shows increased blood flow.
  • Inguinal hernia - Palpable mass that may extend into the groin; less likely to cause absent cremasteric reflex.
  • Hydrocele - Non-tender scrotal swelling with transillumination, no acute pain.
  • Appendix testis torsion - Less severe pain, often with a palpable "blue dot" sign near the upper testis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Urgent surgical detorsion and fixation - Within 6 hours to preserve testicular viability. Orchidopexy of the contralateral testicle is often performed to prevent future torsion.
  • Orchiectomy - Required if ischaemic damage is too extensive and revascularisation is unachievable.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Testicular infarction - Most common complication, occurring if torsion exceeds 6 hours without resolution.
  • Infertility - Possible if bilateral torsion or severe damage to one testicle; preservation possible if detorsion occurs promptly.
  • Chronic pain - Persistent scrotal pain can develop post-detorsion, possibly due to nerve damage or fibrosis.
