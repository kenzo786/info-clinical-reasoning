Muscle Cramps
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Muscle cramps are sudden, involuntary, and often painful contractions of a muscle or group of muscles. They most commonly affect the lower limbs, particularly the calves, and typically occur during rest or physical activity. They can be idiopathic or secondary to various underlying conditions, including electrolyte imbalances or neurological disorders.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 20% of individuals experience muscle cramps regularly, with higher prevalence in older adults.
  • Hospital prevalence: Common in hospitalised patients, especially those with chronic diseases, with prevalence up to 50% in intensive care settings.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 40% in the general population, varying by age and health status.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Muscle cramps occur due to hyperexcitation of motor neurons in the spinal cord, leading to sustained muscle contraction.
  • Contributing factors include:
    • Electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia) disrupting neuromuscular function.
    • Dehydration reducing intramuscular fluid, altering excitability.
    • Impaired circulation leading to ischaemia and accumulation of metabolites.
    • Neurological conditions causing heightened neuronal activity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset, typically lasting seconds to minutes.
  • Resolution phase: Residual soreness or tightness lasting hours to days.
  • Chronic recurrence: Episodes may recur sporadically or frequently, depending on underlying causes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Painful muscle contractions - Sudden, sharp pain due to sustained muscle contraction and accumulation of lactic acid.
  • Stiffness or tightness - Persistent muscle tension following the cramp, due to prolonged muscle fibre engagement.
  • Functional impairment - Temporary inability to use the affected muscle, reflecting ongoing contraction and fatigue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Visible muscle tightening - Muscle appears hard or knotted during a cramp, caused by hypercontracted fibres.
  • Tenderness - Pain on palpation due to inflammation or microtrauma.
  • Reduced range of motion - Stiffness in the affected muscle limits movement temporarily.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Cramping with systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss) - May indicate systemic illness such as malignancy or connective tissue disease.
  • Recurrent cramps with neurological deficits - Suggests conditions like motor neuron disease or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Cramping resistant to hydration/electrolyte correction - Raises concern for underlying metabolic or endocrine disorders.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Nocturnal cramps only - Could be associated with vascular insufficiency or idiopathic causes.
  • Cramps during low exertion - Possible indicator of electrolyte imbalances or endocrine dysfunctions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - Electrolytes (e.g., potassium, magnesium), renal function tests to identify imbalances or kidney dysfunction.
  • EMG (electromyography) - Assesses neuromuscular activity for nerve or muscle disorders.
  • Imaging - Doppler ultrasound for vascular insufficiency.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman presents with sudden, painful cramps in the calves, occurring mostly at night, with a history of poor hydration and mild hypokalaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Peripheral vascular disease - Pain worsens with exertion; diminished pulses on examination.
  • Neuropathy - Paresthesia or sensory deficits with cramps.
  • Electrolyte imbalances - Cramps associated with systemic symptoms like weakness or confusion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications - Adequate hydration, stretching exercises.
  • Pharmacotherapy - Quinine sulphate (under specialist advice), magnesium supplements if deficient.
  • Treat underlying cause - Correct electrolyte imbalances, manage vascular or neurological disorders.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Muscle Cramps"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain or discomfort - Persistent muscle damage or inflammation.
  • Functional impairment - Limitation in mobility or daily activities.
  • Sleep disturbances - Frequent nocturnal cramps disrupting rest.
