Metastatic Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Metastatic disease refers to the spread of malignant tumour cells from a primary cancer site to distant organs or tissues. It occurs when cancer cells detach from the original tumour, enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system, and establish secondary tumours in other parts of the body, such as the lungs, liver, bones, or brain.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 30-40% of cancer patients will develop metastases. Metastatic disease is a leading cause of cancer-related mortality, accounting for around 90% of cancer deaths worldwide. The most common primary sites for metastatic disease include breast, prostate, lung, and colorectal cancers.
  • Globally, metastatic disease affects millions of patients, particularly in advanced-stage cancers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Metastasis occurs in several stages:
    • Local Invasion - Cancer cells invade surrounding tissues, breaking through the basement membrane.
    • Intravasation - Tumour cells enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
    • Circulation - Circulating tumour cells (CTCs) survive in the blood or lymph, evading immune detection.
    • Extravasation - Tumour cells exit the vessels and invade distant tissues.
    • Colonisation - Cells proliferate and establish secondary tumours in the new tissue.
    • Angiogenesis - The metastatic tumour forms its own blood supply, promoting further growth.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Early Stage - Often asymptomatic as cancer cells begin to metastasise.
  • Mid Stage - Gradual onset of symptoms depending on metastatic location (e.g., bone pain, neurological deficits).
  • Late Stage - Significant tumour burden with organ dysfunction, worsening systemic symptoms, and high mortality risk.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Bone Pain - Common in bone metastases (e.g., from breast or prostate cancer), due to tumour invasion causing localised destruction and inflammation.
  • Fatigue - A frequent systemic symptom caused by cancer-related anaemia, inflammatory cytokines, and metabolic demands of the tumour.
  • Weight Loss - Cachexia results from metabolic alterations induced by tumour growth, systemic inflammation, and loss of muscle mass.
  • Shortness of Breath - May result from lung metastases, pleural effusion, or tumour burden affecting respiratory function.
  • Neurological Deficits - Symptoms like headaches, seizures, or motor deficits occur in brain metastases due to increased intracranial pressure or focal damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Palpable Masses - Metastases may present as detectable masses (e.g., lymph nodes, liver enlargement) due to tumour growth.
  • Pathological Fractures - Bone metastases weaken bones, increasing the risk of fractures with minimal trauma.
  • Neurological Signs - Focal deficits, such as hemiparesis or cranial nerve palsies, suggest brain or spinal metastasis.
  • Hepatomegaly - Liver metastases can cause an enlarged, palpable liver due to tumour infiltration.
  • Pleural Effusion - Lung metastases may lead to fluid accumulation in the pleural space, detectable on auscultation or percussion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe, Intractable Pain - Suggests extensive bone involvement or nerve compression, often indicating advanced disease.
  • Unexplained Weight Loss - May be a sign of widespread metastatic burden or cachexia, often associated with poor prognosis.
  • Neurological Deficits - Sudden-onset neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, seizures) can indicate brain metastases or spinal cord compression, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Jaundice - Suggests liver metastases or biliary obstruction due to tumour involvement, associated with significant morbidity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Silent Liver Metastases - May remain asymptomatic until significant liver involvement, presenting late with jaundice or hepatic failure.
  • Spinal Cord Compression - Presents with subtle back pain before progressing to neurological deficits such as leg weakness or bladder dysfunction.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • CT Scan - Commonly used for imaging metastases in various organs. Findings include masses, lytic lesions (bone), or effusions (lungs/pleura).
  • PET Scan - Identifies areas of high metabolic activity, useful for detecting both primary and metastatic lesions.
  • Bone Scan - Detects areas of increased bone turnover, common in bone metastases from cancers such as prostate and breast.
  • Liver Function Tests - Elevated alkaline phosphatase or transaminases may suggest liver metastases.
  • Biopsy - Histopathological confirmation of metastases, identifying cancer type and origin.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • 65-year-old male with a history of prostate cancer presenting with progressive lower back pain, fatigue, and recent weight loss. Imaging reveals lytic lesions in the lumbar spine consistent with bone metastases.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Primary Bone Tumour - Typically presents with localised pain but often lacks systemic symptoms seen in metastatic disease.
  • Multiple Myeloma - Bone pain, anaemia, and renal dysfunction, but characterised by "punched-out" bone lesions on imaging, rather than lytic or sclerotic metastases.
  • Osteoporosis with Compression Fractures - May cause pain but is associated with normal systemic markers, unlike the elevated tumour markers in metastatic disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Palliative Care - Symptom control through analgesics, radiotherapy, or bisphosphonates for bone pain.
  • Systemic Therapy - Chemotherapy, hormonal therapy (e.g., for breast or prostate cancer), or targeted therapy based on tumour type and molecular markers.
  • Radiotherapy - Targeted radiotherapy for localised metastases, particularly in bone or brain metastases, for symptom control.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Spinal Cord Compression - Can result from vertebral metastases, causing paralysis if untreated.
  • Pathological Fractures - Increased fracture risk in bone metastases, particularly in weight-bearing bones like the femur.
  • Organ Failure - Metastases to organs like the liver or lungs can lead to organ dysfunction or failure.
  • Cachexia - Severe weight loss and muscle wasting due to systemic effects of the tumour, contributing to poor prognosis.
