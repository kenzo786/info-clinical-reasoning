Dysmenorrhoea
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Dysmenorrhoea is defined as painful menstrual cramps that occur just before and/or during menstruation. It can be classified into primary dysmenorrhoea, with no identifiable underlying pathology, and secondary dysmenorrhoea, which is often associated with underlying pelvic pathology, such as endometriosis or adenomyosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Dysmenorrhoea is common, affecting around 45-95% of menstruating women in the UK at some point.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Dysmenorrhoea is one of the leading causes of gynaecological consultations and accounts for around 12% of referrals to secondary care.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Approximately 50-90% of adolescent females and young women experience dysmenorrhoea worldwide, with prevalence decreasing with age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Dysmenorrhoea involves uterine contractions triggered by an increase in prostaglandin levels, particularly prostaglandin F2Î± and prostaglandin E2, during menstruation. These compounds cause increased myometrial tone and contractions, reducing uterine blood flow and resulting in ischemic pain.
  • In primary dysmenorrhoea, pain is due to physiological prostaglandin release, while in secondary dysmenorrhoea, pain results from pathological conditions, which may cause inflammation or obstruction in the pelvic region, intensifying the pain experience.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically begins 1-2 days before menstruation and peaks within the first 24-48 hours of menstrual flow.
  • Duration: Pain often persists for 2-3 days but may last longer in cases of secondary dysmenorrhoea.
  • Chronicity: Primary dysmenorrhoea is cyclical, occurring with each menstrual period, while secondary dysmenorrhoea may present with progressively worsening or more persistent pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Lower Abdominal Pain - Typically crampy and located in the lower abdomen, due to uterine contractions and ischemia caused by prostaglandin release.
  • Back Pain - Often radiating to the lower back, this is linked to referred pain from uterine contractions affecting surrounding muscle and nerve structures.
  • Thigh Pain - Radiates from pelvic pain pathways, common due to shared innervation of the lower pelvis and thighs.
  • Nausea and Vomiting - Associated with the high prostaglandin levels that stimulate smooth muscle in the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Headaches - Often due to hormonal fluctuations associated with menstruation, as well as heightened sensitivity to pain.
  • Diarrhoea - Resulting from prostaglandin E2 action on the gut, increasing motility.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Tender Lower Abdomen - Upon palpation, tenderness can be noted in the lower abdomen due to underlying uterine contractions and inflammation.
  • Pallor or Sweating - Often present in patients with severe pain, likely due to sympathetic activation in response to intense discomfort.
  • Unremarkable Pelvic Exam in Primary Dysmenorrhoea - Pelvic exam usually shows no abnormalities, distinguishing it from secondary causes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Severe or Worsening Pain - May indicate secondary causes like endometriosis or pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), where pathological tissue growth leads to increased inflammation and discomfort.
  • Intermenstrual or Postcoital Bleeding - Suggests secondary pathology, such as endometrial polyps or malignancy, which disrupts the normal menstrual cycle.
  • Dyspareunia (pain during intercourse) - Commonly associated with endometriosis, PID, or adenomyosis, indicating potential inflammation or scarring in the pelvic area.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Mid-Cycle Pain - Occasionally, dysmenorrhoea may be mistaken for ovulatory pain (mittelschmerz) in patients who experience discomfort between cycles due to ovarian follicle rupture.
  • Persistent Pain without Menstrual Relation - Some may have dysmenorrhoea overlapping with chronic pelvic pain, often seen in secondary causes like endometriosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Ultrasound - Utilised to rule out secondary causes such as ovarian cysts or structural abnormalities; findings may include endometriomas or fibroids.
  • Laproscopy - In cases of suspected secondary dysmenorrhoea where endometriosis or adhesions are suspected, laparoscopy can provide a definitive diagnosis.
  • Blood Tests - FBC to check for anaemia in case of heavy bleeding, CRP or ESR to rule out infectious or inflammatory processes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • A 21-year-old female presents with cyclical lower abdominal pain that begins one day before menstruation, radiates to the lower back, and resolves after 48 hours. Pain is associated with nausea but no abnormal bleeding or dyspareunia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Endometriosis - Differentiated by chronic pelvic pain, dyspareunia, and abnormal uterine bleeding; confirmed by laparoscopy.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - Presents with purulent cervical discharge, intermenstrual bleeding, fever, and positive pelvic tenderness.
  • Ovarian Cysts - Acute pain onset with palpable mass on examination, often visible on ultrasound.
  • Adenomyosis - Presents with heavy and prolonged menstrual bleeding, often with a boggy and tender uterus on examination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) - First-line to reduce prostaglandin production, decreasing pain and inflammation.
  • Combined Oral Contraceptives - Used to suppress ovulation and reduce menstrual flow and prostaglandin release.
  • Hormonal IUD - Levonorgestrel-releasing IUD can help by reducing endometrial thickness and menstrual flow.
  • Secondary Dysmenorrhoea Treatments - May include surgery (e.g., laparoscopy for endometriosis) or GnRH analogues in cases of refractory endometriosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dysmenorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pelvic Pain - Especially if secondary causes like endometriosis remain untreated, potentially leading to long-term disability.
  • Psychosocial Impact - Dysmenorrhoea, particularly when severe, can lead to depression, anxiety, and decreased quality of life due to limitations in daily functioning.
