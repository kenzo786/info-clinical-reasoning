Sprains and Strains
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Sprains refer to ligament injuries caused by excessive stretching or tearing, typically occurring around joints. Ligaments are fibrous tissues that connect bones. A sprain often results from a sudden twist, fall, or impact. Common areas affected include the ankle, knee, and wrist.
  • Strains involve injury to muscles or tendons, usually caused by overstretching or overuse. Tendons are fibrous tissues that attach muscles to bones. Strains commonly affect the lower back, hamstring, or shoulder.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • In the UK, sprains and strains account for approximately 3% of all general practice (GP) consultations.
  • These injuries are a frequent reason for emergency department visits, particularly for musculoskeletal injuries, with ankle sprains being the most common.
  • Globally, sprains and strains are among the most common musculoskeletal injuries, especially in athletes, physically active individuals, and in occupational settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Sprains: Ligaments are subjected to forces beyond their normal range of motion, leading to partial or complete tears of the ligament fibres. This results in inflammatory responses, including swelling, pain, and loss of function at the affected joint.
  • Strains: Overstretching or repetitive use of muscles/tendons causes micro-tears or larger tears in the muscle or tendon fibres. This leads to localised pain, inflammation, and sometimes muscle weakness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (hours to days): Immediate pain, swelling, and reduced function due to tissue injury and inflammation.
  • Subacute phase (days to weeks): Gradual reduction in swelling, with restoration of some function. Mild pain and stiffness may persist.
  • Chronic phase (weeks to months): Full recovery in mild cases, although severe strains or sprains may result in long-term instability or weakness without proper rehabilitation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Pain - Localised pain in the affected area is due to tissue damage and inflammation. In sprains, this pain worsens with joint movement, while in strains, it is aggravated by muscle use.
  • Swelling - Swelling occurs due to localised inflammatory response, where fluids accumulate in the tissue around the injury site.
  • Bruising - Bruising results from damage to small blood vessels, causing blood to pool in the surrounding tissues.
  • Reduced mobility - Pain and swelling can limit movement, particularly in sprains where joint stability is compromised, or in strains where muscle contraction is painful.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Swelling - Visible swelling due to fluid accumulation in response to the tissue damage.
  • Bruising (Ecchymosis) - Discolouration due to bleeding under the skin following ligament or muscle damage.
  • Joint instability (for sprains) - The joint may feel unstable or give way due to ligament damage, particularly in moderate to severe sprains.
  • Muscle weakness (for strains) - Reduced muscle strength due to pain or disruption of muscle fibres.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Severe pain with inability to bear weight - May suggest a more severe injury, such as a complete ligament tear or bone fracture, which requires urgent intervention.
  • Numbness or tingling - Suggests possible nerve damage or compression, requiring further evaluation to prevent long-term neurological impairment.
  • Severe or progressive swelling - May indicate compartment syndrome, a surgical emergency characterised by increased pressure within a muscle compartment, compromising blood flow and tissue viability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Chronic strain with gradual onset - Particularly in athletes or manual labourers, the injury may develop over time with no clear traumatic event, leading to under-diagnosis.
  • Minimal swelling or bruising - In elderly patients or those with compromised vascular health, the inflammatory response may be blunted, leading to less obvious signs of injury.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Physical Examination - Manual testing of the affected joint or muscle (e.g., anterior drawer test for ankle sprain) can help assess ligament or muscle integrity.
  • X-ray - Typically used to rule out fractures in cases where severe pain, swelling, or inability to bear weight are present.
  • MRI - In cases of suspected severe ligament or tendon damage, MRI can provide detailed imaging of soft tissue injury.
  • Ultrasound - Can be useful in evaluating tendon or muscle injuries, particularly in dynamic assessments.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with acute onset of ankle pain and swelling after twisting his ankle while playing football. He is unable to bear weight on the affected side and reports significant tenderness over the lateral ankle.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Fracture - Typically presents with more severe pain, inability to bear weight, and possible deformity. X-rays confirm diagnosis.
  • Tendon rupture - Presents with loss of active movement (e.g., Achilles rupture leading to inability to plantarflex). Diagnosis confirmed with ultrasound or MRI.
  • Dislocation - Presents with deformity and severe pain. Joint appears misaligned. X-ray or clinical examination confirms diagnosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) - Initial management for both sprains and strains to reduce pain and swelling.
  • Analgesia - Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen are commonly recommended for pain and inflammation control.
  • Physiotherapy - Key for rehabilitation to restore range of motion, strength, and stability.
  • Bracing or splinting - May be used for more severe sprains to provide support and limit movement during the healing phase.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Chronic instability - Particularly with ankle sprains, repeated injuries can result in long-term joint instability and increased risk of future sprains.
  • Arthritis - Repeated ligament damage or severe sprains can increase the risk of joint degeneration and subsequent osteoarthritis.
  • Tendinopathy - Chronic strains can lead to tendinopathy, with persistent pain and stiffness in the affected tendon.
