Medication-induced Hearing Loss
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced hearing loss is sensorineural hearing impairment caused by ototoxic drugs. It typically affects the cochlea or auditory nerve, leading to permanent or reversible hearing loss, depending on the drug and duration of exposure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Medication-induced hearing loss is uncommon, but patients taking ototoxic drugs like aminoglycosides or chemotherapy agents have a higher risk, estimated at 1-2% of those prescribed.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Ototoxicity is seen in 10-20% of patients undergoing treatment with high-dose aminoglycosides or cisplatin.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Ototoxicity affects up to 60% of patients receiving cisplatin therapy globally, particularly in paediatric cancer patients.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Ototoxic drugs damage the cochlear hair cells, spiral ganglion cells, or stria vascularis. Aminoglycosides generate reactive oxygen species, leading to apoptosis in hair cells. Cisplatin similarly induces oxidative stress, targeting outer hair cells and leading to high-frequency hearing loss. Loop diuretics can disrupt the ionic balance in the endolymph, causing temporary auditory dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset of hearing loss within hours to days after drug administration, often starting with high-frequency loss.
  • Subacute phase: Progressive worsening over weeks if exposure continues, particularly with cumulative drug dosing (e.g., multiple cisplatin cycles).
  • Chronic phase: Persistent or permanent hearing loss if damage is irreversible, as with long-term use of certain medications (aminoglycosides, chemotherapy).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss (initially high-frequency) - Due to damage to cochlear outer hair cells, patients first notice difficulty with high-pitched sounds.
  • Tinnitus - Often presents alongside hearing loss due to cochlear damage disrupting auditory nerve signals, leading to the perception of ringing or buzzing.
  • Difficulty with speech discrimination - Particularly in noisy environments, as hair cell damage affects sound clarity and processing.
  • Vertigo or imbalance (less common) - Some ototoxic drugs affect the vestibular system, causing balance disturbances.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • High-frequency hearing loss on audiometry - Initial and most common finding, typically affecting frequencies above 4,000 Hz.
  • Absent or diminished otoacoustic emissions - Reflecting cochlear hair cell damage, commonly seen in ototoxicity.
  • Normal otoscopic exam - As the hearing loss is sensorineural, the outer and middle ear are unaffected.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset profound hearing loss - May indicate irreversible cochlear damage and the need for immediate drug discontinuation to prevent further loss.
  • Severe vertigo - Suggests vestibular toxicity, commonly seen with aminoglycoside use. Requires prompt intervention to prevent permanent balance issues.
  • Unilateral hearing loss - Although rare, unilateral involvement may raise concerns for an alternative pathology, such as vestibular schwannoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic high-frequency hearing loss - Early stages of ototoxicity may present without noticeable symptoms, especially in older adults or those with pre-existing hearing impairment.
  • Delayed onset of symptoms - In some cases, hearing loss may not manifest until weeks after drug exposure has ceased, particularly with cisplatin therapy.

  • Otoacoustic emissions (OAE) - Diminished or absent OAEs indicate cochlear outer hair cell dysfunction, an early sign of ototoxicity.
  • Vestibular testing - In cases of associated balance disturbances, ENG or caloric testing may reveal reduced vestibular function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old female presents with progressive high-frequency hearing loss and tinnitus after completing 3 cycles of cisplatin chemotherapy. Audiometry confirms high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss, consistent with medication-induced ototoxicity.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Presbycusis - Age-related bilateral high-frequency hearing loss; tends to progress more gradually than medication-induced loss.
  • Noise-induced hearing loss - Typically associated with a history of loud noise exposure; audiometry often shows a characteristic notch at 4 kHz.
  • Vestibular schwannoma - Presents with unilateral hearing loss and possible tinnitus; imaging would show a mass affecting cranial nerve VIII.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation of the ototoxic drug - Immediate cessation of the offending drug (if possible) to prevent further damage.
  • Hearing aids or cochlear implants - For irreversible hearing loss, assistive devices may improve quality of life.
  • Antioxidant therapy - Some studies suggest that antioxidants (e.g., N-acetylcysteine) may help mitigate ototoxicity by reducing oxidative damage.
  • Regular audiometric monitoring - For patients on high-risk medications, serial audiograms can detect early changes in hearing function.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-induced Hearing Loss"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss - High-dose or prolonged exposure to ototoxic drugs can result in irreversible sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Chronic tinnitus - Persistent ringing in the ears may significantly affect quality of life, particularly if hearing loss is profound.
  • Balance disorders - Vestibular toxicity can lead to chronic balance problems, especially in aminoglycoside toxicity.
