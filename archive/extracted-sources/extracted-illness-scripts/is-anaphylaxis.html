Anaphylaxis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening systemic hypersensitivity reaction characterised by rapidly developing airway, breathing, or circulatory compromise, typically associated with skin or mucosal changes. It is triggered by an exaggerated immune response to allergens, such as foods, insect stings, or drugs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 0.3-2% of the population has experienced anaphylaxis at some point.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Anaphylaxis accounts for around 0.1% of hospital admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: The incidence of anaphylaxis is rising globally, with rates ranging from 50 to 112 episodes per 100,000 person-years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis results from an IgE-mediated immune response where an allergen binds to IgE antibodies on the surface of mast cells and basophils, leading to the release of histamine and other inflammatory mediators.
  • This causes vasodilation, increased vascular permeability (leading to swelling), bronchoconstriction, and activation of the coagulation system, which can result in shock, airway obstruction, and multi-system involvement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis typically develops within minutes to hours after exposure to the allergen. Initial symptoms may be mild but can progress rapidly, peaking within 5-30 minutes.
  • In biphasic anaphylaxis, symptoms may recur 1-72 hours after the initial reaction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Skin/mucosal changes (flushing, urticaria, angioedema) - These are the most common symptoms, occurring in up to 90% of cases. They result from histamine-induced vasodilation and increased vascular permeability.
  • Respiratory symptoms (wheezing, dyspnoea, stridor) - Bronchoconstriction and laryngeal oedema due to histamine release can cause airway obstruction and respiratory distress.
  • Hypotension and shock - Histamine and other mediators cause vasodilation and capillary leakage, leading to distributive shock and circulatory collapse in severe cases.
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea) - Less common, occurring in about 30-45% of cases, and result from smooth muscle contraction and increased fluid secretion in the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Dizziness or syncope - This results from hypotension and cerebral hypoperfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - A compensatory response to hypotension caused by systemic vasodilation and fluid loss into the interstitial space.
  • Wheezing - Indicative of bronchoconstriction due to histamine release affecting the lower airways.
  • Angioedema - Swelling of the lips, eyelids, and face, resulting from increased vascular permeability.
  • Stridor - A high-pitched breathing sound caused by upper airway obstruction from laryngeal oedema.
  • Hypotension - A severe drop in blood pressure due to systemic vasodilation and leakage of fluid from the blood vessels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of respiratory distress - May signify severe bronchoconstriction or laryngeal oedema, both of which can quickly lead to complete airway obstruction.
  • Hypotension or shock unresponsive to fluid resuscitation - Indicates profound vasodilation and fluid extravasation requiring immediate administration of adrenaline (epinephrine).
  • Biphasic reaction - A second wave of symptoms occurring after the initial reaction may indicate a more severe or prolonged immune response, warranting extended observation and treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Isolated gastrointestinal symptoms - In rare cases, anaphylaxis may present predominantly with nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Absence of skin symptoms - While uncommon, anaphylaxis may occur without the classic skin findings, especially in cases of drug or insect venom-induced anaphylaxis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Serum tryptase levels - Elevated tryptase within 1-2 hours of onset indicates mast cell degranulation, supporting the diagnosis. Tryptase returns to baseline within 12-24 hours.
  • Skin prick testing or specific IgE levels - Used to identify the causative allergen after recovery from the acute episode.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - While non-specific, eosinophilia may suggest an allergic component.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old female with no significant medical history presents with sudden onset of generalised urticaria, dyspnoea, and hypotension after eating a peanut-containing meal, progressing over 15 minutes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope - Unlike anaphylaxis, syncope does not involve skin changes or respiratory symptoms and is usually triggered by emotional distress.
  • Asthma exacerbation - Asthma typically presents with wheezing but lacks the systemic features of anaphylaxis, such as hypotension or gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Panic attack - Can mimic the respiratory symptoms of anaphylaxis but lacks the hypotension, skin manifestations, and rapid progression.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Adrenaline (epinephrine) - 500 micrograms (0.5 mL of 1:1000 solution) intramuscularly into the anterolateral thigh is the first-line treatment. Repeat after 5 minutes if no improvement.
  • Antihistamines - Cetirizine or chlorphenamine can help reduce itching and urticaria but are not life-saving.
  • Corticosteroids - Hydrocortisone may help prevent biphasic reactions, though the evidence is weak.
  • Fluids and oxygen - Intravenous fluids and supplemental oxygen are critical in managing hypotension and hypoxaemia.
  • Observation - Patients should be observed for at least 6-12 hours due to the risk of biphasic reactions.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction - Laryngeal oedema can lead to fatal airway compromise if not treated promptly.
  • Anaphylactic shock - Circulatory collapse due to widespread vasodilation may cause multi-organ failure if not rapidly reversed with adrenaline and fluids.
  • Biphasic anaphylaxis - A recurrence of symptoms after initial resolution, requiring prolonged monitoring and treatment.
