Gestational Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Gestational hypertension is defined as new-onset hypertension (blood pressure â‰¥140/90 mmHg) occurring after 20 weeks of pregnancy in the absence of proteinuria or other systemic features of pre-eclampsia. It is typically transient and resolves after delivery.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: ~6-8% of pregnancies.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 8-10% of pregnancies are affected by gestational hypertension or pre-eclampsia combined.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar rates of 5-10% depending on the population, with increased prevalence in certain regions due to risk factors such as obesity and diabetes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of gestational hypertension is not fully understood, but it is thought to result from abnormal placental development and endothelial dysfunction. This leads to increased systemic vascular resistance and elevated blood pressure.
  • There is likely a disruption in angiogenic factors (e.g., placental growth factor), causing an imbalance in vasoconstriction and vasodilation mechanisms.
  • In some cases, gestational hypertension may be an early form of pre-eclampsia, although the absence of proteinuria distinguishes it from the latter initially.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Gestational hypertension typically develops after 20 weeks of gestation.
  • In most cases, it resolves within 6 weeks postpartum. However, some women may develop chronic hypertension post-pregnancy.
  • If left unmanaged, gestational hypertension can progress to pre-eclampsia, which may develop suddenly and unpredictably during the second half of pregnancy or shortly after delivery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Often asymptomatic - Many women with gestational hypertension do not experience any symptoms, which is why regular antenatal check-ups are essential for detection.
  • Headaches - Due to increased blood pressure leading to cerebral vasodilation and irritation.
  • Visual disturbances (blurred vision) - Indicative of raised blood pressure affecting cerebral perfusion or early signs of pre-eclampsia.
  • Oedema - Swelling, especially in the hands and face, is less common in gestational hypertension compared to pre-eclampsia but may occur due to fluid retention from endothelial dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Elevated blood pressure (â‰¥140/90 mmHg) - The defining sign of gestational hypertension, caused by increased systemic vascular resistance.
  • Oedema - Swelling in the extremities, particularly in more severe cases. Oedema is related to capillary leak and sodium retention.
  • Absence of proteinuria - Distinguishes gestational hypertension from pre-eclampsia, which would present with proteinuria.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Severe hypertension (â‰¥160/110 mmHg) - Indicates a high risk of progression to pre-eclampsia or eclampsia, requiring urgent management.
  • Severe headache, visual disturbances, or epigastric pain - May suggest pre-eclampsia or impending eclampsia, with potential complications like cerebral haemorrhage or liver damage.
  • Reduced fetal movements - May indicate placental insufficiency or fetal distress, a complication of worsening hypertension or pre-eclampsia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Mild or fluctuating hypertension - Some patients may present with borderline elevated blood pressure or intermittent hypertensive episodes, which can complicate diagnosis.
  • Late-onset hypertension - Some women may develop hypertension after 36 weeks, which can be mistaken for normal physiological changes in late pregnancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Blood pressure monitoring - Repeated measurements are essential to confirm the diagnosis of gestational hypertension.
  • Urinalysis - Used to rule out proteinuria (key in distinguishing from pre-eclampsia).
  • Blood tests - Full blood count, liver function tests, and kidney function tests to monitor for signs of pre-eclampsia (e.g., thrombocytopenia or elevated liver enzymes).
  • Ultrasound - Fetal growth and wellbeing assessment, as hypertension can lead to placental insufficiency and fetal growth restriction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman, 28 weeks pregnant, presents with asymptomatic elevated blood pressure of 145/95 mmHg noted during a routine antenatal visit. No proteinuria is found on dipstick urinalysis. She has no headaches, visual changes, or epigastric pain.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Pre-eclampsia - Differentiated by the presence of proteinuria and other systemic features (e.g., severe headache, visual disturbances).
  • Chronic hypertension - Pre-existing before 20 weeks of pregnancy or persists post-delivery.
  • White coat hypertension - Elevated blood pressure only in clinical settings but normal at home; confirmed via 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Antihypertensives - Labetalol is the first-line treatment in the UK. Nifedipine or methyldopa can be used as alternatives.
  • Monitoring - Regular antenatal appointments with blood pressure monitoring and urinalysis for early detection of progression to pre-eclampsia.
  • Delivery - If hypertension is severe or pre-eclampsia develops, early delivery may be necessary to protect maternal and fetal health.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gestational Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Progression to pre-eclampsia - Occurs in 15-25% of cases and is associated with more severe complications like eclampsia, HELLP syndrome, or placental abruption.
  • Fetal growth restriction - Due to placental insufficiency, can lead to small-for-gestational-age infants and poor perinatal outcomes.
  • Chronic hypertension - Gestational hypertension can persist or reoccur in subsequent pregnancies, increasing the long-term risk of cardiovascular disease.
