Cerebrovascular Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Cerebrovascular disease refers to a group of conditions that affect the blood vessels and blood supply to the brain, leading to conditions such as stroke, transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs), and vascular dementia. It occurs due to vessel blockage (ischaemia) or rupture (haemorrhage), causing brain tissue damage and neurological deficits.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, cerebrovascular disease is one of the leading causes of mortality and morbidity. Stroke affects about 1 in 6 people during their lifetime. In the UK GP population, the prevalence is approximately 1.5â€“2%, with higher rates in older populations. Globally, stroke is the second leading cause of death, with nearly 13 million new strokes occurring each year.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic cerebrovascular disease is caused by atherosclerosis or embolism, leading to narrowing or occlusion of cerebral arteries, causing ischaemia and infarction. Haemorrhagic cerebrovascular disease involves rupture of weakened vessels, leading to bleeding within the brain. Both mechanisms result in neuronal death due to oxygen and nutrient deprivation or direct damage from blood products.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Stroke or TIA symptoms occur suddenly, reaching their peak within minutes to hours.
  • Subacute phase: Neurological recovery may occur over weeks to months, depending on the extent of the damage.
  • Chronic phase: Permanent deficits may persist, and in some cases, vascular dementia or recurrent strokes can develop over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden weakness or numbness - Often unilateral (hemiparesis), caused by ischaemia affecting motor or sensory areas in the brain.
  • Speech disturbances - Aphasia or dysarthria occurs when language or motor speech areas are involved (e.g., Brocaâ€™s area).
  • Visual disturbances - Can include vision loss in one eye (amaurosis fugax) or hemianopia due to involvement of the optic pathways.
  • Dizziness and loss of coordination - Caused by cerebellar or brainstem involvement.
  • Headache - Often associated with haemorrhagic stroke due to the sudden increase in intracranial pressure from bleeding.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Hemiparesis - Weakness on one side of the body, indicating damage to the motor cortex or descending motor pathways.
  • Facial droop - Due to involvement of the facial nerve, often seen in ischaemic strokes.
  • Slurred speech - Indicative of involvement of motor speech areas or cerebellar dysfunction.
  • Altered consciousness - Can be seen in large strokes, especially haemorrhagic strokes, due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Ataxia - Loss of coordination, commonly seen with cerebellar infarcts.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe headache - Suggestive of subarachnoid haemorrhage, a medical emergency.
  • Sudden loss of consciousness - Could indicate a large haemorrhagic stroke or massive ischaemic stroke with herniation.
  • Seizures - May indicate a haemorrhagic stroke or cortical involvement in an ischaemic stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Silent strokes - Asymptomatic strokes, often detected incidentally on imaging, occur when small infarcts do not produce overt neurological symptoms.
  • Confusion or memory loss - May be misdiagnosed as dementia, especially in older patients, reflecting vascular dementia or multiple small strokes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • CT Brain - Non-contrast CT is used to differentiate ischaemic from haemorrhagic stroke. Ischaemic strokes appear as hypodense areas after a few hours, while haemorrhage appears hyperdense immediately.
  • MRI Brain - More sensitive for detecting early ischaemic changes, especially in small or posterior circulation strokes.
  • Carotid Doppler - Assesses carotid artery stenosis, which can lead to ischaemic strokes.
  • ECG - To detect atrial fibrillation, a common cause of embolic strokes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with sudden onset of right-sided weakness and difficulty speaking. CT brain confirms an ischaemic stroke in the left middle cerebral artery territory.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Migraine with aura - Differentiated by gradual onset of neurological symptoms, often with a history of recurrent headaches.
  • Seizure with post-ictal paralysis (Todd's paralysis) - Transient weakness following a seizure, typically with a history of epilepsy.
  • Hypoglycaemia - Can cause focal neurological deficits, but typically presents with systemic symptoms like sweating and confusion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute ischaemic stroke: Thrombolysis with alteplase if within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Mechanical thrombectomy for large vessel occlusion.
  • Haemorrhagic stroke: Blood pressure control (target systolic <140 mmHg) and neurosurgical intervention in select cases.
  • Secondary prevention: Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin or clopidogrel), management of risk factors (e.g., antihypertensives, statins, anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Disability - Permanent neurological deficits (e.g., hemiparesis, aphasia) are common following large strokes.
  • Vascular dementia - Multiple strokes can lead to progressive cognitive decline.
  • Post-stroke seizures - More common in haemorrhagic strokes or large cortical strokes.
