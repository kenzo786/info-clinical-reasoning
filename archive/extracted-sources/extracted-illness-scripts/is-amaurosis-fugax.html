Amaurosis Fugax
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Amaurosis fugax is a transient, painless monocular loss of vision caused by reduced blood flow to the retina. It is often described as a "curtain falling" or "shade descending" over the visual field and typically resolves within minutes to hours.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 1 in 5000 per year.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Frequently seen in stroke clinics, as it often indicates transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar patterns seen globally, with higher rates in older populations and those with cardiovascular risk factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Amaurosis fugax results from transient retinal ischaemia, often due to emboli originating from carotid artery disease or atrial fibrillation. Less commonly, it may result from vasospasm or systemic hypoperfusion.
  • Emboli cause obstruction of retinal arterioles, reducing blood supply to retinal ganglion cells, leading to temporary visual loss.
  • Resolution occurs as emboli dissolve or perfusion improves, restoring retinal blood flow.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of monocular vision loss lasting seconds to minutes, rarely up to an hour.
  • Intermittent episodes: May occur sporadically over days to weeks if underlying embolic source persists.
  • Resolution: Visual recovery is typically complete without residual deficits once perfusion is restored.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Sudden monocular vision loss - Painless and transient, caused by retinal ischaemia from reduced blood flow.
  • Described as a "curtain" or "shade" - This visual disturbance reflects the specific loss of blood flow to retinal ganglion cells.
  • Complete visual recovery - Indicative of transient ischaemic episodes where retinal perfusion is quickly restored.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Normal fundoscopic examination - Most episodes resolve before assessment; however, embolic plaques (Hollenhorst plaques) may occasionally be visible.
  • Carotid bruits - Suggests significant carotid artery stenosis as a source of emboli.
  • Neurological examination - Typically normal unless a concurrent transient ischaemic attack (TIA) occurs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Concurrent weakness or aphasia - Suggests a TIA involving the cerebral hemisphere, requiring urgent intervention to prevent stroke.
  • Severe headache - May indicate giant cell arteritis, especially in patients over 50 with systemic symptoms.
  • Prolonged vision loss - Raises concern for retinal artery occlusion or other serious vascular events.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Bilateral transient visual loss - May occur with vertebrobasilar ischaemia instead of typical monocular loss.
  • Gradual onset - Rare but may suggest optic neuropathy or other non-vascular causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Carotid Doppler ultrasound - Detects carotid artery stenosis; plaques are a common embolic source.
  • ECG and echocardiography - Identifies atrial fibrillation or cardiac sources of emboli.
  • ESR/CRP - Elevated in giant cell arteritis, which may mimic or cause amaurosis fugax.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with hypertension presents with sudden, painless monocular vision loss lasting 10 minutes, described as a "curtain falling," with complete resolution by the time of assessment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Retinal artery occlusion - Persistent vision loss, often with a pale retina on fundoscopy.
  • Giant cell arteritis - Associated with headache, jaw claudication, and elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Optic neuritis - Painful vision loss, often with colour vision changes and afferent pupillary defect.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Address underlying cause: Antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin) and management of carotid stenosis (e.g., carotid endarterectomy if significant).
  • Secondary prevention: Control cardiovascular risk factors, including hypertension, diabetes, and smoking cessation.
  • Giant cell arteritis: High-dose corticosteroids initiated immediately to prevent vision loss.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic stroke - High risk in untreated patients with embolic amaurosis fugax.
  • Permanent vision loss - May occur if the underlying cause is not addressed.
  • Recurrent episodes - Suggest poor control of embolic or cardiovascular risk factors.
