Vitamin B12 Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency is a condition characterised by low levels of vitamin B12 (cobalamin) in the body, which is essential for DNA synthesis, red blood cell production, and neurological function. The deficiency often results from malabsorption, inadequate dietary intake, or autoimmune conditions such as pernicious anaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Prevalence ranges from 5-15%, especially in older adults and those with gastrointestinal disorders.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher in hospitalised elderly patients, especially those with malabsorption syndromes.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 6% of people under 60 and 20% over 60 may have low B12 levels.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 is absorbed in the ileum after binding with intrinsic factor (IF), produced by parietal cells in the stomach. Deficiency typically arises from:
    • Pernicious anaemia: Autoimmune destruction of parietal cells reduces intrinsic factor.
    • Malabsorption syndromes: Conditions like coeliac disease or surgical resection of the ileum impair absorption.
    • Dietary insufficiency: Particularly in vegans or those with restricted diets.
  • Deficiency affects rapidly dividing cells (e.g., red blood cells) and the nervous system, leading to megaloblastic anaemia and neurological manifestations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Asymptomatic or subtle symptoms such as fatigue.
  • Progressive phase: Over months to years, leading to anaemia, cognitive dysfunction, and peripheral neuropathy.
  • Late phase: Severe neurological impairment (e.g., ataxia, spasticity) if left untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to impaired red blood cell production leading to anaemia and decreased oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Paresthesia - Abnormal sensations, often in hands and feet, from demyelination of peripheral nerves.
  • Glossitis - Inflammation of the tongue due to epithelial cell turnover disruption.
  • Memory loss - Cognitive impairment arises from reduced synthesis of neurotransmitters and myelin.
  • Dyspnoea on exertion - Due to anaemia and reduced oxygen carrying capacity of blood.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Result of anaemia and reduced red blood cell production.
  • Ataxia - Loss of coordination due to degeneration of the posterior and lateral columns of the spinal cord.
  • Loss of vibration and proprioception - Caused by damage to the dorsal columns of the spinal cord.
  • Smooth, red tongue - Due to epithelial atrophy (glossitis).
  • Tachycardia - Compensatory response to anaemia as the heart attempts to increase oxygen delivery.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe neurological symptoms: Progressive numbness, difficulty walking, and incontinence. May indicate irreversible spinal cord damage (subacute combined degeneration).
  • Severe anaemia: If left untreated, can lead to heart failure, especially in older adults or those with pre-existing heart conditions.
  • Psychiatric symptoms: Cognitive decline and depression in advanced cases, particularly in elderly patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic anaemia: Mild vitamin B12 deficiency may present only with low haemoglobin and macrocytosis on blood tests, especially in elderly patients.
  • Isolated psychiatric symptoms: Confusion, memory impairment, or depression may occur without the classic signs of anaemia or neuropathy.
  • Normocytic anaemia: In some cases, anaemia may be normocytic rather than macrocytic, particularly if iron deficiency coexists.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Full blood count: Macrocytic anaemia (MCV >100 fL), often with low haemoglobin. Reflects impaired DNA synthesis.
  • Serum B12 levels: Low vitamin B12 (<150 pmol/L). Indicates poor absorption or inadequate intake.
  • Methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine: Elevated in B12 deficiency due to impaired metabolism. More sensitive markers than serum B12.
  • Intrinsic factor antibodies: Positive in pernicious anaemia, the most common cause of B12 deficiency in the UK.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old vegan female presents with progressive fatigue, paraesthesia in her feet, and mild cognitive impairment over the past 6 months. Blood tests reveal macrocytic anaemia and low serum B12.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Folate deficiency: Also causes macrocytic anaemia, but lacks neurological symptoms and often due to dietary insufficiency or alcoholism.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause macrocytic anaemia, fatigue, and cognitive impairment, but with other signs like bradycardia and dry skin.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Usually causes microcytic anaemia and presents with pallor, fatigue, and tachycardia, without neuropathy or cognitive issues.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Can mimic neurological symptoms, but typically has more acute, relapsing-remitting presentations with normal blood counts.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Initial treatment: Intramuscular hydroxocobalamin 1 mg every 2-3 days for 2 weeks (per NICE guidelines).
  • Maintenance: Hydroxocobalamin 1 mg every 3 months for life in pernicious anaemia or ongoing malabsorption.
  • Dietary insufficiency: Oral cyanocobalamin may be sufficient if malabsorption is not present.
  • Monitoring: Check serum B12 and blood counts periodically, particularly in those on maintenance therapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin B12 Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord: Irreversible damage to the posterior and lateral columns, leading to long-term disability.
  • Megaloblastic anaemia complications: Severe anaemia can lead to heart failure, particularly in elderly patients.
  • Neuropsychiatric disorders: Untreated B12 deficiency can lead to permanent cognitive impairment and psychiatric disorders.
