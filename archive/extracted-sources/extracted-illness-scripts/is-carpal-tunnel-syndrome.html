Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) is a compressive neuropathy caused by entrapment of the median nerve as it passes through the carpal tunnel at the wrist. It is characterised by pain, numbness, tingling, and weakness in the hand, particularly affecting the thumb, index, and middle fingers.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 7-16% of adults report symptoms suggestive of CTS.
  • UK hospital prevalence: CTS is the most common entrapment neuropathy and accounts for a significant proportion of elective hand surgeries.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by study, ranging from 3-6% in the general population, higher in individuals with specific occupational risk factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel, a narrow passage bounded by carpal bones and the transverse carpal ligament.
  • Increased pressure due to inflammation, oedema, repetitive motion, or anatomical variations.
  • Nerve ischemia and impaired axonal transport lead to sensory and motor dysfunction, primarily affecting the first three digits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Intermittent symptoms, often at night or after specific activities.
  • Progressive phase: Persistent symptoms with increasing functional impairment and weakness.
  • Chronic phase: Potential irreversible nerve damage and muscle atrophy if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Numbness and tingling - Typically affects the thumb, index, and middle fingers due to median nerve distribution. Caused by sensory nerve compression.
  • Night-time symptoms - Worsening at night due to wrist positioning and fluid redistribution increasing tunnel pressure.
  • Pain - Localised to the wrist or radiating proximally or distally, related to nerve inflammation.
  • Weakness - Difficulty gripping or pinching due to motor fibre involvement and muscle denervation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Tinel's sign - Tingling or discomfort when tapping over the median nerve at the wrist. Indicates nerve irritation.
  • Phalen's manoeuvre - Symptoms reproduced with wrist flexion, reflecting increased pressure in the carpal tunnel.
  • Thenar muscle atrophy - Wasting of the thenar eminence due to prolonged median nerve compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe weakness - May indicate acute nerve injury or ischaemia.
  • Persistent numbness or atrophy - Suggests chronic and potentially irreversible nerve damage.
  • Pain unrelated to activity - May indicate alternative diagnoses such as cervical radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Symptoms outside the median nerve distribution - Can occur due to coexistent conditions like double crush syndrome.
  • Pain without numbness - May occur in early stages or atypical anatomical presentations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Nerve conduction studies - Prolonged distal latency and reduced conduction velocity. Reflects impaired nerve transmission.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Evidence of denervation in thenar muscles in advanced cases.
  • Ultrasound - Median nerve enlargement at the wrist, indicating compression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of nocturnal paraesthesia and intermittent pain in the right hand, worsening with prolonged computer use. Examination reveals positive Phalen's manoeuvre and Tinel's sign.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Radiating pain and paraesthesia originating from the neck, often with neck pain and dermatomal distribution.
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Diffuse rather than focal symptoms, often bilateral and associated with a history of diabetes.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Joint pain, swelling, and stiffness in addition to nerve compression symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Conservative - Wrist splinting, particularly at night, and avoidance of exacerbating activities.
  • Medical - Corticosteroid injections for symptom relief.
  • Surgical - Carpal tunnel release surgery for severe or refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent nerve damage - Leading to chronic pain and functional impairment.
  • Muscle atrophy - Particularly of the thenar muscles, resulting in diminished hand function.
  • Post-surgical complications - Including scar tenderness and incomplete symptom resolution.
