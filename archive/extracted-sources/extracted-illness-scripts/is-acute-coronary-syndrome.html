Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
ACS Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) refers to a spectrum of conditions caused by sudden, reduced blood flow to the heart muscle (myocardium). This spectrum includes [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How are unstable angina NSTEMI and STEMI different?" instructions="Unstable Angina (UA): Ischaemic symptoms (e.g., chest pain at rest or minimal exertion, worsening pattern) WITHOUT myocardial necrosis (normal troponin level). ECG may show transient ST/T wave changes or be normal.
    NSTEMI (Non-ST Elevation MI): Ischaemic symptoms WITH evidence of myocardial necrosis (elevated troponin). ECG shows ST depression, T-wave inversion, or may be normal. No ST elevation.
    STEMI (ST Elevation MI): Ischaemic symptoms WITH evidence of myocardial necrosis (elevated troponin) AND persistent ST-segment elevation on ECG (or new LBBB). Usually indicates complete coronary occlusion." text="unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI"][/sc]. ACS typically results from the disruption of [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Explain atherosclerotic plaque rupture pathophysiology" instructions="Atherosclerosis involves the build-up of fatty plaques within artery walls. In ACS, a plaque often becomes 'vulnerable' due to inflammation, developing a thin fibrous cap.
    Rupture/Erosion: This cap can rupture or erode, exposing the thrombogenic lipid core and matrix to the bloodstream.
    Thrombosis: This triggers platelet aggregation and the coagulation cascade, forming a thrombus (clot) that obstructs blood flow partially (UA/NSTEMI) or completely (STEMI)." text="atherosclerotic plaques"][/sc] within the coronary arteries, leading to thrombus formation and vessel occlusion.

ACS Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • ACS typically results from the instability, rupture, or erosion of an atherosclerotic plaque in a coronary artery.
  • This event triggers [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Describe the process of coronary thrombosis" instructions="Plaque disruption exposes thrombogenic substances (like tissue factor, collagen).
    1. Platelet Adhesion & Activation: Platelets stick to the site and activate.
    2. Platelet Aggregation: Activated platelets release mediators (e.g., ADP, thromboxane A2) recruiting more platelets, forming a plug.
    3. Coagulation Cascade Activation: Tissue factor triggers the clotting cascade, generating fibrin.
    4. Thrombus Formation: Fibrin mesh stabilises the platelet plug, forming an occlusive thrombus." text="intracoronary thrombus formation"][/sc].
  • The thrombus obstructs coronary blood flow, causing myocardial [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What happens to the heart muscle during ischaemia?" instructions="Ischaemia is an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand, usually from reduced blood flow. The heart muscle shifts to anaerobic metabolism (producing lactate), leading to cellular dysfunction, contractile failure, and potentially ECG changes (ST depression, T-wave inversion). Symptoms like angina result from stimulation of cardiac nociceptors by ischaemic metabolites (e.g., adenosine, bradykinin)." text="ischaemia"][/sc] (insufficient oxygen supply).
  • Prolonged or severe ischaemia leads to myocardial injury and potentially irreversible cell death, known as [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is myocardial necrosis?" instructions="Necrosis is pathological cell death due to injury like severe ischaemia. In MI, ischaemia lasting > 20-40 mins depletes ATP, disrupts ion pumps, causes cell swelling, membrane damage, and release of intracellular proteins (like troponin) into the blood. This triggers inflammation and eventually scar tissue formation. It differs from apoptosis (programmed cell death)." text="necrosis"][/sc].
  • The extent and duration of occlusion, alongside factors like collateral blood supply, determine whether the patient develops [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How does pathophysiology relate to UA NSTEMI STEMI diagnosis?" instructions="UA: Ischaemia without necrosis (normal troponin). Often due to transient or partial occlusion.
    NSTEMI: Ischaemia causing necrosis (raised troponin) but typically without full-thickness (transmural) injury pattern on ECG (no ST elevation). Often subtotal occlusion or distal embolisation.
    STEMI: Ischaemia causing necrosis (raised troponin) WITH transmural injury pattern (ST elevation or new LBBB). Usually implies complete, persistent occlusion of a major coronary artery." text="unstable angina, NSTEMI, or STEMI"][/sc].
ACS Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms typically develop acutely over minutes to hours.
  • Progression: Without intervention, ischaemia can lead to [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How quickly does myocardial infarction develop?" instructions="Significant myocardial necrosis can begin within 20-40 minutes of complete coronary occlusion. The infarct typically expands over the next 3-6 hours, sometimes longer ('wavefront phenomenon' - necrosis progresses from subendocardium outwards). Early reperfusion aims to interrupt this process and limit infarct size. 'Time is muscle'." text="myocardial infarction (necrosis)"][/sc] within hours.
  • Recovery: Timely [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is reperfusion therapy in ACS?" instructions="Reperfusion means restoring blood flow to the ischaemic myocardium.
    Methods: Primarily Primary PCI (angioplasty/stenting) for STEMI, or fibrinolytic ('clot-busting') drugs if Primary PCI delayed.
    Goal: To salvage viable ('stunned' or 'hibernating') heart muscle, limit infarct size, preserve LV function, and improve survival.
    Timing is critical, especially for STEMI." text="reperfusion"][/sc] can salvage myocardium. Established necrosis leads to permanent scarring and potential long-term [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Explain ventricular remodelling after MI" instructions="Ventricular remodelling involves changes in the size, shape, structure, and function of the left ventricle after MI. It includes infarct expansion, scar thinning, hypertrophy of non-infarcted segments, and progressive LV dilation. While initially compensatory, it often becomes maladaptive, contributing to heart failure development. Key treatments like ACE inhibitors and beta-blockers help attenuate adverse remodelling." text="ventricular remodelling"][/sc] over weeks to months.

ACS Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How to interpret ECG findings in suspected ACS?" instructions="Perform and interpret within 10 mins of first medical contact! Look for:
    STEMI Criteria: New ST elevation at the J-point in ≥2 contiguous leads (≥1mm in limb leads, ≥2mm in V2-V3, ≥1.5mm in V2-V3 for women, ≥1mm in other precordial leads) OR new LBBB.
    NSTEMI/UA Findings: New horizontal or down-sloping ST depression (≥0.5mm) or T-wave inversion (≥1mm) in ≥2 contiguous leads.
    Territory: Leads indicate infarct location (e.g., II, III, aVF = inferior; V1-V4 = anterior; I, aVL, V5-V6 = lateral).
    Crucially: A normal ECG does NOT exclude ACS! Repeat ECGs (e.g., at 15-30 min intervals) are vital if suspicion remains high." text="Electrocardiogram (ECG)"][/sc]:     Critical first test; identifies STEMI vs NSTEMI/UA, guides immediate reperfusion decisions.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How are high-sensitivity troponins used in ACS diagnosis?" instructions="Cardiac Troponins (T or I) are highly sensitive and specific biomarkers for myocardial necrosis.
    Diagnostic Criteria: MI diagnosis requires a significant rise and/or fall pattern in serial measurements (e.g., at 0 and 1-3 hours depending on assay/protocol) with at least one value above the 99th percentile upper reference limit (URL), in the appropriate clinical context.
    High-Sensitivity Assays: Enable rapid rule-out/rule-in protocols (e.g., 0/1h or 0/2h algorithms). Check local hospital guidelines.
    UA: Troponins remain normal by definition." text="Cardiac Troponins (T or I)"][/sc]:     Key biomarker confirming myocardial necrosis (elevated in NSTEMI/STEMI). Serial measurements using local protocols are essential.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What routine blood tests are needed for ACS workup?" instructions="FBC: Check haemoglobin (anaemia can worsen ischaemia), platelet count (relevant for antiplatelets), WCC (inflammation/infection).

    Glucose: Check for diabetes, assess for stress hyperglycaemia.
    Lipid Profile: Assess cardiovascular risk, guide long-term statin therapy (usually started inpatient).
    Coagulation Screen: Baseline PTT/INR may be needed before anticoagulation." text="Other Blood Tests"][/sc]:     Include FBC, U&Es, glucose, lipids as baseline and risk assessment.

ACS Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate Management: Aim to relieve pain, reduce myocardial oxygen demand, and inhibit thrombus progression. Key initial steps include:
    • Aspirin 300mg loading dose (chewed or soluble) - give immediately unless clear contraindication/allergy.
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What drugs are used in the immediate management of ACS?" instructions="Think 'MONA-B' +/- antiplatelet/anticoagulant, but tailor to patient:
      Morphine: IV titration for severe pain. Use cautiously (can cause hypotension, bradycardia, N&V).
      Oxygen: ONLY if hypoxic (SpO2 <94% UK). No benefit/potential harm if normoxic.
      Nitrates (GTN): Sublingual spray/tablet or IV infusion for ongoing pain/ischaemia. Contraindicated in hypotension (SBP <90), suspected RV infarct, recent PDE5 inhibitor use.
      Aspirin: 300mg loading dose STAT.
      Beta-blocker: Oral (e.g., bisoprolol) usually started within 24h if haemodynamically stable (no HF, shock, bradycardia). Reduces myocardial oxygen demand.
      Additional Antiplatelet: Add a P2Y12 inhibitor (ticagrelor preferred if PCI planned, or clopidogrel) to aspirin ASAP (DAPT). Loading dose varies.
      Anticoagulation: Usually initiated early (e.g., fondaparinux for NSTEMI medical Rx, LMWH/UFH if PCI planned). Choice depends on ACS type & Rx strategy (check local guidelines)." text="Anti-ischaemic and anti-thrombotic therapies"][/sc].
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the reperfusion strategies for STEMI?" instructions="The goal is rapid restoration of flow in the occluded coronary artery to salvage myocardium.
    1. Primary Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (Primary PCI): Preferred strategy. Involves immediate coronary angiography +/- balloon angioplasty/stenting. Aim for door-to-balloon time <90 mins, or <120 mins from diagnostic ECG if transfer needed.
    2. Fibrinolysis: Pharmacological 'clot-busting' (e.g., tenecteplase). Used if timely Primary PCI (<120 mins) is not achievable. Aim for door-to-needle time <30 mins. Requires careful contraindication check. Often followed by transfer for 'rescue' or routine angiography within 24h.
    'Time is muscle!' is the key principle." text="Reperfusion Therapy (for STEMI)"][/sc]:     Urgent restoration of coronary blood flow via Primary PCI (preferred) or fibrinolysis.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How is NSTEMI / Unstable Angina managed?" instructions="Initial medical therapy includes DAPT (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor), anticoagulation (e.g., fondaparinux, LMWH), beta-blocker, statin, nitrates PRN.
    Risk Stratification: Essential using clinical features, ECG, troponin, and risk scores (e.g., GRACE score) to guide further management.
    Invasive Strategy (Coronary Angiography +/- Revascularisation): Offered to most NSTEMI patients. Timing depends on risk:
    • Immediate/Urgent (<2h or <24h): For very high/high-risk features (refractory pain, haemodynamic instability, arrhythmias, dynamic ST changes).
    • Within 72 hours: For intermediate risk.
    • Elective/Conservative: For selected low-risk patients (medical management +/- outpatient angiography)." text="Management of NSTEMI/UA"][/sc]:     Involves initial medical therapy and risk stratification (e.g., GRACE score) to guide timing of coronary angiography +/- revascularisation.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is long term secondary prevention after ACS?" instructions="Crucial to reduce risk of future cardiovascular events. Key components:
    Medications:
    • Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT): Aspirin 75mg daily lifelong + P2Y12 inhibitor (e.g., ticagrelor, clopidogrel) usually for up to 12 months.
    • High-Intensity Statin: (e.g., Atorvastatin 80mg daily) long-term for all patients.
    • ACE inhibitor (or ARB): Recommended long-term, esp. post-MI with LVEF ≤40%, heart failure, hypertension, or diabetes.
    • Beta-blocker: Recommended long-term, particularly if LV systolic dysfunction (LVEF ≤40%) or heart failure.
    • Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonist (MRA e.g., Eplerenone): Consider if LVEF ≤40% and heart failure or diabetes.
    Lifestyle Modification: Smoking cessation, healthy diet (e.g., Mediterranean), regular physical activity, weight management, optimal BP and diabetes control.
    Cardiac Rehabilitation: Structured, supervised programme - strongly recommended for all." text="Long-term Secondary Prevention"][/sc]:     Essential lifelong strategy involving medications, lifestyle changes, and cardiac rehabilitation.
ACS Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What arrhythmias commonly complicate ACS?" instructions="Arrhythmias are common, especially early post-MI.
    Ventricular Arrhythmias: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)/Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) are causes of sudden death (need ALS). Sustained VT can cause haemodynamic compromise. Premature ventricular contractions (PVCs) are common.
    Atrial Arrhythmias: Atrial Fibrillation (AF) is common, increasing stroke risk (may need anticoagulation).
    Bradyarrhythmias: Sinus bradycardia or Atrioventricular (AV) block (esp. inferior MI affecting AV node supply) can occur, may require atropine or temporary pacing." text="Arrhythmias"][/sc]:     Frequent, ranging from AF and PVCs to life-threatening VT/VF or high-degree AV block.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Explain heart failure and cardiogenic shock post MI" instructions="Acute Heart Failure: Develops due to loss of contractile function in the infarcted area ('pump failure'), leading to pulmonary oedema or signs of low cardiac output.
    Chronic Heart Failure: Can result from adverse ventricular remodelling over time.
    Cardiogenic Shock: Severe form of acute HF with tissue hypoperfusion due to extensive myocardial damage (typically >40% of LV). Characterised by persistent hypotension, low cardiac output, and end-organ dysfunction. High mortality rate." text="Heart Failure and Cardiogenic Shock"][/sc]:     Resulting from myocardial damage impairing pump function.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the mechanical complications of MI?" instructions="Occur due to necrosis and weakening of myocardial tissue; rarer now with early reperfusion but life-threatening.
    Ventricular Free Wall Rupture: Catastrophic tear leading to cardiac tamponade and usually rapid death.
    Ventricular Septal Rupture (VSR): Creates a hole between ventricles → left-to-right shunt, acute HF. Presents with new loud harsh pansystolic murmur.
    Papillary Muscle Rupture/Dysfunction: Leads to acute severe mitral regurgitation → pulmonary oedema/shock. Presents with new apical systolic murmur.
    All require urgent diagnosis (echocardiogram) and cardiothoracic surgical intervention." text="Mechanical Complications"][/sc]:     Rare but severe; include ventricular free wall rupture, VSR, and acute mitral regurgitation from papillary muscle rupture.

Suspect if hypotension, elevated JVP, and clear lung fields. Requires specific management (e.g., IV fluids carefully, avoid nitrates/diuretics initially).     LV Mural Thrombus: Clot forms within LV, esp. over akinetic areas (e.g., anterior MI, aneurysm). Risk of systemic embolisation (e.g., stroke). Usually requires anticoagulation.
    Recurrent Ischaemia/Reinfarction.
    Bleeding Complications: Related to necessary antiplatelet and anticoagulant therapies." text="Other complications"][/sc]:     Including RV infarction, pericarditis (early or Dressler's), LV aneurysm or mural thrombus, recurrent events, and bleeding.
