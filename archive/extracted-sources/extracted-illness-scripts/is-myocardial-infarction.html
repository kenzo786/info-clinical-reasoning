Myocardial Infarction (MI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction (MI), commonly referred to as a heart attack, is the irreversible necrosis of cardiac muscle resulting from prolonged ischaemia. It typically occurs due to the rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque in a coronary artery, leading to thrombus formation and a critical reduction in blood supply to the myocardium.

  • Risk Factors: Major risk factors include hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes mellitus, smoking, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and family history of premature coronary artery disease (CAD). Additional risk factors include chronic kidney disease, excessive alcohol consumption, and psychosocial stress. Emerging risk factors such as inflammatory conditions (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) and air pollution have also been implicated.

Secondary prevention includes the use of aspirin, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, and statins in patients with established coronary artery disease to prevent recurrent events.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Atherosclerosis leads to the formation of plaques in coronary arteries.
  • Step 2: Plaque rupture exposes the underlying subendothelial tissue, promoting platelet aggregation and activation of the clotting cascade.
  • Step 3: A thrombus forms, occluding the artery, and reducing blood flow to the myocardium.
  • Step 4: Prolonged ischaemia results in myocyte injury and irreversible necrosis, typically starting in the subendocardium and extending transmurally if untreated.
  • Step 5: Necrotic tissue releases cardiac biomarkers (e.g., troponins), which are detectable in the blood.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Ischaemia and infarction occur within minutes to hours. If untreated, necrosis progresses transmurally.
  • Subacute Phase: Over days to weeks, the infarcted myocardium begins healing through scar tissue formation, but this can lead to complications like left ventricular (LV) remodelling.
  • Chronic Phase: Weeks to months after MI, patients may develop heart failure or arrhythmias as long-term sequelae of infarcted myocardial tissue.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Chest Pain - Typically retrosternal, crushing or pressure-like pain that radiates to the left arm, neck, or jaw. It results from myocardial ischaemia triggering nociceptive pathways.
  • Shortness of Breath - Due to impaired left ventricular function leading to pulmonary congestion and decreased oxygen delivery.
  • Nausea and Vomiting - Often a result of vagal stimulation during an acute coronary event.
  • Diaphoresis - Sympathetic nervous system activation in response to the stress of the MI.
  • Fatigue or Weakness - Caused by reduced cardiac output, leading to decreased tissue perfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Sympathetic activation and compensatory increase in heart rate to maintain cardiac output.
  • Hypotension - May indicate cardiogenic shock or severe left ventricular dysfunction.
  • S3 or S4 Heart Sounds - Suggestive of left ventricular dysfunction or stiffening due to ischaemia or infarction.
  • Crackles on Lung Auscultation - Due to pulmonary oedema from left-sided heart failure.
  • Cool, Clammy Skin - Due to sympathetic vasoconstriction as a compensatory response to low cardiac output.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Sudden Severe Chest Pain - May indicate massive MI or aortic dissection. Requires immediate intervention.
  • Syncope - Suggestive of severe ischaemia affecting electrical conduction (e.g., arrhythmia) or cardiac output.
  • Persistent Hypotension - May indicate cardiogenic shock, a life-threatening complication requiring urgent intervention.
  • New Onset Heart Failure - Signifies significant myocardial damage and poor cardiac function.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Silent MI - Especially in older adults and diabetics, MI may present without classic chest pain but rather with fatigue, weakness, or even confusion due to autonomic dysfunction.
  • Epigastric Pain - Some patients, particularly women, may experience indigestion-like discomfort rather than typical chest pain, due to referred pain patterns.
  • Dyspnoea without Chest Pain - In some cases, especially in the elderly, the primary symptom may be shortness of breath due to heart failure without the classic pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • ECG - ST-segment elevation indicates an acute transmural infarction, while ST depression or T wave inversion suggests ischaemia or non-ST elevation MI (NSTEMI).
  • Troponins - Elevated troponin I or T is a marker of myocardial injury and is diagnostic of MI. Troponins rise 4-6 hours post-infarct and peak at 24-48 hours.
  • Coronary Angiography - Identifies the location and extent of coronary artery occlusion. Itâ€™s typically performed in acute settings to guide revascularisation therapy.
  • Echocardiography - Assess regional wall motion abnormalities and overall ventricular function, indicating areas of infarcted myocardium.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Stable Angina - Typically presents with exertional chest pain relieved by rest, unlike the prolonged pain of MI.
  • Aortic Dissection - Causes sudden, tearing chest or back pain, often associated with asymmetry of blood pressures in the limbs.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - Presents with pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea, and tachycardia, but often accompanied by signs of deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Pericarditis - Characterised by sharp, pleuritic chest pain that improves with leaning forward, and may have a pericardial rub on auscultation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Acute Management (UK Guidelines) - Immediate treatment with MONA: Morphine, Oxygen (if hypoxic), Nitrates, and Aspirin. Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel/ticagrelor) and prompt revascularisation (either percutaneous coronary intervention [PCI] or thrombolysis).
  • Post-MI Care - Beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins, and continued antiplatelet therapy to reduce further cardiovascular risk.
  • Lifestyle Modification - Smoking cessation, diet optimisation, and regular exercise are key to secondary prevention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Heart Failure - Occurs due to impaired ventricular function and remodelling post-infarction. May be chronic or acute (e.g., cardiogenic shock).
  • Arrhythmias - Ventricular fibrillation or tachycardia is common post-MI and may result from ischaemia-induced conduction abnormalities.
  • Pericarditis - Inflammation of the pericardium may occur as part of Dresslerâ€™s syndrome, an immune-mediated response weeks after MI.
  • Left Ventricular Aneurysm - Scar tissue from a large infarction can form an aneurysm, increasing the risk of thromboembolism or heart failure.
