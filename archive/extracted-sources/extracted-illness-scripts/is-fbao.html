Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO) occurs when a foreign object becomes lodged in the respiratory tract, partially or completely blocking airflow to the lungs. It can range from mild obstruction, where some air exchange is still possible, to severe obstruction, which can lead to life-threatening asphyxia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: FBAO is uncommon, but most cases are seen in children under 5 years or older adults over 65 years.
  • UK hospital prevalence: FBAO represents around 1 in 1,000 emergency department visits annually, with higher rates among paediatric and elderly populations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, FBAO is a significant cause of accidental deaths in young children and the elderly, accounting for approximately 17,000 deaths per year.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • FBAO occurs when a foreign object enters the airway (larynx, trachea, or bronchi) and obstructs airflow. This causes an inability to ventilate distal airspaces and may lead to hypoxia. Depending on the size and location of the obstruction, it can result in partial or complete airway blockage. If the object fully obstructs the trachea, it can lead to a cessation of air exchange, causing rapid hypoxia, unconsciousness, and potential death if not treated immediately.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Immediate phase: Sudden onset of choking, coughing, or respiratory distress upon obstruction.
  • Progressive phase: Partial obstructions may progress over seconds to minutes to full airway obstruction or lead to complications like pneumonia or atelectasis.
  • Recovery phase: If the foreign body is removed, rapid recovery is possible; however, prolonged obstruction can cause anoxic brain injury.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Sudden choking or gagging - Reflexive action caused by irritation of the airway when a foreign body is aspirated.
  • Inability to speak or cough - Seen in more severe obstructions where airflow is significantly impaired.
  • Dyspnoea - Difficulty breathing as a result of partial or complete obstruction of the airway, reducing air flow into the lungs.
  • Stridor - A high-pitched sound during inspiration, indicating partial obstruction of the upper airway.
  • Wheezing - Due to partial obstruction in the lower airway, leading to turbulent airflow in the bronchi.
  • Respiratory arrest - Complete airway obstruction leads to inability to ventilate, resulting in respiratory arrest.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the skin due to hypoxia from reduced oxygenation.
  • Use of accessory muscles - Observed as the patient strains to breathe during partial obstruction, using neck and intercostal muscles.
  • Absent breath sounds - No air movement upon auscultation in severe airway obstruction.
  • Consciousness changes - Loss of consciousness occurs in severe or prolonged obstruction due to hypoxia.
  • Hypoxia - Oxygen saturation below normal range due to inadequate oxygen intake.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Complete inability to breathe or speak - Signifies total airway obstruction and the need for immediate intervention.
  • Loss of consciousness - Indicates severe hypoxia, suggesting that urgent airway management is required to prevent cardiac arrest or brain injury.
  • Sudden collapse - Often a sign of severe obstruction leading to hypoxic cardiac arrest.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Silent aspiration - Some patients, particularly elderly or neurologically impaired, may aspirate foreign bodies without showing obvious signs of distress.
  • Chronic cough - Occurs when a small foreign body remains lodged in the lower airway, causing persistent symptoms mistaken for asthma or bronchitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - May show hyperinflation or atelectasis if the foreign body is in the lower airway, but often normal if located in the upper airway. Radiopaque foreign bodies can be visualised.
  • Flexible bronchoscopy - Diagnostic and therapeutic, allowing direct visualisation of the foreign body and its removal. Considered the gold standard in diagnosing FBAO.
  • Pulse oximetry - Oxygen saturation monitoring can indicate hypoxia, but normal results do not exclude partial obstruction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old boy suddenly chokes on a peanut while playing, presents with coughing and stridor, followed by respiratory distress and cyanosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Acute asthma exacerbation - Differs by history of asthma, wheezing, and response to bronchodilators.
  • Epiglottitis - Differs by the presence of fever, drooling, and dysphagia.
  • Anaphylaxis - Differs by rapid onset of airway swelling, urticaria, and hypotension following allergen exposure.
  • Croup - Viral infection presenting with barking cough, low-grade fever, and inspiratory stridor.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Initial management: Encourage coughing if partial obstruction. For severe obstruction, immediate back blows and abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre).
  • Advanced airway management: If basic life support measures fail, proceed with laryngoscopy and bronchoscopy to remove the foreign body.
  • Oxygen therapy: Administer supplemental oxygen to maintain oxygen saturation above 94%.
  • Post-removal care: Monitor for complications such as aspiration pneumonia or airway trauma, and provide antibiotics if indicated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Foreign Body Airway Obstruction (FBAO)"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia - Foreign body in the lower airway can lead to infection.
  • Bronchiectasis - Chronic obstruction may cause irreversible bronchial dilation and recurrent infections.
  • Anoxic brain injury - Prolonged hypoxia from complete airway obstruction may cause permanent brain damage.
