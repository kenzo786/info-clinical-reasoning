Achalasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Achalasia is a rare motility disorder of the oesophagus characterised by the failure of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS) to relax properly, accompanied by an absence of effective peristalsis in the oesophageal body. This leads to progressive difficulty in swallowing (dysphagia) and other related symptoms due to food and liquid stasis in the oesophagus.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of achalasia involves progressive degeneration of ganglion cells in the oesophageal myenteric plexus, which controls peristalsis and the relaxation of the LOS. This degeneration, of uncertain aetiology, leads to decreased nitric oxide production, impairing LOS relaxation. Without this relaxation, the LOS remains tonically contracted, preventing normal food passage into the stomach and causing food stasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Achalasia progresses slowly over several months to years. Symptoms may initially be mild, with intermittent dysphagia, but become progressively severe, often with regurgitation, chest pain, and eventually weight loss.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing solids and liquids from early stages, due to the lack of LOS relaxation and peristalsis.
  • Regurgitation - Food and liquid may be regurgitated due to oesophageal stasis, commonly during sleep or when bending over.
  • Chest Pain - Substernal chest discomfort may occur due to oesophageal distension and spasm.
  • Weight Loss - Chronic dysphagia and regurgitation lead to decreased intake and weight loss over time.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition signs - Muscle wasting and weight loss may occur due to poor oral intake.
  • Halitosis - Foul breath due to food retention and fermentation in the oesophagus.
  • Positive water siphon test - Difficulty in swallowing a glass of water, sometimes observed in clinical assessments.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Significant weight loss - Suggestive of advanced disease or possible malignancy (pseudoachalasia).
  • Severe chest pain - May mimic cardiac conditions; raises concern for oesophageal perforation if sudden.
  • Onset in older patients - Raises suspicion for pseudoachalasia due to malignancy, as primary achalasia typically presents in younger adults.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Isolated chest pain - Occurs in some cases without dysphagia, possibly due to oesophageal spasm.
  • No weight loss - Patients with early or intermittent symptoms may not have substantial weight loss, leading to potential under-recognition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]

  • Endoscopy - Rules out mechanical obstruction or malignancy, particularly pseudoachalasia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with progressive dysphagia to both solids and liquids over the past year, with associated regurgitation and a 5 kg weight loss.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Typically presents with heartburn; dysphagia is less common.
  • Oesophageal stricture - Progressive dysphagia for solids only, usually due to chronic acid exposure or malignancy.
  • Pseudoachalasia - Mimics achalasia symptoms but is secondary to malignancy, often with rapid onset in older patients.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Pneumatic dilation - Mechanical dilation of LOS using a balloon, often requiring repeated sessions.
  • Laparoscopic Heller myotomy - Surgical cutting of LOS muscle; effective but carries risk of GORD.
  • Botulinum toxin injection - Temporarily relaxes LOS but has short duration; used in patients unfit for surgery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal perforation - Rare complication, especially following dilation, presenting with severe chest pain.
  • Chronic aspiration - Due to regurgitation, leading to recurrent respiratory infections or aspiration pneumonia.
  • Increased risk of oesophageal cancer - Chronic stasis and inflammation are associated with a higher risk of squamous cell carcinoma.
