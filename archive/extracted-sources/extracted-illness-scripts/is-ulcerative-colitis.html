Ulcerative Colitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Ulcerative colitis (UC) is a chronic, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) characterised by relapsing and remitting inflammation of the colon's mucosal layer. It usually begins in the rectum and extends proximally in a continuous manner to involve part or all of the colon. The disease causes ulceration and bleeding, leading to symptoms such as diarrhoea, rectal bleeding, and abdominal pain.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 240 per 100,000 people.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 100-200 cases per 100,000 annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher in Western countries, with rates of 150-250 per 100,000 in Europe and North America; lower prevalence in Asia and Africa.
  • Peak incidence occurs in individuals aged 15-30, with a smaller second peak in those aged 50-70.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Ulcerative colitis is characterised by inappropriate immune responses in genetically predisposed individuals. The immune system mistakenly targets the colon's mucosa, leading to inflammation.
  • Inflammation is confined to the mucosa and submucosa and is most prominent in the distal colon (rectum). This leads to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-Î±, which cause damage to the mucosal lining, ulceration, and increased permeability of the epithelial barrier.
  • Neutrophil infiltration leads to the formation of crypt abscesses, which can further damage the epithelium and contribute to the development of ulcers.
  • Chronic inflammation results in architectural distortion of the colonic crypts and loss of goblet cells, contributing to the disease's chronicity and symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Ulcerative colitis typically follows a relapsing and remitting course. Periods of active disease (flares) alternate with periods of remission, which can last from weeks to years.
  • In acute flares, symptoms such as diarrhoea, bleeding, and pain can develop over days to weeks. Mild cases may resolve on their own or with treatment, while more severe cases may persist and require hospitalisation.
  • Chronic disease may lead to complications, such as colorectal cancer, after years of inflammation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea - Often the most common presenting symptom, usually bloody, resulting from inflammation and ulceration of the colonic mucosa that reduces its absorptive capacity and increases fluid secretion.
  • Rectal bleeding - A hallmark feature due to mucosal ulceration, which exposes blood vessels, leading to visible blood in stool.
  • Abdominal pain - Commonly in the left lower quadrant due to inflammation of the distal colon. Pain is often crampy and associated with bowel movements.
  • Tenesmus - A frequent sensation of needing to pass stool even when the bowels are empty, due to rectal inflammation.
  • Urgency - Due to irritability of the inflamed bowel, patients often feel a sudden, intense urge to defecate.
  • Fatigue - Often present in active disease due to chronic inflammation, blood loss, and malnutrition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Reflects chronic blood loss leading to iron-deficiency anaemia.
  • Tenderness on palpation - Especially over the left lower quadrant, correlating with the site of inflammation.
  • Tachycardia - May indicate significant blood loss, dehydration, or systemic inflammatory response in severe cases.
  • Fever - Suggests systemic inflammation or infection, particularly in severe flares or complications like toxic megacolon.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Could indicate toxic megacolon, a life-threatening complication where inflammation disrupts colonic motility, leading to distension and risk of perforation.
  • High fever and tachycardia - May indicate fulminant colitis or sepsis, requiring urgent hospitalisation.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Persistent weight loss might signal malnutrition or progression to colorectal cancer.
  • Persistent bleeding - Suggests severe disease or complications like dysplasia or cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Some patients, particularly those with limited proctitis, may present with only rectal bleeding and tenesmus without significant diarrhoea, which can lead to a delay in diagnosis.
  • Children may present with poor growth and delayed puberty as primary features rather than gastrointestinal symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy - The gold standard diagnostic test, showing continuous mucosal inflammation, erythema, loss of vascular pattern, friability, and ulcers. Biopsies confirm chronic inflammation.
  • Faecal calprotectin - Elevated in active UC due to neutrophilic inflammation. A marker of intestinal inflammation with values >250 Âµg/g suggesting active disease.
  • CRP and ESR - Non-specific markers of inflammation, typically elevated during flares.
  • Full blood count - Anaemia due to chronic blood loss and elevated white blood cell count during active inflammation.
  • Stool cultures - To exclude infectious causes of colitis, particularly if diarrhoea is acute.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of bloody diarrhoea, crampy left lower quadrant pain, and urgency. He reports tenesmus and fatigue. Examination reveals mild pallor and left-sided tenderness on palpation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Crohnâ€™s disease - Can affect any part of the GI tract and causes patchy, transmural inflammation, often with fistulae, whereas UC affects only the colon with continuous, superficial inflammation.
  • Infectious colitis - Typically presents acutely with diarrhoea and fever, with positive stool cultures, while UC is a chronic, relapsing condition.
  • Ischaemic colitis - Sudden-onset abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, typically in older patients with vascular risk factors. Imaging shows segmental colonic ischaemia.
  • Diverticulitis - Left lower quadrant pain and fever, but typically without diarrhoea or rectal bleeding. CT imaging confirms diverticulosis with inflammation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Aminosalicylates (5-ASAs) - First-line treatment for inducing and maintaining remission, particularly for mild to moderate disease. Mesalazine is commonly used.
  • Corticosteroids - Used in short courses for moderate to severe flares. Prednisolone is commonly prescribed.
  • Immunomodulators - Azathioprine or mercaptopurine are used for patients with steroid-dependent disease or those who do not respond to 5-ASAs.
  • Biologics - Anti-TNF agents (e.g., infliximab) or newer biologics (e.g., vedolizumab) are used in moderate to severe disease refractory to standard treatments.
  • Colectomy - Surgical intervention may be necessary for patients with refractory disease, dysplasia, or severe complications such as toxic megacolon.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ulcerative Colitis"][/sc]
  • Toxic megacolon - Acute, life-threatening complication of UC where the colon dilates massively due to loss of motility and excessive inflammation. Risk of perforation is high.
  • Colorectal cancer - Risk increases with disease duration, extent of colitis, and the presence of dysplasia. Patients with pancolitis are particularly at risk.
  • Strictures - Chronic inflammation can lead to fibrosis and narrowing of the colon, causing bowel obstruction.
