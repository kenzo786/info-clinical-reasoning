Liver Cirrhosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Liver cirrhosis is a chronic liver disease characterised by diffuse fibrosis, abnormal liver architecture, and regenerative nodules. It results from long-term liver damage from various causes, including alcohol, viral hepatitis, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease. Cirrhosis progresses through compensated and decompensated stages, leading to liver failure and portal hypertension.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of cirrhosis is estimated at 0.1% in the general population and 1-2% in at-risk populations such as heavy alcohol users. Hospital prevalence is higher, with cirrhosis accounting for around 60,000 hospital admissions annually. Worldwide, it is the 11th leading cause of death, with approximately 2 million deaths per year.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic liver injury leads to activation of hepatic stellate cells, causing excessive collagen deposition and fibrosis. Over time, this fibrosis disrupts normal liver architecture, forming nodules. Blood flow through the liver becomes restricted, causing portal hypertension. With disease progression, liver function deteriorates, leading to impaired synthesis of proteins (e.g., albumin, clotting factors), impaired detoxification, and impaired bile production. Portal hypertension also leads to complications such as varices and ascites.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Early/Compensated phase: Patients are often asymptomatic or have mild symptoms. This phase can last for years, particularly with risk factor modification.
  • Decompensated phase: Occurs when complications such as ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, or variceal bleeding develop. This phase is associated with a worse prognosis and often requires hospitalisation.
  • End-stage liver disease: Represents irreversible liver failure requiring liver transplantation for survival. Mortality is high without transplantation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Results from impaired metabolic function of the liver and is often one of the earliest symptoms.
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of the skin and sclera due to the liver's inability to conjugate bilirubin. Occurs in advanced stages.
  • Abdominal distension - Caused by ascites due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Confusion or altered consciousness (hepatic encephalopathy) - Results from the liverâ€™s inability to clear toxins like ammonia from the blood.
  • Pruritus - Itching due to the accumulation of bile acids in the skin.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Spider naevi - Dilated superficial capillaries caused by increased oestrogen levels, common in liver disease.
  • Palmar erythema - Reddening of the palms due to altered hormone metabolism.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver due to fibrosis, typically seen in earlier stages.
  • Ascites - Fluid accumulation in the abdomen due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Asterixis - Flapping tremor seen in hepatic encephalopathy due to impaired ammonia metabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting of blood, which may indicate variceal bleeding, a life-threatening complication of portal hypertension.
  • Rapidly worsening confusion or coma - Suggestive of hepatic encephalopathy, indicating liver failure.
  • Severe abdominal pain with fever - May indicate spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, a complication of ascites and liver failure.
  • Sudden onset of jaundice - Could indicate acute liver failure superimposed on cirrhosis (acute-on-chronic liver failure).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Cirrhosis can be entirely asymptomatic, especially in the early compensated stage, and may be discovered incidentally on imaging or abnormal liver function tests.
  • Subtle cognitive changes - Early hepatic encephalopathy may present with mild cognitive impairment, which is easily missed.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Raised ALT/AST, with AST:ALT ratio > 1 in alcoholic liver disease. Reduced albumin and prolonged prothrombin time (PT) in decompensated disease.
  • Ultrasound - May show liver nodularity, splenomegaly, and ascites. Can also assess portal vein flow.
  • Fibroscan - Non-invasive measure of liver stiffness, used to assess fibrosis and cirrhosis.
  • Endoscopy - To assess for varices in patients with portal hypertension.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with progressive fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal distension over the past 6 months. On examination, he has hepatomegaly, ascites, and spider naevi.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Similar presentation with hepatomegaly and abnormal LFTs, but often without a history of alcohol use.
  • Chronic hepatitis B/C - Can lead to cirrhosis, but may present with viral markers and less association with alcohol.
  • Heart failure - Can cause hepatomegaly and ascites (cardiac cirrhosis), but often with additional features of heart failure such as oedema and raised JVP.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Alcohol cessation - Key intervention in alcoholic cirrhosis to prevent further liver damage.
  • Management of complications - Diuretics for ascites, beta-blockers for varices, lactulose for hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Liver transplantation - The definitive treatment for end-stage liver disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Variceal bleeding - Life-threatening complication of portal hypertension.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome - Renal failure due to advanced cirrhosis.
  • Liver cancer - Cirrhosis is a major risk factor for hepatocellular carcinoma.
