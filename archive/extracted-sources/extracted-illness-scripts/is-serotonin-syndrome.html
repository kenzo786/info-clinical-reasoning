Serotonin Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Serotonin syndrome is a potentially life-threatening condition caused by excessive serotonergic activity in the central and peripheral nervous systems. It typically occurs due to the use of serotonergic medications, particularly when multiple agents are combined. Key features include a triad of autonomic instability, neuromuscular hyperactivity, and altered mental status.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Serotonin syndrome is relatively rare but underreported. In the UK GP population, its incidence is low, largely due to careful prescribing practices. In UK hospitals, it is more often encountered in critical care settings or emergency departments, especially when patients have polypharmacy or overdose on serotonergic agents. Worldwide, its incidence is rising due to the increasing use of antidepressants and other serotonergic drugs.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Serotonin syndrome results from overstimulation of the serotonin (5-HT) receptors in the CNS and peripheral systems, primarily the 5-HT1A and 5-HT2A subtypes. Increased serotonin activity affects neuromuscular transmission, thermoregulation, and autonomic function, leading to hyperreflexia, agitation, and hyperthermia. Drugs that enhance serotonin release, inhibit its metabolism (e.g., MAO inhibitors), or block its reuptake (e.g., SSRIs, SNRIs) can precipitate this condition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Serotonin syndrome often develops rapidly within hours of exposure to serotonergic drugs. Symptoms may progress swiftly if not recognised early, but the syndrome usually resolves within 24 hours once the offending agents are discontinued and treatment is initiated. Severe cases can lead to multi-organ failure and death if not promptly treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Agitation - Due to overstimulation of the serotonin system in the CNS, leading to altered mental status and restlessness.
  • Diaphoresis - Increased serotonin activity affects autonomic regulation, causing excessive sweating.
  • Hyperthermia - Uncontrolled serotonergic stimulation leads to excessive muscle activity, generating heat and causing hyperthermia.
  • Diarrhoea - Serotonin is a key neurotransmitter in the gut; its excess can lead to hypermotility and diarrhoea.
  • Tremor - Neuromuscular hyperactivity caused by increased serotonin levels, leading to involuntary muscle activity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Hyperreflexia - Due to serotonin-mediated neuromuscular overactivity, leading to exaggerated reflexes.
  • Clonus (inducible or spontaneous) - Repetitive, rhythmic muscle contractions, especially in the lower limbs, caused by heightened serotonergic activity in the spinal cord.
  • Mydriasis - Pupillary dilation due to autonomic dysregulation from excessive serotonergic stimulation.
  • Tachycardia - Autonomic hyperactivity leading to increased heart rate.
  • Hypertension - Excessive serotonin causes vasoconstriction and autonomic dysregulation, resulting in elevated blood pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Hyperthermia (>41Â°C) - Indicates severe neuromuscular activity and risk of rhabdomyolysis and multi-organ failure.
  • Rigidity - Severe muscular stiffness can indicate advanced toxicity and may lead to metabolic acidosis and renal injury.
  • Seizures - Occur due to excessive serotonergic activity in the CNS and can signal a life-threatening level of toxicity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • In mild cases, patients may present with non-specific symptoms like mild agitation, tremor, and diarrhoea, which can easily be mistaken for a viral illness or anxiety disorder.
  • Delayed onset of symptoms may occur if patients have taken drugs with a long half-life (e.g., fluoxetine), potentially delaying recognition of the syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Serotonin syndrome is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on history of serotonergic drug use and the presence of typical symptoms and signs.
  • CK levels - Elevated creatine kinase due to muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis), especially in severe cases with rigidity and hyperthermia.
  • Serum electrolytes - Electrolyte imbalances may occur secondary to dehydration, diarrhoea, or renal involvement.
  • ABG (arterial blood gas) - Metabolic acidosis may occur in severe cases due to hyperthermia and muscle breakdown.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) - NMS is also characterised by hyperthermia and altered mental status but tends to have more prominent muscle rigidity and slower onset compared to serotonin syndrome.
  • Malignant hyperthermia - Presents with hyperthermia and rigidity but is triggered by anaesthetic agents, not serotonergic drugs.
  • Anticholinergic toxicity - Can cause hyperthermia and agitation but typically presents with dry skin and urinary retention, unlike serotonin syndrome, which causes diaphoresis and diarrhoea.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate cessation of serotonergic agents - The first step in management is stopping the offending drugs.
  • Supportive care - IV fluids, cooling measures, and benzodiazepines for sedation and to reduce neuromuscular activity.
  • Cyproheptadine - A serotonin antagonist used in severe cases.
  • ICU admission - Required for severe cases with hyperthermia, seizures, or multi-organ dysfunction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Serotonin Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis - Muscle breakdown from severe rigidity and hyperthermia, leading to renal failure if untreated.
  • Multi-organ failure - A consequence of severe hyperthermia and metabolic derangement, leading to liver, kidney, and cardiac dysfunction.
  • Seizures - Can result from CNS overstimulation and may lead to permanent neurological damage if not controlled.
