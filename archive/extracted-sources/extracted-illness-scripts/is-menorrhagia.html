Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Menorrhagia, also known as heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB), is defined as excessive menstrual blood loss (>80 mL per cycle) that interferes with a woman's physical, emotional, social, or material quality of life. It is usually measured by prolonged menstrual periods lasting more than 7 days or significant blood loss requiring frequent pad or tampon changes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Menorrhagia affects approximately 20-30% of women of reproductive age attending GPs.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 5-10% of referrals to gynaecology outpatient clinics are for menorrhagia.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, it is estimated that 10-20% of women experience menorrhagia during their reproductive years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Menorrhagia can result from various pathophysiological processes:
    • Hormonal imbalances: Excessive oestrogen or insufficient progesterone can cause endometrial hyperplasia, leading to excessive bleeding during menstruation.
    • Uterine abnormalities: Fibroids, polyps, or adenomyosis can interfere with normal uterine contractions, preventing the uterine lining from shedding efficiently, resulting in heavy bleeding.
    • Coagulopathies: Bleeding disorders like von Willebrand disease can impair normal clotting mechanisms, leading to prolonged or heavy bleeding.
    • Iatrogenic causes: Intrauterine devices (IUDs) such as copper-based devices can increase menstrual blood flow.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Menorrhagia is typically a chronic condition, manifesting with each menstrual cycle. If associated with fibroids or polyps, the bleeding may progressively worsen. Hormonal imbalances may cause fluctuating severity. Postpartum women or those nearing menopause may experience episodic menorrhagia. Sudden onset menorrhagia should prompt evaluation for coagulopathy or malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Heavy menstrual flow - Patients often report soaking through pads/tampons within 1-2 hours, or needing double protection. This results from hormonal imbalances or uterine abnormalities like fibroids that disrupt normal uterine function.
  • Prolonged menstrual periods - Bleeding lasting more than 7 days is common, caused by inefficient shedding of the endometrium, especially in conditions like adenomyosis or hormonal imbalances.
  • Clots in menstrual blood - Large blood clots may occur due to pooling of blood in the uterine cavity, especially with fibroids or polyps.
  • Fatigue or dizziness - Due to chronic blood loss leading to iron deficiency anaemia, women may experience fatigue, light-headedness, or shortness of breath on exertion.
  • Painful periods (dysmenorrhoea) - Associated with conditions like adenomyosis or endometriosis, pain results from abnormal uterine contractions and tissue invasion into the uterine muscle.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Due to anaemia from chronic blood loss, patients may present with pale skin or mucous membranes.
  • Tachycardia - As a compensatory mechanism for anaemia, the heart rate may increase to maintain adequate oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Abdominal or pelvic mass - A large fibroid or uterine polyp may be palpable on abdominal or pelvic examination.
  • Oedema - In severe cases of anaemia, fluid retention and oedema may occur due to low serum protein levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - This can indicate endometrial cancer, which requires urgent evaluation via biopsy.
  • Sudden onset heavy bleeding - May be a sign of coagulopathies or malignancies. Immediate assessment is necessary to rule out life-threatening conditions.
  • Severe anaemia - Symptoms such as fainting, shortness of breath, and chest pain may indicate critical anaemia and require urgent transfusion and investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Some women may present with minimal overt symptoms but have severe anaemia due to chronic low-grade blood loss. This may be missed without thorough questioning about their menstrual history.
  • Patients with coagulopathies may have heavy bleeding only after surgical procedures or childbirth, but have otherwise normal menstrual histories.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Expected to show low haemoglobin levels and low mean corpuscular volume (MCV) in iron deficiency anaemia caused by chronic blood loss.
  • Serum ferritin - Decreased ferritin indicates depleted iron stores secondary to chronic blood loss.
  • Pelvic ultrasound - Used to detect uterine abnormalities such as fibroids, polyps, or adenomyosis that could cause menorrhagia.
  • Coagulation studies - If a bleeding disorder is suspected, tests such as prothrombin time (PT) and activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) can help assess clotting function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • A 34-year-old woman presents with a 12-month history of increasingly heavy menstrual bleeding, soaking through pads every 1-2 hours, and associated fatigue. Her periods last for 8-9 days. She reports clots in her menstrual blood and dizziness upon standing.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Endometrial polyps - Usually cause intermenstrual or postmenopausal bleeding, with less pronounced heavy menstrual bleeding.
  • Adenomyosis - Can present with similar menorrhagia, but tends to have more associated pain and a boggy, enlarged uterus on examination.
  • Coagulopathies (e.g., von Willebrand disease) - Suspect if there is a personal or family history of bleeding disorders or excessive bleeding after surgical procedures.
  • Hypothyroidism - Can cause menstrual irregularities including menorrhagia, often accompanied by symptoms such as weight gain, cold intolerance, and fatigue.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • First-line treatment: Tranexamic acid (1 g three times daily) or non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (e.g., mefenamic acid 500 mg three times daily) to reduce menstrual blood loss.
  • Hormonal therapies: Combined oral contraceptives, oral progestogens (norethisterone 5 mg three times daily), or the levonorgestrel intrauterine system (LNG-IUS) are effective in regulating cycles and reducing bleeding.
  • Surgical options: In cases unresponsive to medical treatment, endometrial ablation or hysterectomy may be considered.
  • Iron supplementation: To address iron deficiency anaemia, oral or intravenous iron therapy is recommended depending on the severity.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding)"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Chronic blood loss can lead to significant anaemia, requiring iron supplementation or blood transfusion.
  • Fatigue and reduced quality of life - The excessive bleeding and anaemia can lead to chronic fatigue, affecting daily activities and work performance.
  • Infertility - Conditions such as fibroids or endometrial polyps causing menorrhagia may also impair fertility if left untreated.
