Connective Tissue Diseases
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Connective tissue diseases (CTDs) are a group of autoimmune disorders characterised by inflammation and dysfunction of the connective tissues, including collagen and elastin. These diseases often involve multiple organ systems, including the skin, joints, muscles, blood vessels, and internal organs, and are typified by immune system dysregulation and chronic inflammation. Common examples include systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis (RA), systemic sclerosis (scleroderma), and SjÃ¶gren's syndrome.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: CTDs vary widely in prevalence. SLE affects approximately 1 in 1000 people, while RA affects around 1% of the UK population.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: CTDs account for a significant proportion of rheumatological admissions, particularly in specialist centres.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Worldwide, the prevalence of CTDs varies. RA has an estimated global prevalence of 0.5-1%, while SLE is rarer but affects around 5 million people globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • CTDs are characterised by an abnormal immune response in which the body produces autoantibodies that attack its own tissues, causing chronic inflammation.
  • Step 1: Genetic predisposition and environmental factors (e.g., infections, UV exposure) trigger immune dysregulation.
  • Step 2: This leads to the production of autoantibodies (e.g., anti-nuclear antibodies in SLE) and immune complexes.
  • Step 3: Immune complexes deposit in various tissues, causing inflammation and tissue damage (e.g., vasculitis, fibrosis).
  • Step 4: The result is multi-organ involvement, including skin rashes, arthritis, and damage to organs like kidneys (glomerulonephritis) and lungs (interstitial lung disease).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • CTDs are typically chronic conditions with periods of relapse and remission.
  • Initial phase: Symptoms may be nonspecific, such as fatigue, joint pain, or skin rashes. The disease can remain undiagnosed for months to years.
  • Progressive phase: As the disease advances, specific organ involvement becomes more apparent, e.g., renal involvement in SLE or fibrosis in systemic sclerosis.
  • Long-term phase: Without adequate control, CTDs can lead to irreversible organ damage and disability.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common in all CTDs, due to chronic inflammation and immune activation.
  • Joint pain (arthralgia) - Often migratory, symmetrical, and involves small joints; due to synovitis and inflammation of joint tissues.
  • Skin rashes - Photosensitive rashes (e.g., malar rash in SLE) occur due to immune complex deposition in the skin.

  • Shortness of breath - Common in advanced stages with lung involvement, such as interstitial lung disease (systemic sclerosis) or pleuritis (SLE).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Malar rash - Butterfly-shaped rash across the cheeks in SLE, caused by immune-mediated skin inflammation.
  • Swollen, tender joints - Due to synovitis, typically in RA and SLE.
  • Sclerodactyly - Tight, shiny skin on the fingers seen in systemic sclerosis, caused by fibrosis.
  • Oral ulcers - Painless ulcers commonly seen in SLE, due to immune-mediated tissue damage.
  • Digital ulcers - Seen in severe Raynaudâ€™s phenomenon, due to ischaemia from prolonged vasospasm.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Renal involvement (proteinuria, haematuria) - Indicates lupus nephritis, which can lead to renal failure if untreated.
  • Pulmonary hypertension - Seen in systemic sclerosis, due to fibrosis of pulmonary vessels, leading to heart failure.
  • Severe digital ulceration - Indicates critical ischaemia in Raynaudâ€™s, which may lead to gangrene.
  • Neurological symptoms (seizures, psychosis) - Seen in SLE, potentially indicating cerebral lupus, a life-threatening condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Isolated serositis - Some patients with SLE present only with pleuritis or pericarditis without typical skin or joint involvement.
  • Isolated Raynaud's phenomenon - Can be the initial manifestation of systemic sclerosis, especially in younger patients.
  • Unexplained chronic fatigue - This may be the only presenting feature in early CTDs and could lead to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Autoantibodies (ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Ro, anti-La) - Positive in most CTDs, reflecting autoimmunity. ANA is sensitive for SLE, while anti-dsDNA is more specific.
  • Rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-CCP - Positive in RA, anti-CCP being highly specific.
  • ESR and CRP - Typically elevated due to systemic inflammation.
  • Renal function tests (eGFR, creatinine, urinalysis) - To assess for renal involvement, especially in SLE with lupus nephritis.
  • Chest X-ray and CT - To assess for lung involvement such as interstitial lung disease in systemic sclerosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of joint pain, fatigue, and photosensitive rashes. She reports episodes of cold-induced finger blanching. Blood tests show elevated ESR and positive ANA.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Distinguished by more prominent joint involvement and lack of skin manifestations like rash.
  • Vasculitis - Can present similarly with systemic inflammation but often lacks the specific autoantibodies of CTDs.
  • Fibromyalgia - Causes widespread pain and fatigue but lacks the objective inflammatory findings of CTDs.
  • Infective endocarditis - Presents with joint pain and systemic symptoms, but with positive blood cultures and often cardiac involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Corticosteroids - To control acute inflammation, commonly used in SLE and systemic sclerosis flares.
  • Hydroxychloroquine - First-line for SLE to control skin, joint, and systemic symptoms.
  • Immunosuppressants (methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate) - Used for severe disease or organ involvement.
  • Biologics (rituximab, belimumab) - Targeted therapies used in resistant cases of SLE and RA.
  • Management of complications - Pulmonary hypertension in systemic sclerosis is managed with endothelin receptor antagonists, and ACE inhibitors are used for renal crisis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Connective Tissue Diseases"][/sc]
  • Renal failure - Especially in SLE with lupus nephritis, due to immune-mediated glomerular damage.
  • Pulmonary fibrosis - Seen in systemic sclerosis, caused by chronic inflammation and scarring of lung tissue.
  • Cardiac complications - Pericarditis and myocarditis can occur in SLE, leading to arrhythmias and heart failure.
  • Joint deformities - Common in RA, due to chronic synovitis and joint destruction.
