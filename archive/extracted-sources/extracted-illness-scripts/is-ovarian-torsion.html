Ovarian Torsion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion is the partial or complete rotation of the ovary around its supporting ligaments, leading to vascular compromise. This condition can lead to ischaemia and necrosis if not treated urgently. It is a gynaecological emergency that requires prompt intervention to prevent ovarian damage or loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Ovarian torsion is rare in primary care; most cases present directly in emergency settings.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Represents approximately 2.7% of emergency gynaecological surgeries.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar across countries, with an incidence of 5-10 per 100,000 females annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion often involves the twisting of both the ovary and fallopian tube around the infundibulopelvic and utero-ovarian ligaments. This twist occludes venous outflow, causing congestion, oedema, and ultimately, arterial occlusion. Without prompt intervention, this leads to tissue necrosis and ovarian loss.
  • Risk factors include ovarian enlargement (e.g., cysts, tumours), ovulation induction, pregnancy, and reproductive age. These factors increase the ovary's mobility and likelihood of torsion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe pain, lasting hours, that can progress rapidly to constant severe pain. Vascular compromise can lead to necrosis within hours to days.
  • Subacute phase: Occasionally, the pain fluctuates as the ovary intermittently untwists, leading to spontaneous resolution in rare cases. However, risk of permanent damage remains high.
  • Chronic phase: Chronic pelvic pain may develop if untreated, though cases rarely progress this far due to the severe nature of symptoms in acute cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Severe unilateral pelvic pain - Often the most common presenting symptom; results from vascular occlusion and ischaemia of the ovarian tissue.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Common in response to the severe pain and autonomic activation.
  • Radiating pain to the back or thigh - Occurs due to involvement of the pelvic nerve plexus, which may lead to referred pain.
  • Acute abdominal pain - Generalised abdominal pain may occur, often prompting initial suspicion of an intra-abdominal cause.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Tenderness in the lower abdomen - Localised tenderness, especially over the affected ovary, due to the ischaemic pain.
  • Guarding or rebound tenderness - Indicates peritoneal irritation from the ischaemic ovarian tissue.
  • Palpable adnexal mass - Occasionally found on pelvic examination if the ovary is enlarged due to a cyst or tumour.
  • Tachycardia - Occurs due to pain-induced sympathetic stimulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe unilateral pain - Signifies acute torsion, indicating potential ischaemia and risk of necrosis.
  • Vomiting and nausea - Suggests severe pain that may accompany an emergency condition.
  • Fever - May indicate ovarian necrosis with potential for infection if torsion persists untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Intermittent or fluctuating pain - Torsion can be intermittent if the ovary untwists, leading to confusing or atypical pain presentation.
  • Mild or no initial pain - Rarely, mild pain or discomfort may be present initially, especially in early or partial torsion cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) - Preferred imaging modality; may show an enlarged ovary, peripheralised follicles, and free pelvic fluid, indicating torsion.
  • Doppler ultrasound - May demonstrate absent or reduced ovarian blood flow, although normal flow does not rule out torsion as arterial supply can remain intact initially.
  • CT or MRI - Used if ultrasound findings are inconclusive. MRI is particularly useful for complex cases and provides high-resolution imaging.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old female presents with sudden, severe right-sided pelvic pain, nausea, and vomiting, lasting for several hours. Examination reveals right lower quadrant tenderness and guarding.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Acute appendicitis - Typically presents with right lower quadrant pain and tenderness, though does not usually cause adnexal tenderness.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Presents with unilateral pelvic pain and can cause nausea; ruled out by a negative pregnancy test.
  • Ruptured ovarian cyst - Sudden pain onset similar to torsion, though pain may be less intense and Doppler ultrasound usually shows preserved blood flow.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - Pain is often bilateral with cervical motion tenderness and associated fever; Doppler flow is usually normal.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Laparoscopic detorsion - Gold-standard intervention for ovarian torsion; prompt detorsion restores blood flow and can prevent necrosis.
  • Oophorectomy - Considered if ovarian necrosis has occurred. More common in postmenopausal women if torsion is identified late.
  • Pain management - Analgesics are administered pre-operatively to manage severe pain.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian necrosis - Occurs if blood flow is not restored promptly, leading to loss of ovarian function and potential fertility issues.
  • Infection and peritonitis - Necrotic ovarian tissue may become infected, leading to peritonitis if untreated.
  • Chronic pelvic pain - Long-term pain may develop after torsion, particularly if there has been significant tissue damage.
