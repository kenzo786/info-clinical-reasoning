Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Xerostomia, or dry mouth, refers to a condition where there is a decrease in the production of saliva by the salivary glands, leading to a sensation of dryness in the oral cavity. It is a symptom rather than a disease itself and can result from various causes, including medications, autoimmune diseases (like SjÃ¶gren's syndrome), radiation therapy to the head and neck, and dehydration.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of xerostomia in the general UK GP population is around 10-30%, depending on age and comorbidities.
  • In hospitalised patients, xerostomia is seen more frequently, especially in those receiving polypharmacy, affecting up to 50% of elderly inpatients.
  • Worldwide prevalence varies but is estimated to affect approximately 5-46% of people, with higher prevalence in older adults and those on medications that reduce salivary flow.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Saliva production is regulated by both the parasympathetic and sympathetic nervous systems. Stimulation of the parasympathetic nerves (via acetylcholine) increases the production of watery saliva, while sympathetic stimulation produces thicker mucous-rich saliva.
  • Xerostomia occurs when the salivary glands either produce insufficient saliva or when the salivary composition is altered. Causes include salivary gland destruction, dysfunction, or inhibition through medications, dehydration, autoimmune diseases, or direct injury (e.g., from radiation therapy).
  • Drugs like anticholinergics, antihistamines, and diuretics inhibit parasympathetic stimulation, leading to reduced saliva production. Autoimmune diseases such as SjÃ¶gren's syndrome cause lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of the salivary glands.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Xerostomia can develop acutely, such as with medication use or dehydration, or chronically, as in cases of autoimmune conditions like SjÃ¶grenâ€™s syndrome or after radiation therapy.
  • Chronic xerostomia can be insidious, often progressing over months to years, particularly in autoimmune diseases, and may be irreversible if gland damage is significant.
  • Acute cases may resolve with discontinuation of the causative agent or adequate hydration, whereas chronic forms usually require ongoing management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Dry sensation in the mouth - This is the hallmark symptom of xerostomia, resulting from inadequate saliva production due to glandular dysfunction or inhibition.
  • Difficulty speaking or swallowing - The absence of saliva impairs the lubrication of the oral cavity, making it difficult to form words or swallow food.
  • Altered taste - Saliva is crucial for taste perception; reduced saliva affects taste receptor function, leading to dysgeusia (distorted taste).
  • Burning or sore tongue - This may be due to the lack of protective saliva and subsequent irritation of the oral mucosa.
  • Frequent thirst - A compensatory response to the bodyâ€™s sensation of dryness and fluid loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Dry, cracked lips - Due to the lack of moisture in the oral cavity, leading to dehydration of surrounding tissues.
  • Dry and fissured tongue - The tongue may appear rough, with deep grooves due to the absence of protective saliva.
  • Oral mucosal dryness - A visibly dry oral cavity upon examination, sometimes with a lack of pooling saliva.
  • Dental caries - Saliva plays a critical role in protecting teeth from decay, and xerostomia predisposes to dental caries, especially at the gum line.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Absence of dry mouth sensation in elderly patients - Older adults may not report classic symptoms but present with dental caries or oral infections instead, possibly due to reduced sensory perception.
  • Intermittent symptoms - In cases of medication-induced xerostomia, symptoms may fluctuate depending on the timing of the drug.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Salivary flow rate test - Measures the quantity of saliva produced over time. A reduced flow rate (<0.1 mL/min unstimulated) indicates xerostomia.
  • Sialometry - Quantifies salivary gland output. Abnormal findings suggest impaired salivary function.
  • Schirmer's test - A test primarily for SjÃ¶grenâ€™s syndrome, where paper strips are placed under the lower eyelid to measure tear production (indirectly assessing autoimmune gland dysfunction).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]

  • Medication-induced xerostomia - Often caused by anticholinergics, antihistamines, or diuretics, with symptoms tied to drug usage and reversible upon discontinuation.
  • Diabetes mellitus - Polydipsia and dry mouth due to osmotic diuresis, often associated with polyuria and weight loss.
  • Dehydration - Acute dry mouth related to reduced fluid intake or excessive fluid loss, with rapid improvement upon rehydration.

  • Good oral hygiene - Regular dental visits and fluoride treatments are crucial to prevent dental caries and infections.
