Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency (VBI), also known as posterior circulation ischemia, refers to the reduced blood flow to the vertebral and basilar arteries, which supply the brainstem, cerebellum, occipital lobes, and thalamus. This condition can lead to transient or permanent neurological deficits, such as dizziness, vision disturbances, and ataxia. It typically results from atherosclerosis, embolism, or arterial dissection in the vertebrobasilar system.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • VBI is more common in older adults, particularly those over 60 years of age, with the risk increasing due to atherosclerotic changes. In the UK GP population, the condition is rare, accounting for around 5-10% of transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs). Worldwide, the prevalence varies but is estimated to be responsible for approximately 20-25% of strokes affecting the posterior circulation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • The vertebrobasilar arteries supply critical regions of the brain, including the brainstem and cerebellum. VBI occurs due to a reduction in blood flow from atherosclerosis, emboli, or dissection. Atherosclerosis leads to stenosis, reducing perfusion to these areas. Emboli or thrombi can occlude the arteries, leading to transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs) or stroke, while arterial dissection may result from trauma or underlying vessel fragility.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • VBI can present acutely, such as during a TIA, or progress over time in chronic cases of atherosclerosis. Symptoms typically last minutes to hours but may recur over weeks or months in episodic attacks. Acute stroke may result in more permanent deficits.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Dizziness/Vertigo - Common due to impaired perfusion of the brainstem and cerebellum, affecting balance and spatial orientation.
  • Diplopia (Double Vision) - Results from ischaemia of the cranial nerves controlling eye movements.
  • Dysarthria (Slurred Speech) - Ischaemia affecting the brainstem can impair speech articulation.
  • Ataxia - Lack of coordination occurs due to cerebellar hypoperfusion.
  • Syncope - Transient global ischaemia may lead to fainting due to insufficient blood flow to the brainstem.
  • Occipital Headache - Ischaemia in the occipital lobes may present with headache or visual disturbances.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Nystagmus - Abnormal eye movements due to cerebellar or brainstem involvement.
  • Ataxia - Unsteady gait or difficulty with coordination, indicating cerebellar ischaemia.
  • Dysmetria - Impaired ability to control the distance, speed, and power of a movement, often observed in cerebellar dysfunction.
  • Cranial Nerve Palsies - Involvement of cranial nerves III, IV, and VI may result in eye movement abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of symptoms - May suggest a TIA or stroke, requiring urgent evaluation and management.
  • Loss of consciousness - Could indicate extensive brainstem involvement or severe ischaemia.
  • Persistent focal neurological deficits - Likely represents progression to a stroke and requires immediate intervention to prevent further damage.
  • Severe headache - Could indicate dissection or haemorrhagic transformation of an ischaemic event.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Isolated vertigo - While vertigo is a common symptom, it can be easily misattributed to benign causes like labyrinthitis if not accompanied by other neurological signs.
  • Transient visual disturbances - Patients may present with only fleeting visual changes without other overt symptoms, particularly in elderly patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • MRI with Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI) - Most sensitive for detecting acute posterior circulation ischaemia.
  • CT Angiography (CTA) - Visualises the vertebrobasilar arteries and can detect stenosis, dissection, or occlusion.
  • Carotid Doppler Ultrasound - Often performed to assess for atherosclerosis but less sensitive for vertebrobasilar pathology.
  • ECG and Echocardiogram - To evaluate for cardiac emboli as a source of posterior circulation ischaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old man with hypertension and hyperlipidaemia presents with episodic dizziness, double vision, and ataxia lasting 10 minutes. Examination reveals nystagmus and dysmetria. MRI confirms vertebrobasilar ischaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Unlike VBI, BPPV is triggered by head movements and lacks neurological deficits such as diplopia or ataxia.
  • Labyrinthitis - Typically presents with isolated vertigo and hearing loss, whereas VBI involves multiple neurological symptoms.
  • Meniere's Disease - Features episodic vertigo, hearing loss, and tinnitus; however, there is no ataxia or cranial nerve involvement.
  • Stroke - Stroke causes more prolonged neurological deficits compared to the transient nature of VBI episodes, though both may have similar risk factors.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Antiplatelet Therapy - Aspirin or clopidogrel is recommended to reduce the risk of stroke in patients with VBI.
  • Statins - For patients with atherosclerosis, statin therapy is indicated to reduce cholesterol levels and stabilise plaques.
  • Blood Pressure Control - Target BP below 140/90 mmHg to reduce further vascular risk.
  • Endovascular Intervention - In cases of significant vertebral artery stenosis or occlusion, angioplasty or stenting may be considered.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Stroke - The most significant complication, leading to permanent neurological deficits. The risk of posterior circulation stroke is significant if VBI is untreated.
  • Dissection - Vertebral artery dissection can occur, particularly after trauma, and may lead to stroke.
  • Recurrent TIAs - Without management, patients may experience repeated episodes, increasing their risk of permanent damage.
