Discitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Discitis is an infection or inflammation of the intervertebral disc space, often caused by bacterial infection. This condition typically involves the vertebral bodies adjacent to the infected disc and is characterised by severe back pain and systemic symptoms. It can occur as an isolated infection but is commonly associated with vertebral osteomyelitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, discitis is rare, with an estimated incidence of 0.2-2 cases per 100,000 people per year, though it is more frequent in immunocompromised individuals and in hospitalised patients due to healthcare-associated infections.
  • Worldwide, discitis remains an uncommon condition but has increasing incidence due to the rise in invasive spinal procedures and prolonged hospitalisations.
  • It is more common in older adults and children under 5 years of age, with a male predominance.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Discitis usually begins as a haematogenous infection from a distant source or following spinal surgery. Bacteria enter the bloodstream and lodge in the avascular disc space, where immune response is limited. The infection causes local inflammation, leading to disc destruction and potentially spreading to adjacent vertebrae, resulting in osteomyelitis.
  • Staphylococcus aureus is the most common causative organism, but other organisms, including coagulase-negative staphylococci, Gram-negative bacteria, and, rarely, fungi, can also cause discitis, especially in immunocompromised patients.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Discitis often presents with the sudden onset of severe back pain, fever, and systemic symptoms within days to weeks.
  • Subacute phase: If untreated, symptoms persist with progressive worsening over weeks, leading to increased spinal damage and possible neurological compromise.
  • Chronic phase: Chronic cases may develop over months, with smouldering pain and potential structural damage that can cause long-term disability and deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Severe back pain - Typically worsens with movement due to the inflammatory response in the disc and adjacent structures.
  • Fever and malaise - Caused by systemic inflammatory response to infection, though fever may be absent in immunocompromised patients.
  • Reduced mobility - Due to pain and inflammation, patients may have difficulty moving the affected region.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, numbness) - In advanced cases, the infection may compress nerves, leading to radicular pain or myelopathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Localised tenderness - Pain upon palpation over the affected spinal segment is due to inflammation and pressure on the infected area.
  • Limited range of motion - Muscle spasm and pain restrict movement, especially in the affected spinal segment.
  • Neurological deficits - In cases where the infection spreads to neural structures, signs of motor or sensory deficit can occur.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Severe, unrelenting pain at night - Suggests a serious underlying infection and possible osteomyelitis.
  • Neurological impairment - Such as weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction, indicating possible spinal cord compression or abscess.
  • Systemic signs of sepsis - Hypotension, tachycardia, and confusion may indicate bacteraemia or disseminated infection, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with chronic, low-grade back pain without fever, especially if immunocompromised or elderly. Symptoms may be subtle and attributed to degenerative disease, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Blood tests: Raised inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) are typical; blood cultures may identify the causative organism.
  • MRI scan: The gold standard for imaging, showing disc space narrowing, vertebral involvement, and possible abscesses.
  • CT-guided biopsy: May be performed for microbiological diagnosis if blood cultures are negative, especially in cases with atypical organisms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old male presents with severe lumbar back pain worsening over two weeks, accompanied by fever and decreased mobility. MRI reveals disc space infection with vertebral involvement.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis: Similar infection but primarily involves bone rather than disc space; imaging differentiates these conditions.
  • Spinal epidural abscess: Causes severe pain and neurological deficits; MRI shows abscess around the dura rather than the disc space.
  • Degenerative disc disease: Common in older adults, presenting with chronic pain but without fever or systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Antibiotic therapy: IV antibiotics (e.g., flucloxacillin or vancomycin if MRSA suspected) for 4-6 weeks followed by oral antibiotics, based on sensitivity.
  • Analgesia: Pain management with NSAIDs and opioids as required.
  • Spinal stabilisation: Surgery may be needed if there is significant structural damage or neurological compromise.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Discitis"][/sc]
  • Spinal abscess: Formation of an abscess within the spinal canal can lead to cord compression and permanent neurological impairment.
  • Chronic pain and disability: Persistent infection or inflammation may lead to chronic pain and reduced function, particularly if untreated or in advanced cases.
  • Sepsis and systemic spread: The infection can disseminate, leading to septic arthritis or endocarditis, which are potentially fatal.
