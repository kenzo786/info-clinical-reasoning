Anorectal Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • An anorectal abscess is a collection of pus in the anorectal region, typically resulting from an infection in the anal glands, crypts, or surrounding tissue. It often presents as a painful swelling in the perianal or ischiorectal area and can lead to systemic symptoms such as fever. If untreated, it may progress to a fistula or spread to adjacent structures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Anorectal abscesses are relatively uncommon, with a reported incidence of about 1 in 10,000 per year.
  • UK hospital prevalence: In UK hospital admissions, anorectal abscesses account for a significant number of surgical emergencies, with approximately 15-20 cases per 100,000 individuals annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar incidence worldwide, with slight variations depending on hygiene and access to healthcare.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of anorectal abscess begins with obstruction of the anal crypt glands. This blockage leads to bacterial proliferation, most commonly involving Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, and anaerobic organisms. The infection progresses as pus accumulates, causing tissue destruction and formation of an abscess cavity. If untreated, the infection can spread to the surrounding perianal spaces or result in the formation of a fistula.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically begin over a few days to a week, with increasing pain and swelling in the anorectal region.
  • Chronic phase: Without treatment, some abscesses may persist or lead to complications such as fistula formation, which can result in chronic, recurrent infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Perianal pain - Localised, throbbing pain that worsens with sitting or defecation, due to pressure and inflammation from the abscess.
  • Swelling - A palpable lump in the perianal or rectal area as a result of the abscess formation.
  • Fever - Systemic signs of infection, often due to bacteraemia or the bodyâ€™s response to localised infection.
  • Pus drainage - In cases where the abscess spontaneously ruptures, patients may notice purulent discharge from the anal region.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Perianal swelling - Visible or palpable swelling in the perianal or ischiorectal area due to pus accumulation.
  • Erythema - Redness overlying the abscess, indicating inflammation and infection of the skin.
  • Tenderness - Marked tenderness on palpation of the affected area due to the pressure from the abscess.
  • Fluctuance - A soft, compressible area on palpation, characteristic of a fluid-filled abscess cavity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Severe systemic symptoms (fever, chills) - May indicate sepsis, a life-threatening complication of the infection spreading systemically.
  • Spreading erythema or induration - Suggests cellulitis or progression of the infection into deeper tissues.
  • Necrotising fasciitis - Rapidly progressing pain, necrosis, and skin changes could indicate this severe, life-threatening infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Deep-seated abscesses (e.g., supralevator abscesses) may present with vague lower abdominal pain or discomfort rather than typical perianal symptoms.
  • Immunocompromised patients (e.g., diabetics, HIV) may present with less pronounced symptoms due to a blunted immune response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Often sufficient for diagnosis. Inspection and digital rectal exam may reveal tenderness, swelling, and fluctuance.
  • Ultrasound - Performed if the abscess is deep or not easily palpable; can confirm fluid collection.
  • MRI or CT scan - Used for complex, recurrent, or deep abscesses to assess the extent and rule out fistula formation or deeper spread.
  • Blood tests - Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, WBC) indicate infection but are non-specific.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old man presents with a 3-day history of severe perianal pain, swelling, and fever. Examination reveals erythema, swelling, and tenderness in the perianal region with fluctuance.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Pilonidal abscess - Occurs in the sacrococcygeal area, unlike anorectal abscesses, which are perianal.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa - Often presents with multiple abscesses and sinus tracts, usually in the groin, axilla, or perianal region, but with a chronic course.
  • Fistula-in-ano - Can present similarly if infected, but fistulas have an external opening and may have a history of chronic drainage.
  • Perirectal cellulitis - Diffuse infection of the perianal tissue without a defined abscess cavity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Surgical drainage - The mainstay of treatment, typically performed under local or general anaesthesia. Incision and drainage of the abscess cavity relieve pressure and remove infection.
  • Antibiotics - Generally reserved for patients with cellulitis, systemic symptoms, or immunocompromised status (e.g., co-amoxiclav or metronidazole).
  • Pain management - Adequate analgesia with NSAIDs or opioids is often necessary post-drainage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Anorectal fistula - Occurs in about 30-50% of cases, developing as a communication between the infected crypt gland and the skin surface.
  • Sepsis - A rare but serious complication, especially in immunocompromised patients or if the abscess is left untreated.
  • Recurrent abscess - Inadequate drainage or untreated fistula can lead to recurrence.
