Bleeding Disorders
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Bleeding disorders are conditions that affect the body's ability to form a proper blood clot. These can be due to abnormalities in the blood vessel walls, platelets, clotting factors, or the coagulation cascade. Common bleeding disorders include haemophilia, von Willebrand disease, and thrombocytopenia. These conditions often lead to excessive or spontaneous bleeding that is difficult to control.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • In the UK, haemophilia affects approximately 1 in 10,000 individuals, while von Willebrand disease is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, affecting around 1% of the population.
  • Globally, the prevalence of haemophilia is around 400,000 cases. Von Willebrand disease has a worldwide prevalence of approximately 0.6-1.3%.
  • Hospital presentations for bleeding disorders tend to be related to uncontrolled bleeding following surgery, trauma, or spontaneous bleeds.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Bleeding disorders result from defects in one or more components of the haemostatic system, including vascular, platelet, or coagulation pathway abnormalities. For example:
  • Haemophilia A and B: Caused by deficiencies in factor VIII and IX respectively, which disrupts the intrinsic pathway of the coagulation cascade, leading to impaired thrombin generation.
  • Von Willebrand disease: A deficiency or dysfunction of von Willebrand factor, which impairs platelet adhesion and interaction with factor VIII, affecting both primary haemostasis and clotting factor stability.
  • Thrombocytopenia: Results from reduced platelet production or increased destruction, leading to inadequate platelet plug formation during vascular injury.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Bleeding disorders may be congenital (e.g., haemophilia, von Willebrand disease) or acquired (e.g., liver disease, disseminated intravascular coagulation).
  • Chronic conditions such as von Willebrand disease are lifelong but often mild and may go unnoticed until significant trauma or surgery occurs.
  • Haemophilia may present in early childhood with severe bleeding episodes, while thrombocytopenia may develop acutely in the context of drug reactions or bone marrow suppression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Easy bruising - Occurs due to fragile blood vessels and/or inadequate platelet aggregation (common in all bleeding disorders).
  • Prolonged bleeding after injury - Defective clotting mechanisms cause inability to stop bleeding efficiently (seen in haemophilia and von Willebrand disease).
  • Spontaneous joint bleeds - Common in haemophilia, as recurrent bleeding episodes in weight-bearing joints lead to haemarthrosis.
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) - Common in von Willebrand disease, due to defective primary haemostasis.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding - Can occur in severe cases due to vascular fragility and impaired clot formation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Petechiae - Small, non-blanching spots caused by capillary haemorrhage, typically seen in thrombocytopenia.
  • Ecchymoses - Large bruises due to blood extravasation into subcutaneous tissues, commonly seen in coagulation factor deficiencies.
  • Haemarthrosis - Swollen, painful joints due to repeated bleeding episodes, classic in severe haemophilia.
  • Splenomegaly - May be present in cases of chronic thrombocytopenia or conditions causing increased platelet destruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Cerebral haemorrhage - A life-threatening complication, especially in haemophilia, due to spontaneous or trauma-related bleeding in the brain.
  • Severe gastrointestinal bleeding - May indicate severe underlying coagulopathy or vascular abnormality.
  • Severe anaemia - Can result from chronic blood loss, suggesting ongoing internal bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Mild von Willebrand disease - May present only during times of significant haemostatic challenge (e.g., surgery or trauma).
  • Acquired thrombocytopenia - Could manifest subtly with mild petechiae or prolonged bleeding from minor cuts.
  • Female patients - May first present with menorrhagia or postpartum haemorrhage, especially with von Willebrand disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Low platelet count in thrombocytopenia; normal in haemophilia and von Willebrand disease.
  • Coagulation Screen (PT, aPTT) - Prolonged activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) in haemophilia; normal PT. Von Willebrand disease may also show a mildly prolonged aPTT.
  • Factor assays - Reduced factor VIII or IX levels in haemophilia. Von Willebrand factor antigen/activity levels reduced in von Willebrand disease.
  • Bleeding time - Prolonged in von Willebrand disease due to impaired platelet adhesion.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old boy with severe haemophilia A presents with spontaneous joint bleeds and prolonged bleeding following minor injuries. His mother reports several episodes of ecchymoses and swelling around the knees.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) - Features widespread clotting and bleeding, often in critically ill patients, with prolonged PT and aPTT, and low fibrinogen.
  • Liver Disease - Presents with both coagulopathy and thrombocytopenia due to impaired clotting factor synthesis and splenic sequestration of platelets.
  • Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) - Isolated low platelet count with normal coagulation tests, presents with petechiae and ecchymoses.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Haemophilia - Regular prophylactic factor VIII or IX replacement therapy. Desmopressin for minor bleeds in mild cases.
  • Von Willebrand disease - Desmopressin to release stored von Willebrand factor, and factor concentrate for severe cases.
  • Thrombocytopenia - Treatment depends on cause; platelet transfusions for severe bleeding or very low counts.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bleeding Disorders"][/sc]
  • Joint damage (haemophilia) - Recurrent haemarthroses can lead to chronic joint damage and arthropathy.
  • Life-threatening haemorrhage - Cerebral or gastrointestinal bleeding can be fatal in untreated cases.
  • Severe anaemia - Resulting from chronic or acute blood loss in cases with significant bleeding tendency.
