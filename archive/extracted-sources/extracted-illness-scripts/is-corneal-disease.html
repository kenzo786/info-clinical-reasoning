Corneal Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Corneal disease encompasses a range of conditions that affect the cornea, the transparent, dome-shaped surface that covers the front of the eye. These conditions can include infections (keratitis), dystrophies, trauma, and degenerative changes, often leading to visual impairment or blindness if untreated.

  • Worldwide, corneal opacities are a leading cause of blindness, affecting approximately 4â€“5 million people.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • The cornea is avascular and relies on the tear film and aqueous humour for nutrients and immune defence.
  • Infections disrupt the epithelial barrier, allowing pathogens to penetrate and trigger an inflammatory response, often mediated by cytokines and neutrophils.
  • Degenerative or dystrophic changes impair stromal organisation, leading to loss of transparency and biomechanical strength.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Corneal infections such as bacterial keratitis may develop over 24â€“48 hours with rapid progression.
  • Chronic phase: Degenerative conditions like keratoconus evolve over years, causing gradual visual decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Blurred vision: Caused by disruption of corneal transparency or surface irregularities.
  • Pain: Results from corneal nerve irritation, common in infections or trauma.
  • Photophobia: Secondary to corneal inflammation affecting light-sensitive nerve endings.
  • Redness: Reflects underlying inflammation and vasodilation of conjunctival vessels.
  • Tearing: Increased tear production due to irritation of the ocular surface.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Corneal opacity: Indicative of stromal scarring or active infection.
  • Hypopyon: Presence of inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber, seen in severe infections.
  • Epithelial defect: Fluorescein staining reveals breaches in the corneal epithelium.
  • Corneal thinning: Seen in degenerative conditions such as keratoconus.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and rapid vision loss: May indicate acute infectious keratitis or perforation.
  • Hypopyon: Suggestive of a severe infection requiring urgent intervention.
  • Non-healing epithelial defect: Could signify neurotrophic keratopathy or underlying systemic disease such as diabetes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Peripheral ulcerative keratitis: Often associated with systemic autoimmune diseases like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Recurrent erosion syndrome: Presents as transient episodes of pain and blurring due to unstable corneal epithelium.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Slit-lamp examination: Reveals corneal defects, opacities, and inflammation.
  • Fluorescein staining: Highlights epithelial defects under blue light.
  • Corneal cultures: Identifies pathogens in infectious cases.
  • Pachymetry: Measures corneal thickness, useful in keratoconus.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 2-day history of severe pain, photophobia, and blurred vision in the left eye following contact lens use. Examination reveals a central corneal ulcer with an epithelial defect and hypopyon.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis: Lacks corneal involvement and significant pain.
  • Uveitis: Typically associated with photophobia and anterior chamber inflammation but no corneal opacity.
  • Dry eye disease: Symptoms are chronic and milder, with no fluorescein staining abnormalities.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Bacterial keratitis: Intensive topical antibiotics (e.g., moxifloxacin) guided by culture sensitivity.
  • Corneal dystrophies: Lubricants for mild cases, corneal transplantation for advanced disease.
  • Keratoconus: Collagen cross-linking for progressive disease, and contact lenses or surgery for visual correction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring: Leads to permanent vision loss and may require transplantation.
  • Corneal perforation: Results from untreated infectious keratitis or severe thinning, potentially causing endophthalmitis.
