Pelvic Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • A pelvic abscess is a localised collection of pus within the pelvic cavity, typically resulting from infection in adjacent structures such as the reproductive organs, intestines, or urinary tract. It is commonly associated with conditions such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), postoperative infections, or bowel perforations. Abscesses may develop in spaces like the rectouterine pouch (Pouch of Douglas), rectovesical pouch, or adnexal regions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Pelvic abscesses are relatively uncommon but occur more frequently in women with pelvic inflammatory disease, especially in those requiring hospitalisation or surgical intervention.
  • In the UK, pelvic abscesses are most commonly seen in gynaecological practice and following abdominal surgeries like appendicectomies or colorectal procedures. Prevalence is difficult to estimate but ranges from 0.3% to 3% in post-surgical populations.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence is higher in areas with increased rates of PID and limited access to medical care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Pelvic abscess formation typically occurs when bacteria enter and infect pelvic tissues, often due to breaches in anatomical barriers caused by surgery, perforation of the bowel, or spread of infection from adjacent organs. Bacteria proliferate in poorly vascularised tissues, leading to the formation of a pus-filled cavity, surrounded by an inflammatory wall.
  • The abscess is walled off by fibrin, trapping bacteria and immune cells, which can prevent further spread but also limits antibiotic penetration.
  • Over time, the abscess enlarges, causing pressure on surrounding structures and leading to symptoms of pain, fever, and possibly sepsis if left untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (1-2 weeks): Initial infection and abscess formation, marked by pelvic pain, fever, and general malaise. In this phase, the abscess may still be evolving, and symptoms are variable.
  • Subacute Phase (2-6 weeks): The abscess enlarges, with more pronounced symptoms such as persistent pelvic pain, fevers, and possibly localised peritonitis.
  • Chronic Phase (6+ weeks): In untreated or inadequately treated cases, the abscess may become chronic, leading to fistula formation, chronic pelvic pain, or sepsis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Pain: Often dull or aching, localised to the lower abdomen, and worsens with movement or pressure. This is due to inflammation and pressure from the abscess on surrounding structures.
  • Fever: Occurs due to the body's immune response to infection, with cytokine release triggering the hypothalamus to increase body temperature.
  • Malaise and Fatigue: Generalised feeling of illness due to systemic inflammatory response and the body's energy consumption fighting the infection.
  • Vaginal Discharge: Common in pelvic abscesses related to PID, due to concurrent infection in the reproductive tract.
  • Dysuria: Painful urination occurs if the abscess irritates the bladder or urethra.
  • Constipation or Diarrhoea: Bowel function can be affected if the abscess is located near the intestines, leading to irritation or partial obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Tenderness in the Lower Abdomen: Typically localised to the site of the abscess, due to inflammation and tissue irritation.
  • Rebound Tenderness: Indicative of peritonitis, as the infection causes localised peritoneal inflammation.
  • Guarding: Involuntary muscle contraction over the site of the abscess, a sign of underlying inflammation.
  • Fever (Pyrexia): A systemic sign of infection due to the body's inflammatory response.
  • Tachycardia: Increased heart rate as the body tries to compensate for infection and systemic inflammation.
  • Decreased Bowel Sounds: If the abscess is large enough, it can impair bowel motility.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Severe, Generalised Abdominal Pain: May indicate rupture of the abscess, leading to diffuse peritonitis and a surgical emergency.
  • Hypotension and Tachycardia: Signs of septic shock, where the infection overwhelms the bodyâ€™s defences and causes cardiovascular collapse.
  • Altered Mental State: Confusion or drowsiness can be a sign of sepsis, particularly in elderly or immunocompromised patients.
  • Recurrent High Fevers: May signify an inadequately drained or enlarging abscess.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Chronic Low-Grade Fever and Fatigue: A slowly enlarging pelvic abscess may present with chronic symptoms, especially in immunocompromised individuals where acute inflammatory responses are blunted.
  • Minimal Abdominal Pain: In elderly or immunocompromised patients, pain may be absent, or minimal due to altered immune responses or decreased pain perception.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Elevated white blood cell (WBC) count suggests infection.
  • C-reactive Protein (CRP): A raised CRP indicates inflammation and is often markedly elevated in abscesses.
  • Ultrasound: Often the first imaging modality used; it can visualise a fluid-filled cavity in the pelvic region.
  • CT Scan: The most definitive imaging test for pelvic abscess, showing the size, location, and possible communication with other structures (e.g., bowel).
  • Blood Cultures: Should be performed if sepsis is suspected, identifying the causative organism for targeted antibiotic therapy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 5-day history of worsening lower abdominal pain, fever, and dysuria. She has a history of pelvic inflammatory disease treated 2 months ago and is now tachycardic, febrile, and tender on palpation of the lower abdomen, with guarding.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis: Unlike pelvic abscess, appendicitis often presents with more localised right lower quadrant pain and migratory pain from the umbilical region.
  • Diverticulitis: Can mimic pelvic abscess, but typically associated with left lower quadrant pain and altered bowel habits.
  • Ovarian Torsion: Presents with sudden, severe unilateral pelvic pain, without the systemic signs of infection seen in a pelvic abscess.
  • Endometriosis: Chronic pelvic pain worsens with menstruation, but there is no associated fever or acute inflammatory signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum antibiotics covering anaerobes, Gram-negative, and Gram-positive organisms are recommended (e.g., ceftriaxone with metronidazole), typically guided by microbiology results.
  • Drainage: Percutaneous drainage under ultrasound or CT guidance is often required to evacuate the abscess.
  • Surgical Intervention: In cases of ruptured abscess or failure of percutaneous drainage, surgical drainage or laparoscopy may be necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pelvic Abscess"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: A serious complication where infection spreads into the bloodstream, leading to systemic inflammatory response and possible multi-organ failure.
  • Rupture: Abscess rupture can cause generalised peritonitis, necessitating emergency surgery.
  • Fistula Formation: Chronic abscesses can form abnormal connections between the bowel, bladder, or uterus, causing persistent discharge and infection.
