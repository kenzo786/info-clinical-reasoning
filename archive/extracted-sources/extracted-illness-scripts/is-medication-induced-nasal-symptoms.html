Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced nasal symptoms, also known as drug-induced rhinitis, refer to nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea, or other nasal discomforts resulting from the side effects of certain medications. The core characteristics involve nasal obstruction, discharge, or irritation without an underlying infectious or allergic cause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced nasal symptoms are not widely reported, making prevalence data limited. However, it is recognised among patients using medications such as antihypertensives, NSAIDs, oral contraceptives, and decongestant nasal sprays. A significant number of cases can occur in long-term users of topical nasal decongestants (rebound rhinitis). The condition is often underdiagnosed.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Medications trigger nasal symptoms through several mechanisms, including:
    • Alpha-adrenergic agonists (e.g., nasal decongestants): Prolonged use leads to receptor downregulation and rebound vasodilation, causing nasal congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa).
    • Beta-blockers: Block adrenergic receptors in nasal mucosa, reducing vasoconstriction and increasing nasal congestion.
    • NSAIDs: Block prostaglandin synthesis, leading to dysregulation of nasal blood flow and inflammation in predisposed individuals.
    • ACE inhibitors: Increase bradykinin levels, causing inflammation and nasal hypersecretion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • The onset of medication-induced nasal symptoms can range from days to months depending on the drug. Topical decongestants may cause symptoms after prolonged use (>3-5 days), while beta-blockers or ACE inhibitors may cause gradual symptoms over weeks or months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Nasal congestion - The most common symptom due to medication-induced vasodilation and mucosal oedema.
  • Rhinorrhoea - Increased secretion may result from mucosal irritation or bradykinin accumulation (seen with ACE inhibitors).
  • Dryness or irritation - Some medications can lead to mucosal drying, causing discomfort and irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Swollen nasal turbinates - Visible on examination, representing mucosal oedema from medication effects.
  • Nasal erythema - May accompany inflammation due to vasodilation, particularly in rhinitis medicamentosa.
  • Watery nasal discharge - Clear, watery secretion is often seen, especially with ACE inhibitors.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Severe or sudden worsening of nasal congestion - Could indicate rebound rhinitis or severe drug reaction, requiring prompt discontinuation of offending medication.
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, joint pain) - May indicate an associated hypersensitivity reaction or vasculitis (e.g., drug-induced lupus from hydralazine).
  • Epistaxis (nosebleeds) - Recurrent or severe nosebleeds could suggest mucosal damage or atrophy, particularly from long-term decongestant use.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Isolated nasal dryness - Seen with systemic anticholinergics, often without accompanying congestion or discharge, which may delay diagnosis.
  • Chronic postnasal drip - Less common but may be seen with certain antihypertensive medications, misattributed to gastro-oesophageal reflux disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Clinical history and examination - Key to diagnosis, often excluding allergic or infectious causes of rhinitis.
  • Nasal endoscopy - In cases where structural abnormalities or other causes of nasal obstruction need exclusion, endoscopy may reveal swollen turbinates without purulent discharge.
  • Allergy testing - Skin prick or RAST tests to rule out allergic rhinitis if history is unclear.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with persistent nasal congestion and intermittent rhinorrhoea for 3 months. She reports long-term use of nasal decongestants, with worsening symptoms despite continued use.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis - Characterised by sneezing, itching, and watery eyes, with positive allergy tests, unlike drug-induced symptoms.
  • Infectious rhinitis - Accompanied by fever, purulent discharge, and systemic symptoms, absent in drug-induced cases.
  • Vasomotor rhinitis - Similar presentation but not linked to medication use, often triggered by irritants like strong odours or temperature changes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation of offending medication - The main treatment for drug-induced nasal symptoms, with gradual weaning off in the case of nasal decongestants.
  • Intranasal corticosteroids - Can help manage symptoms during the withdrawal phase of nasal decongestants or for those with persistent inflammation.
  • Saline nasal sprays - Used to reduce dryness and irritation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Nasal Symptoms"][/sc]
  • Rhinitis medicamentosa - A complication of long-term decongestant use, leading to rebound nasal congestion.
  • Nasal mucosal atrophy - Seen with prolonged decongestant use, leading to chronic dryness and crusting.
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis - May develop in cases where symptoms persist, leading to secondary bacterial infections.
