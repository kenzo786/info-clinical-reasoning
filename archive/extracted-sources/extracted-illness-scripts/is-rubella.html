Rubella (German Measles)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting children and young adults. It is characterised by a mild maculopapular rash, lymphadenopathy, and sometimes fever. It is generally self-limiting but poses a significant risk in pregnant women due to the risk of congenital rubella syndrome (CRS) in the fetus.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • UK Prevalence: Very low in the general population due to routine MMR vaccination.
  • Hospital Prevalence: Rarely seen in hospitals in the UK outside of imported cases.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Still prevalent in countries with low vaccination coverage.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • The rubella virus, a single-stranded RNA virus, enters the body via respiratory droplets. It infects the upper respiratory tract, then spreads through the bloodstream (viraemia) to other organs.
  • Replication in lymphoid tissue leads to immune response activation, causing lymphadenopathy. Viral replication in endothelial cells contributes to the characteristic rash.
  • During pregnancy, rubella virus can cross the placenta, potentially causing severe developmental abnormalities in the fetus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 2-3 weeks post-exposure.
  • Prodromal Phase: Mild fever, malaise, and upper respiratory symptoms lasting 1-5 days.
  • Rash Phase: Pink maculopapular rash appears, starting on the face and spreading, lasting 3-5 days.
  • Recovery Phase: Symptoms generally resolve within 1 week; immunity is typically lifelong.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Rash - Pink maculopapular rash, often starting on the face, then spreading to the trunk and limbs; caused by viral replication and immune response.
  • Fever - Usually mild; linked to the body's systemic inflammatory response to the virus.
  • Headache - Common; likely related to systemic viral illness effects.
  • Malaise - A general feeling of discomfort due to systemic viral infection.
  • Arthralgia - More common in adults, potentially due to immune complexes affecting the joints.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Maculopapular Rash - Pink-red rash, typically starting on the face and spreading; due to viral replication and immune response.
  • Post-auricular and Suboccipital Lymphadenopathy - Swollen lymph nodes, particularly behind the ears and at the base of the skull, due to immune activation.
  • Conjunctivitis - Mild redness and irritation of the eyes, reflecting viral replication in the conjunctival tissue.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy Exposure - Critical in pregnant women due to the risk of congenital rubella syndrome (CRS), which can lead to severe congenital malformations.
  • Persistent Fever beyond 1 week - Uncommon for rubella; may indicate secondary infection or other complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Presentation - Up to 50% of rubella cases are asymptomatic, especially in younger children, which can contribute to unintentional spread.
  • Adults - May experience more prominent arthralgia or arthritis compared to children, reflecting immune response variations with age.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Rubella IgM and IgG Serology - Positive IgM indicates recent infection; IgG indicates past infection or immunity.
  • RT-PCR (nasopharyngeal swab or blood) - Detects viral RNA, confirming acute infection; particularly useful in pregnant women.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old child presents with a mild fever, malaise, and a pink rash that started on the face and spread to the trunk, along with post-auricular lymphadenopathy, suggestive of rubella.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Measles - Presents with higher fever, conjunctivitis, and a cough, with a rash starting at the hairline.
  • Scarlet Fever - Rash typically involves a "sandpaper" texture, and often includes a sore throat.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Supportive care, including antipyretics for fever and hydration.
  • Public Health Notification - Report to local health authorities to prevent further spread.
  • Pregnant Women Exposure - Prompt referral for specialist management to assess for potential congenital infection.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rubella (German Measles)"][/sc]
  • Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS) - Occurs when rubella infects the fetus, causing hearing impairment, heart defects, cataracts, and other developmental issues.
  • Encephalitis - Rare but severe complication due to direct viral involvement of the CNS.
  • Arthritis - Can occur in adults, especially women, as a post-infectious immune response.
