Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Ventricular fibrillation (VF) is a life-threatening cardiac arrhythmia characterised by rapid, erratic electrical impulses in the ventricles. This results in the heart being unable to pump blood effectively, leading to sudden cardiac arrest if not treated immediately. It is a medical emergency requiring defibrillation and advanced life support.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: VF is rare in primary care settings but accounts for a significant proportion of sudden cardiac deaths.
  • UK hospital prevalence: VF occurs in approximately 6-10% of in-hospital cardiac arrests.
  • Worldwide prevalence: VF is responsible for 25-30% of all sudden cardiac arrests globally, with incidence varying based on factors like age, comorbidities, and access to emergency care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • VF results from disorganised electrical activity in the ventricles, often triggered by myocardial ischaemia, electrolyte imbalances, or underlying heart disease. Normally, the ventricles contract in a coordinated manner to pump blood. In VF, multiple electrical circuits form within the ventricular myocardium, causing chaotic electrical impulses that prevent coordinated contraction. The ventricles "quiver" rather than pump blood, leading to a sudden drop in cardiac output and perfusion, ultimately causing cardiovascular collapse and death without immediate intervention.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: VF can occur abruptly, often in patients with a history of ischaemic heart disease, myocardial infarction, or structural heart abnormalities.
  • Progression: VF rapidly leads to loss of consciousness and cardiovascular collapse within seconds to minutes, with brain death occurring in 4-6 minutes without intervention.
  • Recovery phase: If defibrillation and resuscitation are successful, normal sinus rhythm may be restored, but patients remain at high risk for recurrence and complications like anoxic brain injury.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Sudden collapse - Occurs due to the immediate loss of cardiac output, resulting in cerebral hypoperfusion and loss of consciousness.
  • Loss of consciousness - Caused by cerebral hypoxia from inadequate blood flow during VF.
  • No pulse - As the heart is unable to pump blood effectively, no pulse can be detected.
  • Preceding chest pain or palpitations - These may occur if VF is triggered by myocardial ischaemia or arrhythmias.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Unresponsiveness - Due to loss of cerebral perfusion.
  • No detectable pulse - Reflects the absence of effective cardiac contraction during VF.
  • Agonal breathing or absent respiration - Brainstem-driven reflex breathing patterns may occur, but normal respiration is absent due to lack of blood flow.
  • Absent heart sounds - There is no coordinated myocardial contraction, so no heart sounds are audible.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Loss of consciousness - Immediate collapse without warning is a red flag indicating cardiac arrest, necessitating immediate intervention.
  • No pulse - Signifies no effective blood circulation, an emergency that indicates ongoing cardiac arrest.
  • Absent respiration - Suggests severe cardiovascular collapse with cessation of ventilation, requiring urgent resuscitation to prevent brain injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Intermittent syncope - In some patients, VF may manifest as sudden syncope episodes that revert spontaneously, especially if VF is brief and converts back to sinus rhythm spontaneously.
  • Brief palpitations followed by collapse - This may occur if VF is preceded by other arrhythmias like ventricular tachycardia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • ECG - Shows erratic, chaotic electrical activity without identifiable QRS complexes, confirming VF.
  • Cardiac enzymes - Raised troponin levels may indicate myocardial ischaemia or infarction as the underlying cause of VF.
  • Electrolyte panel - Hypokalaemia, hyperkalaemia, or hypomagnesaemia can predispose to VF, so these values should be assessed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old male with a history of coronary artery disease presents with sudden collapse, unresponsiveness, and no pulse. Immediate initiation of cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is required, and ECG confirms ventricular fibrillation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Ventricular tachycardia - May present with a similar collapse, but the ECG shows wide QRS complexes with an organised rhythm rather than chaotic activity.
  • Pulseless electrical activity (PEA) - There is electrical activity on ECG, but no pulse. PEA is different from VF in that there is organised but ineffective electrical activity.
  • Asystole - Complete absence of electrical activity (flatline) on ECG, whereas VF shows chaotic electrical activity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Defibrillation - Immediate defibrillation is the primary treatment, delivering electrical shocks to restore normal cardiac rhythm.
  • Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) - Continuous chest compressions and ventilation are essential until defibrillation can be performed.
  • Advanced Life Support (ALS) - Administration of adrenaline (1 mg every 3-5 minutes) and amiodarone (300 mg bolus after the third shock) as per Resuscitation Council UK guidelines.
  • Treat underlying cause - Correction of reversible causes such as electrolyte imbalances or myocardial infarction should be performed concurrently.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Anoxic brain injury - Prolonged lack of cerebral perfusion can result in permanent brain damage, especially if defibrillation is delayed.
  • Recurrence of VF - Without addressing the underlying cause, patients remain at high risk for recurrent VF.
  • Heart failure - Extensive myocardial damage from recurrent VF can lead to long-term ventricular dysfunction.
