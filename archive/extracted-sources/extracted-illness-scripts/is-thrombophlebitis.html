Thrombophlebitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Thrombophlebitis is the inflammation of a vein, typically occurring with the presence of a thrombus (blood clot) within the affected vein. It most commonly affects superficial veins (superficial thrombophlebitis), but it can also involve deeper veins (deep vein thrombosis or DVT).

  • Worldwide Prevalence: Rates vary but are higher in settings with prevalent risk factors such as immobility or vascular disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Initiation: Endothelial injury, venous stasis, or hypercoagulability (Virchowâ€™s triad) predisposes to thrombus formation.
  • Inflammation: The thrombus triggers an inflammatory response in the venous wall, resulting in pain, swelling, and erythema.
  • Progression: Superficial thrombophlebitis may progress to deeper veins, causing DVT or pulmonary embolism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Pain, redness, and swelling develop over hours to days.
  • Subacute Phase: Symptoms stabilise or improve within 1â€“2 weeks with appropriate treatment.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent venous insufficiency or recurrent episodes may occur in untreated cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Pain in the affected vein: Localised pain occurs due to inflammation and thrombosis.
  • Swelling: Venous obstruction leads to localised oedema.
  • Erythema and warmth: Inflammation of the vein causes localised redness and warmth.
  • Palpable cord: A hardened vein may be felt due to thrombus formation.
  • Fever (occasionally): Reflects systemic inflammation or infection in septic thrombophlebitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Local tenderness: Palpation of the affected area elicits pain due to vein inflammation.
  • Palpable cord: A thrombosed vein feels like a firm cord beneath the skin.
  • Skin changes: Erythema and warmth over the vein due to inflammation.
  • Oedema: Swelling proximal to the thrombosed vein from impaired venous return.
  • Varicosities: May be present as a predisposing factor in chronic cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of swelling and pain: Suggests progression to DVT.
  • Dyspnoea or chest pain: May indicate pulmonary embolism, a life-threatening complication.
  • Systemic signs of infection: High fever or purulent discharge may signify septic thrombophlebitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Isolated pain without visible erythema: Occurs in less visible veins or in early stages.
  • Asymptomatic progression to DVT: In some cases, the initial superficial thrombophlebitis resolves, but thrombus extends to deeper veins.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Doppler ultrasound: Confirms thrombus and evaluates for progression to DVT.
  • D-dimer: Elevated in thrombotic conditions but non-specific. Useful in ruling out DVT in low-risk cases.
  • Blood cultures: Indicated in suspected septic thrombophlebitis to identify causative pathogens.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with acute onset of pain, redness, and swelling along a superficial vein of the left calf for 3 days. She has a history of varicose veins.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis: Diffuse skin erythema without a palpable cord.
  • DVT: Involves deeper veins, often with systemic symptoms like leg oedema.
  • Lymphangitis: Erythematous streaking, often associated with systemic infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • Conservative Management: Warm compresses, leg elevation, and NSAIDs for symptomatic relief.
  • Anticoagulation: Low-molecular-weight heparin (e.g., enoxaparin) for high-risk cases to prevent DVT.
  • Treatment of septic thrombophlebitis: Intravenous antibiotics and possible surgical intervention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thrombophlebitis"][/sc]
  • DVT: Progression of thrombus to deep veins, increasing risk of pulmonary embolism.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Life-threatening complication from thrombus dislodgement.
  • Chronic venous insufficiency: Results from recurrent thrombosis and damage to venous valves.
