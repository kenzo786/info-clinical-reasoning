Sinusitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Sinusitis, also known as rhinosinusitis, is inflammation of the mucosal lining of the paranasal sinuses. It is commonly caused by viral infections, bacterial infections, or allergic reactions, leading to blockage of sinus drainage and subsequent infection or irritation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, sinusitis affects around 1 in 7 adults annually. It is one of the most common reasons for GP consultations, representing 2% of all GP visits. Hospital prevalence is lower, but complications like orbital or intracranial spread require secondary care.
  • Worldwide, sinusitis prevalence is approximately 10-15% of the population, with higher rates in urban environments due to pollution and allergen exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Sinusitis begins with inflammation of the nasal mucosa (often viral or allergic), which leads to obstruction of the sinus ostia. This impairs mucus clearance, creating an ideal environment for bacterial overgrowth. Inflammation also triggers vasodilation, increasing vascular permeability and fluid accumulation, further worsening sinus congestion.
  • Acute sinusitis typically lasts less than four weeks and is often viral. Chronic sinusitis (>12 weeks) is associated with persistent inflammation, sometimes involving bacterial biofilms or fungal infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Acute sinusitis: Typically lasts less than 4 weeks, with rapid onset of symptoms following a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).
  • Subacute sinusitis: Symptoms persist between 4-12 weeks.
  • Chronic sinusitis: Symptoms last for more than 12 weeks, often with less severe intensity but persistent discomfort.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Facial pain/pressure - This is the most common symptom, caused by increased pressure within obstructed sinuses. It often localises to the affected sinus, such as the maxillary or frontal regions.
  • Nasal congestion - Inflammation causes swelling of the nasal mucosa, obstructing airflow and drainage of sinuses.
  • Purulent nasal discharge - Mucus thickens due to infection, turning yellow or green, indicating an immune response with neutrophil infiltration.
  • Cough - Mucus drainage into the throat, especially when lying down, irritates the upper airway, triggering a cough reflex.
  • Headache - Increased sinus pressure can refer pain to the forehead, temples, or teeth.
  • Reduced sense of smell (hyposmia) - Inflammation impairs olfactory nerve function in the nasal mucosa.
  • Fever - Often present in bacterial sinusitis due to systemic inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over the sinuses - Direct palpation over the affected sinuses (especially maxillary and frontal) may elicit pain, reflecting underlying inflammation.
  • Nasal mucosal swelling - Examination with an otoscope may show erythematous, swollen nasal turbinates, blocking airflow.
  • Purulent nasal secretions - Thick, discoloured mucus can often be visualised at the nasal meatus.
  • Postnasal drip - Secretions flowing down the posterior pharynx may be visible upon oral examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Periorbital swelling or erythema - This may indicate orbital cellulitis, a serious complication due to the spread of infection to the orbit.
  • Severe headache - May suggest the spread of infection to the central nervous system (CNS), such as meningitis or intracranial abscess.
  • Visual disturbances - Diplopia or vision loss could indicate orbital or cavernous sinus involvement.
  • High fever and confusion - Systemic signs of severe infection or potential sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic sinusitis without acute symptoms - Patients may only experience mild nasal congestion or anosmia, lacking the typical pain or purulent discharge seen in acute cases.
  • Sinusitis in immunocompromised patients - These patients may have more subtle presentations with fewer systemic signs despite serious infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Sinusitis is typically diagnosed based on history and physical examination. Imaging is reserved for complications or chronic cases.
  • Nasal endoscopy - May be used to directly visualise sinus drainage pathways, particularly in chronic cases.
  • CT scan of sinuses - Indicated in chronic or complicated cases to assess the extent of sinus involvement or detect complications such as abscess formation.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated CRP may suggest bacterial aetiology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 10-day history of facial pain, nasal congestion, and purulent nasal discharge following a viral upper respiratory infection. Examination reveals tenderness over the maxillary sinuses and thick nasal secretions.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis - Similar nasal congestion, but lacks purulent discharge and facial pain. Associated with itching and sneezing.
  • Viral URTI - Similar onset with nasal congestion, but symptoms resolve within a week without facial pain or prolonged discharge.
  • Dental abscess - Maxillary sinusitis and dental abscess can mimic each other. Dental examination reveals a clear source of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Acute viral sinusitis - Supportive treatment with nasal decongestants, saline nasal irrigation, and analgesics.
  • Bacterial sinusitis - Antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin-clavulanate) are recommended for prolonged or severe cases. Intranasal corticosteroids may be used for symptom relief.
  • Chronic sinusitis - Long-term intranasal corticosteroids, nasal irrigation, and sometimes surgical intervention (functional endoscopic sinus surgery) for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital cellulitis - Infection spreads to the orbit, potentially leading to vision loss.
  • Meningitis - Direct extension into the cranial cavity, causing life-threatening infection.
  • Abscess formation - Either intracranial or within the sinuses themselves, requiring surgical drainage.
  • Chronic sinusitis - Prolonged inflammation can lead to irreversible damage to the sinus lining, requiring surgical management.
