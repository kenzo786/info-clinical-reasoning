Peripheral Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy is a disorder that affects the peripheral nerves, resulting in sensory, motor, or autonomic dysfunction. It can be caused by various aetiologies including diabetes, autoimmune diseases, infections, toxins, or idiopathic factors. Characterised by damage to the peripheral nervous system, it may present as polyneuropathy (widespread nerve damage) or mononeuropathy (damage to a single nerve).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, about 8% of the general population has peripheral neuropathy, with diabetic neuropathy being the most common subtype. In UK hospitals, it accounts for up to 3-4% of admissions. Worldwide, the prevalence varies widely based on aetiology, but it's estimated to affect around 2.4% of the population, rising significantly with age and diabetes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • The underlying mechanism involves damage to the peripheral nerves, either through direct axonal injury or demyelination. This can be caused by metabolic dysfunction (e.g., in diabetes), toxins (e.g., alcohol or chemotherapy), immune-mediated attacks (e.g., Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome), or inherited disorders (e.g., Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease). Axonal damage impairs the transmission of electrical signals, while demyelination slows conduction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy can develop acutely (over days to weeks) in conditions such as Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome, subacutely (weeks to months) in autoimmune or toxin-induced causes, or chronically (months to years) in conditions like diabetes or hereditary neuropathies. Chronic cases may present insidiously, making early detection difficult.
