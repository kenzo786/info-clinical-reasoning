Thyroid Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Thyroid cancer is a malignant tumour that arises from the thyroid gland, typically classified into four main histological types: papillary, follicular, medullary, and anaplastic. Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) is the most common, accounting for about 80% of cases. The disease can present with a solitary nodule or multiple nodules in the thyroid, and it often grows slowly. Some subtypes, particularly anaplastic thyroid cancer, are more aggressive and associated with poor prognosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Thyroid cancer is rare, with an annual incidence of around 3.8 cases per 100,000 population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Thyroid cancer represents about 1% of all cancers diagnosed in UK hospitals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: The global incidence is increasing, partly due to increased detection, with higher rates in women. It constitutes about 3% of all cancers worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Thyroid cancer originates from different types of thyroid cells. Papillary and follicular carcinomas arise from thyroid follicular cells, while medullary carcinoma originates from parafollicular C-cells. The pathogenesis typically involves mutations in key oncogenes or tumour suppressor genes (e.g., BRAF mutations in papillary carcinoma and RET mutations in medullary carcinoma). These genetic mutations lead to uncontrolled cellular proliferation, local invasion, and sometimes distant metastases. The more aggressive types, like anaplastic carcinoma, often exhibit rapid progression and a high degree of vascular and lymphatic invasion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Often asymptomatic or presents as a slow-growing neck lump. Papillary thyroid cancer may remain indolent for years.
  • Progression: Over months to years, the lump may enlarge, causing local compressive symptoms or lymph node involvement.
  • Advanced stage: Involves regional lymph nodes or distant metastasis, particularly in the lungs or bones. Anaplastic thyroid cancer, by contrast, can progress within weeks to a rapidly fatal course.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Neck lump - Often the only symptom, it results from tumour growth in the thyroid, especially in papillary and follicular carcinoma.
  • Hoarseness - Caused by tumour invasion or compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) - Occurs when the tumour compresses the oesophagus.
  • Difficulty breathing (dyspnoea) - May occur with large tumours that press on the trachea, leading to airway obstruction.
  • Pain - Localised pain in the neck or throat can develop as the tumour enlarges or invades surrounding structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Palpable thyroid nodule - A firm, irregular mass in the thyroid region is often the initial physical finding.
  • Enlarged cervical lymph nodes - Lymphatic spread of the tumour often results in palpable cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Stridor - This occurs due to tracheal compression from the tumour, leading to respiratory distress.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Rapidly enlarging neck mass - Indicates aggressive tumour behaviour, especially in anaplastic thyroid cancer, and suggests potential airway compromise.
  • Hoarseness - Suggests vocal cord paralysis due to recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement, indicating local invasion.
  • Stridor - A sign of significant tracheal compression, which may lead to respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic thyroid nodules detected incidentally during imaging for unrelated issues are a common atypical presentation. Pathologically, this occurs because early thyroid cancers often do not cause compression or invasion of surrounding structures.
  • Non-specific symptoms such as fatigue or weight loss may be present in more advanced stages but are often overlooked or attributed to other conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound of the neck - Detects thyroid nodules, their size, vascularity, and suspicious features like irregular margins or microcalcifications. Ultrasound is essential for guiding fine-needle aspiration (FNA).
  • Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) biopsy - Cytological examination of the aspirated material is the definitive diagnostic test. The presence of malignant cells confirms the diagnosis.
  • Thyroid function tests - Typically normal in thyroid cancer, though thyroglobulin levels may be elevated in differentiated thyroid cancers (papillary and follicular).
  • CT/MRI scans - Useful for evaluating local extension and distant metastases, especially in cases of anaplastic thyroid cancer.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a painless, slowly enlarging neck mass over the past 6 months, associated with recent onset of hoarseness and difficulty swallowing. Examination reveals a firm thyroid nodule with enlarged cervical lymph nodes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Benign thyroid nodule: Typically non-tender and asymptomatic with no associated lymphadenopathy or compressive symptoms. Ultrasound may show a well-circumscribed, homogenous nodule.
  • Thyroiditis: Often presents with a painful, tender thyroid and systemic symptoms of hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism. Inflammatory changes will be seen on thyroid ultrasound.
  • Thyroid lymphoma: Presents with a rapidly enlarging neck mass, often in the context of autoimmune thyroid disease (e.g., Hashimotoâ€™s thyroiditis). Biopsy shows lymphocytic infiltration rather than carcinoma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection: Total or partial thyroidectomy is the primary treatment for most thyroid cancers, followed by neck dissection if there is lymph node involvement.
  • Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy: Used postoperatively in patients with differentiated thyroid cancers (papillary or follicular) to ablate residual thyroid tissue and treat metastases.
  • Thyroid hormone suppression therapy: Levothyroxine is given to suppress TSH, which may stimulate cancer cell growth in differentiated thyroid cancers.
  • External beam radiotherapy: Typically reserved for anaplastic thyroid cancer or as adjunctive therapy in cases with significant local invasion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thyroid Cancer"][/sc]
  • Local invasion - In advanced cases, thyroid cancer may invade nearby structures like the trachea, oesophagus, or recurrent laryngeal nerve, leading to hoarseness, dysphagia, and airway obstruction.
  • Distant metastasis - The most common sites are the lungs and bones, leading to respiratory symptoms or pathological fractures. Metastasis is more common in follicular and anaplastic types.
  • Post-surgical complications - These may include hypocalcaemia due to parathyroid gland injury or damage to the recurrent laryngeal nerve, causing persistent hoarseness.
