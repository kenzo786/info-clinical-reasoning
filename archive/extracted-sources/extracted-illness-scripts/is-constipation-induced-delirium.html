Constipation-Induced Delirium
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Constipation-induced delirium refers to an acute confusional state triggered by severe constipation. It occurs when the accumulation of faecal matter in the bowel leads to physiological disturbances, including metabolic imbalances, dehydration, and increased bowel pressure, which subsequently disrupt cognitive functions. Delirium is often seen in elderly or medically vulnerable populations, particularly those with comorbidities or cognitive impairments, such as dementia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, constipation is common, affecting approximately 10-20% of adults, with higher prevalence in older adults. Constipation-induced delirium is less common but predominantly affects the elderly, especially in hospital settings.
  • In UK hospitals, delirium has a prevalence of around 20-30% in older adults, with constipation being a recognised but under-reported cause.
  • Worldwide, similar trends are seen, particularly in aged care and post-surgical settings where constipation and cognitive impairment often overlap.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Severe constipation leads to faecal impaction, distending the bowel.
  • Step 2: This distention triggers increased bowel wall pressure, which can lead to autonomic dysregulation, causing metabolic imbalances such as electrolyte disturbances (hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia).
  • Step 3: Reduced oral intake due to discomfort from constipation can cause dehydration, exacerbating the electrolyte imbalance.
  • Step 4: These imbalances disrupt cerebral neurotransmission, particularly acetylcholine and dopamine, leading to cognitive dysfunction and delirium.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Constipation presents first, often developing over several days to weeks.
  • Progressive Phase: Cognitive changes such as confusion, disorientation, and reduced attention span appear, typically within hours to days after significant faecal impaction or metabolic disruption.
  • Recovery Phase: With appropriate treatment, delirium usually resolves within days, but full recovery may take longer in those with underlying cognitive impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Constipation: Primary symptom; manifests as infrequent, hard stools and difficulty defecating. Prolonged stool retention leads to faecal impaction, which triggers systemic effects.
  • Confusion: The main feature of delirium, arising from electrolyte imbalances and impaired neurotransmission.
  • Disorientation: Patients may struggle to recognise their surroundings due to altered cerebral function.
  • Agitation: Restlessness or irritability is common in delirium, linked to neurotransmitter disruption, especially acetylcholine deficits.
  • Lethargy: Dehydration and metabolic imbalances can cause weakness and drowsiness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction: On abdominal examination, there may be a palpable mass in the lower abdomen or on rectal examination.
  • Tachycardia: Often present due to dehydration or stress caused by faecal retention.
  • Altered mental status: Fluctuations in cognitive function are hallmarks of delirium, with periods of lucidity interspersed with confusion.
  • Dry mucous membranes: Indicative of dehydration, often contributing to the metabolic disturbances causing delirium.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate bowel obstruction or perforation due to faecal impaction.
  • Sepsis-like symptoms: Fever, hypotension, and altered mental status can suggest a perforation or serious infection.
  • Severe electrolyte imbalances: Can manifest as muscle cramps, arrhythmias, or seizures, signifying life-threatening metabolic disturbances.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Silent confusion: Some patients, especially the elderly, may present primarily with cognitive decline, without overt gastrointestinal complaints.
  • Delirium without obvious constipation: In cognitively impaired individuals, signs of constipation may be missed, and delirium could be attributed to other causes, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Abdominal X-ray: Shows faecal loading in the colon, particularly in cases of faecal impaction.
  • Serum electrolytes: Hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, or hypercalcaemia can be detected, reflecting the metabolic derangements associated with constipation and dehydration.
  • Full blood count (FBC): May reveal dehydration (haemoconcentration) or infection (raised white cell count in cases of complications).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • An 82-year-old female with a history of dementia presents with worsening confusion and abdominal discomfort over three days. She has not opened her bowels for a week and appears disoriented, with dry mucous membranes and a palpable abdominal mass.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI): Both UTI and constipation-induced delirium can present with confusion, especially in older adults, but UTI often includes dysuria and fever.
  • Electrolyte imbalance from other causes: Could be secondary to diuretics or renal failure, without faecal impaction on imaging.
  • Dementia exacerbation: A chronic decline in cognitive function differs from the acute onset and fluctuating nature of delirium.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Laxatives: First-line treatment involves oral laxatives (e.g., macrogol), with suppositories or enemas for more severe cases.
  • Hydration: Correction of dehydration with oral or intravenous fluids.
  • Electrolyte correction: Supplementation or correction of imbalances based on blood test results.
  • Delirium management: Non-pharmacological interventions such as reorientation, avoiding sedative medications unless absolutely necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction: Can result from untreated faecal impaction.
  • Bowel perforation: A rare but life-threatening complication of severe faecal impaction.
  • Severe dehydration: Can lead to kidney injury and significant electrolyte disturbances.
