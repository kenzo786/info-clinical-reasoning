Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome (GBS) is an acute, immune-mediated polyneuropathy primarily affecting the peripheral nervous system, leading to rapid-onset muscle weakness and areflexia. It is often triggered by infections such as Campylobacter jejuni, viral illnesses, or vaccinations. GBS involves both motor and sensory dysfunction, with the primary feature being progressive, symmetrical, ascending paralysis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: 1-2 cases per 100,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 1,500-2,000 hospital admissions annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar globally, with a range of 0.8-1.9 cases per 100,000 annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • The pathogenesis of GBS involves an autoimmune response, typically triggered by a preceding infection. Molecular mimicry between microbial antigens (e.g., from Campylobacter or viral agents) and peripheral nerve components (such as gangliosides) leads to immune-mediated damage of the myelin sheath and, in some cases, the axon itself. This results in conduction block and progressive muscle weakness. The demyelinating form, known as acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (AIDP), is the most common variant.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Initial phase (1-3 weeks): Progressive weakness, starting in the legs and ascending. Weakness may peak within 2-4 weeks.
  • Plateau phase (up to 4 weeks): Stabilisation of weakness, with no further progression but significant functional impairment.
  • Recovery phase (weeks to months): Gradual improvement of muscle strength, typically beginning 2-4 weeks after the plateau phase.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Symmetrical, ascending weakness - Starts in the lower limbs and progresses upwards, explained by peripheral nerve demyelination disrupting motor nerve conduction.
  • Paresthesia - Tingling and numbness in the extremities, due to sensory nerve involvement.
  • Autonomic dysfunction - Fluctuations in blood pressure, arrhythmias, and bladder dysfunction, stemming from autonomic nerve involvement.
  • Facial weakness - Bilateral facial palsy occurs in about 50% of cases due to cranial nerve involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Areflexia - Loss of deep tendon reflexes in the affected limbs, due to nerve conduction impairment.
  • Symmetric muscle weakness - Generally flaccid paralysis in a distal-to-proximal pattern.
  • Respiratory weakness - Diaphragmatic paralysis may occur, leading to respiratory failure, seen in severe cases.
  • Cranial nerve involvement - Facial nerve weakness (bilateral) and dysphagia can be seen, particularly affecting the VIIth and IXth nerves.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Respiratory distress - Indicative of respiratory muscle weakness, may suggest impending respiratory failure.
  • Bulbar dysfunction - Difficulty swallowing and speaking due to cranial nerve involvement can lead to aspiration and requires urgent attention.
  • Autonomic instability - Severe fluctuations in blood pressure or arrhythmias signal autonomic nervous system involvement and may lead to life-threatening complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Miller Fisher syndrome - A variant of GBS that presents with ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, and areflexia, but without significant limb weakness.
  • Pure motor or pure sensory variants - Some patients may present with isolated motor or sensory involvement, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • CSF analysis - Elevated protein levels with a normal white cell count (albuminocytological dissociation), typically seen 1-2 weeks after symptom onset.
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) - Shows slowed conduction velocities and prolonged F-wave latencies, indicating demyelination.
  • MRI spine - May show thickened nerve roots or enhancement, though this is not always necessary unless ruling out other causes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with rapidly progressive, symmetrical, ascending muscle weakness over 5 days, areflexia, and difficulty walking, following a gastrointestinal illness 2 weeks prior. He also reports new onset facial weakness and mild dyspnoea.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (CIDP) - Similar to GBS but with a slower, chronic progression (over weeks to months).
  • Myasthenia gravis - Fluctuating muscle weakness without sensory involvement, typically worsens with activity.
  • Spinal cord compression - Presents with motor and sensory deficits but often with a sensory level and hyperreflexia below the lesion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) - Given for 5 days to neutralise harmful antibodies, according to UK guidelines.
  • Plasma exchange - Alternative to IVIG, removes circulating autoantibodies.
  • Supportive care - Including respiratory support if necessary (up to 30% may require ventilation), pain management, and physiotherapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Guillain-BarrÃ© Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Occurs in up to 30% of patients, requiring mechanical ventilation.
  • Autonomic dysfunction - Severe blood pressure fluctuations or arrhythmias may lead to sudden death.
  • Chronic weakness - Approximately 20% of patients have long-term residual weakness.
