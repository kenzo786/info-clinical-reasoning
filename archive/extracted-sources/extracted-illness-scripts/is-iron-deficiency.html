Iron Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency is a condition characterised by inadequate iron levels to meet the bodyâ€™s requirements for normal erythropoiesis, leading to impaired production of haemoglobin. It is the most common cause of anaemia worldwide and results in diminished oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • UK General Population: Iron deficiency anaemia affects around 2â€“5% of men and postmenopausal women in the UK.
  • UK Hospital Population: Iron deficiency is present in 30-40% of hospitalised patients, particularly those with chronic diseases.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, iron deficiency affects over 2 billion people, making it the most widespread nutritional disorder.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Reduced Iron Intake or Absorption: Iron deficiency often begins due to low dietary iron or impaired absorption (e.g., coeliac disease, gastrectomy).
  • Increased Demand: Growth spurts, pregnancy, and lactation increase the body's iron requirement, making deficiency more likely.
  • Increased Loss: Chronic blood loss, such as from heavy menstruation or gastrointestinal bleeding (peptic ulcers, malignancy), depletes iron stores.
  • Impaired Erythropoiesis: As iron becomes depleted, haemoglobin synthesis is reduced, leading to hypochromic, microcytic anaemia and a subsequent reduction in oxygen delivery to tissues.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Asymptomatic period where iron stores are depleted, but haemoglobin remains normal.
  • Intermediate Phase: Mild anaemia develops, with symptoms like fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance.
  • Late Phase: Severe anaemia with marked symptoms such as pallor, breathlessness, and possible cardiovascular strain (tachycardia, heart failure).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fatigue: Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues causes diminished energy production and tiredness.
  • Dyspnoea on exertion: Inadequate oxygen-carrying capacity makes physical activity more difficult.
  • Dizziness: Cerebral hypoxia can lead to feelings of light-headedness.
  • Palpitations: The heart works harder to compensate for reduced oxygenation, leading to awareness of heartbeat.
  • Cold intolerance: Impaired oxygen delivery affects thermoregulation, making patients more sensitive to cold.
  • Pica: A craving for non-nutritive substances (e.g., ice or clay) may occur, though the pathophysiology is unclear.
  • Often asymptomatic: In mild cases, iron deficiency can present without any overt symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Reduced haemoglobin gives a pale appearance to the skin and mucous membranes.
  • Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails): Long-standing iron deficiency affects nail growth and structure.
  • Tachycardia: The heart compensates for anaemia by increasing heart rate to boost oxygen delivery.
  • Angular cheilitis: Inflammation and cracking at the corners of the mouth, related to reduced epithelial regeneration.
  • Glossitis: Atrophy and inflammation of the tongue, possibly due to impaired mucosal turnover.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss: May suggest underlying malignancy, particularly in the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Persistent GI symptoms (e.g., abdominal pain, change in bowel habit): Could indicate peptic ulcer disease, coeliac disease, or malignancy.
  • Post-menopausal bleeding: Associated with gynaecological malignancies or other serious pathology.
  • Signs of heart failure (e.g., oedema, breathlessness): Severe, chronic anaemia can result in high-output cardiac failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Mild cognitive impairment: Elderly patients may present with subtle mental status changes due to chronic cerebral hypoxia.
  • Restless legs syndrome: An uncommon but recognised symptom in chronic iron deficiency, possibly due to reduced dopamine production in the brain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Low haemoglobin, microcytic (MCV <80 fL), hypochromic (MCHC <320 g/L) indices.
  • Serum Ferritin: Low (<30 ng/mL) confirms iron deficiency. Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant and can be falsely elevated in inflammation.
  • Serum Iron & Transferrin Saturation: Low serum iron with raised total iron-binding capacity (TIBC), leading to low transferrin saturation (<15%).
  • Peripheral Blood Film: Shows microcytic, hypochromic red cells and possible anisocytosis (variation in cell size).
  • Stool Occult Blood Test: To detect GI blood loss if the source of iron deficiency is unclear.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with a 6-month history of fatigue, dizziness, and cold intolerance, reports heavy menstrual bleeding. FBC shows microcytic, hypochromic anaemia. Serum ferritin is low at 12 ng/mL.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia: Distinguished by normal or raised ferritin and a family history. Blood film shows target cells.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Presents with normocytic or microcytic anaemia, but ferritin is raised or normal and transferrin saturation is low.
  • Sideroblastic anaemia: Causes microcytic anaemia but is distinguished by the presence of ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow biopsy.
  • Coeliac disease: Can cause iron deficiency due to malabsorption; check tissue transglutaminase antibodies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Oral Iron Supplementation: First-line therapy is ferrous sulphate 200 mg twice daily. Treatment should continue for 3â€“6 months after normalisation of haemoglobin to replenish iron stores.
  • IV Iron Therapy: Indicated for patients intolerant to oral iron, or in cases of severe anaemia requiring rapid replenishment.
  • Treat Underlying Cause: Management of blood loss (e.g., endoscopic treatment for GI bleeding or hormonal treatment for menorrhagia) is crucial.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Heart Failure: Chronic iron deficiency and severe anaemia can lead to high-output heart failure.
  • Immune Dysfunction: Impaired immune response, leading to increased susceptibility to infections.
  • Impaired Cognitive Development: In children, prolonged iron deficiency can cause developmental delays and behavioural disturbances.
  • Prognosis: Generally good with appropriate treatment; however, untreated severe iron deficiency can lead to significant morbidity.
