Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis is a medical condition characterised by the breakdown of skeletal muscle fibres, leading to the release of intracellular components such as myoglobin, creatine kinase (CK), and electrolytes into the bloodstream. It can result in complications such as acute kidney injury (AKI) due to myoglobinuria and electrolyte imbalances like hyperkalaemia. Statin-induced rhabdomyolysis is a rare but severe side effect of statin therapy, typically presenting with muscle pain and significant elevations in CK levels.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • The incidence of rhabdomyolysis in the UK GP population is low, estimated at 26.4 per 100,000 person-years, with statin-induced cases being rare. Hospital prevalence is higher due to the association with trauma, surgery, and severe medical conditions, estimated at 0.2% of hospital admissions. Worldwide, the condition's prevalence is difficult to quantify but varies based on risk factors such as trauma, drugs, and infections.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis occurs due to direct injury to muscle cells (myocytes) or impaired cellular metabolism, leading to the breakdown of cell membranes. This causes the leakage of muscle cell contents, including myoglobin, CK, potassium, and phosphate, into the circulation. Myoglobin can precipitate in the renal tubules, especially in the setting of dehydration or acidic urine, causing acute kidney injury. Electrolyte imbalances, particularly hyperkalaemia, may cause life-threatening arrhythmias.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis can develop acutely, with symptoms occurring within hours to days following muscle injury or exposure to a precipitating factor such as a drug. In cases like statin-induced rhabdomyolysis, symptoms may develop over weeks to months. Complications such as AKI can develop within hours to days, and untreated severe cases may progress rapidly to multi-organ failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Muscle pain (myalgia) - Often the earliest symptom, caused by muscle cell injury and inflammation.
  • Muscle weakness - Due to the destruction of muscle fibres, leading to functional impairment.
  • Dark-coloured urine - Caused by myoglobinuria, where myoglobin from damaged muscles is excreted in the urine, giving it a tea-coloured appearance.
  • Fatigue - Due to muscle damage and the systemic effects of muscle breakdown products.
  • Fever and malaise - Reflect systemic inflammation in response to muscle injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Muscle tenderness - Localised pain over the affected muscles due to inflammation.
  • Decreased muscle strength - On clinical examination, weakness may be evident due to muscle fibre damage.
  • Oliguria or anuria - Decreased urine output, a sign of acute kidney injury due to myoglobinuria.
  • Tachycardia - Can result from systemic effects such as dehydration, hyperkalaemia, or pain.
  • Hypotension - In severe cases, especially with fluid shifts or sepsis-like syndrome from muscle breakdown.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - Oliguria, anuria, or rising creatinine levels; signifies potential kidney damage from myoglobin deposition.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Muscle breakdown releases potassium, which can cause arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.
  • Metabolic acidosis - Caused by the release of intracellular contents, impairing acid-base balance, a precursor to severe systemic effects like shock.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Some patients, particularly those with mild cases or on statin therapy, may have markedly elevated CK levels without significant muscle pain or weakness.
  • Isolated dark urine - Myoglobinuria may be the only sign in some cases, with no overt muscle symptoms, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Creatine kinase (CK) - Elevated (often > 5,000 U/L) due to muscle breakdown.
  • Serum myoglobin - Elevated, though this test is not routinely used.
  • Renal function tests - Elevated creatinine may indicate AKI.
  • Urinalysis - Positive for blood without red cells, indicating myoglobinuria.
  • Electrolytes - Hyperkalaemia, hyperphosphataemia, and hypocalcaemia due to muscle breakdown.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male on statin therapy presents with 3 days of diffuse muscle pain and dark urine. Examination shows muscle tenderness with CK > 10,000 U/L and oliguria.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Myositis - Chronic muscle inflammation, typically lacks the rapid CK rise and renal complications of rhabdomyolysis.
  • Acute kidney injury (non-rhabdomyolysis) - AKI from other causes such as dehydration may lack muscle symptoms and elevated CK.
  • Compartment syndrome - Pain and swelling in a specific limb, not diffuse muscle involvement or systemic CK elevation.
  • Drug-induced myopathy - Statin-related or other medication-induced muscle symptoms without significant CK elevation or myoglobinuria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Intravenous fluids - Aggressive hydration with isotonic saline to prevent AKI and enhance myoglobin clearance.
  • Electrolyte management - Close monitoring and correction of hyperkalaemia or metabolic acidosis.
  • Discontinuation of statins - In statin-related cases, stop the offending drug to prevent further muscle damage.
  • Dialysis - May be required in cases of severe AKI or refractory hyperkalaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rhabdomyolysis (including Statin-related)"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - Occurs in up to 30% of cases, caused by myoglobinuria and renal tubular damage.
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) - Systemic inflammation and coagulation disturbances due to muscle breakdown products.
  • Cardiac arrest - Secondary to hyperkalaemia and electrolyte disturbances.
  • Multi-organ failure - In severe cases, especially with delayed or inadequate treatment.
