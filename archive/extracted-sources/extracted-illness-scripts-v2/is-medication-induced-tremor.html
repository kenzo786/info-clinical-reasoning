Medication-Induced Tremor
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced tremor is an involuntary, rhythmic muscle contraction caused by the side effect of certain medications. It typically manifests as a postural or action tremor and may involve the hands, arms, or other parts of the body. The condition is reversible upon withdrawal of the offending drug.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Exact prevalence data is lacking, but it is estimated to affect up to 5% of patients on certain medications (e.g., beta-agonists, antidepressants).
  • UK hospital prevalence: Tremor due to medications is commonly reported in hospitalised patients, particularly in those receiving neuroleptic, antiepileptic, or bronchodilator therapy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by region and prescribing practices, but medication-induced tremor is seen globally, especially in populations using drugs such as lithium or valproate.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced tremor results from various mechanisms depending on the offending drug:
    • Beta-agonists: Increased peripheral adrenergic activity stimulates beta-receptors, enhancing muscle activity and causing tremor.
    • Antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs, TCAs): Serotonergic overstimulation or dopaminergic modulation may contribute to tremor.
    • Antipsychotics: Blockade of dopamine D2 receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway can induce extrapyramidal symptoms, including tremor.
    • Valproate or lithium: Both drugs can affect ion channels, neurotransmitter release, and central nervous system signalling, leading to tremor.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Onset: Medication-induced tremor typically begins within days to weeks of starting the offending drug, though some agents (e.g., lithium) may cause a delayed onset after months.
  • Progression: Tremor may progressively worsen with continued use of the drug and often becomes more prominent with higher doses.
  • Resolution: Once the causative medication is discontinued, tremor generally resolves within days to weeks, though some residual symptoms may persist for months in cases involving chronic exposure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Postural tremor - Tremor most often occurs when the affected body part is held against gravity, due to enhanced muscle activation from adrenergic or dopaminergic imbalance.
  • Action tremor - Tremor during voluntary movement is common, likely due to increased motor neuron excitability induced by the drug.
  • Anxiety - Some medications (e.g., beta-agonists, SSRIs) may increase sympathetic nervous system activity, leading to both tremor and anxiety.
  • Restlessness - Excessive dopaminergic or adrenergic stimulation may cause a subjective feeling of restlessness, which is often seen alongside tremor.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Visible tremor in the hands - Commonly seen when the patient holds their arms outstretched or performs tasks requiring precision. Enhanced peripheral adrenergic activity is the likely cause.
  • Fine, rapid oscillations - Tremor is typically high-frequency and low-amplitude, associated with the nature of drug-induced muscle activation.
  • Exacerbation by stress or fatigue - Tremor often worsens in stressful situations or when the patient is fatigued, due to increased sympathetic tone.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe tremor - May suggest a serious neurological or metabolic cause, such as neuroleptic malignant syndrome or serotonin syndrome, which can be life-threatening.
  • Altered mental status or confusion - These symptoms alongside tremor may indicate drug toxicity (e.g., lithium toxicity), requiring immediate intervention.
  • Muscle rigidity or bradykinesia - These may suggest an extrapyramidal syndrome or parkinsonism induced by antipsychotics, which can progress to more severe motor impairment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Intermittent tremor - Tremor that appears sporadically may be misattributed to anxiety or physiological tremor, particularly in patients taking beta-agonists or SSRIs intermittently.
  • Tremor isolated to one limb - Unilateral tremor is unusual but can occur in medication-induced tremor, potentially mimicking essential tremor or stroke.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Clinical history and medication review - Reviewing the patient’s drug history is key. Medication-induced tremor is diagnosed by correlating tremor onset with medication initiation.
  • Thyroid function tests - Hyperthyroidism can cause tremor and may be a differential; thus, thyroid function testing helps rule out this cause.
  • Serum lithium levels - In patients on lithium, elevated levels (typically >1.2 mmol/L) may cause tremor and suggest toxicity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a fine postural tremor in her hands, starting two weeks after initiation of salbutamol inhalers for asthma. Symptoms are worse with stress and improve on stopping the medication.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Essential tremor - Differs by being bilateral, typically more prominent during voluntary movement, and familial.
  • Parkinson’s disease - Distinguished by resting tremor, bradykinesia, and rigidity, with onset generally in older adults.
  • Thyrotoxicosis - Accompanied by weight loss, tachycardia, and other hyperthyroid symptoms, confirmed by thyroid function tests.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Medication discontinuation or adjustment - The first-line treatment is to stop or reduce the dosage of the offending drug.
  • Switch to an alternative drug - Where discontinuation is not possible, an alternative drug with a lower propensity for causing tremor may be considered (e.g., switching from a beta-agonist to an anticholinergic for asthma).
  • Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) - May be used to control tremor symptoms, particularly in patients on beta-agonists or stimulants.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Tremor"][/sc]
  • Interference with daily activities - Persistent tremor can impact fine motor tasks like writing or eating.
  • Medication toxicity - Failure to recognise tremor as a sign of drug toxicity (e.g., lithium or valproate) may lead to more severe neurological damage.
  • Psychosocial consequences - Tremor may cause embarrassment or anxiety, potentially leading to social withdrawal in some patients.
