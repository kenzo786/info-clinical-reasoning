Vitamin K Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin K deficiency is a nutritional deficiency in which there is insufficient vitamin K to support normal blood clotting and bone health. Vitamin K is an essential fat-soluble vitamin required for the synthesis of clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, as well as proteins C and S. Deficiency can lead to coagulopathy and increased bleeding risk.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • In the UK, vitamin K deficiency is rare in adults but more common in newborns, where it is known as Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding (VKDB). The prevalence of VKDB in neonates without prophylactic vitamin K is estimated to be around 0.25-1.5% globally.
  • In adults, deficiency is most likely among those with malabsorption disorders, those on prolonged antibiotic therapy, and the elderly. Precise prevalence data in the UK GP population is limited, but the incidence in hospitalised patients with these risk factors is significant.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin K is crucial for the post-translational carboxylation of glutamate residues on clotting factors. Without adequate vitamin K, clotting factors remain inactive, leading to impaired clot formation and increased bleeding risk.
  • The liver requires vitamin K to synthesize these clotting factors. Vitamin K deficiency commonly arises due to poor dietary intake, fat malabsorption (e.g., due to liver disease, bile duct obstruction, or coeliac disease), or use of vitamin K antagonists (e.g., warfarin).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Acute deficiency can develop within days to weeks of inadequate intake, particularly if other risk factors like antibiotic use are present. Chronic deficiency may present subtly with minor bleeding issues that worsen over time if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Easy bruising - Bruising occurs due to impaired coagulation from reduced levels of clotting factors.
  • Bleeding gums - A common bleeding site due to fragile capillaries and reduced clotting capacity.
  • Epistaxis (nosebleeds) - Common due to fragile vessels in the nasal mucosa and reduced coagulation capacity.
  • Menorrhagia in women - Heavy menstrual bleeding can be exacerbated by coagulopathy.
  • Prolonged bleeding from cuts or after procedures - Reflects impaired clot formation in response to injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Ecchymoses (large bruises) - Bruises on skin indicate bleeding into subcutaneous tissue due to poor clotting.
  • Haemarthrosis (joint bleeding) - Rare but may occur with severe deficiency, especially in patients with haemophilia.
  • Purpura and petechiae - Small, non-blanching spots indicative of microvascular bleeding, common in severe deficiencies.
  • Oozing at surgical or wound sites - Due to poor clot formation, seen in moderate to severe deficiency cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Intracranial haemorrhage in neonates - Potentially fatal; signifies severe deficiency and requires urgent intervention.
  • Severe gastrointestinal bleeding - Indicates significant deficiency, particularly in malabsorption disorders, and may lead to hypovolaemia or shock if untreated.
  • Profuse bleeding during or post-surgery - Suggests unrecognised severe deficiency with high-risk implications for patient safety.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic with prolonged PT only - May be identified incidentally, often in patients with mild, subclinical deficiency.
  • Haematuria without other bleeding signs - Less common, may be observed in mild deficiency states or with concurrent urological conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Prothrombin time (PT) - Prolonged in vitamin K deficiency due to reduced synthesis of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors.
  • Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) - May be slightly prolonged in severe deficiency, though often normal in mild cases.
  • Vitamin K serum levels - Low levels confirm deficiency, particularly useful if malabsorption is suspected.
  • Clotting factor assay - Reduced activity of factors II, VII, IX, and X is confirmatory.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • A 5-day-old neonate presents with unexpected bruising and prolonged bleeding from the umbilical stump. Mother declined vitamin K prophylaxis at birth.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Haemophilia - X-linked bleeding disorder; often family history present, normal vitamin K levels.
  • Platelet dysfunction (e.g., ITP) - Often presents with petechiae, normal PT and aPTT.
  • Liver disease - Leads to prolonged PT and aPTT but may present with additional signs of liver failure.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin K supplementation - Administered orally or intramuscularly (e.g., phytomenadione) as per UK guidelines, with neonates routinely receiving a prophylactic dose at birth.
  • Address underlying causes - Treat malabsorption or discontinue vitamin K antagonists where possible.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage - Common in untreated cases, potentially life-threatening (e.g., intracranial bleeding).
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Secondary to severe bleeding, particularly in gastrointestinal cases.
  • Neurological sequelae - In neonates with intracranial bleeding, long-term developmental complications may result.
