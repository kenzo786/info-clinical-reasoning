Aortic Coarctation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Aortic coarctation is a congenital narrowing of the aorta, most commonly just distal to the origin of the left subclavian artery at the site of the ductus arteriosus. This results in a restriction of blood flow to the lower body, leading to increased afterload on the heart and upper body hypertension. It is often associated with other congenital heart defects, such as bicuspid aortic valve or patent ductus arteriosus (PDA).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated at 1 in 2,500 live births. It constitutes 5-8% of congenital heart diseases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Aortic coarctation is diagnosed more frequently in paediatric cardiology, though undiagnosed cases may present later in adulthood.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK figures, with slightly higher rates in areas with less access to early neonatal screening.
  • It is more common in males than females (2:1 ratio).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • The narrowing in the aorta increases the resistance to blood flow, leading to elevated pressures proximal to the constriction (head, neck, and upper extremities) and reduced perfusion distal to it (abdomen and lower extremities).
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy develops as a compensatory mechanism due to increased afterload.
  • Collateral circulation develops over time via intercostal arteries, which helps bypass the constriction and supply blood to the lower body.
  • If untreated, long-term consequences include hypertension, heart failure, and risk of rupture or dissection of the aorta.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Severe coarctation may present in the first few weeks of life with heart failure and shock as the ductus arteriosus closes.
  • Childhood: Mild cases may be asymptomatic, and symptoms often develop gradually as the body compensates with collateral circulation.
  • Adulthood: Undiagnosed cases can present later with hypertension, exertional leg fatigue, or heart failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Leg Claudication or Fatigue - Reduced blood flow to the lower body leads to leg pain or fatigue during exercise, particularly in older children or adults.
  • Headaches - Caused by upper body hypertension due to increased pressure proximal to the coarctation.
  • Failure to Thrive - Seen in infants with severe coarctation as poor perfusion limits oxygen and nutrient delivery to vital organs.
  • Exertional Dyspnoea - Increased workload on the heart due to higher afterload can cause heart failure symptoms, including shortness of breath on exertion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Blood Pressure Discrepancy - Hypertension in the arms with lower blood pressure in the legs due to the obstruction in the aorta.
  • Weak Femoral Pulses - Reduced perfusion to the lower extremities leads to weak or absent femoral pulses.
  • Systolic Murmur - A harsh systolic murmur heard over the left infrascapular region due to turbulent blood flow across the coarctation.
  • Left Ventricular Heave - Due to left ventricular hypertrophy, the apex beat may be displaced and more forceful.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Shock in Newborns - Critical coarctation can present with shock as the ductus arteriosus closes, leading to sudden heart failure and multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Rib Notching on X-ray - This suggests long-standing coarctation with collateral circulation through intercostal arteries, often associated with severe disease.
  • Severe Hypertension - May indicate long-standing untreated coarctation and is a risk factor for aortic rupture or dissection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Hypertension - Some individuals present later in life with isolated high blood pressure, with no other symptoms until the coarctation is found incidentally during investigation.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - Rare, but untreated severe coarctation can lead to hypertensive crises, aortic rupture, or heart failure, resulting in sudden death.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Blood Pressure Measurement - Detects hypertension in the arms and a significant blood pressure gradient between the arms and legs.
  • Chest X-ray - May show rib notching or the classic "3 sign" along the aortic shadow, suggesting aortic narrowing.
  • Echocardiography - Provides a definitive diagnosis by visualising the coarctation and assessing left ventricular function.
  • Cardiac MRI/CT Angiography - Used to map the aorta and any associated anomalies like bicuspid aortic valve or collateral vessels.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • A 10-year-old boy presents with headaches, leg pain during exercise, and a history of hypertension. Physical examination reveals a blood pressure discrepancy between the arms and legs, and weak femoral pulses, raising suspicion of aortic coarctation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy - Presents with exertional dyspnoea and left ventricular hypertrophy but lacks the blood pressure gradient or pulse abnormalities.
  • Renal Artery Stenosis - Can cause secondary hypertension, but typically presents without the pulse or murmur findings associated with coarctation.
  • Takayasu Arteritis - A vasculitis affecting large arteries, leading to limb claudication and blood pressure discrepancies, but often with systemic symptoms like fever and weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Surgical Repair - Resection of the narrowed segment with end-to-end anastomosis or interposition graft. This is the definitive treatment for severe cases.
  • Balloon Angioplasty - In some cases, particularly in older children or adults, balloon dilatation with or without stenting can be considered.
  • Antihypertensive Therapy - Beta-blockers or ACE inhibitors may be used to manage persistent hypertension after repair.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Aortic Aneurysm or Dissection - A long-standing coarctation may weaken the aortic wall, increasing the risk of dissection or rupture.
  • Heart Failure - Due to chronic left ventricular hypertrophy and increased afterload, untreated cases may develop heart failure.
  • Stroke - Severe hypertension can lead to an increased risk of intracranial haemorrhage or stroke, especially in older individuals.
