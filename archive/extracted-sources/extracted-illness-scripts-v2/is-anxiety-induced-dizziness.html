Anxiety-Induced Dizziness
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Anxiety-induced dizziness refers to the sensation of unsteadiness, lightheadedness, or vertigo that occurs as a physical manifestation of anxiety. It is not caused by a primary vestibular dysfunction but is linked to hyperventilation, autonomic nervous system dysregulation, and psychological factors associated with anxiety disorders.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Anxiety disorders are prevalent in 5-6% of the UK population, and dizziness is reported in up to 30% of those with anxiety.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 15-20% of hospital patients presenting with dizziness have an anxiety-related cause.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, anxiety disorders affect approximately 7-10% of the population, with dizziness as a common somatic symptom in up to 40% of cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Anxiety triggers the autonomic nervous system, leading to sympathetic activation ("fight or flight" response), causing hyperventilation, reduced carbon dioxide (CO2) levels, and cerebral vasoconstriction. This results in dizziness or lightheadedness.
  • Hyperventilation increases alkalosis, which affects calcium ion levels and disrupts nerve transmission, contributing to dizziness.
  • Heightened awareness of bodily sensations in anxious individuals can amplify feelings of imbalance and vertigo.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Anxiety-induced dizziness typically occurs in short bursts, lasting from minutes to hours, often associated with acute stress or panic attacks.
  • Chronic Phase: When linked to generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), dizziness can persist over days to weeks, especially during prolonged periods of stress or worry.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Lightheadedness - Results from hyperventilation and reduced CO2 levels leading to cerebral vasoconstriction.
  • Vertigo - Although less common, can occur due to the brain's interpretation of altered sensory input during anxiety episodes.
  • Imbalance - An overactive autonomic response can lead to feelings of unsteadiness or the sensation of floating.
  • Palpitations - A common symptom of anxiety-induced dizziness due to sympathetic nervous system activation.
  • Shortness of breath - A common cause and result of hyperventilation, contributing to the cycle of dizziness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Elevated heart rate due to sympathetic activation during anxiety episodes.
  • Rapid breathing (hyperventilation) - A typical sign in panic attacks or acute anxiety states, which contributes to dizziness.
  • Clammy skin - Sweating is a common autonomic response in anxiety, often seen alongside dizziness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Focal neurological signs - May indicate a primary neurological disorder such as stroke or brain tumour. These include weakness, visual disturbances, or slurred speech.
  • Sudden, severe vertigo - Could signify a vestibular or cardiovascular event rather than anxiety-induced dizziness.
  • Chest pain - Should be evaluated for cardiac causes, such as myocardial infarction, especially in older patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Persistent mild dizziness - Can occur in generalised anxiety disorder without acute episodes but may lead to avoidance of movement or certain activities.
  • Silent hyperventilation - Anxiety without obvious hyperventilation can still cause dizziness due to subtle breathing abnormalities.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - To rule out anaemia, thyroid dysfunction, or other metabolic causes of dizziness (e.g., FBC, TFTs).
  • ECG - To rule out arrhythmias as a cause of palpitations and dizziness.
  • Vestibular function tests - These may be performed to exclude primary vestibular disorders such as BPPV or Ménière’s disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with episodes of lightheadedness and palpitations lasting 15-30 minutes, triggered by stressful situations, without any focal neurological signs. The symptoms resolve with relaxation and breathing exercises.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Vertigo triggered by head movements, diagnosed by a positive Dix-Hallpike test. Unlike anxiety-induced dizziness, it is not related to stress and occurs specifically with positional changes.
  • Ménière’s disease - Recurrent vertigo with hearing loss and tinnitus. Unlike anxiety, it is associated with ear symptoms.
  • Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS) - Dizziness and palpitations upon standing, confirmed by tilt table test. Anxiety-induced dizziness is not typically related to postural changes.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Palpitations, weight loss, and anxiety-like symptoms. Confirmed by elevated free T4 and suppressed TSH on blood tests.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) - First-line treatment for anxiety disorders, including anxiety-induced dizziness. Helps address maladaptive thought patterns and reduce physical symptoms.
  • Breathing exercises - To manage hyperventilation during acute anxiety episodes.
  • Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) - Commonly prescribed for generalised anxiety disorder to reduce anxiety symptoms, including dizziness.
  • Benzodiazepines - May be used short-term in acute anxiety episodes but are not recommended for long-term management due to risk of dependence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anxiety-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Avoidance behaviour - Anxiety-induced dizziness can lead to avoidance of activities or situations that trigger dizziness, potentially worsening anxiety over time.
  • Panic disorder - In some cases, untreated anxiety-induced dizziness can progress to panic disorder, with more frequent and severe panic attacks.
