Cervical Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer is a malignant neoplasm of the cervix, primarily arising from epithelial cells. The most common types are squamous cell carcinoma and adenocarcinoma. Cervical cancer is often associated with persistent infection with high-risk types of human papillomavirus (HPV), especially HPV types 16 and 18.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Approximately 3,200 cases per year, primarily affecting women aged 25–64.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Cervical cancer is the 13th most common cancer among UK women, with a prevalence rate of 9 per 100,000 women.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Cervical cancer is the fourth most common cancer in women, with an estimated 604,000 new cases and 342,000 deaths annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • HPV infection causes integration of viral DNA into cervical epithelial cells, leading to oncogene activation (E6 and E7 proteins) that inhibit tumour suppressor genes (p53 and Rb).
  • This disruption leads to dysregulated cell growth, progressing through stages of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) before becoming invasive cervical cancer.
  • The transformation zone, where squamous and glandular cells meet, is particularly vulnerable to HPV-related carcinogenesis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) progresses over years; early stages often asymptomatic.
  • Progression to Invasive Cancer: Typically spans 10–20 years, with symptoms emerging as invasion occurs.
  • Advanced Disease: Rapid progression once symptoms like pain and bleeding appear, signalling more invasive disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Postcoital bleeding - Common due to fragile tumour vasculature within the cervix.
  • Intermenstrual bleeding - Tumour disruption of normal cervical blood vessels causes irregular bleeding.
  • Abnormal vaginal discharge - Necrosis and infection in tumour tissue result in foul-smelling, purulent discharge.
  • Pelvic pain - Advanced disease may invade pelvic structures, causing deep, aching pain.
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases detected early via screening are asymptomatic.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical lesion (visualised on speculum exam) - Visible friable or ulcerative mass due to cancerous growth.
  • Bleeding on contact - Friable tumour tissue bleeds easily when touched or during intercourse.
  • Pelvic mass (on bimanual examination) - Indicative of more advanced, locally invasive disease.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged pelvic or inguinal lymph nodes in cases of metastasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Persistent postcoital bleeding - Highly indicative of cervical cancer due to neovascularised tumour tissue.
  • Pelvic or lower back pain - May indicate locally advanced cancer affecting pelvic nerves and structures.
  • Unintentional weight loss and fatigue - Suggestive of advanced or metastatic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Absence of bleeding symptoms - Some early adenocarcinomas may present without bleeding, making detection more challenging.
  • Non-specific pelvic discomfort - Misinterpreted as musculoskeletal pain, especially in older patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical screening (smear test) - Detects CIN and early malignancy; HPV DNA testing identifies high-risk HPV subtypes.
  • Colposcopy with biopsy - Direct visualisation of suspicious lesions and histological confirmation.
  • Pelvic MRI or CT scan - Determines tumour staging and extent of local or distant spread.
  • Complete blood count (CBC) - Assesses for anaemia from chronic bleeding or paraneoplastic syndromes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with postcoital bleeding, abnormal discharge, and mild pelvic discomfort over the past six months. Screening history is unclear.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Endometrial cancer - Typically presents with postmenopausal bleeding, not postcoital bleeding.
  • Cervicitis - May cause discharge and bleeding but lacks a mass and is associated with infection signs.
  • Vaginal cancer - Rare; may present similarly but originates within the vaginal wall.
  • Benign cervical polyp - Can cause bleeding but appears as a smooth, non-invasive growth.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early-stage disease: Conisation or hysterectomy for localised disease.
  • Locally advanced disease: Concurrent chemoradiotherapy per UK guidelines.
  • Metastatic disease: Palliative chemotherapy and radiotherapy as appropriate.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pelvic organ invasion: Spread to bladder or rectum, causing haematuria or rectal bleeding.
  • Fistula formation: Advanced disease may cause vesicovaginal or rectovaginal fistulas.
  • Metastasis: Common sites include liver, lungs, and bone, leading to systemic symptoms and poor prognosis.
