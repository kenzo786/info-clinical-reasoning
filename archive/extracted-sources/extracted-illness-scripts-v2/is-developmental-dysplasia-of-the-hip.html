Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH) is a paediatric orthopaedic condition characterised by a spectrum of hip joint abnormalities ranging from mild dysplasia (shallow acetabulum) to complete dislocation of the femoral head. It results from abnormal development or alignment of the hip joint, which can lead to joint instability.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice prevalence: Approximately 1-2% of infants are diagnosed with some form of DDH.
  • UK Hospital prevalence: DDH is a leading cause of paediatric orthopaedic referrals, with a higher incidence in neonates born breech.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by population and screening practices, but incidence ranges between 1 to 3% of all live births.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • DDH results from abnormal development of the hip joint in utero or during infancy. Several risk factors, including genetic predisposition, breech position, and female sex, contribute to improper alignment between the femoral head and acetabulum.
  • This misalignment can lead to a shallow acetabulum, and if the femoral head is not properly seated, it impairs the development of both acetabular depth and femoral head shape, perpetuating instability and increasing the risk of subluxation or dislocation.
  • Left untreated, DDH can lead to joint degeneration and early onset osteoarthritis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal period: DDH may be detectable shortly after birth, particularly in high-risk infants (e.g., breech). The hip joint may be unstable or dislocated.
  • Infancy to early childhood: Dysplasia or dislocation may progress without intervention, leading to further hip instability. Detection is often asymptomatic initially but may present with observable gait abnormalities as the child begins to walk.
  • Adulthood: Untreated DDH can result in chronic hip pain, decreased range of motion, and early onset osteoarthritis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic in infancy - Many cases are asymptomatic early in life, with diagnosis often based on screening or physical examination. Lack of symptoms is due to the absence of weight-bearing activities in neonates.
  • Difficulty or delay in walking - This occurs as the child matures, particularly in cases of unilateral DDH, due to instability and impaired hip joint biomechanics.
  • Limping gait - Common as the child begins ambulating; results from poor hip alignment and stability.
  • Hip pain (in adolescence or adulthood) - Occurs in untreated cases and results from abnormal joint loading and early degenerative changes in the hip.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Positive Ortolani or Barlow test in neonates - A clunk felt as the femoral head relocates (Ortolani) or dislocates (Barlow), indicating instability.
  • Leg length discrepancy - Seen in unilateral DDH due to subluxation or dislocation causing the affected leg to appear shorter.
  • Limited hip abduction - Especially on the affected side, due to abnormal hip joint positioning.
  • Asymmetric skin folds - Extra gluteal or thigh folds on the affected side, due to differences in muscle or joint positioning.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Fixed dislocation or high instability on examination - Indicates more severe DDH that may need urgent referral and intervention to prevent long-term joint complications.
  • Severe limitation in hip abduction - Suggests pronounced hip malalignment and increases the risk of progressive joint degeneration.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Bilateral cases without leg length discrepancy - In bilateral DDH, both legs are affected similarly, making leg length discrepancies less noticeable, potentially delaying diagnosis.
  • Subtle gait abnormalities - Mild cases might present only with minor gait changes, which can be mistaken for normal variation in early walking patterns.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound in infants - Visualises the hip joint structure; a shallow acetabulum or subluxed femoral head suggests DDH.
  • X-rays in older children - May show delayed ossification of the femoral head or acetabular dysplasia; performed if ultrasound results are inconclusive or in children older than 6 months.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • A 6-month-old female, born in breech position, presents with limited abduction of the left hip and asymmetric thigh folds detected during a routine paediatric examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Septic Arthritis - Presents with fever, irritability, and hip pain; unlike DDH, is associated with infection and requires urgent intervention.
  • Perthes Disease - Avascular necrosis of the femoral head in children; presents with pain and limp, typically in older children.
  • Proximal Femoral Focal Deficiency (PFFD) - A congenital condition causing limb length discrepancy and hip joint abnormalities, similar to DDH but more severe in presentation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Pavlik harness - For infants up to 6 months, the harness maintains the femoral head within the acetabulum, promoting proper development.
  • Closed reduction and casting - Used if Pavlik harness fails or in children over 6 months.
  • Surgical intervention - Open reduction and osteotomies may be needed in cases resistant to non-surgical management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis - Increased risk due to abnormal joint loading; often develops prematurely if DDH is untreated.
  • Gait abnormalities - Persistent abnormalities in gait can occur despite treatment, especially in more severe cases.
  • Avascular necrosis of the femoral head - Potential complication of treatment with a harness or surgical intervention due to disrupted blood supply.
