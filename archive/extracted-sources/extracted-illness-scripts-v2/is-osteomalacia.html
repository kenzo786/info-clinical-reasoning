Osteomalacia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia is a metabolic bone disorder characterised by impaired bone mineralisation, leading to soft and weakened bones. It is commonly due to vitamin D deficiency, which results in low calcium and phosphate levels needed for normal bone formation. In adults, osteomalacia can cause bone pain, muscle weakness, and increased risk of fractures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK General Practice: Osteomalacia is relatively uncommon but is more frequently observed in populations with limited sunlight exposure, older adults, and those with dietary restrictions.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: The condition is often seen in hospitalised patients with malabsorption, chronic renal disease, or in those on long-term anticonvulsant therapy.
  • Global Prevalence: More prevalent in regions with limited sunlight exposure and among populations with dietary vitamin D deficiencies or high prevalence of malabsorption syndromes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia develops due to insufficient calcium and phosphate, necessary for bone mineralisation.
  • Step-by-Step: Low vitamin D → decreased intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphate → hypocalcaemia stimulates parathyroid hormone (PTH) → PTH increases bone resorption to normalise calcium but reduces serum phosphate → bone becomes undermineralised, soft, and prone to fractures.
  • Primary Mechanisms: Vitamin D deficiency, phosphate depletion, and impaired vitamin D metabolism in renal or hepatic disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Insidious Onset: Symptoms progress slowly over months to years.
  • Chronic Course: Without intervention, osteomalacia remains progressive, causing worsening symptoms over time.
  • Potential Reversibility: Early intervention with vitamin D, calcium, and phosphate supplementation can stabilise and reverse bone changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Bone Pain: Common in weight-bearing bones due to weakened bone matrix, particularly in hips, lower back, and legs.
  • Muscle Weakness: Due to hypocalcaemia affecting muscle function and causing proximal muscle weakness, particularly in the thighs and shoulders.
  • Fatigue: Systemic symptoms from chronic pain and muscle weakness.
  • Fractures: Low trauma fractures occur due to bone fragility, especially in the vertebrae, pelvis, and long bones.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Waddling Gait: Proximal muscle weakness causes an altered gait pattern.
  • Bony Tenderness: Localised tenderness over ribs, pelvis, and spine due to undermineralised bones.
  • Kyphosis and Skeletal Deformities: Long-term cases can present with spinal deformities due to vertebral weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Pathological Fractures: Indicative of severe undermineralisation and bone fragility, which can lead to immobility.
  • Severe Hypocalcaemia: Can present with tetany or seizures, requiring urgent correction.
  • Rickets (in children): Osteomalacia in children can result in severe deformities and growth retardation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Some patients may be asymptomatic and are diagnosed incidentally on imaging or routine blood work.
  • Diffuse Pain or Fatigue: Non-specific presentations may mimic fibromyalgia or chronic fatigue syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Serum Calcium and Phosphate: Low in osteomalacia, reflecting impaired mineralisation.
  • 25-hydroxyvitamin D: Levels are typically below 25 nmol/L in deficiency states.
  • Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP): Elevated due to increased bone turnover.
  • X-rays: Show Looser zones or pseudofractures, characteristic of osteomalacia.
  • Bone Density Scan (DEXA): Indicates decreased bone density but may not differentiate from osteoporosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female with chronic bone pain, proximal muscle weakness, and fatigue over the past year. Laboratory tests reveal low vitamin D and elevated alkaline phosphatase.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis: Unlike osteomalacia, osteoporosis involves reduced bone density without impaired mineralisation; no elevated ALP is seen.
  • Fibromyalgia: Diffuse pain and fatigue without biochemical abnormalities or X-ray findings typical of osteomalacia.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: May present with pain and fatigue but also joint-specific inflammation and deformities.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D Supplementation: Typically given as cholecalciferol or ergocalciferol to replenish stores.
  • Calcium Supplements: Administered to ensure adequate serum calcium levels.
  • Management of Underlying Causes: Treat malabsorption, renal or hepatic disease, or other contributing factors.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Pathological Fractures: Frequent due to weakened bone structure, impacting mobility and quality of life.
  • Severe Deformities: In untreated cases, especially in children, can lead to permanent skeletal deformities.
  • Chronic Pain and Immobility: Result from ongoing bone weakness and fractures, potentially leading to a dependency on assistive devices.
