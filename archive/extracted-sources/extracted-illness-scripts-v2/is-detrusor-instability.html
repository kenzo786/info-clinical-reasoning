Detrusor Instability
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Detrusor instability, also referred to as overactive bladder (OAB), is characterised by involuntary contractions of the detrusor muscle during the bladder filling phase, leading to symptoms such as urgency, frequency, and urge incontinence. It occurs in the absence of any overt pathological or anatomical abnormalities.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Affects approximately 12% of the adult population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common among urology outpatients, especially women aged 40–70 years.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 16–20%, with higher rates in older populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Detrusor instability arises due to inappropriate detrusor muscle contractions during bladder filling. Potential mechanisms include:
    • Increased afferent signalling from the bladder wall to the brain, leading to premature micturition reflex activation.
    • Impaired central inhibition of bladder contractions due to neurological dysfunction.
    • Local changes in bladder wall compliance or increased sensitivity of urothelial sensory receptors.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Chronic course: Often develops gradually over months or years, with episodic exacerbations.
  • Acute exacerbations: May occur due to urinary tract infections, stress, or caffeine intake.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Urinary urgency: A sudden and compelling need to void that is difficult to defer, caused by involuntary detrusor contractions.
  • Increased urinary frequency: Voiding more than 8 times in 24 hours due to reduced bladder storage capacity.
  • Nocturia: Waking at night to void, often linked to reduced bladder compliance or increased urine production at night.
  • Urge incontinence: Leakage of urine associated with urgency, due to the inability to suppress detrusor contractions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Bladder tenderness: Palpable suprapubic discomfort during bladder filling, due to heightened sensory signalling.
  • Leakage on stress testing: May occur during physical examination, reflecting severe urgency incontinence.
  • Post-void residual: Typically low, as bladder emptying is generally unaffected in detrusor instability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Haematuria: May indicate underlying malignancy or stone disease.
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections: Could signify structural abnormalities or neurogenic bladder.
  • Neurological deficits: Suggests a central or peripheral nervous system lesion contributing to bladder dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases identified during workup for unrelated urinary issues.
  • Isolated nocturia without urgency, potentially due to coexistent nocturnal polyuria.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Urodynamic studies: Demonstrates involuntary detrusor contractions during filling.
  • Urinalysis: Excludes infection and haematuria.
  • Bladder diary: Documents frequency, volume, and incontinence episodes, providing a clinical pattern.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old woman presents with a 2-year history of urinary urgency, frequency (12 times daily), nocturia, and occasional urge incontinence, significantly impacting her quality of life.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection: Differs by the presence of dysuria and pyuria.
  • Interstitial cystitis: Typically associated with chronic pelvic pain and absence of incontinence.
  • Neurogenic bladder: Often linked with underlying neurological disease and significant post-void residual.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications: Reduce caffeine, manage fluid intake.
  • Bladder training: Gradual increase in voiding intervals.
  • Pharmacotherapy: Anticholinergics (e.g., oxybutynin) or β3 agonists (e.g., mirabegron).
  • Refractory cases: Botulinum toxin injections or sacral neuromodulation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Detrusor Instability"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infections: Due to incomplete bladder emptying in severe cases.
  • Psychosocial impact: Anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
