Lung Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Lung cancer is a malignant neoplasm originating from the lung tissue, often from epithelial cells lining the airways. It is broadly divided into two main types: non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), accounting for 85-90% of cases, and small cell lung cancer (SCLC), which is more aggressive and rapidly progressing. The condition is commonly linked to smoking, though non-smokers can also develop lung cancer, especially due to environmental factors or genetic mutations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Smoking is the primary risk factor, responsible for ~85% of cases, with risk increasing with smoking duration and intensity. Passive smoking, occupational exposures (asbestos, diesel fumes, radon gas), genetic predisposition, air pollution, and chronic lung diseases such as COPD also contribute.
  • Prevention: Smoking cessation is the most effective preventive measure, significantly reducing risk within a decade. Workplace safety regulations, radon gas mitigation, and air pollution control further decrease risk. Screening with low-dose CT is recommended for high-risk individuals aged 55-74 with a significant smoking history.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • In lung cancer, genetic mutations cause dysregulated cell proliferation in the bronchial or alveolar epithelium. Carcinogens, such as those in tobacco smoke, cause mutations in oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes (e.g., KRAS, EGFR). This leads to uncontrolled growth and the formation of tumours, which can invade local tissues and metastasise to distant organs like the liver, bones, and brain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Often asymptomatic or presents with vague symptoms like chronic cough or weight loss.
  • Progressive stage: Symptoms worsen over months, with increasing dyspnoea, chest pain, and haemoptysis.
  • Advanced stage: Features of metastasis such as bone pain, neurological deficits, or cachexia may develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough – Persistent due to bronchial irritation by tumour growth.
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) – Tumour infiltration into lung parenchyma or pleura can reduce lung capacity.
  • Haemoptysis – Blood in sputum results from tumour erosion into blood vessels.
  • Chest pain – May be due to pleural invasion or chest wall involvement.
  • Weight loss – Cachexia from metabolic changes and tumour burden.
  • Fatigue – Systemic effect of cancer on metabolism and nutrition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Clubbing of fingers – A common sign of chronic hypoxia or paraneoplastic phenomena.
  • Reduced breath sounds – Due to pleural effusion or tumour obstructing airflow.
  • Tracheal deviation – May occur with large masses or significant effusions.
  • Wheezing – Resulting from bronchial obstruction.
  • Lymphadenopathy – Enlargement of supraclavicular or cervical nodes can signify metastasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis – Suggests invasion of tumour into blood vessels and could indicate advanced disease.
  • Unexplained weight loss – Indicative of a systemic effect of malignancy, possibly due to metastasis.
  • Persistent hoarseness – Suggests invasion or compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
  • Bone pain – May indicate skeletal metastasis, particularly in advanced lung cancer.
  • Neurological deficits – Weakness, seizures, or altered mental state could signify brain metastases.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Shoulder pain – Could be misattributed to musculoskeletal causes, but may indicate Pancoast tumour invading brachial plexus.
  • Unilateral facial swelling – Can be a result of superior vena cava syndrome caused by tumour compression.
  • Hypertrophic osteoarthropathy – Unexplained joint pain or swelling, often a paraneoplastic syndrome of lung cancer.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray – May show a mass, collapse, or pleural effusion.
  • CT Chest – More detailed, can identify tumour size, location, and lymph node involvement.
  • Sputum cytology – Detects malignant cells, particularly useful in central tumours.
  • Bronchoscopy with biopsy – Direct visualisation and sampling of tumour tissue for histological diagnosis.
  • Positron Emission Tomography (PET) – Assesses metabolic activity and helps stage the cancer.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male smoker presents with a persistent cough for three months, haemoptysis, and weight loss. Chest X-ray reveals a 4 cm mass in the left upper lobe, confirmed by biopsy as non-small cell lung cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis – Similar chronic cough and weight loss, but often accompanied by night sweats, and sputum microscopy reveals Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
  • Pulmonary embolism – Sudden onset of dyspnoea and chest pain, with potential haemoptysis, but CT pulmonary angiography can confirm vascular occlusion.
  • Pneumonia – Acute presentation with fever, productive cough, and consolidation on chest X-ray.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) – Often presents with chronic cough and dyspnoea but without weight loss or systemic signs of malignancy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Non-small cell lung cancer: Surgery (lobectomy or pneumonectomy) is the mainstay if operable. Adjuvant chemotherapy or radiotherapy may be required.
  • Small cell lung cancer: Chemotherapy is the primary treatment due to rapid spread. Radiotherapy may be used for local control or palliation.
  • Palliative care: Symptom control with analgesia, bronchodilators, and steroids for advanced, non-resectable cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lung Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis – Commonly to bones, brain, liver, and adrenal glands.
  • Pleural effusion – Fluid accumulation in the pleural space causing dyspnoea.
  • Superior vena cava syndrome – Obstruction of the SVC leading to facial swelling and venous distension.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes – Endocrine or neurological effects unrelated to direct tumour invasion (e.g., hypercalcaemia, SIADH).
