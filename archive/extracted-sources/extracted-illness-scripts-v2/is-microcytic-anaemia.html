Microcytic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Microcytic anaemia is defined as a type of anaemia characterised by smaller-than-normal red blood cells (RBCs), with a mean corpuscular volume (MCV) of less than 80 femtolitres. It typically results from defective haemoglobin production, commonly due to iron deficiency, but may also be caused by chronic disease, thalassaemia, or sideroblastic anaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, iron deficiency anaemia (the most common cause of microcytic anaemia) affects 2-5% of adult men and postmenopausal women.
  • UK hospital prevalence of microcytic anaemia is around 5-10% among admitted patients, often reflecting underlying chronic diseases.
  • Globally, microcytic anaemia due to iron deficiency is highly prevalent, affecting approximately 25% of the population, particularly in low-income regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In microcytic anaemia, defective haemoglobin synthesis reduces RBC size (microcytosis). The major causes include:
    • Iron deficiency: Inadequate iron availability impairs haemoglobin synthesis, resulting in smaller RBCs.
    • Chronic disease: Inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-6, reduce iron availability and erythropoiesis.
    • Thalassaemia: Genetic defects cause impaired globin chain production, affecting RBC morphology and size.
    • Sideroblastic anaemia: Defective haem synthesis within the mitochondria causes iron accumulation in RBC precursors, leading to microcytosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Microcytic anaemia develops gradually over weeks to months as haemoglobin production becomes increasingly impaired. In iron deficiency, there is often a slow progression as body iron stores are depleted. In chronic disease, the onset may coincide with the progression of the underlying condition.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Decreased oxygen-carrying capacity due to reduced haemoglobin leads to tissue hypoxia, causing generalised fatigue.
  • Pallor - Reduced haemoglobin concentration causes pallor, which may be noticed in the skin, nail beds, and mucous membranes.
  • Shortness of breath - In response to anaemia, tissues experience hypoxia, which stimulates the respiratory centre to increase breathing rate.
  • Dizziness - Impaired oxygen delivery to the brain can result in dizziness or light-headedness, particularly upon exertion.
  • Headache - Brain hypoxia due to reduced haemoglobin levels may cause headaches.
  • Exertional dyspnoea - Mild activity may exacerbate symptoms due to the body’s inability to meet increased oxygen demand.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Pale skin and mucous membranes reflect reduced oxygenated haemoglobin.
  • Tachycardia - The heart compensates for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity by increasing heart rate to improve tissue perfusion.
  • Koilonychia - "Spoon-shaped" nails, particularly seen in iron deficiency anaemia, result from altered nail growth in response to poor oxygenation.
  • Angular cheilitis - Cracks at the corners of the mouth are a sign of iron deficiency and associated epithelial changes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe fatigue or dyspnoea at rest - Suggests significant anaemia that may indicate severe iron deficiency or ongoing blood loss.
  • Unexplained weight loss - May suggest malignancy, particularly gastrointestinal cancer leading to chronic blood loss.
  • Melena or haematemesis - Black stools or vomiting blood are suggestive of gastrointestinal bleeding, a potential cause of iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Postural hypotension - Can indicate significant blood loss or severe anaemia leading to hypovolaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic microcytic anaemia, especially in the elderly or those with chronic disease, may present with non-specific symptoms such as lethargy or mild cognitive decline.
  • Iron deficiency in children can present atypically with behavioural issues, developmental delays, or pica (craving for non-food substances).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Low haemoglobin (<130 g/L in men, <120 g/L in women) and MCV <80 fL indicate microcytosis. This is due to impaired haemoglobin production.
  • Serum ferritin: Low ferritin (<30 µg/L) confirms iron deficiency, as ferritin reflects body iron stores.
  • Serum iron and transferrin saturation: Low serum iron and transferrin saturation (<15%) in iron deficiency anaemia due to poor iron availability.
  • Blood film: Hypochromic, microcytic RBCs with possible target cells or poikilocytosis in thalassaemia or sideroblastic anaemia.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) or erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): Elevated levels in anaemia of chronic disease due to underlying inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with fatigue, dizziness, and shortness of breath on exertion over the last 3 months. Blood tests show haemoglobin of 96 g/L and an MCV of 74 fL. Serum ferritin is 10 µg/L.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Presents with low ferritin and a history of poor dietary intake or chronic blood loss. Distinguishes from other causes by low iron stores.
  • Thalassaemia: Microcytic anaemia with normal or raised ferritin and a family history of the condition. Electrophoresis confirms abnormal globin chains.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Mild microcytosis with elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) and normal ferritin levels.
  • Sideroblastic anaemia: Blood film shows ringed sideroblasts and a history of alcohol use or drug exposure. MCV may be low or normal.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Oral iron supplementation (ferrous sulphate 200 mg twice daily) is first-line treatment. In severe cases or where oral iron is ineffective, intravenous iron may be given.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Treat the underlying condition; erythropoiesis-stimulating agents may be used in severe cases.
  • Thalassaemia: Transfusion therapy or iron chelation in severe cases; genetic counselling is essential.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Microcytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Chronic severe anaemia can lead to high-output heart failure due to the heart compensating for reduced oxygen delivery.
  • Delayed growth and cognitive development: Common in children with untreated iron deficiency anaemia, due to impaired oxygenation of tissues.
  • Iron overload: Can occur in repeated transfusion therapy, as seen in thalassaemia major, requiring chelation therapy to prevent organ damage.
