Epiglottitis Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Epiglottitis is an acute, life-threatening inflammatory condition characterised by swelling of the epiglottis and surrounding tissues, leading to potential airway obstruction. It is most often caused by bacterial infections, particularly Haemophilus influenzae type B (Hib), though vaccination has significantly reduced its incidence.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Since the introduction of the Hib vaccine, epiglottitis has become rare in the UK, particularly in children. In adults, epiglottitis accounts for around 1–4 cases per 100,000 annually in developed countries. Hospital prevalence has decreased significantly, though sporadic adult cases still occur. Globally, the incidence is variable, with higher prevalence in countries lacking widespread Hib vaccination.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial infection (most commonly Hib) invades the epiglottis and adjacent tissues, leading to inflammatory oedema. The epiglottis becomes swollen, and its ability to protect the airway during swallowing is compromised. Severe oedema can obstruct the airway, leading to respiratory distress and hypoxia. In unvaccinated individuals, Hib remains the most common causative agent, though Streptococcus pneumoniae and Staphylococcus aureus may also be involved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Epiglottitis typically develops rapidly over hours to days. It begins with mild upper respiratory symptoms, such as sore throat, followed by high fever, worsening dysphagia, and rapidly progressive respiratory distress. Without prompt intervention, airway obstruction can occur within 24-48 hours of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Sore throat – Initial symptom due to local inflammation and infection of the epiglottis.
  • Dysphagia – Difficulty swallowing occurs as the epiglottis swells, impeding the swallowing mechanism.
  • Drooling – Patients cannot swallow saliva due to dysphagia and pain, leading to drooling.
  • High fever – A systemic response to infection, typically seen in bacterial infections like Hib.
  • Stridor – Inspiratory stridor is a hallmark of upper airway obstruction, caused by the swollen epiglottis narrowing the airway.
  • Dyspnoea – Progressive difficulty breathing as the airway becomes obstructed by inflammation.
  • Odynophagia – Painful swallowing due to inflammation of the epiglottis and surrounding structures.
  • Muffled voice – “Hot potato” voice occurs due to oedema of the laryngeal structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Tripod positioning – A posture adopted to maximise airway patency, where the patient leans forward with hands on knees.
  • Stridor – A high-pitched sound heard during inspiration due to partial airway obstruction.
  • Intercostal and suprasternal retractions – Sign of respiratory distress, with visible chest wall retraction during inspiration as the body attempts to overcome airway obstruction.
  • Cyanosis – A late sign of severe hypoxia caused by significant airway obstruction.
  • Tachycardia – A compensatory response to hypoxia.
  • Anxiety or agitation – Resulting from hypoxia and difficulty breathing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset respiratory distress – Can signify impending airway obstruction, requiring immediate intervention to prevent asphyxia.
  • Drooling with stridor – Strongly suggests airway compromise due to epiglottitis, requiring urgent airway management.
  • Cyanosis – Indicates critical hypoxaemia and impending respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • In adults, epiglottitis may present more insidiously with sore throat and mild dysphagia but without classic signs like stridor or severe respiratory distress. This may lead to delayed diagnosis and progression to airway obstruction.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Lateral neck X-ray – A characteristic "thumbprint sign" (swollen epiglottis) may be visible, indicating airway obstruction.
  • Flexible fibreoptic laryngoscopy – Gold standard for visualisation of the swollen, cherry-red epiglottis. This should only be performed by an experienced provider with airway management capability.
  • Blood cultures – Useful for identifying the causative organism, particularly in suspected bacterial epiglottitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old unvaccinated boy presents with a 12-hour history of sore throat, drooling, high fever, and difficulty breathing, in tripod position with inspiratory stridor and respiratory distress.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Croup – Typically presents with a barking cough and low-grade fever, more gradual onset, and responds to nebulised epinephrine. Stridor is inspiratory but milder than in epiglottitis.
  • Laryngotracheitis – More gradual onset, diffuse inflammation of the airway, and less severe obstruction compared to epiglottitis.
  • Peritonsillar abscess – Unilateral sore throat, dysphagia, uvular deviation, and muffled voice but less likelihood of severe airway obstruction.
  • Retropharyngeal abscess – Typically presents with neck stiffness, dysphagia, and fever, often lacking stridor and drooling.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Airway management – Immediate securing of the airway is the priority, often requiring endotracheal intubation or tracheostomy.
  • Empiric antibiotics – UK guidelines recommend broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone or cefotaxime) targeting Hib, Streptococcus, and Staphylococcus species.
  • Supportive care – Oxygen supplementation, IV fluids, and close monitoring in an intensive care setting are typically required.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction – The most feared complication, leading to respiratory failure and death if not promptly managed.
  • Sepsis – Bacterial infection can disseminate, particularly in unvaccinated individuals.
  • Pneumonia – Can occur as a secondary infection due to compromised airway and aspiration.
