Hyperemesis Gravidarum
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Hyperemesis gravidarum (HG) is a severe form of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy (NVP), characterised by intractable vomiting, weight loss (>5% of pre-pregnancy weight), dehydration, and electrolyte imbalances. It is more severe than typical morning sickness and can significantly impair a woman's ability to perform daily activities.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Affects approximately 0.3–2% of pregnant women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for up to 1.5% of hospital admissions during pregnancy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies significantly by population, but occurs in 0.3–2% of pregnancies globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • HG is thought to be related to elevated levels of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), which is produced by the placenta. High hCG levels stimulate the vomiting centre in the brain and alter gastrointestinal motility. Genetic factors and hormonal imbalances, including oestrogen and progesterone, may also contribute. Hypersensitivity to these hormonal changes in susceptible women may lead to severe vomiting, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • HG typically begins between the 4th and 10th weeks of gestation and peaks around the 9th to 12th weeks. Symptoms usually improve by the 20th week of pregnancy, although in some cases, symptoms may persist until delivery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Persistent vomiting - Caused by overstimulation of the vomiting centre in response to elevated hCG levels.
  • Severe nausea - Triggered by the same hormonal mechanisms as vomiting, worsened by delayed gastric emptying.
  • Dehydration - Results from excessive fluid loss due to vomiting.
  • Weight loss - Due to prolonged vomiting and inability to retain food, leading to malnutrition.
  • Fatigue - A consequence of both malnutrition and dehydration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - A sign of dehydration and volume depletion.
  • Dry mucous membranes - Indicative of dehydration.
  • Postural hypotension - A result of fluid depletion and hypovolaemia.
  • Ketosis - Found in urine due to fat breakdown, as the body lacks glucose from malnutrition.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - Can indicate impending electrolyte imbalances and acute kidney injury (AKI).
  • Altered mental state - Suggests Wernicke's encephalopathy from vitamin B1 deficiency, a rare but serious complication of HG.
  • Hypokalaemia - Signifies electrolyte disturbance, which can lead to cardiac arrhythmias.
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting blood could indicate Mallory-Weiss tears or significant upper GI tract irritation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Mild symptoms lasting beyond 20 weeks - Some women may experience mild but prolonged symptoms, rather than a typical resolution around 20 weeks.
  • Absence of dehydration despite frequent vomiting - Some women may maintain adequate hydration, masking the severity of HG.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Presence of ketones indicates starvation and fat breakdown due to insufficient calorie intake.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (may show haemoconcentration), electrolyte panel (hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia), and renal function tests (raised urea/creatinine in dehydration).
  • Ultrasound scan - To confirm intrauterine pregnancy and rule out multiple pregnancies or molar pregnancy, both of which are associated with higher hCG levels and increased risk of HG.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman, G2P1, at 9 weeks' gestation presents with 2 weeks of persistent vomiting, severe nausea, weight loss of 5 kg, and dry mucous membranes. She has ketones on urinalysis and mild hypokalaemia on blood tests.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Molar pregnancy - Typically presents with severe vomiting due to higher-than-expected hCG levels, but ultrasound shows abnormal trophoblastic tissue.
  • Gastroenteritis - Acute onset of vomiting, diarrhoea, and fever, usually with a history of recent infectious exposure.
  • Pancreatitis - Severe vomiting with epigastric pain and elevated amylase/lipase levels.
  • Thyroid disease (e.g., hyperthyroidism) - May cause vomiting but is associated with additional signs such as tachycardia, heat intolerance, and tremors. TSH levels would be suppressed.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Fluid replacement - IV rehydration with normal saline is essential for correcting dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Anti-emetics - First-line options include antihistamines (e.g., promethazine) and dopamine antagonists (e.g., metoclopramide). Ondansetron may be used in more severe cases.
  • Thiamine supplementation - Given to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy in women who are vomiting severely.
  • Hospitalisation - Required for severe cases, especially with electrolyte disturbances or when oral intake is not tolerated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperemesis Gravidarum"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte imbalances - Hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and neurological symptoms.
  • Wernicke's encephalopathy - A rare but serious complication due to thiamine deficiency from prolonged vomiting.
  • Malnutrition - Can lead to intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) and low birth weight in the foetus.
  • Psychological impact - Women with HG often suffer from anxiety, depression, and a sense of isolation due to the chronic and debilitating nature of the condition.
