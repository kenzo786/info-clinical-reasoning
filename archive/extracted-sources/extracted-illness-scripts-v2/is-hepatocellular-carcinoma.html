Hepatocellular Carcinoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common primary malignancy of the liver, arising from hepatocytes, the main liver cells. Characterised by malignant tumour formation within the liver, HCC is strongly associated with chronic liver disease and cirrhosis, often secondary to viral hepatitis, alcohol-related liver disease, or non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Rare in primary care settings but increasing due to rising cases of NAFLD and hepatitis C.
  • UK hospital prevalence: HCC accounts for 90% of primary liver cancers in hospitals; liver cancer incidence has doubled over the past two decades.
  • Worldwide prevalence: High prevalence in areas with endemic hepatitis B and C, including East Asia and sub-Saharan Africa; globally, liver cancer is the sixth most common cancer and the third leading cause of cancer death.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma often develops from chronic liver inflammation and injury, leading to cirrhosis and genetic mutations in hepatocytes.
  • In chronic liver disease, ongoing injury from toxins (e.g., hepatitis B/C viruses, alcohol) leads to fibrosis and compensatory hepatocyte regeneration.
  • In cirrhotic livers, hepatocytes undergo clonal expansion and may accumulate genetic mutations, which can lead to malignant transformation and tumour formation.
  • Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and other angiogenic factors stimulate tumour growth and neovascularisation, promoting tumour spread within the liver and potentially leading to metastasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic Phase: Slow progression, often over years or decades in the presence of cirrhosis.
  • Early Tumour Development: Often asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic; early-stage HCC may remain undetected.
  • Advanced Disease: Rapid progression of symptoms with weight loss, abdominal pain, jaundice, and liver failure in advanced stages.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain - Often in the right upper quadrant, due to liver enlargement and tumour mass effect.
  • Weight loss - Due to cancer cachexia and decreased appetite, common in malignancies.
  • Fatigue - Secondary to liver dysfunction, systemic inflammation, and tumour burden.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Due to liver enlargement, tumour burden, and related gastrointestinal involvement.
  • Jaundice - Occurs in advanced cases due to bile duct obstruction or extensive liver damage; presents as yellowing of skin and sclera.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver palpable below the costal margin, due to tumour mass.
  • Ascites - Accumulation of fluid in the abdomen, often indicating portal hypertension due to liver cirrhosis.
  • Cachexia - Severe weight loss and muscle wasting from cancer-related metabolic changes.
  • Splenomegaly - Enlarged spleen due to portal hypertension.
  • Pallor - Due to chronic blood loss, anaemia, or nutritional deficiencies secondary to liver dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset jaundice - May indicate acute liver failure, severe bile duct obstruction, or extensive tumour infiltration.
  • Severe right upper quadrant pain - May suggest tumour rupture or rapid growth, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Haematemesis or melaena - Indicates potential bleeding varices due to portal hypertension; requires emergency intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Minimal symptoms with abnormal liver function tests - Some cases present only with deranged LFTs in surveillance.
  • Extrahepatic metastasis - Rare, but can present with symptoms related to the lungs, bones, or adrenal glands.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) - Elevated in 60-70% of HCC cases; levels >400 ng/mL are highly suggestive.
  • Liver ultrasound - First-line imaging; identifies liver masses or suspicious areas.
  • MRI/CT scan - Confirmatory imaging; shows tumour vascularity, capsule, and invasion characteristics.
  • Liver biopsy - Reserved for ambiguous cases due to bleeding risk; confirms histopathology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old male with a history of hepatitis C presents with fatigue, unintentional weight loss, and right upper quadrant pain over the past 3 months, with imaging revealing a liver mass.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Metastatic liver disease - Often multiple lesions; primary cancer elsewhere.
  • Intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma - More common in the bile ducts; lacks elevated AFP.
  • Focal nodular hyperplasia - Benign lesion; lacks invasive features on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection - Option for small, localised tumours without extensive cirrhosis.
  • Liver transplant - Suitable for early-stage HCC with limited tumour size; curative potential.
  • Transarterial chemoembolisation (TACE) - Palliative option for intermediate-stage HCC to slow tumour growth.
  • Systemic therapy - Sorafenib, lenvatinib, or immunotherapy used in advanced HCC.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Liver failure - Due to extensive liver damage; can lead to hepatic encephalopathy and death.
  • Portal hypertension - Cirrhosis and tumour effects cause increased portal vein pressure, leading to varices and ascites.
  • Metastasis - Spread to lungs, bones, or adrenal glands in advanced stages, worsening prognosis.
