Bladder Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Bladder cancer is a malignant growth originating in the bladder's epithelial lining, primarily the urothelium. It is characterised by abnormal, uncontrolled cellular proliferation and can range from non-invasive to invasive forms, with the potential to spread to surrounding tissues and distant organs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Bladder cancer accounts for approximately 3% of all new cancers annually, with most patients presenting in primary care with haematuria.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Bladder cancer is the 11th most common cancer in the UK, with higher prevalence among males, particularly over age 65.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Bladder cancer represents 3.2% of all cancers globally, with rates higher in Europe and North America.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Bladder cancer arises from mutations in the urothelial cells of the bladder lining. Common genetic changes include mutations in FGFR3, TP53, and RAS genes. Carcinogens like tobacco metabolites and industrial chemicals may initiate these mutations, leading to dysregulated cell proliferation.
  • The cancer often progresses in a staged manner, from non-invasive papillary tumours (confined to the urothelium) to muscle-invasive disease, impacting the detrusor muscle layer and increasing metastatic risk.
  • High-grade tumours, which grow rapidly, are associated with invasive potential, whereas low-grade tumours tend to grow slowly and remain localised.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with microscopic haematuria.
  • Intermediate Phase: Visible haematuria and urinary symptoms like dysuria or increased frequency, typically over weeks to months.
  • Advanced Phase: Symptoms may progress to pelvic pain, weight loss, and symptoms of metastatic disease if untreated, often over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Haematuria - Visible or microscopic blood in urine due to tumour invasion of the bladder lining, causing vascular disruption.
  • Increased urinary frequency - Tumour irritation of the bladder wall stimulates more frequent voiding.
  • Dysuria - Pain during urination, often from tumour proximity to nerve endings within the bladder lining.
  • Pelvic pain - Indicates localised invasion or pressure on surrounding structures in more advanced disease.
  • Weight loss and fatigue - Associated with systemic effects of advanced malignancy, including cytokine release.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Haematuria on dipstick - Gross or microscopic blood detected due to urothelial disruption by tumour.
  • Suprapubic tenderness - May occur in advanced disease with localised extension or tumour bulk.
  • Palpable pelvic mass - In rare cases of invasive disease; indicates extensive local tumour growth.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Persistent or visible haematuria - Strongly associated with malignancy and warrants urgent investigation.
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue - Suggests possible metastatic spread and systemic involvement.
  • Bone pain or fracture - Indicative of bone metastasis, often seen in advanced bladder cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Microscopic haematuria only - May delay diagnosis as symptoms are often minimal; tumour remains undetected until symptoms progress.
  • Asymptomatic with incidental findings - Often seen in early-stage low-grade bladder cancer detected on imaging or urinalysis for unrelated conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Detects microscopic haematuria; a positive result may prompt further investigation.
  • Cystoscopy - Direct visualisation of bladder interior, identifying tumour presence, size, and extent.
  • CT Urography - Provides imaging of the urinary tract to assess for tumour spread or obstruction.
  • Urine cytology - Examines urine for malignant cells, helpful in high-grade cancer detection.
  • Biopsy - Confirms diagnosis and provides grading and staging information.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • 66-year-old male with a three-month history of visible haematuria, dysuria, and increased urinary frequency, with suprapubic tenderness on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection - Presents with dysuria and frequency, typically without gross haematuria.
  • Kidney stones - Causes severe flank pain, often with microscopic haematuria but lacks persistent urinary frequency.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) - Common in older males with frequency and nocturia, usually without haematuria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Non-invasive tumours: Transurethral resection of bladder tumour (TURBT) with intravesical Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) therapy for high-grade disease.
  • Invasive tumours: Radical cystectomy and urinary diversion or chemoradiotherapy in cases where surgery is contraindicated.
  • Advanced or metastatic disease: Chemotherapy (e.g., cisplatin-based) or immunotherapy (e.g., PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors) based on tumour profile.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bladder Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Common sites include the lungs, liver, and bones, affecting prognosis and complicating treatment.
  • Urinary obstruction - Due to tumour bulk or infiltration into ureters, leading to hydronephrosis and potential renal impairment.
  • Recurrence - Bladder cancer has a high recurrence rate, necessitating regular surveillance post-treatment.
