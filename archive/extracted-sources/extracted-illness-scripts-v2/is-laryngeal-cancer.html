Laryngeal Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Laryngeal cancer refers to malignant tumours that arise from the tissues of the larynx (voice box). The most common type is squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), which originates from the epithelial lining. It can be classified based on its location within the larynx as glottic (affecting the vocal cords), supraglottic (above the vocal cords), or subglottic (below the vocal cords).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Laryngeal cancer is relatively rare, with an incidence of around 3-4 per 100,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hospitalised cases reflect more advanced or symptomatic presentations, with men more frequently affected than women (3:1 ratio).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Global incidence is around 2.8 per 100,000 people, with higher rates in countries with higher tobacco and alcohol consumption.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Most laryngeal cancers are caused by the accumulation of mutations in the squamous epithelium due to carcinogen exposure (primarily tobacco and alcohol).
  • These mutations lead to abnormal cell growth, dysplasia, and eventually carcinoma in situ. Over time, this progresses to invasive cancer, which can spread locally or metastasise to lymph nodes or distant organs.
  • The location of the tumour affects both symptom onset and disease progression; glottic cancers typically present early due to voice changes, while subglottic cancers may remain asymptomatic until more advanced.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Minimal symptoms in the case of supraglottic and subglottic tumours. Voice changes are often the first symptom in glottic tumours.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening of symptoms such as hoarseness, dysphagia, and throat pain. There may be local spread and regional lymph node involvement.
  • Advanced phase: Tumour invasion of surrounding tissues, airway obstruction, weight loss, and distant metastasis can occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Hoarseness - Common in glottic tumours due to direct involvement of the vocal cords, leading to altered voice production.
  • Throat pain - Often experienced as a persistent sore throat, especially with supraglottic cancers, as the tumour irritates nearby tissues.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing occurs when the tumour compresses or invades the pharyngeal structures.
  • Otalgia (referred ear pain) - Caused by the tumour’s effect on the glossopharyngeal nerve, referring pain to the ear.
  • Stridor - A late symptom due to airway obstruction as the tumour grows, particularly in subglottic or large glottic cancers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Hoarseness - Alteration in the vocal quality, often due to involvement of the vocal cords.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy - Enlarged neck nodes can signify regional metastasis, especially in advanced supraglottic cancers.
  • Visible or palpable neck mass - In advanced cases, the tumour may invade nearby tissues, leading to a palpable mass.
  • Stridor - Audible breathing sounds due to narrowed airways from the tumour obstructing airflow.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Progressive hoarseness lasting more than 3 weeks - Suggestive of vocal cord involvement and laryngeal pathology.
  • Dysphagia - Indicates compression or invasion of surrounding structures, potentially signifying advanced disease.
  • Unexplained weight loss - A common sign of malignancy and may indicate systemic spread.
  • Stridor or airway compromise - Urgent sign of airway obstruction, often requiring prompt intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough or throat clearing - May precede more common symptoms and be mistaken for benign conditions like laryngitis.
  • Intermittent ear pain without local ear pathology - Referred pain due to glossopharyngeal nerve involvement may lead to an ear-focused workup, delaying laryngeal cancer diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Flexible nasoendoscopy - Allows direct visualisation of the larynx, identifying lesions and vocal cord abnormalities.
  • CT or MRI neck - Used to assess the extent of local invasion and lymph node involvement.
  • Biopsy - Confirmatory test through histopathological examination, typically via direct laryngoscopy under anaesthesia.
  • FNA of cervical lymph nodes - Fine-needle aspiration to assess metastatic lymph node involvement if enlarged nodes are palpable.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old male smoker presents with a 2-month history of hoarseness, throat pain, and difficulty swallowing. He has a 10 kg weight loss and a palpable cervical mass.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Benign vocal cord nodules - Presents with hoarseness but lacks systemic symptoms or progressive dysphagia.
  • Laryngitis - Typically acute and associated with viral symptoms, such as cough or fever, unlike the persistent course of laryngeal cancer.
  • Thyroid cancer - May present with a neck mass and hoarseness, but often with no direct airway involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early-stage disease: Surgical resection (e.g., partial laryngectomy) or radiation therapy is often curative.
  • Advanced-stage disease: Combined modality treatment with surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy is typically used.
  • Palliative care: For inoperable or metastatic disease, focus on symptom relief and quality of life.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Laryngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction - Due to tumour growth, potentially requiring tracheostomy.
  • Metastasis - Local or distant spread, commonly to the lungs, liver, or bones.
  • Post-treatment complications - Includes laryngeal stenosis, dysphonia, or dysphagia.
