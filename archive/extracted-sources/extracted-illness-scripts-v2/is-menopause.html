Menopause
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Menopause is defined as the permanent cessation of menstruation due to the loss of ovarian follicular activity, confirmed after 12 consecutive months of amenorrhoea in the absence of other pathological or physiological causes. It is a natural part of ageing and typically occurs in women aged 45–55 years.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Around 13 million women in the UK are peri- or postmenopausal, with most experiencing symptoms.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Menopause-related issues are commonly managed in primary care, but severe cases may require secondary care input.
  • Worldwide prevalence: All women experience menopause unless surgically induced or due to rare conditions. The median age is 50–51 years globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • The ovaries progressively lose follicular activity due to ageing, leading to reduced production of oestrogen, progesterone, and inhibins.
  • Reduced oestrogen levels result in an increase in gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) due to loss of negative feedback.
  • Systemic oestrogen deficiency causes symptoms such as vasomotor instability, genitourinary atrophy, and bone loss.
  • Long-term effects include increased risks of osteoporosis, cardiovascular disease, and cognitive decline due to chronic oestrogen depletion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Perimenopause: Transition phase lasting 4–8 years, characterised by menstrual irregularity and initial symptoms.
  • Menopause: Occurs after 12 months of amenorrhoea. Symptoms often peak during this time.
  • Postmenopause: Extends from menopause onwards, with some symptoms resolving but others, such as osteoporosis risk, persisting or worsening.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Hot flushes and night sweats: Vasomotor instability due to hypothalamic dysfunction caused by oestrogen deficiency.
  • Irregular menstruation: Caused by fluctuating ovarian hormone levels during perimenopause.
  • Vaginal dryness and dyspareunia: Genitourinary atrophy from oestrogen depletion leading to thinner vaginal epithelium and reduced lubrication.
  • Mood swings and irritability: Possibly linked to hormonal fluctuations and neurochemical changes in the brain.
  • Fatigue: Multifactorial, including sleep disturbances from night sweats and psychological impacts.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Dry skin: Reduced oestrogen causes decreased collagen and hydration.
  • Decreased breast density: Atrophy of mammary glands due to hormonal changes.
  • Thinning hair: Oestrogen deficiency can disrupt hair growth cycles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding: May indicate endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy; warrants urgent investigation.
  • Severe depression or anxiety: Could signify a need for psychiatric intervention.
  • Fractures: Indicative of osteoporosis, a common complication of menopause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Some women may present with predominantly psychological symptoms such as anxiety or memory difficulties, masking the underlying hormonal cause.
  • Asymptomatic cases are common and may only come to medical attention due to concerns about long-term risks like osteoporosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • FSH levels: Elevated (>30 IU/L) due to loss of ovarian feedback inhibition.
  • Oestradiol: Low levels consistent with oestrogen deficiency.
  • Bone density scan (DEXA): Used to assess osteoporosis risk, with T-score ≤−2.5 indicating osteoporosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old woman presents with hot flushes, night sweats, and irregular menstruation over the past year, accompanied by vaginal dryness and mood swings.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Thyroid dysfunction: Can mimic symptoms such as fatigue and mood changes; differentiated by thyroid function tests.
  • Depression: Psychological symptoms predominate without vasomotor symptoms.
  • Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS): Occurs earlier in life with features like hyperandrogenism and irregular cycles.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT): Mainstay treatment for symptom relief; includes combined oestrogen and progestogen or oestrogen-only (if hysterectomised).
  • Non-hormonal therapies: For vasomotor symptoms, such as SSRIs or gabapentin.
  • Lifestyle modifications: Including weight management, exercise, and smoking cessation to reduce long-term risks.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Menopause"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis: Due to prolonged oestrogen deficiency, increasing fracture risk.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Accelerated atherosclerosis linked to oestrogen depletion.
  • Urogenital atrophy: Leading to chronic urinary and sexual dysfunction.
