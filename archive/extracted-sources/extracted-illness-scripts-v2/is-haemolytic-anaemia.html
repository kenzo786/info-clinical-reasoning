Haemolytic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Haemolytic anaemia is a condition in which red blood cells (RBCs) are destroyed prematurely, leading to a decreased RBC lifespan. The body’s bone marrow cannot compensate for this accelerated destruction, resulting in anaemia. Haemolysis can be intravascular (within blood vessels) or extravascular (mainly in the spleen). It can be due to inherited factors, such as sickle cell disease or hereditary spherocytosis, or acquired causes, such as autoimmune haemolytic anaemia or infections.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, haemolytic anaemia is relatively rare. Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (AIHA), one of the common acquired forms, has an estimated incidence of 1-3 cases per 100,000 people annually. Inherited forms like hereditary spherocytosis have a prevalence of approximately 1 in 2,000 people in northern European populations. Globally, conditions like sickle cell disease are more common in regions with a high prevalence of malaria.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of haemolytic anaemia involves the destruction of RBCs before their normal lifespan of 120 days. This can occur through immune-mediated mechanisms, such as antibody binding in autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, leading to phagocytosis or complement-mediated destruction. In inherited haemolytic anaemias, defects in the RBC membrane (hereditary spherocytosis), enzyme deficiencies (G6PD deficiency), or abnormal haemoglobin (sickle cell disease) result in increased fragility and destruction. The excessive destruction of RBCs leads to elevated bilirubin levels (from haem breakdown), splenomegaly (due to increased RBC clearance), and anaemia due to insufficient erythropoiesis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Haemolytic anaemia can present acutely or chronically, depending on the cause. Acute presentations may occur in infections, drug-induced haemolysis, or acute haemolytic crises in conditions like G6PD deficiency or sickle cell disease. Chronic haemolysis is more typical in inherited conditions like hereditary spherocytosis, where patients may compensate for the anaemia over time but experience intermittent exacerbations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood due to anaemia leads to generalised weakness and tiredness.
  • Jaundice - Increased breakdown of haemoglobin produces excess bilirubin, which deposits in the skin and sclera, causing yellowing.
  • Dark urine - Elevated bilirubin levels or haemoglobinuria (in intravascular haemolysis) can lead to dark-coloured urine.
  • Shortness of breath - Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues causes dyspnoea, particularly on exertion.
  • Palpitations - Tachycardia may develop as a compensatory mechanism for the decreased oxygen supply.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Due to decreased haemoglobin levels, the skin and mucous membranes may appear pale.
  • Splenomegaly - In extravascular haemolysis, the spleen enlarges due to increased RBC destruction.
  • Tachycardia - The heart compensates for anaemia by increasing its rate to maintain adequate tissue perfusion.
  • Scleral icterus - Bilirubin deposits in the sclera, often visible before jaundice appears elsewhere.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia with haemodynamic instability - Can indicate rapid RBC destruction or bone marrow failure, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Dark cola-coloured urine - Suggests significant intravascular haemolysis, which may be associated with acute kidney injury due to haemoglobinuria.
  • Neurological symptoms (confusion, altered mental status) - May indicate hypoxia from severe anaemia or, in cases like paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria, a thrombotic event.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Some cases, especially in mild chronic haemolysis or compensated anaemia, may have minimal or no symptoms and be detected incidentally on blood tests.
  • Misinterpreted symptoms - In elderly patients or those with comorbidities, symptoms like fatigue may be attributed to other causes, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Shows anaemia (low haemoglobin), reticulocytosis (elevated reticulocyte count as the marrow compensates).
  • Peripheral blood smear - May show spherocytes (in hereditary spherocytosis) or schistocytes (in microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia).
  • Unconjugated bilirubin - Raised due to increased RBC breakdown.
  • Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) - Elevated, reflecting tissue breakdown.
  • Haptoglobin - Low or absent in intravascular haemolysis as it binds free haemoglobin.
  • Direct antiglobulin test (DAT) - Positive in autoimmune haemolytic anaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 2-week history of worsening fatigue, jaundice, and dark urine. Blood tests reveal anaemia with reticulocytosis and a positive direct antiglobulin test.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic anaemia - No reticulocytosis; pancytopenia common.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Microcytic hypochromic anaemia, with low serum ferritin and transferrin saturation.
  • Megaloblastic anaemia - Macrocytic anaemia with hypersegmented neutrophils and low vitamin B12 or folate.
  • Thalassaemia - Microcytic anaemia with a characteristic peripheral blood smear showing target cells.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia - Corticosteroids are first-line therapy. In refractory cases, splenectomy or rituximab may be considered.
  • Hereditary spherocytosis - Splenectomy is often performed in severe cases. Folic acid supplementation is recommended.
  • G6PD deficiency - Avoidance of oxidative triggers (e.g., certain drugs, fava beans) is the mainstay of management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemolytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Gallstones - Chronic haemolysis increases bilirubin production, predisposing patients to pigmented gallstones.
  • Acute kidney injury - Intravascular haemolysis can lead to haemoglobinuria, causing renal damage.
  • Cardiac failure - Severe or prolonged anaemia can lead to high-output heart failure.
