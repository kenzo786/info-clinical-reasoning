Hyperprolactinaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Hyperprolactinaemia is a condition characterised by elevated serum prolactin levels, typically above 500 mU/L in females and 450 mU/L in males. Prolactin is a hormone produced by the anterior pituitary gland, primarily responsible for lactation but also involved in reproductive and metabolic processes. The condition can be physiological, pathological, or drug-induced and can lead to menstrual irregularities, infertility, and galactorrhoea.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: 0.4% of adults, more common in women of reproductive age.
  • Hospital prevalence: Hyperprolactinaemia is found in about 9-17% of cases of women presenting with amenorrhoea and infertility in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated to affect 5-10% of women presenting with reproductive issues globally, with a lower prevalence in men.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Prolactin is secreted by lactotroph cells of the anterior pituitary under the inhibitory control of dopamine. Any condition that reduces dopamine levels (e.g., medications, tumours) or increases prolactin release (e.g., pregnancy, primary hypothyroidism) can cause hyperprolactinaemia. Elevated prolactin inhibits gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), leading to decreased levels of luteinising hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which disrupts normal reproductive function. Prolactinomas (benign pituitary tumours) are the most common pathological cause.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset: Hyperprolactinaemia often presents insidiously, with symptoms appearing over weeks to months, depending on the underlying cause (e.g., prolactinoma).
  • Chronic course: If untreated, hyperprolactinaemia can persist long-term, leading to complications such as infertility and bone loss due to chronic hypoestrogenism.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Menstrual irregularities (oligomenorrhoea or amenorrhoea) - Due to prolactin's inhibitory effect on GnRH, leading to decreased LH/FSH and disrupted menstrual cycles.
  • Galactorrhoea - Milk production outside of pregnancy or breastfeeding due to excessive prolactin stimulation of the mammary glands.
  • Infertility - Prolactin suppresses gonadotropins, disrupting ovulation and reducing fertility in women; in men, it may cause hypogonadism and erectile dysfunction.
  • Decreased libido - Both sexes may experience low libido due to the suppression of sex hormones (oestrogen in women and testosterone in men).
  • Headaches - Occurs due to local mass effect in cases of large pituitary adenomas.
  • Visual disturbances - Prolactinomas can compress the optic chiasm, causing bitemporal hemianopia (loss of peripheral vision).
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases of mild hyperprolactinaemia are asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on blood tests.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Galactorrhoea - May be observed upon breast examination, resulting from the stimulatory effects of elevated prolactin on the mammary glands.
  • Bitemporal hemianopia - This visual field defect can be detected on formal visual field testing in cases of macroadenomas compressing the optic chiasm.
  • Signs of hypogonadism - In women: dry skin, breast atrophy, vaginal dryness. In men: testicular atrophy, reduced muscle mass, and sparse body hair, resulting from decreased sex hormones.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe headaches - Can indicate a large pituitary adenoma or a rapidly expanding tumour, which may cause a pituitary apoplexy (sudden haemorrhage).
  • Sudden visual loss - This is a sign of optic chiasm compression, potentially indicating an enlarging prolactinoma.
  • Neurological deficits - May suggest a pituitary tumour invading adjacent structures, such as the cavernous sinus, leading to cranial nerve palsies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Mild hyperprolactinaemia - May be asymptomatic and discovered incidentally, especially in patients on medications such as antipsychotics or those with hypothyroidism.
  • Elderly men - Often present with non-specific symptoms such as fatigue, depression, or erectile dysfunction, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Serum prolactin levels - Elevated, typically >500 mU/L in females and >450 mU/L in males. Extremely high levels (>5000 mU/L) suggest a prolactinoma.
  • Thyroid function tests - Hypothyroidism can cause hyperprolactinaemia, so TSH and free T4 levels are assessed.
  • Pituitary MRI - Used to identify prolactinomas or other pituitary adenomas. Tumours >10 mm are classified as macroadenomas.
  • Visual field testing - Performed in patients with macroadenomas to assess for bitemporal hemianopia due to optic chiasm compression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of amenorrhoea, galactorrhoea, and headaches. Serum prolactin levels are 2,800 mU/L, and MRI reveals a 12 mm pituitary adenoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism - Elevated TSH levels can stimulate prolactin release. Differentiate with TSH and free T4 levels.
  • Drug-induced hyperprolactinaemia - Antipsychotics, metoclopramide, and some antihypertensives can cause elevated prolactin. Medication history helps distinguish this.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) - Can present with menstrual irregularities and elevated prolactin, but also includes hyperandrogenism, acne, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Dopamine agonists - First-line treatment in the UK for prolactinomas, including cabergoline and bromocriptine, which suppress prolactin production and shrink tumours.
  • Surgical resection - Considered in cases of large macroadenomas causing significant visual or neurological impairment, or in patients who fail medical therapy.
  • Thyroid hormone replacement - For patients with hyperprolactinaemia secondary to hypothyroidism, levothyroxine is administered to normalise prolactin levels.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - Long-standing hypoestrogenism in women or low testosterone in men can lead to decreased bone density and fractures.
  • Infertility - Due to prolonged suppression of gonadotropins, leading to chronic anovulation or hypogonadism.
  • Vision loss - Untreated macroadenomas can cause permanent visual field deficits if optic chiasm compression persists.
