Psychogenic Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Psychogenic disorders, also known as functional or somatoform disorders, are conditions where psychological stress or emotional factors lead to physical symptoms without an identifiable organic cause. These symptoms are real and distressing for the patient but are not explained by structural or biochemical abnormalities. Examples include functional neurological symptom disorder (conversion disorder), somatic symptom disorder, and illness anxiety disorder.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 4% of patients seen in GP settings present with symptoms of psychogenic disorders.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Psychogenic disorders account for 10-15% of referrals to neurology outpatient clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by region but estimated to affect 0.1%–0.5% of the general population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of psychogenic disorders is not fully understood. It is hypothesised that psychological stress triggers an exaggerated autonomic response, leading to physical symptoms. Emotional conflicts or unresolved psychological trauma are also thought to be expressed through bodily symptoms. Functional changes in brain areas responsible for emotion regulation, such as the amygdala, and abnormal connectivity between the prefrontal cortex and sensorimotor regions have been observed in some cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms often appear suddenly, usually in response to a stressful or traumatic event.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms may persist for weeks to months, with episodes of symptom fluctuation depending on psychological stressors.
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms that last for more than 6 months, sometimes becoming disabling. Psychological treatment may alleviate symptoms, but they may recur in response to stress.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Weakness or paralysis - Typically affects one limb or one side of the body, reflecting underlying emotional distress rather than neurological damage.
  • Non-epileptic seizures - Episodes resemble epileptic seizures but lack electrical abnormalities in the brain.
  • Chronic pain - Persistent pain without a clear physical cause, often linked to anxiety or depression.
  • Speech disturbances - Dysphonia or aphonia (loss of voice) without organic causes.
  • Gait abnormalities - Difficulty walking or balance issues, typically without evidence of structural abnormalities.
  • Somatic complaints - Patients may report various vague and changing physical complaints, such as fatigue, dizziness, or gastrointestinal upset.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Hoover's sign - Lack of leg extension during hip flexion, suggesting a functional rather than organic weakness.
  • Tremor entrainment test - Inconsistent tremor that changes with distraction, indicating a psychogenic origin.
  • La belle indifférence - A striking lack of concern for significant symptoms (e.g., paralysis), which can suggest a psychogenic disorder.
  • Variable sensory loss - Non-anatomical distribution of sensory deficits, suggesting a psychological rather than organic cause.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of new neurological symptoms - May indicate a genuine neurological event like a stroke and should prompt further investigation.
  • Progressive symptoms - Gradually worsening symptoms suggest an organic cause rather than a psychogenic disorder.
  • Loss of consciousness - If unexplained, this may indicate serious underlying pathology such as cardiac or neurological causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sudden, isolated sensory loss - Often dismissed, but may be related to psychological trauma.
  • Episodes of visual loss - Intermittent and inconsistent, making it difficult to distinguish from genuine ophthalmological disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Neurological examination - Typically normal, but signs such as Hoover’s sign or variability in symptoms help distinguish psychogenic causes.
  • EEG - Normal findings in patients with psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES), distinguishing them from epileptic seizures.
  • MRI or CT scans - Usually performed to rule out organic causes, findings are typically normal in psychogenic disorders.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • A 34-year-old female presents with a 2-week history of left-sided weakness, following a stressful family event. Neurological examination reveals variable strength in the affected limb, and MRI is normal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Sudden onset with clear imaging abnormalities (e.g., infarction) differs from psychogenic disorders, which show no such findings.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Typically presents with demyelination on MRI, whereas psychogenic disorders have normal imaging.
  • Epilepsy - Epileptic seizures show electrical activity on EEG, absent in psychogenic seizures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) - First-line treatment, focusing on addressing underlying psychological stressors and maladaptive thinking patterns.
  • Physiotherapy - Helps with functional symptoms like gait abnormalities and encourages return to normal activities.
  • Antidepressants - Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) may be used if symptoms are associated with depression or anxiety.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Psychogenic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Disability - If untreated, symptoms can become chronic and disabling, leading to social and occupational impairment.
  • Depression - Ongoing physical symptoms often lead to secondary depression and anxiety disorders.
  • Social isolation - Patients may withdraw from social situations due to fear of triggering symptoms.
