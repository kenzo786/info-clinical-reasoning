HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP) is a serious opportunistic infection caused by the fungus Pneumocystis jirovecii, primarily affecting individuals with weakened immune systems, especially those with advanced HIV infection (CD4 count <200 cells/µL).
  • It is characterised by a subacute onset of dyspnoea, non-productive cough, and fever, often leading to respiratory failure if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Rare in the general population, primarily seen in individuals with undiagnosed or untreated HIV/AIDS.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Common in patients with advanced HIV presenting to hospital; approximately 10-20% of HIV-related hospital admissions involve PCP.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Common in regions with limited access to antiretroviral therapy (ART); up to 30-40% of untreated HIV patients develop PCP.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Immune Suppression: CD4 lymphocyte depletion in HIV leads to impaired defence against P. jirovecii.
  • Alveolar Involvement: The fungus proliferates within alveoli, causing inflammation and alveolar damage.
  • Impaired Gas Exchange: Proteinaceous exudate and fungal debris accumulate, reducing oxygen diffusion and leading to hypoxia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 4-8 weeks following exposure in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Early Phase: Gradual onset of symptoms over days to weeks, including mild dyspnoea and low-grade fever.
  • Late Phase: Progression to severe dyspnoea, respiratory failure, and hypoxaemia if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea: Progressive shortness of breath due to impaired oxygen diffusion caused by alveolar exudates.
  • Non-productive cough: Likely due to irritation of alveolar walls without significant mucus production.
  • Fever: Reflects the systemic inflammatory response to fungal infection.
  • Fatigue: Resulting from hypoxaemia and systemic inflammation.
  • Chest pain (rare): Pleuritic chest pain may occur with severe inflammation or pneumothorax.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Hypoxaemia: Low oxygen saturation due to impaired alveolar gas exchange.
  • Tachypnoea: Compensatory mechanism for hypoxaemia.
  • Bilateral crackles: Reflecting alveolar involvement and inflammation.
  • Cyanosis (late): Indicates severe hypoxaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Severe hypoxaemia (PaO₂ <60 mmHg): Suggests impending respiratory failure.
  • Altered mental state: Could indicate respiratory failure or systemic complications.
  • Haemoptysis: May signal pulmonary haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Hypoxaemia: Seen in some cases; only identified during exertion or through pulse oximetry.
  • Extrapulmonary Disease: Rarely, the fungus may spread to lymph nodes or other organs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray: Bilateral interstitial infiltrates; may appear normal early on.
  • High-Resolution CT: Ground-glass opacities typical of PCP.
  • Blood Tests: Elevated LDH, reflecting cellular damage.
  • Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL): Identification of P. jirovecii using PCR or silver staining.
  • Arterial Blood Gas (ABG): Hypoxaemia with a widened alveolar-arterial gradient.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]

A 35-year-old male with untreated HIV presents with a 3-week history of progressive dyspnoea, dry cough, and fever. Examination reveals tachypnoea, hypoxaemia, and bilateral crackles on auscultation.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Bacterial Pneumonia: Acute onset, productive cough, focal consolidation on imaging.
  • Tuberculosis: Chronic symptoms, night sweats, cavitation on imaging.
  • Cryptococcal Pneumonia: Associated with CNS symptoms, nodular infiltrates.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • First-Line: High-dose co-trimoxazole (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) for 21 days.
  • Adjunctive Therapy: Corticosteroids if PaO₂ <70 mmHg or A-a gradient >35 mmHg.
  • Prophylaxis: Low-dose co-trimoxazole for individuals with CD4 count <200 cells/µL.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="HIV-Associated Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory Failure: Due to severe hypoxaemia.
  • Pneumothorax: Resulting from alveolar rupture.
  • Death: High mortality if untreated, particularly in severely immunocompromised patients.
