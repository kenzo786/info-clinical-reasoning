Delirium
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention, awareness, and cognition, commonly occurring in hospitalised patients. It involves altered mental status and typically develops over hours to days, often triggered by underlying medical issues or environmental factors. Delirium is characterised by cognitive impairment, disorientation, and sometimes psychotic symptoms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • In the UK general practice population, delirium affects approximately 1-2% of individuals annually.
  • In UK hospital settings, delirium prevalence is higher, affecting around 10-30% of older inpatients, particularly those over 65 years.
  • Globally, delirium affects 20-40% of elderly hospitalised patients, especially in intensive care units and post-operative wards.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium arises from disrupted neurotransmitter systems, notably acetylcholine and dopamine imbalances. Reduced acetylcholine, often due to systemic inflammation or hypoxia, affects cortical function, while increased dopamine activity contributes to hallucinations and agitation.
  • Inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6, TNF-α) released during infections or surgery can alter the blood-brain barrier, exacerbating neural disruptions.
  • Impaired cerebral perfusion, metabolic disturbances, or acute changes in the internal environment (e.g., electrolyte imbalance) further disrupt neuronal communication.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Onset: Develops rapidly over hours to days, often triggered by acute stressors or medical conditions.
  • Course: Typically fluctuates throughout the day, with symptoms worsening at night (sundowning).
  • Resolution: Can resolve within days with appropriate treatment, but may persist for weeks or even months, especially in older adults.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Confusion - Fluctuating levels of attention and disorientation due to disrupted cortical function.
  • Inattention - Difficulty focusing on simple tasks as a result of impaired acetylcholine pathways affecting attention regulation.
  • Hallucinations - Primarily visual, due to excess dopamine activity in the limbic system.
  • Agitation or Restlessness - Driven by imbalances in dopamine and reduced cortical control.
  • Sleep-Wake Disturbances - Often manifested as worsening of symptoms at night (sundowning), influenced by circadian rhythm dysregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Disorientation - Patients often struggle with knowing time, place, or even personal details, linked to disrupted cortical function.
  • Reduced Awareness - Decreased awareness of surroundings due to impaired neurotransmitter activity.
  • Altered Psychomotor Activity - Hyperactivity or hypoactivity; hyperactivity is linked with dopamine excess, hypoactivity with sedative medications or severe metabolic disturbances.
  • Incoherent Speech - Irregular speech patterns result from cognitive disruption, often related to fluctuating arousal levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Rapid Onset - Sudden onset of symptoms suggests an acute trigger, such as infection or metabolic derangement.
  • Severe Agitation or Violence - Extreme behavioural disturbance may indicate underlying hypoxia or drug withdrawal.
  • Hallucinations with Paranoia - Risk of self-harm or harm to others; commonly associated with severe metabolic or toxic causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Hypoactive Delirium - Presents with drowsiness, lethargy, and reduced interaction, often mistaken for depression, especially in older adults.
  • Subsyndromal Delirium - Milder symptoms without full diagnostic criteria, which may evolve into full delirium if untreated.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May reveal infection (elevated WBC) or anaemia, both common triggers of delirium.
  • Urea and Electrolytes (U&Es) - Electrolyte imbalances can precipitate delirium by altering neural conductivity.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) and Ammonia - Raised ammonia in liver failure can lead to hepatic encephalopathy.
  • CT/MRI of the Brain - To exclude structural causes, e.g., stroke, that could contribute to cognitive disturbances.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • An 82-year-old woman presents with sudden confusion, disorientation, and agitation after recent hospital admission for pneumonia, with symptoms fluctuating throughout the day.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Dementia - Gradual onset and progressive, without the acute fluctuations typical of delirium.
  • Acute Psychosis - Typically lacks inattention and fluctuating course seen in delirium.
  • Depression - Hypoactive delirium may mimic depression, but delirium presents with fluctuating attention.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Identify and Treat Underlying Causes - Treat infections, correct electrolyte disturbances, or manage hypoxia to address the root cause.
  • Environmental Modifications - Provide clocks, calendars, and familiar objects to help orientation.
  • Medication Management - Avoid anticholinergics and sedatives; use low-dose haloperidol or atypical antipsychotics only if the patient is severely agitated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Increased Mortality - Delirium is associated with a higher risk of death, especially if untreated or due to severe illness.
  • Functional Decline - Older adults often experience prolonged functional impairment post-delirium, potentially leading to institutionalisation.
  • Increased Risk of Dementia - Acute delirium episodes are linked to accelerated cognitive decline in vulnerable populations.
