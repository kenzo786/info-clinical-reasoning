Broad Complex Tachycardia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Broad complex tachycardia (BCT) is defined as a tachyarrhythmia with a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute (bpm) and a QRS duration of 120 ms or more on an ECG. It typically arises from ventricular ectopic beats or aberrant conduction through the ventricles, indicating serious underlying heart disease or arrhythmic disorder.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • BCT is most commonly seen in older adults and those with pre-existing structural heart disease. It is less common in the general population but is more frequent in hospital settings due to its association with myocardial infarction, heart failure, and post-surgical conditions. Its global incidence is not well-defined but accounts for 80% of all sustained tachycardias.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • The broad QRS complex arises due to delayed conduction through the ventricles. This occurs either from a ventricular origin (as in ventricular tachycardia) or aberrant conduction through diseased parts of the conduction system (as in bundle branch block). Triggers include myocardial ischaemia, electrolyte imbalances, or re-entrant circuits in scarred myocardium. If untreated, it can lead to haemodynamic instability or progress to ventricular fibrillation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • BCT can have an acute onset, especially in the context of myocardial infarction or electrolyte disturbances. It may present as intermittent episodes (paroxysmal) or sustained arrhythmias. Without timely treatment, it can evolve rapidly into life-threatening conditions like ventricular fibrillation within minutes to hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Most patients feel a rapid and irregular heartbeat due to ventricular tachycardia or aberrant conduction.
  • Syncope - Caused by reduced cerebral perfusion during sustained arrhythmia leading to a brief loss of consciousness.
  • Dyspnoea - The decreased cardiac output reduces oxygen delivery, resulting in shortness of breath.
  • Chest pain - Ischaemia due to increased myocardial oxygen demand or underlying coronary artery disease may manifest as chest pain.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness - This is due to transient hypotension or poor perfusion of the brain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia (>100 bpm) - The defining feature of BCT due to rapid ventricular depolarisation.
  • Hypotension - A result of reduced stroke volume and cardiac output during prolonged arrhythmia.
  • Jugular venous distension (JVD) - Reflects increased central venous pressure due to impaired cardiac filling during arrhythmia.
  • Cold, clammy extremities - Indicative of poor peripheral perfusion caused by the low cardiac output state.
  • Absent or weak peripheral pulses - Occurs when the arrhythmia leads to reduced cardiac output, making pulses difficult to palpate.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension, altered mental state) - This suggests poor end-organ perfusion and may require immediate cardioversion to prevent cardiac arrest.
  • Polymorphic broad complex tachycardia (Torsades de Pointes) - This is associated with QT prolongation and can quickly degenerate into ventricular fibrillation.
  • Chest pain with ST-segment changes - Indicates myocardial ischaemia, potentially complicating into a full myocardial infarction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with generalised weakness or fatigue, especially in the elderly. Asymptomatic presentations occur, particularly in patients with implanted devices like pacemakers. Symptoms may be missed in individuals with underlying heart failure who already have reduced exercise tolerance and dyspnoea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • 12-lead ECG - Shows broad QRS complexes (>120 ms) with a rate >100 bpm. A regular rhythm suggests ventricular tachycardia, while irregular rhythm could indicate atrial fibrillation with aberrant conduction.
  • Serum electrolytes - Hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, or hyperkalaemia may predispose to arrhythmias, especially in those on diuretics or with renal dysfunction.
  • Cardiac enzymes (e.g., troponins) - Elevated levels may suggest myocardial infarction as a cause or complication of the arrhythmia.
  • Imaging (echocardiogram) - Useful for assessing structural heart disease, such as dilated ventricles or regional wall motion abnormalities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of coronary artery disease and heart failure presents with palpitations, dizziness, and dyspnoea over the past two hours. ECG shows broad complex tachycardia at 140 bpm, with a blood pressure of 85/60 mmHg.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Supraventricular tachycardia with aberrancy - May present similarly but is usually initiated by a supraventricular focus; QRS morphology often shows bundle branch block.
  • Torsades de Pointes - Characterised by a twisting morphology on ECG and associated with QT prolongation.
  • Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response - Irregularly irregular rhythm with varying QRS duration, typically presenting in patients with pre-existing bundle branch block.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Immediate treatment: In haemodynamically unstable patients, immediate synchronised cardioversion is required. Intravenous amiodarone or procainamide may be used in more stable patients.
  • Long-term management: Addressing underlying causes (e.g., myocardial ischaemia, electrolyte imbalances) is essential. Antiarrhythmic drugs like beta-blockers or amiodarone may be indicated for long-term rhythm control. Patients may require implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs) for recurrent, life-threatening arrhythmias.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Ventricular fibrillation - Without intervention, BCT can deteriorate into ventricular fibrillation, a life-threatening arrhythmia leading to cardiac arrest.
  • Cardiac arrest - Prolonged untreated BCT leads to poor cardiac output, ultimately resulting in arrest.
  • Heart failure exacerbation - Chronic tachyarrhythmias worsen pre-existing heart failure by reducing ventricular filling time and increasing myocardial oxygen demand.
