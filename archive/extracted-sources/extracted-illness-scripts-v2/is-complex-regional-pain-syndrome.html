Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) is a chronic pain condition characterised by regional pain that is disproportionate to any inciting event, often accompanied by sensory, motor, autonomic, and trophic abnormalities. It is subdivided into Type 1 (no confirmed nerve injury) and Type 2 (associated with confirmed nerve injury).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Approximately 1 in 4,000 patients annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Occurs in 5-7% of patients following fractures or surgery.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimates range from 0.005% to 0.01% of the general population.
  • More common in women, with a peak incidence between 40 and 60 years of age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Initial injury or trauma (e.g., fracture, surgery) triggers nociceptive pain.
  • Maladaptive central and peripheral sensitisation lead to an exaggerated pain response.
  • Autonomic dysregulation causes vasomotor changes and swelling.
  • Persistent inflammation and altered immune responses contribute to symptoms.
  • Neuroplastic changes in the central nervous system exacerbate chronic pain and sensory disturbances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (0-3 months): Pain, swelling, redness, warmth, and hypersensitivity.
  • Dystrophic Phase (3-6 months): Progressive swelling, coolness, mottling, and stiff joints.
  • Chronic Phase (>6 months): Atrophic changes, decreased function, and potential deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Severe pain - Disproportionate to any preceding injury; due to central sensitisation and autonomic dysregulation.
  • Burning sensation - A neuropathic component linked to peripheral nerve involvement.
  • Swelling - Caused by vasomotor changes and inflammatory mediators.
  • Stiffness - Resulting from immobility and joint changes.
  • Temperature changes - Autonomic dysfunction leads to hyperthermia or hypothermia in the affected region.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Allodynia - Pain response to non-painful stimuli; due to sensitisation of peripheral and central pain pathways.
  • Hyperalgesia - Exaggerated pain response; linked to central sensitisation.
  • Skin colour changes - Mottling or pallor due to autonomic dysregulation.
  • Decreased range of motion - Secondary to pain and joint contractures.
  • Trophic changes - Skin thinning, hair loss, and brittle nails due to chronic autonomic changes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Severe and progressive pain - May indicate unchecked disease progression or secondary complications like infection.
  • Sudden worsening of symptoms - Suggests possible nerve compression or secondary injury.
  • Signs of systemic infection - Fever or cellulitis might indicate complications requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Pain without autonomic features - May lead to misdiagnosis as non-specific chronic pain.
  • Bilateral symptoms - Rare but can occur, complicating differentiation from systemic conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Three-phase bone scan - Shows increased uptake in the affected limb, indicating inflammation.
  • X-rays - May reveal osteopenia in chronic phases.
  • Thermography - Demonstrates asymmetry in skin temperature due to autonomic dysregulation.
  • Blood tests - Typically normal; used to exclude infection or inflammatory arthritis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with severe burning pain, swelling, and temperature changes in her right arm three months after a wrist fracture.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Post-traumatic arthritis - Less pronounced autonomic and sensory changes.
  • Infective arthritis - Presence of systemic symptoms like fever and raised inflammatory markers.
  • Neuropathy - Typically follows a specific nerve distribution.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Physiotherapy - Early mobilisation to maintain function and prevent stiffness.
  • Medications - Neuropathic pain agents (e.g., gabapentin, amitriptyline); NSAIDs for inflammation.
  • Psychological support - Cognitive behavioural therapy for chronic pain management.
  • Interventional therapies - Sympathetic nerve blocks in refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and disability - Due to persistent sensitisation and trophic changes.
  • Joint contractures - Result from immobility and fibrosis.
  • Psychological sequelae - Depression and anxiety are common in long-term cases.
