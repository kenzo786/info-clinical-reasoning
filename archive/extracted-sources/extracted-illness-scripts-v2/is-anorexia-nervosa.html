Anorexia Nervosa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Anorexia nervosa is a severe mental health disorder characterised by a persistent restriction of energy intake, intense fear of gaining weight or becoming fat, and a distorted body image, leading to significantly low body weight. It primarily affects adolescents and young adults and is associated with severe physical and psychological morbidity.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: 0.3-0.5% of females and 0.1% of males.
  • UK hospital admissions: 1-2% of all hospital admissions for eating disorders.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 1% of adolescent females, significantly lower in males (around 0.1-0.3%).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Anorexia nervosa involves psychological, biological, and sociocultural factors. The pathophysiology includes dysregulation in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, abnormal secretion of ghrelin and leptin (appetite hormones), and neurotransmitter imbalances (serotonin, dopamine). Chronic starvation leads to severe malnutrition, affecting nearly all organ systems, particularly the cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, and endocrine systems.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically during adolescence or early adulthood. The early phase often includes subtle weight loss and increased attention to food, dieting, and body image.
  • Progression: Gradual and insidious, with increasing restriction of food intake, excessive exercise, and further psychological decline.
  • Chronicity: The disorder may persist for years without treatment, with fluctuating periods of remission and relapse.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - A primary symptom due to persistent caloric restriction and fear of weight gain. Pathophysiology includes metabolic slowing and fat and muscle catabolism.
  • Amenorrhoea - Common in females due to reduced body fat and hypothalamic suppression, leading to decreased gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH).
  • Fatigue - Caused by inadequate nutrition, dehydration, and anaemia secondary to malnutrition.
  • Cold intolerance - Results from decreased fat stores and impaired thermoregulation due to low energy availability.
  • Preoccupation with food - Pathological obsession with food, cooking, and body image due to psychological factors.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia - A common sign due to malnutrition and decreased metabolic demand. Pathophysiology involves reduced sympathetic activity and vagal hyperactivity.
  • Hypotension - Results from dehydration, electrolyte disturbances, and malnutrition.
  • Lanugo hair - Fine body hair that develops as a compensatory mechanism for heat loss due to lack of subcutaneous fat.
  • Dry skin - A manifestation of dehydration and malnutrition.
  • Peripheral oedema - Occurs due to hypoalbuminaemia from protein malnutrition, which causes decreased oncotic pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Often signify electrolyte imbalances (particularly hypokalaemia), which can lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • Severe bradycardia (<40 bpm) - Indicates profound malnutrition and increased risk of cardiovascular collapse.
  • Severe hypotension - Signals dehydration and cardiovascular instability, possibly leading to shock.
  • Electrolyte disturbances - Particularly hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia, which may lead to life-threatening complications such as cardiac arrest or seizures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Atypical anorexia nervosa can occur in individuals who meet all criteria for anorexia except for being underweight. They still face similar risks of cardiovascular and metabolic complications despite not presenting with obvious cachexia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia, leucopenia, or thrombocytopenia due to bone marrow suppression from malnutrition.
  • Electrolytes - Hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, or hypochloraemia may be present due to dehydration or purging behaviours.
  • ECG - Bradycardia, QT prolongation, or arrhythmias are common due to electrolyte disturbances and malnutrition.
  • Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs) - Often reveal low T3 and T4 (euthyroid sick syndrome), reflecting the body’s adaptation to conserve energy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • A 17-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of significant weight loss, amenorrhoea, cold intolerance, and preoccupation with body image. BMI is 16 kg/m², with signs of bradycardia and lanugo hair.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa - Characterised by binge eating followed by purging, but typically patients are normal weight or overweight.
  • Depression - May lead to weight loss and poor appetite but lacks the intense fear of weight gain or body image distortion.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Weight loss and increased metabolism are common, but patients exhibit increased appetite and often present with tachycardia and tremors.
  • Malignancy - Weight loss and fatigue are common but usually associated with systemic symptoms like night sweats, lymphadenopathy, or palpable masses.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Psychotherapy - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is first-line treatment, focusing on normalising eating patterns and addressing distorted thoughts about weight and body image.
  • Nutritional support - Gradual refeeding with close monitoring of electrolytes to prevent refeeding syndrome. In severe cases, nasogastric feeding may be necessary.
  • Medical management - Address complications like electrolyte imbalances, and in severe cases, admission to a specialist eating disorder unit may be required.
  • Pharmacotherapy - Limited evidence for pharmacological treatment, though SSRIs may be used to treat coexisting depression or anxiety.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Refeeding syndrome - Life-threatening condition triggered by rapid refeeding, leading to severe electrolyte imbalances (hypophosphataemia, hypokalaemia).
  • Cardiovascular collapse - Due to severe bradycardia, hypotension, and arrhythmias from malnutrition.
  • Osteoporosis - Chronic malnutrition and oestrogen deficiency lead to reduced bone density and increased fracture risk.
  • Infertility - Secondary to prolonged amenorrhoea and hormonal disruption.
