Sepsis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis is a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. It is defined as an infection complicated by acute organ dysfunction, often characterised by systemic inflammation, hypotension, and multi-organ failure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Sepsis is relatively rare in primary care, with a prevalence of <1% of consultations.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Sepsis accounts for 2-6% of hospital admissions and is a leading cause of ICU admissions.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, there are an estimated 49 million cases of sepsis annually, resulting in approximately 11 million deaths.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis begins with an infection, often bacterial, fungal, or viral. The invading pathogen triggers the release of inflammatory mediators, such as cytokines, which promote vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and leukocyte activation.
  • The immune response becomes dysregulated, leading to widespread endothelial damage, capillary leakage, and microvascular thrombosis. This results in impaired oxygen delivery to tissues and subsequent organ dysfunction.
  • The cardiovascular response is marked by systemic vasodilation, reduced vascular resistance, and hypotension. If untreated, this can lead to septic shock, multi-organ failure, and death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Early signs include fever, tachycardia, and mild hypotension, often within hours to days of infection.
  • Progressive Phase: Over 12-24 hours, organ dysfunction develops, with altered mental status, renal impairment, or respiratory distress.
  • Late Phase: Septic shock may ensue within 24-48 hours, characterised by persistent hypotension, multi-organ failure, and increased mortality risk.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Fever or hypothermia - Fever occurs due to the release of pyrogens from inflammatory cells, while hypothermia may indicate a severe, overwhelming response in the elderly or immunocompromised.
  • Tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - Increased respiratory rate is a compensatory mechanism for metabolic acidosis caused by lactic acid build-up from poor tissue perfusion.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - This results from cerebral hypoperfusion and inflammation, reflecting acute organ dysfunction.
  • Fatigue or malaise - Systemic inflammation leads to cytokine release, causing general malaise and weakness.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Gastrointestinal dysfunction from poor perfusion or direct involvement of the GI tract by infection can cause these symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Increased heart rate is a compensatory mechanism for maintaining cardiac output in response to hypotension and hypoxia.
  • Hypotension - Systemic vasodilation from inflammatory mediators leads to decreased systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure.
  • Oliguria - Reduced urine output is a sign of renal hypoperfusion and early renal dysfunction.
  • Cold, clammy skin - In later stages, peripheral vasoconstriction occurs to preserve blood flow to vital organs, causing cold extremities.
  • Jaundice - Liver dysfunction due to hypoperfusion or direct septic injury can lead to hyperbilirubinaemia and jaundice.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension unresponsive to fluids - This indicates septic shock, a critical stage where multi-organ failure is imminent.
  • Altered mental status - Rapid changes in cognition or consciousness suggest acute cerebral hypoperfusion and are predictive of poor outcomes.
  • Oliguria - Signifies severe renal impairment due to prolonged hypoperfusion, a key marker of organ failure.
  • Respiratory distress - Indicates acute respiratory failure, often a precursor to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Afebrile sepsis - Elderly or immunocompromised patients may present without fever due to a blunted immune response, increasing the risk of delayed diagnosis.
  • Hypothermia in the elderly - Instead of fever, older patients may have low body temperature, signifying a poor prognostic factor.
  • Isolated organ dysfunction - Some patients may present with single-organ dysfunction, such as altered mental status or renal failure, without systemic signs of sepsis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Leukocytosis or leukopenia, reflecting the immune response. In severe cases, neutropenia may occur due to overwhelming sepsis.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) and Procalcitonin - Elevated markers of inflammation and bacterial infection.
  • Serum lactate - Elevated lactate indicates tissue hypoxia and poor perfusion, a key marker of sepsis severity.
  • Blood cultures - Positive cultures help identify the causative organism, essential for targeted antibiotic therapy.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - May show metabolic acidosis with a raised lactate level, reflecting systemic hypoperfusion.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of diabetes presents with fever, confusion, and shortness of breath for 24 hours. On examination, he is hypotensive, tachycardic, and oliguric, with elevated lactate and CRP levels.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - Presents with severe abdominal pain and elevated lipase, whereas sepsis typically has more generalised symptoms.
  • Pneumonia - May present with fever and tachypnoea, but localising chest signs (e.g. crackles) are more prominent.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Can cause hypotension and tachycardia but lacks the systemic inflammatory response seen in sepsis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Initial resuscitation - Administer IV fluids (30 ml/kg) within 3 hours to correct hypoperfusion.
  • Antibiotics - Broad-spectrum antibiotics should be started within 1 hour of suspected sepsis, targeting likely pathogens until culture results are available (NICE guidelines recommend empiric therapy based on local resistance patterns).
  • Vasopressors - If hypotension persists despite fluid resuscitation, start vasopressors (e.g., noradrenaline) to maintain a MAP ≥65 mmHg.
  • Source control - Remove or drain any infection source (e.g., abscess drainage, infected device removal).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Septic shock - Characterised by refractory hypotension, it occurs in approximately 30-50% of sepsis cases and significantly worsens the prognosis.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) - Inflammatory damage to the lungs can lead to severe hypoxaemia and is a common cause of death in sepsis.
  • Multi-organ failure - Prolonged hypoperfusion can result in irreversible damage to the kidneys, liver, lungs, and heart.
  • Death - Mortality rates for septic shock range from 30-50%, depending on the speed of recognition and treatment.
