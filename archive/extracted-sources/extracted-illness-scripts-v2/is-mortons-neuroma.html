Morton's Neuroma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Morton's neuroma is a benign, painful condition characterised by fibrous thickening of the interdigital nerve, most commonly between the third and fourth metatarsal heads. It is not a true tumour but rather a neuropathic lesion associated with repetitive mechanical irritation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 30-50 cases per 100,000 population annually.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Higher in orthopaedic or podiatric clinics, particularly among individuals aged 40–60 years.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to the UK, with a slightly higher prevalence in populations wearing constrictive footwear.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Repeated trauma or compression leads to perineural fibrosis and thickening of the nerve sheath.
  • This often occurs due to mechanical stress, such as wearing tight shoes or engaging in activities involving repetitive forefoot pressure.
  • Fibrosis irritates the nerve, resulting in neuropathic pain and sensory disturbances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Often asymptomatic or presenting with mild discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual onset of burning pain and paraesthesia in the affected interdigital space.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent pain exacerbated by weight-bearing activities and relieved by rest or shoe removal.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Burning pain in the forefoot - Caused by nerve irritation and inflammation in the affected intermetatarsal space.
  • Paraesthesia - Tingling or numbness radiating to the toes due to compression of the interdigital nerve.
  • Sensation of a lump or "pebble in the shoe" - Reflects localised swelling and fibrosis.
  • Symptom relief on removing tight shoes - Indicates a mechanical component to symptom exacerbation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Tenderness on palpation of the intermetatarsal space - Indicative of localised inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Mulder’s click - A palpable or audible click when the metatarsal heads are compressed, caused by the thickened nerve moving between the bones.
  • Exacerbation of symptoms with compression - Confirms the role of mechanical irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive pain or swelling - Could indicate a malignancy or infection.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - May suggest an alternative diagnosis such as a systemic inflammatory condition or cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Isolated paraesthesia without pain, often mistaken for peripheral neuropathy.
  • Symptoms bilaterally, which can mimic systemic neuropathic conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Mulder’s test and palpation are diagnostic.
  • Ultrasound - Identifies nerve thickening and surrounding inflammation.
  • MRI - Useful for ruling out other causes like tumours or joint pathology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of burning forefoot pain, worsened by wearing heels and relieved by removing shoes, with paraesthesia radiating to the fourth toe.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Metatarsalgia - Pain without nerve involvement; lacks paraesthesia and Mulder’s sign.
  • Stress fracture - Localised tenderness and radiographic changes on X-ray.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Bilateral, diffuse symptoms, often associated with systemic conditions like diabetes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Conservative - Footwear modification (wide-toed shoes, padding), physiotherapy.
  • Pharmacological - NSAIDs for pain relief, corticosteroid injections.
  • Surgical - Neurectomy for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - From untreated or severe cases.
  • Neuropathy - Due to surgical complications.
  • Recurrence - Can occur post-surgery in up to 20% of cases.
