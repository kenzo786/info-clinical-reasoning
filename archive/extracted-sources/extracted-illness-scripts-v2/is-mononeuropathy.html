Mononeuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Mononeuropathy refers to a condition in which there is damage to a single peripheral nerve, leading to specific motor, sensory, or autonomic dysfunction. It results in localised symptoms based on the affected nerve and is commonly due to trauma, compression, or entrapment, but can also be caused by inflammation or ischaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of mononeuropathy in UK GP settings is estimated at 1-2% of patients presenting with neurological complaints, with carpal tunnel syndrome being the most common form. Hospital prevalence may be higher due to trauma-related cases. Worldwide, mononeuropathy vary in prevalence depending on occupational risks and trauma exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Mononeuropathy occurs when a single nerve is injured, often through direct trauma, compression (e.g., from tumours or anatomical narrowing), or ischaemia (e.g., from vascular compromise). Compression and trauma can lead to demyelination of the nerve or axonal damage, which affects the nerve's ability to transmit electrical signals effectively. In cases like carpal tunnel syndrome, repetitive stress leads to compression, resulting in ischaemia and nerve dysfunction. Inflammatory mononeuropathy, such as Bell's palsy, involve immune-mediated damage to the nerve.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • The time course of mononeuropathy can vary: acute forms (e.g., traumatic nerve injury) develop over hours to days, whereas chronic forms (e.g., carpal tunnel syndrome) may progress over months to years. Inflammatory mononeuropathy like Bell’s palsy can present abruptly and recover over weeks to months, with or without residual deficits.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Numbness or tingling in a specific distribution - Due to sensory nerve involvement, leading to altered sensory perception in the affected area.
  • Weakness in muscles supplied by the affected nerve - Occurs due to disruption of motor nerve signals, leading to muscle weakness or paralysis in severe cases.
  • Pain - Neuropathic pain may occur due to damage or irritation of the nerve, often described as burning, sharp, or shooting pain.
  • Muscle atrophy - In chronic cases, muscle wasting may occur due to prolonged nerve dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Sensory loss in the nerve distribution - Specific areas of numbness or loss of sensation, based on which nerve is affected.
  • Weakness or paralysis of specific muscles - Inability to perform certain movements related to muscles controlled by the damaged nerve.
  • Tinel’s sign - Percussion over the affected nerve produces tingling or electric shock-like sensation, especially in compressive neuropathies like carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • Muscle wasting - Observable reduction in muscle bulk in long-standing cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressing weakness - May indicate nerve entrapment that is worsening or a more serious underlying cause such as a mass lesion.
  • Severe or intractable pain - Suggests nerve ischaemia or possible compression by a tumour.
  • Motor or sensory deficits in multiple areas - This may indicate a polyneuropathy rather than a mononeuropathy, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Painless motor weakness - In some cases of entrapment neuropathies, there may be minimal pain but significant motor dysfunction, which can lead to delayed diagnosis.
  • Intermittent symptoms - Symptoms like tingling or weakness may come and go initially, which can be confused with other intermittent conditions like multiple sclerosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) - Shows delayed signal conduction in the affected nerve due to demyelination or axonal damage.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Demonstrates denervation of muscles innervated by the affected nerve, indicating nerve damage.
  • MRI or ultrasound of the affected region - Can reveal structural causes of nerve compression, such as a tumour or entrapment in an anatomical space.
  • Blood tests - May be performed to rule out systemic causes of neuropathy, such as diabetes or vasculitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of intermittent numbness and tingling in the right hand, associated with weakness in the thumb. Symptoms worsen at night, suggestive of carpal tunnel syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathy - Involves nerve root compression, which presents with pain and sensory loss in a dermatomal pattern, differing from the focal distribution of mononeuropathy.
  • Polyneuropathy - Generalised nerve damage affecting multiple nerves, typically presents bilaterally and symmetrically, unlike mononeuropathy.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Can present with intermittent neurological deficits but involves the central nervous system rather than peripheral nerves.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Includes rest, splinting (e.g., wrist splint for carpal tunnel), and avoidance of repetitive movements.
  • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) - For pain management and reduction of inflammation around the nerve.
  • Corticosteroid injections - Used in cases of severe nerve compression, such as in carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • Surgical decompression - Required if conservative measures fail, particularly for entrapment neuropathies like carpal tunnel or ulnar neuropathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mononeuropathy"][/sc]
  • Permanent motor or sensory loss - Due to prolonged or severe nerve damage, which can become irreversible if not treated early.
  • Muscle atrophy - Chronic nerve damage can lead to significant muscle wasting, particularly in the hands or feet depending on the nerve affected.
  • Chronic neuropathic pain - Persistent nerve injury can result in long-term pain that is difficult to manage.
