Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Acute ulcerative gingivitis (AUG), also known as necrotising ulcerative gingivitis (NUG), is a severe and rapidly progressive infection of the gums characterised by gingival inflammation, ulceration, and necrosis. It primarily affects the gingival tissues and can lead to tissue destruction if left untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Relatively uncommon, with incidence higher in individuals with poor oral hygiene, smokers, and those under stress.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Rarely leads to hospital admissions, usually managed in primary care or dental settings unless severe.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher in low-income countries, particularly in areas with poor access to dental care and malnutrition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • AUG is primarily caused by the overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria, particularly Fusobacterium nucleatum and Treponema species. These bacteria thrive in the gingival sulcus, where poor oral hygiene, malnutrition, stress, and immunosuppression create an ideal environment for their proliferation. The bacterial toxins, combined with a host immune response, lead to the breakdown of the gingival tissue, resulting in necrosis and ulceration. The condition can progress to involve the periodontal ligament and alveolar bone in more severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The condition can develop rapidly over 2-3 days with the onset of pain, inflammation, and gum bleeding.
  • Acute phase: Ulceration and necrosis of the gingival tissues occur, often within a week.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, the infection may persist and progress to periodontitis, involving deeper tissues and potentially leading to tooth loss and systemic complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Severe gum pain - Due to the destruction of gingival tissues and ulceration, causing localised inflammation and irritation of sensory nerves.
  • Bleeding gums - Resulting from necrosis and ulceration of the gingival tissue, which disrupts the integrity of blood vessels.
  • Halitosis (bad breath) - Caused by bacterial overgrowth and tissue necrosis, leading to the release of foul-smelling volatile sulphur compounds.
  • Metallic taste - Thought to be due to a combination of necrotic tissue and bacterial by-products affecting the taste receptors.
  • Fever and malaise (less common) - Systemic symptoms may occur in more severe cases due to infection spreading beyond local tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Gingival ulceration - Ulcers with a greyish or pseudomembranous covering, often with sharply defined borders due to necrosis.
  • Gingival erythema - Redness of the gums due to inflammation caused by bacterial invasion and the immune response.
  • Gingival bleeding on touch - Spontaneous or provoked bleeding due to compromised blood vessels in the necrotic gingival tissue.
  • Fetor oris (foul-smelling breath) - A characteristic sign of AUG, reflecting the bacterial overgrowth and tissue breakdown.
  • Pseudomembrane formation - A grey, necrotic covering over the ulcers which can slough off, leaving raw, bleeding tissue beneath.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Fever - May indicate systemic involvement or a more severe infection such as necrotising periodontitis.
  • Facial swelling - Can suggest spread of infection to deeper tissues, such as cellulitis or an abscess, which may require urgent intervention.
  • Dysphagia or trismus - Difficulty swallowing or jaw stiffness may suggest deeper infection spread, such as Ludwig’s angina, a life-threatening condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - In some cases, particularly in immunocompromised individuals, symptoms may be mild or absent despite significant tissue damage.
  • Chronic, low-grade symptoms - Instead of the acute onset, patients may present with persistent gingival discomfort and intermittent bleeding.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on the visualisation of necrotic ulcers, pseudomembrane, and associated signs of infection.
  • Microbial culture: Not routinely required but may be done in cases of treatment failure to identify resistant or unusual organisms.
  • Full blood count (FBC): May show elevated white cell count if systemic infection is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of severe gum pain, bleeding, halitosis, and gum ulceration. He is a smoker and reports significant stress due to upcoming exams.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Herpetic gingivostomatitis: Differentiated by the presence of multiple vesicles and systemic viral symptoms such as fever and malaise.
  • Acute periodontitis: Involves deeper periodontal structures and is associated with pocket formation and tooth mobility.
  • Oral candidiasis: White, curd-like plaques that can be scraped off, commonly seen in immunocompromised individuals or those on antibiotics.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Oral hygiene: Regular, thorough toothbrushing and interdental cleaning to reduce bacterial load.
  • Antibiotics: Metronidazole 400mg 3 times daily for 3 days is commonly prescribed in the UK due to its efficacy against anaerobic bacteria.
  • Chlorhexidine mouthwash: 0.12% or 0.2% mouthwash twice daily to help reduce bacterial load.
  • Smoking cessation: Strongly advised, as smoking is a significant risk factor for AUG and impairs healing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Ulcerative Gingivitis (AUG)"][/sc]
  • Necrotising periodontitis: Progression of the disease to involve periodontal ligaments and alveolar bone, leading to tooth mobility and loss.
  • Systemic spread of infection: Rare, but the infection can spread to surrounding tissues, potentially leading to cellulitis or even life-threatening conditions like Ludwig's angina.
