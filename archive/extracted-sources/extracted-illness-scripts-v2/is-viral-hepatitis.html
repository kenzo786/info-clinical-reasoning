Viral Hepatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Viral hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver caused by a viral infection. The most common types include hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each caused by a different virus. The condition ranges from asymptomatic infections to severe, life-threatening liver disease and can be acute or chronic. Chronic hepatitis is primarily associated with hepatitis B, C, and D.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Hepatitis B and C affect approximately 0.5-1% of the population.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Acute viral hepatitis cases are less common; however, chronic hepatitis B and C account for a significant portion of hepatology referrals and liver-related morbidity.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Hepatitis B and C affect around 296 million and 58 million people globally, respectively, with high prevalence in regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia, and Central Asia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Hepatitis viruses infect hepatocytes, prompting an immune response as the body attempts to eliminate the virus. Immune-mediated damage to hepatocytes leads to liver inflammation.
  • Acute Hepatitis: Rapid viral replication triggers immune-mediated hepatocyte destruction, causing inflammation. In self-limiting cases, the immune response clears the virus.
  • Chronic Hepatitis (mainly hepatitis B, C): Persistent infection leads to ongoing liver inflammation, causing fibrosis and increasing the risk of cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).
  • Viral-Specific Mechanisms: Each virus has unique features—for instance, hepatitis B integrates into the host genome, while hepatitis C mutates rapidly to evade immune detection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Incubation: Varies by virus—2–6 weeks for hepatitis A, 6 weeks–6 months for hepatitis B, and 2–26 weeks for hepatitis C.
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms, if present, usually emerge within weeks, including malaise, jaundice, and abdominal pain.
  • Chronic Phase (HBV, HCV): Infections may persist for years to decades, with gradual progression to liver fibrosis or cirrhosis if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to hepatic inflammation impacting energy metabolism and increased immune response.
  • Anorexia - Loss of appetite from systemic inflammatory response and liver dysfunction.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Likely due to liver inflammation affecting metabolic waste processing and toxin buildup.
  • Jaundice - Accumulation of bilirubin due to impaired liver function; manifests as yellowing of skin and sclera.
  • Right upper quadrant (RUQ) pain - Due to liver capsule stretching from inflammation.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Especially common in chronic cases; patients may not experience symptoms until advanced liver disease develops.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of skin and eyes, due to hyperbilirubinemia from impaired liver function.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver, commonly palpable in the RUQ, due to inflammation and hepatocyte swelling.
  • Scleral icterus - Yellow discolouration of the sclera, often one of the earliest signs of jaundice.
  • Spider naevi - Often seen in chronic liver disease, due to increased estrogen levels from impaired liver metabolism.
  • Palmar erythema - Redness in palms, associated with chronic liver disease and hormonal imbalances.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status - Suggests hepatic encephalopathy, potentially due to severe liver dysfunction with ammonia buildup.
  • Bleeding tendency - Can indicate coagulopathy due to decreased synthesis of clotting factors by the liver.
  • Ascites - Abnormal fluid accumulation in the abdomen, often a sign of advanced cirrhosis and portal hypertension.
  • Severe abdominal pain with fever - May suggest spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) in patients with cirrhosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Non-specific symptoms like fatigue and malaise, particularly in chronic hepatitis, may lead to delayed diagnosis.
  • Extra-hepatic manifestations, such as arthritis or vasculitis, especially in hepatitis B and C, due to immune complex deposition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated ALT and AST suggest liver injury; elevated bilirubin levels indicate jaundice severity.
  • Viral serology - Identifies specific hepatitis viruses (e.g., HBsAg for hepatitis B, anti-HCV for hepatitis C).
  • Hepatitis B DNA / Hepatitis C RNA PCR - Quantifies viral load and confirms active infection.
  • Ultrasound - Useful to assess liver structure, ruling out complications like cirrhosis or malignancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with fatigue, jaundice, and RUQ pain over two weeks, with a history of unprotected travel. Elevated ALT and HBsAg positive.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic hepatitis - Often associated with history of alcohol abuse, with elevated GGT levels and AST>ALT ratio.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Positive autoantibodies and elevated IgG; lacks viral markers.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Common in patients with metabolic syndrome; LFTs may be mildly elevated without viral markers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Hepatitis A - Supportive care, as it is usually self-limiting.
  • Hepatitis B - Antiviral therapy (e.g., tenofovir or entecavir) for chronic cases, following NICE guidelines.
  • Hepatitis C - Direct-acting antivirals (e.g., sofosbuvir) for chronic infection, per UK and EASL guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Viral Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Chronic liver inflammation leading to fibrosis; increased risk in hepatitis B and C.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) - Arises from chronic inflammation and cirrhosis, particularly in untreated hepatitis B and C.
  • Liver failure - Resulting from progressive liver damage and loss of function; can be acute or chronic.
