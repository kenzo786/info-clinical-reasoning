Hiatus Hernia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Hiatus hernia is a condition where part of the stomach pushes up into the chest through the diaphragm's oesophageal hiatus. It is typically classified into two main types: sliding hiatus hernia, where the gastro-oesophageal junction and a portion of the stomach slide up into the chest; and para-oesophageal hernia, where part of the stomach herniates next to the oesophagus while the gastro-oesophageal junction remains below the diaphragm.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Estimated 10-20%, with many cases asymptomatic.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Commonly found incidentally in endoscopies, especially in individuals over 50 years old.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Up to 50% of adults over 50 years may have a hiatus hernia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • A weakening of the diaphragmatic muscles or an increase in intra-abdominal pressure (from obesity, heavy lifting, or chronic cough) can lead to herniation of the stomach through the hiatus.
  • The sliding type occurs when both the gastro-oesophageal junction and stomach slide upward, typically leading to symptoms due to acid reflux.
  • In para-oesophageal hernia, the gastro-oesophageal junction remains fixed while part of the stomach herniates, potentially causing obstruction, ischaemia, or strangulation.
  • Prolonged acid exposure in the oesophagus can lead to oesophagitis, Barrett’s oesophagus, and increased risk of oesophageal carcinoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Often asymptomatic, with herniation detected incidentally on imaging.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may develop gradually, especially in the presence of a sliding hernia due to gastro-oesophageal reflux.
  • Complicated phase: Para-oesophageal hernias can cause acute symptoms if strangulation or obstruction occurs, requiring emergency intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Heartburn - Common in sliding hernias due to gastro-oesophageal reflux, where acid irritates the lower oesophagus.
  • Regurgitation - Refluxed gastric contents entering the oesophagus and sometimes mouth, causing sour taste and throat irritation.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing, often due to oesophageal inflammation or mechanical obstruction in larger hernias.
  • Chest pain - Caused by reflux or pressure of herniated stomach pressing against surrounding structures.
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases, especially smaller hernias, may have no symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Epigastric tenderness - Due to gastric irritation and possible reflux.
  • Reflux symptoms on physical examination - Observed as acid regurgitation during tests that increase intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., valsalva manoeuvre).
  • Signs of anaemia - In chronic cases with bleeding, pallor or signs of anaemia might be present.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - Indicates possible oesophagitis or ulceration; chronic bleeding may suggest erosive damage or even malignancy.
  • Severe, unrelenting chest pain - May indicate strangulation of a para-oesophageal hernia, which can lead to ischaemia and necrosis.
  • Progressive dysphagia - Could signal a structural narrowing, advanced inflammation, or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough or hoarseness - Reflux can lead to laryngeal irritation and chronic cough, which can mask respiratory conditions.
  • Worsening asthma - Acid reflux associated with hiatus hernia may exacerbate asthma symptoms, especially in nocturnal cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Upper GI endoscopy - Visualisation of the gastro-oesophageal junction to confirm herniation and assess for oesophagitis, ulcers, or Barrett’s oesophagus.
  • Barium swallow - Shows anatomical displacement of the stomach and confirms type of hernia.
  • Oesophageal manometry - Assesses motility to rule out dysmotility issues, particularly useful in dysphagia cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female with a history of heartburn and intermittent dysphagia, presenting with worsening chest pain, reflux, and regurgitation for six months.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Similar presentation but may lack anatomical herniation; confirmed by endoscopy and pH monitoring.
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Pain often epigastric and relieved by food, unlike reflux symptoms which worsen post-meal.
  • Oesophageal spasm - Presents with dysphagia and chest pain but lacks anatomical changes; diagnosed with manometry.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications - Weight loss, smoking cessation, and avoiding heavy lifting can reduce intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) - Reduce acid production and prevent mucosal damage, especially in reflux symptoms.
  • Surgical repair - Considered in large para-oesophageal hernias or those with refractory symptoms; laparoscopic fundoplication is commonly used.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hiatus Hernia"][/sc]
  • Oesophagitis - Chronic acid exposure leads to inflammation, increasing risk of ulceration and bleeding.
  • Barrett’s oesophagus - Metaplastic changes due to chronic acid exposure, with potential for malignant transformation.
  • Strangulation or obstruction - In para-oesophageal hernias, particularly at risk for ischaemia and perforation, requiring emergency intervention.
