Alzheimer's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder and the most common cause of dementia, characterised by the gradual decline in cognitive functions such as memory, reasoning, language, and executive function. It is primarily due to the accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques and tau protein tangles in the brain, leading to neuronal damage and loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 2% among individuals over age 65.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Often accounts for around 60-70% of dementia cases seen in specialist memory clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 6 million cases in the US; over 44 million people are estimated to have Alzheimer’s disease or related dementias globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Alzheimer’s disease is marked by the accumulation of amyloid-beta (Aβ) peptides forming plaques in the brain. These plaques disrupt cell function, particularly in the hippocampus and cortical areas essential for memory and cognition.
  • Tau protein in neurons becomes hyperphosphorylated, leading to neurofibrillary tangles that destabilise microtubules, impeding intracellular transport and resulting in neuronal death.
  • Neuroinflammation is triggered, exacerbating neuronal damage and disrupting synaptic communication. This ongoing cycle leads to progressive brain atrophy, particularly in the temporal and parietal lobes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Preclinical: Silent accumulation of Aβ and tau proteins occurs years before symptoms appear.
  • Mild cognitive impairment (MCI): Subtle cognitive difficulties emerge, but daily functioning is not significantly impaired.
  • Mild Alzheimer’s disease: Noticeable memory loss and cognitive decline with difficulty in complex tasks.
  • Moderate to severe stages: Increasing dependency, severe memory impairment, language loss, behavioural changes, and inability to manage daily activities.
  • End stage: Profound cognitive and physical debilitation, leading to total care dependency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Memory loss - Often the earliest symptom, especially with recent events, due to hippocampal neuron loss and disruption of memory consolidation pathways.
  • Difficulty with complex tasks - Challenges in planning or organising, as frontal and parietal lobes are affected by plaque and tangle deposition.
  • Language difficulties - Trouble finding words or following conversations, stemming from progressive loss in temporal lobe function.
  • Disorientation - Confusion regarding time, place, and events due to widespread cortical atrophy.
  • Behavioural changes - Apathy, mood swings, or aggression arise as a result of changes in frontal lobe and amygdala function.
  • Reduced motivation - Loss of interest in activities due to neurodegeneration in areas involved in reward and motivation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Memory impairment on cognitive testing - Poor recall on tasks such as word-list learning, reflecting hippocampal degeneration.
  • Impaired executive function - Shown by difficulty with multi-step tasks, as frontal lobe function declines.
  • Language deficits - Observed as word-finding difficulty or reduced fluency due to temporal lobe involvement.
  • Visuospatial deficits - Issues with perception and spatial tasks, linked to parietal lobe atrophy.
  • Affective symptoms - Anxiety, depression, or apathy often appear, potentially due to limbic system involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Rapid cognitive decline - May suggest alternative diagnoses like prion disease or dementia with Lewy bodies.
  • Severe behavioural changes early in disease course - Indicates possible frontotemporal dementia (FTD) rather than Alzheimer’s.
  • Motor symptoms - Tremors or rigidity could point to dementia with Lewy bodies or Parkinson's disease dementia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Posterior cortical atrophy - Primarily visuospatial impairments, due to early parietal involvement.
  • Early-onset Alzheimer’s - Symptoms may begin before age 65 with faster progression, often linked to genetic mutations.
  • Primary progressive aphasia - Language dysfunction without memory loss, often due to temporal lobe involvement in early disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination) - Common screening tool, with scores ≤24/30 indicating cognitive impairment.
  • CT/MRI brain scan - Shows generalised cortical atrophy, particularly in temporal and parietal lobes.
  • PET scan - May reveal decreased glucose metabolism in affected brain regions.
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis - Shows reduced Aβ42 levels and elevated tau protein, indicative of Alzheimer’s pathology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman presents with an 18-month history of progressive memory loss, language difficulties, and disorientation, increasingly unable to manage daily tasks, with findings of hippocampal atrophy on MRI.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Vascular dementia - More abrupt onset with stepwise progression, often accompanied by motor symptoms.
  • Dementia with Lewy bodies - Includes visual hallucinations and parkinsonism, with fluctuating cognition.
  • Frontotemporal dementia (FTD) - Early behavioural changes or language difficulties, often younger onset.
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus - Triad of dementia, gait disturbance, and urinary incontinence.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Cholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil) - First-line for mild to moderate Alzheimer’s to improve cognitive symptoms.
  • Memantine - N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist, for moderate to severe Alzheimer’s, often combined with cholinesterase inhibitors.
  • Non-pharmacological interventions - Cognitive stimulation therapy, physical activity, and structured social engagement.
  • Supportive care - For carers and support for end-stage care planning.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Increased risk of falls - Due to cognitive and motor impairments, potentially leading to fractures or head injury.
  • Infections - Aspiration pneumonia risk increases as swallowing deteriorates in advanced stages.
  • Malnutrition and dehydration - Result from impaired feeding and self-care abilities, leading to general decline.
  • Prognosis - Progressive with a median survival of 8–10 years post-diagnosis; disease-modifying treatments are limited.
