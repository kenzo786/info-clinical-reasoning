Malignant Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Malignant hypertension, also known as hypertensive emergency, is a severe form of hypertension characterised by extremely high blood pressure (typically ≥180/120 mmHg) accompanied by evidence of acute target organ damage, such as encephalopathy, retinopathy, renal impairment, or heart failure. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment to prevent life-threatening complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is low, with an incidence of less than 1% of those with hypertension.
  • In UK hospitals, hypertensive emergencies, including malignant hypertension, account for around 1-2% of hypertensive patients admitted for care.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence is highest in low- and middle-income countries due to poorer access to healthcare and hypertension management.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Malignant hypertension results from a sudden, severe increase in systemic vascular resistance (SVR) due to the failure of autoregulation in the small blood vessels (arterioles). This leads to endothelial injury, increased vascular permeability, and fibrinoid necrosis in various organs, causing ischemia and damage to vital organs like the brain, kidneys, and heart.
  • Renal ischaemia due to narrowing of the renal arteries activates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), which further increases blood pressure in a vicious cycle.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Malignant hypertension can develop rapidly over hours to days, leading to acute organ damage.
  • If untreated, the condition can progress to life-threatening complications such as stroke, heart failure, or renal failure within hours or days.
  • With appropriate treatment, blood pressure can be gradually lowered over 24-48 hours to avoid precipitating organ hypoperfusion.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Severe headache - Due to raised intracranial pressure secondary to hypertensive encephalopathy.
  • Blurred vision - From hypertensive retinopathy, papilloedema, and retinal haemorrhages.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Resulting from increased intracranial pressure.
  • Chest pain - May be indicative of myocardial ischaemia or aortic dissection.
  • Shortness of breath - Due to heart failure and pulmonary oedema from left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - From cerebral oedema or ischaemia, contributing to hypertensive encephalopathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Severely elevated blood pressure - Typically ≥180/120 mmHg, this is the key diagnostic criterion.
  • Papilloedema - Optic disc swelling due to raised intracranial pressure and retinal damage.
  • Retinal haemorrhages and exudates - Indicative of severe hypertensive retinopathy.
  • Signs of heart failure (e.g., bilateral crackles) - From acute left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Neurological deficits - Focal deficits such as hemiparesis, dysarthria, or visual field defects can occur due to stroke or hypertensive encephalopathy.
  • Renal impairment - Often evidenced by oliguria or haematuria.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Chest pain - May indicate myocardial infarction or aortic dissection, both life-threatening complications.
  • Altered mental status - Suggests hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke, both of which require immediate intervention.
  • Oliguria or anuria - Indicative of acute kidney injury, which may progress to renal failure.
  • Severe headache with visual changes - Could signify papilloedema, retinal haemorrhage, or impending stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Silent malignant hypertension - Patients may present with only mild symptoms or be asymptomatic despite severe organ damage, which can be uncovered during routine evaluations.
  • Non-specific symptoms - Fatigue, mild headache, or dizziness without clear organ damage may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Blood pressure measurement - Severely elevated readings (≥180/120 mmHg) are diagnostic.
  • Fundoscopy - Reveals papilloedema, retinal haemorrhages, and exudates, indicative of hypertensive retinopathy.
  • Blood tests - Urea and electrolytes (U&Es) may show acute kidney injury, raised serum creatinine, and hyperkalaemia.
  • Urinalysis - May show proteinuria or haematuria, reflecting kidney damage.
  • ECG - May show signs of left ventricular hypertrophy or ischaemia.
  • Chest X-ray - To assess for heart failure or pulmonary oedema.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a sudden onset of severe headache, blurred vision, and chest pain. His blood pressure is recorded at 220/130 mmHg, and fundoscopy reveals papilloedema and retinal haemorrhages. Urinalysis shows haematuria, and ECG demonstrates left ventricular hypertrophy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytoma - May cause paroxysmal hypertension with episodic symptoms (headache, palpitations), unlike sustained malignant hypertension.
  • Acute kidney injury - While it may cause hypertension, the absence of other target organ damage (e.g., retinopathy) differentiates it from malignant hypertension.
  • Pre-eclampsia - Seen in pregnant women, with hypertension, proteinuria, and oedema, but not usually the severe end-organ damage seen in malignant hypertension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Immediate IV antihypertensive therapy - Medications such as sodium nitroprusside, labetalol, or nicardipine are used to reduce blood pressure gradually (by no more than 25% within the first hour).
  • Oral antihypertensive medications - Used after stabilisation, including ACE inhibitors (e.g., ramipril), calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine), and beta-blockers (e.g., bisoprolol).
  • Management of complications - Acute renal failure may require dialysis; heart failure may need diuretics or inotropes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Resulting from hypertensive encephalopathy or intracerebral haemorrhage.
  • Acute kidney injury - Due to renal vascular damage and ischaemia.
  • Heart failure - From left ventricular dysfunction and increased afterload.
  • Retinopathy - Can lead to permanent visual loss.
