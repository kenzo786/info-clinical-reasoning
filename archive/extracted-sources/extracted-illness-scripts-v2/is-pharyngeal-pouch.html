Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • A pharyngeal pouch, or Zenker's diverticulum, is a pulsion diverticulum of the hypopharynx. It occurs when the mucosal layer of the pharynx herniates through a muscular weakness between the thyropharyngeus and cricopharyngeus muscles, typically in the posterior wall of the pharynx. This outpouching traps food and leads to symptoms such as dysphagia and regurgitation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Rare, affecting approximately 0.01% of the general population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common in older adults referred for dysphagia, with a higher incidence in men over 60 years.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to the UK, rare but most prevalent in older populations in Western countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Zenker's diverticulum forms due to increased intraluminal pressure from incomplete relaxation or dysfunction of the cricopharyngeus muscle during swallowing. This pressure causes herniation of the mucosal and submucosal layers through a weak area of the pharyngeal wall, known as Killian's dehiscence, between the thyropharyngeus and cricopharyngeus muscles.
  • Over time, this pouch enlarges and traps food particles, leading to obstructive symptoms such as dysphagia, regurgitation, and in severe cases, aspiration.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be minimal or absent. Small pouches often present asymptomatically.
  • Progressive phase: Over time, symptoms such as dysphagia and regurgitation worsen as the pouch enlarges and retains more food.
  • Late phase: Large pouches may lead to aspiration, malnutrition, and significant discomfort due to frequent regurgitation of undigested food.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Dysphagia – Difficulty swallowing due to obstruction from the pouch itself or retained food. This is the most common symptom.
  • Regurgitation – Regurgitation of undigested food occurs when food is trapped in the pouch and refluxes back into the mouth.
  • Chronic cough – This occurs due to irritation of the airways or aspiration of food contents from the pouch.
  • Halitosis – Bad breath results from the fermentation of trapped food in the pouch.
  • Unintentional weight loss – Occurs in more severe cases due to difficulty eating and malnutrition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Palpable neck mass – A soft, fluctuating mass in the neck may be palpable in large pouches due to the outward bulging of the diverticulum.
  • Gurgling sounds – Audible gurgling during swallowing or when pressure is applied to the pouch.
  • Signs of aspiration – Such as crackles or wheezing, may be present if aspiration has occurred.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia – Frequent aspiration of food into the lungs can lead to recurrent chest infections or pneumonia, which may be life-threatening.
  • Unexplained weight loss – Can signify severe dysphagia and nutritional deficiencies, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Dysphonia or hoarseness – May indicate involvement of the recurrent laryngeal nerve or malignancy in the surrounding area.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with isolated chronic cough or throat clearing without overt dysphagia, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • Occasionally, the pouch may cause recurrent chest infections due to microaspiration, without obvious swallowing difficulties.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Barium swallow – The gold standard for diagnosis. This fluoroscopic imaging test reveals the outpouching of the diverticulum and any pooling of contrast within the pouch.
  • Flexible endoscopy – Visualises the pouch directly, assesses its size, and identifies any complications such as inflammation or malignancy. Endoscopy should be performed with caution due to the risk of perforation.
  • Manometry – Used to assess the function of the upper oesophageal sphincter and confirm cricopharyngeal dysfunction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old male presents with progressive dysphagia, frequent regurgitation of undigested food, and a chronic cough over the past 6 months. Barium swallow reveals a large pharyngeal pouch.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Achalasia – Differs by presenting with both dysphagia to solids and liquids, and oesophageal manometry shows absent peristalsis rather than a pouch.
  • Oesophageal cancer – May present with progressive dysphagia and weight loss, but without the characteristic regurgitation of undigested food. Endoscopy and biopsy are required for diagnosis.
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GORD) – Presents with heartburn and regurgitation, but lacks the anatomical pouching seen in pharyngeal diverticulum.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Surgical management – Endoscopic stapling or open surgical resection is the treatment of choice for symptomatic pouches, especially large ones. Endoscopic procedures are preferred due to their less invasive nature.
  • Cricopharyngeal myotomy – Often performed alongside pouch resection to reduce the chance of recurrence by addressing the underlying cricopharyngeal dysfunction.
  • Conservative management – Small, asymptomatic pouches can be managed conservatively with dietary modifications (e.g., smaller, softer meals).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's Diverticulum)"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia – Frequent regurgitation can lead to aspiration of food or liquids into the lungs, resulting in pneumonia. This is more common in larger pouches.
  • Malnutrition – Due to significant dysphagia and reduced oral intake, patients may suffer from malnutrition and weight loss.
  • Perforation – Endoscopic manipulation of the pouch can result in perforation, a rare but serious complication.
