Cholesteatoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma is a non-cancerous growth of keratinizing squamous epithelium in the middle ear or mastoid. It can erode nearby structures, leading to complications like hearing loss and infections. It is characterised by its invasive nature and ability to cause chronic middle ear inflammation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the incidence of cholesteatoma is around 9.2 per 100,000 in adults and 3 per 100,000 in children. It accounts for around 0.5% of all ear, nose, and throat (ENT) consultations in general practice. Globally, the prevalence varies, but it is more common in regions with poor access to healthcare due to chronic ear infections remaining untreated.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma forms due to the accumulation of squamous epithelium in the middle ear space. The primary mechanism involves eustachian tube dysfunction leading to negative middle ear pressure, retraction of the tympanic membrane, and the trapping of keratin debris. Over time, the trapped debris forms a cyst, which can become infected and erode surrounding structures such as the ossicles, facial nerve, or even the skull base.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma typically develops slowly over months to years. In its early stages, it may be asymptomatic or present with mild hearing loss. Over time, as the cyst grows, more significant symptoms develop, such as chronic ear drainage, more profound hearing loss, and complications like facial nerve palsy or intracranial infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Typically conductive hearing loss occurs as the growing cholesteatoma destroys ossicles or obstructs the middle ear. This is due to impaired sound conduction.
  • Chronic ear discharge (otorrhoea) - Persistent, often foul-smelling discharge is due to secondary bacterial infection within the cholesteatoma.
  • Ear fullness - Patients may experience a feeling of pressure or fullness due to middle ear inflammation or obstruction by the cholesteatoma.
  • Tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing in the ear can result from damage to the middle or inner ear structures.
  • Pain - Typically mild or absent unless there is secondary infection or bone erosion. When present, it may indicate an advanced or complicated case.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Retracted tympanic membrane - A retracted or perforated tympanic membrane is often visible on otoscopy. This results from negative middle ear pressure, which pulls the eardrum inward.
  • Granulation tissue or a pearly white mass - Visible in the ear canal or behind a perforated tympanic membrane, this is the cholesteatoma itself, which may appear as a whitish mass.
  • Conductive hearing loss - Detected on examination with a tuning fork test (Weber and Rinne). It occurs when sound transmission through the middle ear is impaired.
  • Foul-smelling otorrhoea - Thick, malodorous discharge is often seen in cases with infection or advanced disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve palsy - Indicates possible erosion of the facial nerve canal by the cholesteatoma, which can lead to paralysis if untreated.
  • Dizziness or vertigo - Suggests involvement of the inner ear or labyrinth, possibly indicating progression towards labyrinthine fistula or erosion of the semicircular canals.
  • Headache, fever, neurological symptoms - These could indicate intracranial extension, such as meningitis or brain abscess, which are life-threatening complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases, particularly in early stages or in elderly patients, can present only with mild hearing loss and may lack the classic discharge or pain, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Otoscopy - Visualises retraction pockets, perforation, or the presence of cholesteatoma. Essential for diagnosis, revealing the characteristic pearly mass.
  • Audiometry - Typically shows conductive hearing loss, though sensorineural hearing loss may occur in advanced cases with inner ear involvement.
  • CT Scan of the temporal bone - Helps assess the extent of bone erosion, ossicular destruction, and potential complications such as labyrinthine fistulae or intracranial extension.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of progressive hearing loss in the left ear, associated with intermittent foul-smelling discharge and a sensation of fullness in the ear. Otoscopy reveals a retracted tympanic membrane with a visible pearly mass in the middle ear.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic Otitis Media - Similar symptoms of hearing loss and discharge, but without the characteristic mass seen in cholesteatoma. Usually lacks destructive bone changes on imaging.
  • Acute Otitis Media - More acute onset of pain and discharge, usually associated with systemic symptoms like fever. Tympanic membrane bulging rather than retraction is seen.
  • Otosclerosis - Gradual conductive hearing loss without discharge or tympanic membrane changes. Typically seen in younger adults, often with a positive family history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Surgical excision - The mainstay of treatment is surgical removal of the cholesteatoma, typically via tympanomastoidectomy. The aim is to eradicate the disease and reconstruct the ossicles if necessary.
  • Topical antibiotic drops - Used to manage infection preoperatively if there is active discharge.
  • Regular follow-up - Necessary to monitor for recurrence, which can occur even after surgical removal.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Conductive or mixed hearing loss due to ossicular destruction or damage to the cochlea.
  • Labyrinthine fistula - Erosion into the inner ear leading to dizziness or vertigo.
  • Meningitis or brain abscess - Intracranial extension of infection can lead to these life-threatening complications.
