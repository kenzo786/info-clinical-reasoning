Renal Carcinoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Renal carcinoma, also known as renal cell carcinoma (RCC), is the most common type of kidney cancer, originating from the renal tubular epithelial cells. It primarily affects the renal cortex and accounts for 85-90% of all primary renal neoplasms. RCC can be classified into different subtypes, including clear cell, papillary, and chromophobe, based on histological characteristics.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • In the UK, renal carcinoma accounts for 2-3% of adult malignancies, with around 13,000 new cases diagnosed each year. Globally, RCC incidence varies, with higher rates in developed countries. The worldwide incidence is around 330,000 cases annually. RCC is more common in men than women, with a ratio of about 2:1, and typically occurs in individuals aged 50-70.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Renal carcinoma arises due to genetic mutations, such as loss of the von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) tumour suppressor gene in clear cell RCC. These mutations lead to abnormal cellular signalling, promoting angiogenesis and uncontrolled cellular proliferation. Over time, this results in tumour growth within the kidney, which can eventually invade surrounding tissues or metastasise to distant sites, such as the lungs, bones, and liver.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Renal carcinoma often progresses slowly, with many cases being detected incidentally on imaging. Early stages may be asymptomatic, but as the tumour grows, symptoms such as haematuria or flank pain may develop. Metastatic disease, if untreated, typically worsens over months to years. Early detection significantly improves prognosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Haematuria - Present in 40-60% of cases, haematuria results from tumour invasion of renal vasculature or the renal pelvis, leading to blood in the urine.
  • Flank pain - Due to the mass effect of the tumour stretching the renal capsule or compressing surrounding tissues.
  • Painless abdominal mass - Palpable in some patients when the tumour grows large enough, often presenting in the late stages.
  • Weight loss - Unexplained weight loss may occur due to systemic effects of cancer, including increased metabolic demands or paraneoplastic syndromes.
  • Fatigue - A non-specific symptom related to the cancer’s systemic effects and anaemia, which can develop from tumour-related blood loss or inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass - Often discovered in thin individuals or advanced disease when the tumour is large enough.
  • Hypertension - Can occur due to compression of renal vasculature, leading to activation of the renin-angiotensin system.
  • Varicocele - A left-sided varicocele can develop due to obstruction of the left renal vein by the tumour.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Gross haematuria - May indicate advanced disease with invasion into the renal pelvis or urinary tract.
  • Sudden onset of varicocele - Suggests vascular obstruction by the tumour.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Concerning for systemic disease or metastasis.
  • Bone pain or pathological fractures - May indicate bone metastases.
  • Dyspnoea or haemoptysis - Suggestive of pulmonary metastases.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes - These include hypercalcaemia (due to ectopic production of parathyroid hormone-related peptide) or erythrocytosis (due to overproduction of erythropoietin by the tumour). These syndromes can present without obvious local tumour symptoms.
  • Incidental finding - Many renal carcinomas are discovered incidentally during imaging for unrelated reasons, as RCC can remain asymptomatic for long periods.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • CT abdomen and pelvis - Primary imaging modality for diagnosing RCC. Typically shows a heterogeneous mass with contrast enhancement, indicating a vascular tumour.
  • Urinalysis - May reveal haematuria. Lack of infection or other benign causes suggests malignancy.
  • Renal biopsy - Can confirm the histological subtype of RCC, though often avoided if imaging is diagnostic due to risks of seeding tumour cells.
  • Blood tests - May show anaemia or elevated alkaline phosphatase (suggestive of bone metastasis) and elevated calcium levels in paraneoplastic hypercalcaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old man presents with painless haematuria and a 3-month history of weight loss and fatigue. Examination reveals a left-sided abdominal mass and a newly developed left varicocele. Abdominal CT confirms a large left renal mass consistent with renal carcinoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Transitional cell carcinoma - Unlike RCC, this tumour originates from the renal pelvis and is more likely to present with haematuria without a palpable mass.
  • Renal calculi - Presents with haematuria and pain, but pain is typically colicky and there is no mass or systemic symptoms.
  • Polycystic kidney disease - May cause haematuria and hypertension, but presents bilaterally with enlarged kidneys on imaging and without weight loss or systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Nephrectomy - Surgical removal of the affected kidney is the primary treatment for localised disease. Partial nephrectomy may be performed for smaller tumours.
  • Targeted therapies - In advanced or metastatic RCC, drugs like tyrosine kinase inhibitors (e.g., sunitinib) or immune checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., nivolumab) are used.
  • Radiotherapy - Typically reserved for palliation in metastatic cases, particularly for bone metastases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - RCC commonly metastasises to the lungs, bones, and liver, leading to symptoms such as dyspnoea, bone pain, or liver dysfunction. Metastasis occurs in about 25% of cases at diagnosis.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes - These include hypercalcaemia and erythrocytosis, leading to metabolic imbalances and cardiovascular complications.
  • Local invasion - Tumour invasion into the renal vein or inferior vena cava can lead to thrombus formation, with a risk of embolism.
