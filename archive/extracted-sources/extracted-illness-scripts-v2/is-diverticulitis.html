Diverticulitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Diverticulitis is the inflammation or infection of diverticula, which are small bulging pouches that can form in the lining of the colon (most commonly the sigmoid colon). It represents a complication of diverticulosis and can range from mild inflammation to severe infection, perforation, or abscess formation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, about 5-10% of people aged 40-60 years and over 50% of those above 60 years have diverticulosis, with 10-25% of them developing diverticulitis.
  • Hospital admissions in the UK for diverticulitis have increased, with approximately 200 cases per 100,000 population annually.
  • Globally, diverticulitis prevalence increases with age, being higher in developed countries due to lower dietary fibre intake.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Diverticulitis develops when diverticula, usually located in the sigmoid colon, become obstructed by faecal matter, leading to bacterial overgrowth. This results in inflammation, and in more severe cases, the inflammation can progress to microperforation, localised abscesses, or even free perforation with peritonitis. Low fibre diets, which increase intraluminal pressure, play a key role in the formation of diverticula, while impaired colonic motility and changes in gut microbiota may also contribute.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • The initial phase of diverticulitis is characterised by acute, localised inflammation, typically over several days. Most mild cases resolve with conservative treatment. However, more severe cases, or those left untreated, can progress to complications such as abscess formation, perforation, or fistula over days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Left lower quadrant abdominal pain - Most common symptom, occurring due to inflammation in the sigmoid colon. Localised pain reflects the anatomical location of most diverticula.
  • Fever - Results from the body’s inflammatory response to infection within the diverticula.
  • Change in bowel habits - Patients may present with constipation (from colonic spasm) or diarrhoea (from localised irritation).
  • Nausea and vomiting - Occur secondary to ileus (bowel obstruction or reduced bowel motility) caused by localised inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Left lower quadrant tenderness - A key finding, correlating with the inflamed diverticula, typically in the sigmoid colon.
  • Guarding or rigidity - Indicates localised peritoneal irritation or more severe complications such as perforation.
  • Rebound tenderness - Suggests peritonitis, a potential complication of diverticulitis if there is perforation.
  • Palpable mass - Can indicate an abscess formation if localised inflammation has progressed to an organised collection of pus.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Severe or generalised abdominal pain - May indicate perforation and peritonitis.
  • Hypotension and tachycardia - Signs of sepsis, which can occur in complicated diverticulitis with perforation, abscess, or fistula.
  • Haematochezia (rectal bleeding) - Suggests colonic involvement with significant erosion or rupture of blood vessels.
  • Persistent fever and pain despite treatment - Could indicate an abscess or a complicated case requiring further intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Right lower quadrant pain - Less common but possible if diverticulitis occurs in a redundant sigmoid colon or if diverticula are located in the ascending colon.
  • Absence of fever - Occurs in immunosuppressed or elderly patients, who may fail to mount a robust inflammatory response.
  • Chronic, intermittent abdominal discomfort - Can occur in patients with recurrent diverticulitis or those with chronic inflammation and fibrosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Elevated white cell count (leukocytosis) is common due to the body’s inflammatory response.
  • CRP (C-reactive protein) - Raised levels correlate with the degree of inflammation or infection.
  • CT abdomen/pelvis - Gold standard imaging for diagnosis, often showing colonic wall thickening, diverticula, fat stranding, or abscesses.
  • Ultrasound - May be used if CT is unavailable, but it is less sensitive. It can identify abscesses or thickened bowel segments.
  • Plain abdominal X-ray - Can show free air under the diaphragm if there is perforation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with a 3-day history of progressively worsening left lower quadrant abdominal pain, fever, and constipation. He has no previous history of diverticulitis, but his diet is low in fibre.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer - Presents with weight loss, rectal bleeding, or a palpable mass. Diagnosis confirmed by colonoscopy and biopsy.
  • Ischaemic colitis - Sudden-onset abdominal pain, often associated with bloody diarrhoea. CT imaging shows segmental bowel thickening.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn’s or ulcerative colitis) - Chronic diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and extraintestinal manifestations (e.g. arthritis). Diagnosis via colonoscopy with biopsy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Uncomplicated diverticulitis - Managed conservatively with oral antibiotics (e.g. co-amoxiclav), analgesia, and a clear liquid diet followed by gradual reintroduction of fibre.
  • Complicated diverticulitis - Requires hospital admission. Intravenous antibiotics (e.g. piperacillin/tazobactam) are often needed. Abscesses may require percutaneous drainage, and surgery may be indicated for perforation or obstruction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Abscess formation - Occurs in approximately 15% of patients with complicated diverticulitis, often requiring drainage.
  • Perforation - Leads to peritonitis and sepsis, requiring emergency surgery. Mortality rates increase significantly if not treated promptly.
  • Fistula formation - Occurs when inflammation causes an abnormal connection between the colon and adjacent organs, often the bladder or vagina. Surgery is required to correct this.
  • Bowel obstruction - Can result from chronic inflammation and fibrosis, leading to narrowing of the bowel lumen.
