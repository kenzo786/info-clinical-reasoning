Foreign Body
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • A foreign body refers to any object originating outside the body that has entered into or lodged within the body tissues or cavities, often unintentionally. This can occur in various locations such as the respiratory tract, gastrointestinal tract, eyes, ears, or skin.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, foreign body ingestion is most common among children, with around 150 cases per 100,000 per year.
  • In hospital settings, foreign body cases (mainly inhaled or ingested) are a frequent cause of emergency visits in children and certain adult groups, particularly those with neurological or swallowing disorders.
  • Worldwide, foreign body ingestions or insertions are most commonly reported in paediatric populations, but also commonly in elderly patients, patients with psychiatric conditions, and in occupational incidents involving inhalation or ocular foreign bodies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Foreign bodies that are ingested, inhaled, or inserted can cause direct injury to local tissues due to their shape, sharpness, or chemical composition (e.g., button batteries causing caustic injury).
  • The foreign body can obstruct or compress local structures, leading to symptoms like pain, swelling, or dyspnoea in airway cases.
  • In cases where the foreign body migrates, it can cause further tissue damage and infection, leading to inflammation and, in some cases, abscess formation or localised necrosis.
  • Unresolved foreign bodies may trigger an immune response, leading to granuloma formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Entry of the foreign body causing immediate symptoms like choking, coughing, or discomfort, depending on the site.
  • Early (Hours to Days): Local inflammation and possible obstruction symptoms, e.g., pain, swelling, infection, or respiratory distress.
  • Delayed (Days to Weeks): Potential for complications such as abscess formation, ulceration, or tissue necrosis if not removed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Discomfort or pain at the site - Caused by direct tissue irritation or injury.
  • Coughing or choking (if respiratory) - Due to airway irritation or obstruction from inhaled objects.
  • Dysphagia or odynophagia (if gastrointestinal) - From oesophageal obstruction or mucosal injury.
  • Asymptomatic (in some cases) - Small or inert objects may initially cause no symptoms, especially if not obstructing or irritating nearby tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Localised tenderness - Indicates inflammation or tissue irritation at the site.
  • Swelling - Due to local inflammatory response.
  • Wheezing or stridor (if respiratory) - Signifies partial airway obstruction from a foreign body.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Severe respiratory distress - Suggests complete or high-grade airway obstruction.
  • Haematemesis or haemoptysis - May indicate mucosal erosion or vascular injury due to foreign body.
  • High fever and signs of sepsis - Suggests a secondary infection or abscess formation, which can occur if a foreign body causes prolonged local irritation or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Silent or asymptomatic ingestion - Common in children or those with cognitive impairments; can lead to delayed presentation and complications.
  • Recurrent infections - Unresolved or retained foreign bodies may cause chronic infection, especially in respiratory or urinary tracts.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Plain X-ray - First-line imaging for metallic and some radiopaque objects. Expected findings include visible object outline.
  • CT scan - Used for non-radiopaque objects or complications; helps in locating and assessing foreign body impact on nearby tissues.
  • Ultrasound - Useful for soft tissue foreign bodies, especially in cases involving the extremities or soft tissues of the neck.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old child presents with sudden onset coughing, dyspnoea, and wheezing after playing with small toys, suggesting possible inhaled foreign body.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia - Often has fever and productive cough, unlike typical foreign body which may lack infection on initial presentation.
  • Angioedema - Acute airway swelling with no foreign object but may cause similar respiratory symptoms.
  • Epiglottitis - Severe respiratory distress with a sore throat and fever, and a more gradual onset than foreign body inhalation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Airway foreign body: Immediate removal if possible using bronchoscopy; supportive care if distress is present.
  • Gastrointestinal foreign body: Endoscopic retrieval for high-risk objects (e.g., sharp or battery items); observation for non-hazardous objects expected to pass.
  • Soft tissue foreign body: Local anaesthetic and removal if accessible; antibiotics if signs of infection.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Infection - Due to retained foreign bodies causing localised or systemic infection, more common if removal is delayed.
  • Perforation or erosion - Sharp or caustic foreign bodies can perforate tissues, particularly in gastrointestinal or respiratory tracts.
  • Airway obstruction - High-risk in respiratory foreign bodies, with potential for complete obstruction if the foreign body migrates.
