Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Renal colic is an acute, severe flank or abdominal pain caused by the obstruction of the urinary tract, typically due to a calculus (stone) within the ureter or renal pelvis. This condition is characterised by sudden, intense pain, often radiating to the groin, and is accompanied by symptoms of urinary tract irritation. The pain is due to increased intraluminal pressure and spasm of the ureter as it tries to dislodge the obstructing stone.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, renal colic has a prevalence of approximately 0.6% in the general practice population and accounts for about 1% of emergency department visits. Worldwide, the lifetime prevalence of kidney stones is estimated to range from 5-10%, with higher rates in men compared to women. Incidence peaks between 20-40 years of age and tends to recur in affected individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Renal colic is caused by a stone obstructing part of the urinary tract, commonly the ureter. This obstruction causes an increase in renal pelvic pressure, leading to distension, which activates local nerve fibres causing pain. The ureter attempts to expel the stone through peristaltic contractions, further exacerbating the pain and resulting in spasmodic discomfort.
  • Stones commonly consist of calcium oxalate or calcium phosphate, which form due to supersaturation in the urine, often influenced by dehydration, high calcium levels, or high oxalate levels. Metabolic disorders, diet, and genetic factors may contribute to stone formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • The onset of renal colic is usually acute, with sudden, severe pain. Symptoms can persist as long as the stone remains obstructive, varying from hours to days. Pain typically waxes and wanes due to ureteral spasms and movement of the stone. Once the stone passes, symptoms generally resolve.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Severe flank or abdominal pain - Typically starts suddenly and can be excruciating, caused by ureteral distension and increased intraluminal pressure.
  • Pain radiating to the groin - As the stone progresses through the ureter, pain often radiates to the lower abdomen, groin, or genital area, reflecting the referred pain along the T10-L2 dermatomes.
  • Nausea and vomiting - These are common due to visceral stimulation from the intense pain.
  • Haematuria (visible or microscopic) - Caused by irritation and abrasion of the urinary tract lining as the stone moves.
  • Frequent urination or urinary urgency - Due to irritation of the bladder or lower ureter, particularly if the stone is near the vesicoureteral junction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Costovertebral angle tenderness - Localised pain on palpation over the kidney area, indicative of ureteral or renal distension.
  • Restlessness and discomfort - Patients are often visibly distressed and unable to find a comfortable position due to severe pain.
  • Microscopic haematuria on urinalysis - Detectable blood in urine due to mucosal irritation or injury from the stone.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Fever and chills - May indicate a concurrent infection, such as pyelonephritis or urosepsis, due to the obstructed urinary flow.
  • Severe, persistent pain - Unrelieved pain could suggest a large or impacted stone requiring urgent intervention.
  • Decreased urine output or anuria - Could indicate complete obstruction, a urological emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Mild or no pain - Stones in the renal pelvis may present without colic symptoms until they cause significant obstruction.
  • Isolated haematuria - Stones in the kidney or bladder may cause haematuria without severe pain, especially in the elderly or patients with neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Typically shows microscopic haematuria; proteinuria or pyuria may also be present if infection is present.
  • Non-contrast CT KUB - First-line imaging modality showing the stone location and size, with higher sensitivity and specificity than ultrasound or X-ray.
  • Ultrasound - Used as an alternative in pregnant patients or as initial assessment, especially when hydronephrosis is suspected.
  • Blood tests - Urea, creatinine, and electrolyte levels help assess renal function and detect any associated renal impairment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute, severe left flank pain radiating to the groin, accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and haematuria, persisting over the last 12 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis - Presents with fever, chills, and consistent flank pain; positive urine culture often present.
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm - May cause severe abdominal or flank pain but often with a pulsatile abdominal mass and hypotension.
  • Biliary colic - Causes right upper quadrant pain radiating to the shoulder, usually triggered by fatty meals.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Analgesia - NSAIDs such as diclofenac are first-line for pain management. Opioids may be used if NSAIDs are contraindicated.
  • Hydration - Encouraged to aid spontaneous passage of the stone, but overhydration should be avoided.
  • Medical expulsion therapy - Alpha-blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) can facilitate stone passage in stones less than 10mm in the distal ureter.
  • Surgical intervention - Ureteroscopy or lithotripsy for stones that do not pass spontaneously or cause severe obstruction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renal Colic (Urinary Tract Stone)"][/sc]
  • Hydronephrosis - Prolonged obstruction leads to kidney swelling, potentially causing kidney damage if untreated.
  • Infection and sepsis - Obstructed stones increase the risk of infections that may progress to sepsis, especially if pyonephrosis occurs.
  • Renal impairment - Chronic obstruction or recurrent stones can reduce renal function over time, potentially leading to chronic kidney disease.
