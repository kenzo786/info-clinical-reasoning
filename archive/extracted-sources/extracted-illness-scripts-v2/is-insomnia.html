Insomnia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Insomnia is a sleep disorder characterised by difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking up too early, with poor sleep quality that results in daytime impairment. It is often classified as either acute (lasting a few days or weeks) or chronic (lasting for three months or more, occurring at least three times a week).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Insomnia is highly prevalent worldwide, affecting 10-30% of the adult population. In the UK, chronic insomnia affects approximately 10-15% of the general population and up to 40% in some primary care settings. Hospitalised patients often experience transient insomnia due to environmental changes, stress, or illness.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of insomnia involves hyperarousal, both at night and during the daytime. This hyperarousal may be due to increased levels of stress hormones (e.g., cortisol) or neurotransmitter imbalances (e.g., increased orexin, decreased GABA). Factors such as anxiety, depression, chronic pain, and circadian rhythm disturbances can also exacerbate insomnia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Acute insomnia: Lasts days to weeks, often triggered by stress or illness. It is typically transient and resolves once the triggering factor is addressed.
  • Chronic insomnia: Persists for more than three months, often without a clear precipitating factor. It can become self-perpetuating as individuals develop maladaptive sleep habits, such as irregular sleep schedules and anxiety about sleep.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Difficulty falling asleep – Often due to hyperarousal and increased activity of stress hormones, making it hard for the brain to transition into sleep.
  • Frequent awakenings during the night – Fragmented sleep can result from an inability to maintain deep sleep, often associated with anxiety or medical conditions.
  • Early morning awakenings – Often linked with depression, individuals wake up earlier than intended and cannot fall back asleep.
  • Daytime sleepiness or fatigue – A direct consequence of insufficient or poor-quality sleep, leading to impaired cognitive and physical functioning during the day.
  • Irritability or mood changes – Sleep deprivation can alter brain function, increasing sensitivity to stress and reducing the brain’s ability to regulate mood.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue – Lack of sleep leads to physical exhaustion and low energy levels throughout the day.
  • Poor concentration – Sleep deprivation impairs cognitive function, affecting attention, memory, and decision-making abilities.
  • Increased sympathetic tone – Insomnia is often associated with elevated heart rate, higher blood pressure, and increased levels of stress hormones (e.g., cortisol).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss – May indicate an underlying serious condition, such as malignancy, hyperthyroidism, or a severe mental health disorder.
  • Severe depression or suicidal ideation – Chronic insomnia is closely linked to mood disorders. Worsening depression or thoughts of self-harm require immediate mental health evaluation.
  • Significant decline in cognitive function – Severe cognitive impairment may suggest neurodegenerative diseases or metabolic imbalances (e.g., hyperthyroidism, hypoglycaemia).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Short sleep duration with no daytime impairment – Some individuals report short sleep (e.g., 4-5 hours) but do not experience daytime impairment, making the condition harder to diagnose.
  • Primary complaint of daytime fatigue without recognised sleep disturbance – This may be more common in older adults, who attribute their daytime fatigue to aging or other conditions rather than poor sleep.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Sleep diary – Patients record sleep and wake times, quality of sleep, and any awakenings over a 1-2 week period. This helps assess patterns and identify potential triggers.
  • Polysomnography – Overnight sleep study used in cases where a sleep disorder (e.g., sleep apnoea) is suspected. Findings may include increased sleep latency, reduced sleep efficiency, or frequent awakenings.
  • Actigraphy – A wrist-worn device that tracks sleep and activity patterns over a week. It provides an objective measure of sleep-wake cycles in the home environment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a three-month history of difficulty falling asleep, frequent awakenings during the night, and feeling fatigued during the day. She reports increased work-related stress, but no history of depression or significant physical illness.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Sleep apnoea – Unlike insomnia, sleep apnoea causes repeated breathing interruptions during sleep, leading to daytime sleepiness. Diagnosed with polysomnography showing apnoeic episodes.
  • Restless leg syndrome – Characterised by an uncomfortable urge to move the legs at night, causing sleep disturbance. Patients often describe a "creeping" sensation in the legs.
  • Anxiety disorders – Anxiety-related insomnia is associated with ruminations and worry that prevent sleep. Anxiety symptoms are more prominent than in primary insomnia.
  • Depression – Early morning awakenings are a hallmark of depression-related sleep disturbances. Other depressive symptoms are typically present.
  • Hyperthyroidism – Causes symptoms like insomnia, weight loss, and palpitations. TSH and free T4 blood tests will reveal abnormal thyroid function.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Sleep hygiene education – Encouraging a regular sleep schedule, avoiding caffeine and alcohol before bed, and creating a relaxing bedtime routine.
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) – A first-line treatment that addresses negative thoughts and behaviours around sleep.
  • Pharmacological treatment – Short-term use of hypnotics (e.g., zolpidem or zopiclone) may be considered for acute insomnia, but long-term use is not recommended due to the risk of dependence.
  • Addressing comorbid conditions – Managing underlying anxiety, depression, or medical conditions such as pain or hyperthyroidism can improve insomnia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Impaired cognitive function – Chronic sleep deprivation can lead to poor concentration, memory impairment, and reduced decision-making ability.
  • Increased risk of cardiovascular disease – Insomnia, especially if associated with increased sympathetic activity, may increase the risk of hypertension, heart disease, and stroke.
  • Mood disorders – Insomnia is closely linked with depression and anxiety, and untreated insomnia can worsen these conditions over time.
