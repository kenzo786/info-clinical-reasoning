Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced nausea and vomiting (MINV) refers to the nausea, vomiting, or both that occur as a side effect of taking certain medications. This condition results from pharmacological agents triggering nausea via various central and peripheral mechanisms, including stimulation of the chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ), gastrointestinal irritation, and vestibular system effects.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 10-20% of patients on common medications, particularly opioids, chemotherapy, and antibiotics, experience MINV.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher prevalence in inpatient settings, especially in patients receiving chemotherapy or postoperative analgesics (30-60%).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar trends, particularly in high-risk populations such as oncology patients receiving chemotherapy (50-80%).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Certain medications activate the chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ) in the area postrema, a structure that is sensitive to emetic stimuli.
  • Step 2: Direct irritation of the gastrointestinal mucosa by medications (e.g., NSAIDs, antibiotics) can lead to local inflammatory reactions, stimulating vagal afferents and triggering the vomiting centre.
  • Step 3: Other drugs (e.g., opioids) slow gastric emptying, leading to increased gastric distension, which also stimulates nausea pathways via the vagus nerve.
  • Step 4: In some cases, medications (e.g., chemotherapy, antibiotics) disrupt the balance of the inner ear, causing vestibular dysfunction and subsequent nausea and vomiting.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms usually occur within hours to days after starting the medication, especially with agents that act quickly (e.g., opioids, chemotherapy).
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms can persist with long-term medication use, especially with drugs like opioids and some antibiotics, as tolerance or adaptation may not develop.
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms typically resolve within a few days to weeks after discontinuation or dose adjustment of the offending medication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Nausea - A sensation of queasiness often felt in the upper stomach, primarily caused by activation of the CTZ and vagal afferents in response to drug-induced irritation or neurochemical imbalances.
  • Vomiting - The physical expulsion of stomach contents, mediated by the vomiting centre in the brainstem after stimulation by the CTZ or vagal afferents.
  • Anorexia - Loss of appetite often accompanies nausea and vomiting due to the gastrointestinal discomfort and vagal stimulation.
  • Dizziness - Certain medications, especially those affecting the vestibular system, can cause dizziness, which is closely linked to nausea.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Dehydration (dry mucous membranes, tachycardia) - Repeated vomiting can lead to significant fluid loss, causing these clinical signs.
  • Hypotension - Volume depletion from fluid loss due to vomiting may result in low blood pressure.
  • Electrolyte imbalance (e.g., hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia) - Vomiting leads to the loss of electrolytes, particularly potassium and sodium, contributing to these abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration (sunken eyes, lethargy) - Signifies significant volume depletion, which can lead to acute kidney injury or shock if untreated.
  • Persistent vomiting (>24 hours) - May suggest severe toxicity or drug overdose, potentially requiring urgent intervention.
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting of blood may indicate gastrointestinal mucosal injury (e.g., erosions, ulcers) from medications like NSAIDs.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Mild, intermittent nausea without vomiting - In some cases, especially with drugs like selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), only mild nausea may be present without vomiting, making diagnosis more difficult.
  • Delayed onset nausea/vomiting - In patients taking certain medications (e.g., chemotherapy), nausea and vomiting may occur days after the drug is administered.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show signs of dehydration (e.g., elevated haematocrit).
  • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es) - Typically reveals electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia) from vomiting.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - To rule out hepatic causes of nausea and vomiting.
  • Imaging (e.g., abdominal ultrasound) - Used if structural causes (e.g., bowel obstruction) are suspected in addition to medication effects.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old male presents with persistent nausea and vomiting 3 days after starting opioids for postoperative pain. He reports no appetite and occasional dizziness. Examination reveals dry mucous membranes and tachycardia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - Distinguished by accompanying fever and diarrhoea, which are absent in MINV.
  • Vestibular neuronitis - Predominantly causes vertigo, with nausea secondary to vestibular dysfunction, unlike direct gastrointestinal effects in MINV.
  • Diabetic gastroparesis - Often associated with longstanding diabetes, characterised by delayed gastric emptying similar to some medication effects, but accompanied by poorly controlled blood glucose levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Medication review - Identify and discontinue or reduce the offending medication where possible.
  • Antiemetics - Agents like cyclizine or ondansetron are often used to control symptoms in the UK. Metoclopramide may be considered for its prokinetic effects, especially if opioid-induced gastroparesis is suspected.
  • Hydration and electrolyte correction - Oral or IV fluids with electrolyte replacement in cases of dehydration and significant vomiting.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbances - Frequent vomiting can cause life-threatening imbalances such as hypokalaemia, which can lead to cardiac arrhythmias.
  • Mallory-Weiss tears - Severe or forceful vomiting can cause tears in the oesophagus, leading to haematemesis.
  • Acute kidney injury - Secondary to dehydration, particularly in elderly or frail patients.
