Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD) refers to a group of over 200 chronic lung disorders characterised by inflammation and fibrosis of the lung interstitium (the tissue surrounding alveoli), leading to impaired gas exchange. ILD includes conditions such as idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), sarcoidosis, and hypersensitivity pneumonitis. These disorders share similar features like dyspnoea and reduced lung compliance, but vary in their aetiologies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of ILD in the UK GP population: Around 1 in 5,000.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF) affects 15-25 per 100,000 people annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: ILD prevalence varies with IPF being the most common form in many countries, with an estimated prevalence of 13-20 per 100,000 people.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • ILD involves injury to the alveolar epithelial cells, which triggers an abnormal repair process, leading to chronic inflammation and fibrosis. Over time, fibrosis thickens the interstitium, limiting oxygen diffusion and reducing lung compliance. The immune response also plays a role, with activated macrophages, neutrophils, and lymphocytes contributing to tissue damage and fibrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: ILD often begins with asymptomatic or mild symptoms like exertional dyspnoea and a dry cough, which can last months to years depending on the underlying cause.
  • Progressive Phase: Over time, as fibrosis progresses, symptoms become more severe, with worsening breathlessness, fatigue, and reduced exercise tolerance. Disease progression can vary from slow (over years) to rapid (within months).
  • Late Phase: End-stage ILD is marked by significant lung fibrosis, pulmonary hypertension, and respiratory failure, leading to significant disability and reduced survival.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Exertional dyspnoea - Due to reduced lung compliance and impaired gas exchange from interstitial fibrosis.
  • Dry cough - Often non-productive, caused by fibrosis and inflammation irritating the airways.
  • Fatigue - Related to the chronic hypoxia and increased work of breathing.
  • Pleuritic chest pain - In some forms of ILD (e.g., connective tissue-related), this occurs due to pleural involvement.
  • Weight loss - Common in advanced disease due to systemic inflammation and reduced appetite.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Fine end-inspiratory crackles (velcro-like) - Heard on auscultation, reflecting the opening of collapsed airways in fibrotic lungs.
  • Clubbing - Often present in advanced IPF and related to chronic hypoxia.
  • Cyanosis - Indicates severe hypoxaemia, usually in the later stages.
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate due to impaired oxygenation.
  • Reduced chest expansion - A result of reduced lung compliance and fibrosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of dyspnoea - May indicate acute exacerbation of ILD, which has a poor prognosis and may require urgent intervention.
  • Haemoptysis - Could signify pulmonary vasculitis, infection, or malignancy, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Severe hypoxaemia and cyanosis - Suggest advanced disease and impending respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Early-stage ILD may be detected incidentally on imaging, particularly in patients with risk factors such as occupational exposures or autoimmune diseases.
  • Isolated cough - Some patients present primarily with a chronic cough, without significant dyspnoea until later stages.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Spirometry - Shows a restrictive pattern (reduced FVC, normal or increased FEV1/FVC ratio) due to reduced lung volumes.
  • Chest X-ray - May show bilateral reticulonodular opacities, often in the lower zones.
  • High-resolution CT (HRCT) - The gold standard for diagnosis, showing patterns like honeycombing (in IPF) or ground-glass opacities (inflammation).
  • Lung biopsy - May be required to confirm the diagnosis in uncertain cases, showing varying degrees of fibrosis and inflammation.
  • Blood tests - Autoimmune panels may be relevant for connective tissue disease-associated ILD.
  • Arterial blood gases (ABG) - Show hypoxaemia and, in later stages, hypercapnia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of worsening exertional dyspnoea and a dry cough. He has bilateral fine inspiratory crackles on examination, and a chest X-ray shows reticular shadowing in the lower zones.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Differs by showing an obstructive pattern on spirometry and hyperinflation on imaging.
  • Heart failure - Features include a history of cardiovascular disease, raised JVP, and cardiomegaly on imaging.
  • Bronchiectasis - Presents with a productive cough and recurrent infections, with dilated bronchi on imaging.
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis - Exposure history is crucial; imaging shows centrilobular nodules and ground-glass changes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF): Antifibrotic agents (pirfenidone or nintedanib) slow disease progression.
  • Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis: Remove the offending antigen and consider corticosteroids for severe cases.
  • Oxygen therapy: For patients with hypoxaemia at rest or exertion.
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation: To improve exercise tolerance and quality of life.
  • Lung transplantation: Considered for end-stage disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)"][/sc]
  • Acute exacerbation of ILD - Sudden worsening of respiratory symptoms, often with increased ground-glass opacities on CT, leading to respiratory failure.
  • Pulmonary hypertension - Caused by chronic hypoxia leading to vascular remodelling and increased pulmonary arterial pressure.
  • Cor pulmonale - Right-sided heart failure due to pulmonary hypertension, contributing to peripheral oedema and worsening fatigue.
