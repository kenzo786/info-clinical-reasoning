Malnutrition
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition refers to an imbalance between nutrient intake and the body's requirements, leading to either undernutrition (inadequate intake of energy and nutrients) or overnutrition (excessive intake). In clinical settings, it is most commonly associated with undernutrition, characterised by weight loss, muscle wasting, and deficiencies in essential vitamins and minerals.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Malnutrition affects approximately 5% of the UK general population, with higher rates seen in older adults and individuals with chronic diseases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: 30-40% of hospitalised patients are at risk of malnutrition, particularly those with long-term illnesses such as cancer, gastrointestinal diseases, or chronic respiratory conditions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Malnutrition is a global issue, with undernutrition prevalent in low-income countries (around 9.2% in 2020) and overnutrition (obesity-related) increasing in both developed and developing nations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition develops when there is a chronic imbalance between nutrient intake and the body’s demands. In undernutrition, insufficient intake of calories, proteins, and essential vitamins leads to catabolism of fat stores and muscle mass. This triggers metabolic, hormonal, and immune dysfunctions, reducing muscle strength, impairing wound healing, and increasing vulnerability to infection. In overnutrition, excessive intake of nutrients, especially fats and sugars, leads to obesity, metabolic syndrome, and cardiovascular complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Occurs over days to weeks, often triggered by illness or injury, leading to rapid weight loss and nutrient depletion.
  • Chronic phase: Develops over months or years due to gradual inadequacy in dietary intake, often exacerbated by chronic diseases like cancer or COPD. Symptoms may be subtle initially but worsen over time, leading to progressive weight loss and muscle wasting.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss - Occurs due to a deficit in caloric intake and increased catabolism of fat and muscle.
  • Fatigue and weakness - A result of decreased energy stores and muscle wasting.
  • Loss of appetite - Often due to underlying conditions such as infections, cancer, or gastrointestinal issues that reduce the desire or ability to eat.
  • Reduced immune function (frequent infections) - Malnutrition impairs the immune response due to protein-energy deficiencies, making individuals more susceptible to infections.
  • Cold intolerance - Loss of fat stores leads to decreased insulation and thermoregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Muscle wasting - Visible loss of muscle mass, particularly in the temples, shoulders, and limbs, due to protein catabolism.
  • Oedema - Peripheral oedema, especially in the ankles, due to low serum protein levels causing decreased oncotic pressure.
  • Dry, flaky skin - Indicative of deficiencies in essential fatty acids and vitamins such as vitamin A.
  • Brittle hair or hair loss - Results from protein and micronutrient deficiencies.
  • Glossitis (swollen, inflamed tongue) - Suggestive of vitamin B deficiencies, particularly B12 and folate.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Severe weight loss (>10% body weight in 3-6 months) - Indicates significant nutritional deficiency, often seen in advanced disease or acute severe illness (e.g., cancer cachexia).
  • Recurrent or severe infections - Suggests immune compromise due to protein-energy malnutrition, increasing the risk of complications like sepsis.
  • Altered mental status or confusion - May indicate severe vitamin or electrolyte deficiencies (e.g., thiamine deficiency in Wernicke’s encephalopathy).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Obesity with micronutrient deficiency - Individuals may have an excess intake of calories but remain deficient in essential vitamins and minerals, particularly in overconsumption of processed foods.
  • Subtle or no weight loss in early chronic disease - Especially in elderly patients or those with chronic illness, weight loss may be gradual or absent initially, masking malnutrition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Body mass index (BMI) - A BMI <18.5 kg/m² suggests undernutrition. Severe malnutrition is classified as BMI <16 kg/m².
  • Serum albumin - Low levels (<35 g/L) are common in chronic malnutrition, indicating protein deficiency, though this can also be affected by inflammation.
  • Prealbumin (transthyretin) - A more sensitive marker of acute malnutrition, as its half-life is shorter than albumin.
  • Serum electrolytes - Electrolyte imbalances, particularly hypokalaemia or hyponatraemia, can suggest malnutrition or refeeding syndrome.
  • Micronutrient assays - Deficiencies in vitamins (A, B12, folate, D) and trace elements (zinc, selenium) may be detected through targeted testing.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old woman with COPD presents with progressive weight loss over six months, fatigue, and frequent infections. On examination, she has visible muscle wasting and bilateral ankle oedema. BMI is 17 kg/m², and serum albumin is 28 g/L.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Cachexia - Differs from malnutrition by being driven by systemic inflammation (e.g., cancer) with marked muscle wasting, often despite adequate calorie intake.
  • Anorexia nervosa - Psychological disorder with extreme food restriction leading to severe undernutrition, often with distinct psychological symptoms (e.g., distorted body image).
  • Chronic gastrointestinal disease (e.g., Crohn's disease) - Malabsorption of nutrients due to intestinal inflammation, presenting with weight loss, diarrhoea, and anaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Dietary management - High-calorie, high-protein diet with oral nutritional supplements. Referral to a dietitian is recommended.
  • Micronutrient supplementation - Replace deficient vitamins and minerals as needed (e.g., vitamin D, B12).
  • Enteral feeding - For patients unable to meet nutritional needs orally (e.g., via nasogastric or percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy tubes).
  • Treatment of underlying conditions - Address underlying disease driving malnutrition, such as optimising COPD management or treating infections.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Infection risk - Frequent infections occur due to impaired immune response, potentially leading to sepsis in severe cases.
  • Refeeding syndrome - Rapid reintroduction of nutrition can cause severe electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypophosphataemia), leading to arrhythmias and multi-organ failure.
  • Pressure ulcers - Malnourished patients are at increased risk of skin breakdown and pressure ulcers due to immobility and poor tissue repair.
