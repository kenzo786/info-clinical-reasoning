Sepsis in Children
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Sepsis in children is a life-threatening condition caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, leading to organ dysfunction. It is characterised by systemic inflammation, abnormalities in perfusion, and multi-organ involvement.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Rare but critical, presenting as an emergency.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 5–10 cases per 1,000 paediatric admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated 22,000 cases per 100,000 children annually, with significant variation based on healthcare access.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Initial infection triggers activation of the immune system.
  • Pro-inflammatory mediators (e.g., cytokines) cause systemic inflammation.
  • Endothelial dysfunction and microvascular thrombosis result in impaired perfusion and organ hypoxia.
  • Severe cases involve multi-organ failure due to persistent hypoperfusion and metabolic derangements.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Symptoms and signs develop within hours to a few days.
  • Progression: Rapid deterioration if untreated, often leading to septic shock and organ failure.
  • Recovery phase: Variable; can involve prolonged convalescence or persistent sequelae in severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Fever or hypothermia - Result of the host inflammatory response; fever due to cytokine release (e.g., IL-1, TNF-α).
  • Lethargy or irritability - Indicative of central nervous system dysfunction due to hypoperfusion or metabolic disturbances.
  • Breathing difficulties - Tachypnoea and laboured breathing result from metabolic acidosis and cytokine-induced pulmonary involvement.
  • Poor feeding - Early sign of systemic illness due to metabolic imbalance and altered gastrointestinal perfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Reflects compensatory response to hypovolaemia and systemic inflammation.
  • Hypotension - Late finding due to cardiovascular decompensation and septic shock.
  • Cool, mottled extremities - Indicate poor perfusion and microvascular dysfunction.
  • Altered mental status - Reflects cerebral hypoperfusion or metabolic encephalopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Non-blanching rash - May indicate disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
  • Severe tachycardia or bradycardia - Suggests imminent cardiovascular collapse.
  • Altered consciousness - Reflects impending organ failure, often involving the CNS.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Isolated respiratory distress - May mimic primary respiratory conditions.
  • Prolonged fever without focus - Seen in partially treated sepsis or less virulent pathogens.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Blood cultures - Identify causative organisms; crucial for targeted therapy.
  • Full blood count - Leukocytosis or leukopenia with neutrophil changes indicate systemic infection.
  • Lactate levels - Elevated in hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old girl presents with high fever, lethargy, tachypnoea, and cool extremities over the past 12 hours, suggestive of early sepsis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Meningitis - Differentiated by neck stiffness and positive Brudzinski's sign.
  • Urinary tract infection - Often presents with fever and dysuria but lacks systemic features early on.
  • Viral infections - Typically associated with milder systemic involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Initial resuscitation - Fluid boluses (20 mL/kg isotonic fluids) for shock.
  • Antibiotics - Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone) within 1 hour of recognition.
  • Monitoring - Frequent reassessment of vitals and perfusion parameters.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sepsis in Children"][/sc]
  • Septic shock - High mortality if unrecognised and untreated.
  • Multi-organ failure - May involve kidneys, lungs, heart, or brain.
  • Long-term sequelae - Cognitive and physical disabilities in severe cases.
