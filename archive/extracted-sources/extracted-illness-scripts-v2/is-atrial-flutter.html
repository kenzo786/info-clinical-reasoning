Atrial Flutter
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Atrial flutter is a type of supraventricular tachycardia characterised by a rapid, regular atrial rate, typically between 240 and 340 beats per minute. It is caused by a reentrant circuit in the atria, leading to ineffective atrial contractions. The ventricular rate is usually slower, as the atrioventricular (AV) node limits conduction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated to be around 0.1–0.2%.
  • Atrial flutter is less common than atrial fibrillation, with a prevalence of 0.1–0.4% in hospitalised patients in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence is variable, but similar patterns of occurrence are noted, often linked to co-morbidities such as hypertension and structural heart disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Atrial flutter is primarily caused by a macro-reentrant circuit within the atria, most commonly located in the right atrium. This leads to rapid and regular atrial depolarisation.
  • The AV node blocks some of the impulses, typically resulting in a ventricular response rate of about 150 bpm, as only every second or third impulse is conducted.
  • The ineffective atrial contractions lead to impaired ventricular filling and, if prolonged, can result in atrial thrombus formation due to blood stasis, increasing the risk of stroke.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: May occur suddenly, often triggered by a stressor such as illness, surgery, or electrolyte imbalance.
  • Chronic: Episodes can become recurrent or persistent, especially in patients with underlying heart disease or other co-morbidities.
  • Paroxysmal: Episodes that come and go spontaneously or in response to treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Patients commonly report a sensation of rapid or irregular heartbeat due to the rapid atrial rate, which can be felt as a fluttering or racing sensation.
  • Fatigue - The inefficiency of atrial contractions can lead to reduced cardiac output, causing fatigue and generalised weakness.
  • Dyspnoea - Shortness of breath can result from the reduced cardiac output and impaired ventricular filling.
  • Chest pain - Especially in patients with underlying coronary artery disease, decreased perfusion may cause chest pain or discomfort.
  • Dizziness or syncope - Reduced cerebral perfusion due to lower stroke volume can cause light-headedness or even fainting in severe cases.
  • Asymptomatic - Some patients, particularly those with a lower ventricular response, may present with no symptoms, with atrial flutter being incidentally discovered during routine exams.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Rapid pulse - The most common sign is a rapid but regular pulse if the AV node conducts every 2nd or 4th atrial impulse, leading to a ventricular rate of ~150 bpm.
  • Jugular venous pulsation - Visible jugular venous pulse waves ("flutter waves") may be seen in the neck, particularly during episodes of atrial flutter.
  • Irregular heart sounds - Although atrial flutter is typically regular, an irregular ventricular response may be noted if AV conduction is variable.
  • Signs of heart failure - In cases of long-standing atrial flutter, patients may exhibit peripheral oedema, raised jugular venous pressure (JVP), and pulmonary crackles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Indicates potential haemodynamic instability due to rapid ventricular response or insufficient cardiac output.
  • Severe chest pain - May signify myocardial ischaemia, especially in those with underlying coronary artery disease.
  • Signs of heart failure - Peripheral oedema, pulmonary congestion, or marked dyspnoea may indicate heart failure as a complication of prolonged atrial flutter.
  • Neurological deficits - Sudden onset of neurological symptoms such as weakness or speech difficulties may indicate a stroke, which is a serious complication of atrial flutter due to embolisation of atrial thrombi.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia - Atypical cases may present with bradycardia due to advanced AV block, leading to a slower ventricular response.
  • Silent atrial flutter - Particularly in elderly patients or those with a slower ventricular rate, atrial flutter can present with vague symptoms like fatigue or may be discovered incidentally on ECG.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • ECG - Characteristic "sawtooth" flutter waves are seen, most prominent in leads II, III, and aVF. The atrial rate is typically between 240–340 bpm.
  • Echocardiography - Used to assess for structural heart disease or atrial enlargement, which can predispose to atrial flutter.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count, U&Es, thyroid function tests (to exclude electrolyte imbalances and thyrotoxicosis as potential causes).
  • Holter monitoring - Ambulatory ECG monitoring may be required to detect paroxysmal episodes of atrial flutter.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • A 67-year-old male presents with a two-day history of palpitations, shortness of breath, and light-headedness. He has a history of hypertension. ECG shows atrial flutter with a ventricular rate of 150 bpm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation - Typically presents with an irregularly irregular pulse, whereas atrial flutter often has a regular rhythm.
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) - SVT has a narrow QRS complex and a regular rhythm, but flutter waves are absent on the ECG.
  • Ventricular tachycardia - Usually presents with a broad QRS complex and no evidence of atrial flutter waves on ECG.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Rate control - Beta-blockers (e.g., bisoprolol) or calcium channel blockers (e.g., diltiazem) are used to control the ventricular rate.
  • Rhythm control - Cardioversion is often used, either electrical (DC cardioversion) or pharmacological (e.g., amiodarone), to restore sinus rhythm.
  • Anticoagulation - Due to the risk of stroke, anticoagulation (e.g., DOACs or warfarin) is recommended in most patients based on CHA₂DS₂-VASc score.
  • Catheter ablation - In cases of recurrent or refractory atrial flutter, ablation of the reentrant circuit can be curative.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atrial Flutter"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Embolisation of thrombi formed in the atria can lead to ischaemic stroke, particularly in those without anticoagulation.
  • Heart failure - Prolonged atrial flutter can lead to heart failure due to sustained tachycardia and impaired ventricular filling.
  • Cardiomyopathy - Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy can develop in patients with chronic, uncontrolled atrial flutter.
