Herpes Zoster (Shingles)
Herpes Zoster Definition [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="101" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Herpes zoster, also known as shingles, is a viral infection caused by the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), the same virus responsible for chickenpox. It typically presents as a painful, unilateral vesicular rash that follows a dermatomal distribution. The condition can lead to complications such as postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) and is more common in older or immunocompromised individuals.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Increasing age, immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, chemotherapy, organ transplantation), psychological stress, and conditions like diabetes mellitus elevate the risk of herpes zoster. Patients with a history of varicella infection are at risk of viral reactivation, particularly if they did not receive childhood vaccination.
  • Prevention: The shingles vaccine (Shingrix) is recommended for adults aged 70–79 in the UK, significantly reducing the risk of herpes zoster and postherpetic neuralgia. Maintaining a healthy immune system through a balanced diet, adequate sleep, and managing chronic illnesses can also help prevent reactivation.
Herpes Zoster Pathophysiology [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="103" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Initial varicella-zoster virus (VZV) infection occurs during childhood, presenting as chickenpox.
  • Step 2: After the initial infection, the virus becomes latent in the dorsal root ganglia or cranial nerve ganglia.
  • Step 3: Years to decades later, in the context of weakened immunity (ageing, stress, or immunosuppression), VZV reactivates.
  • Step 4: Reactivated virus travels along sensory nerves to the skin, leading to inflammation and a vesicular rash along a dermatome.
  • Step 5: The immune response to the virus and nerve involvement leads to the pain and other symptoms characteristic of herpes zoster.
Herpes Zoster Time Course [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="104" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: 1–5 days of localised pain, itching, or paraesthesia in the affected dermatome before the rash appears.
  • Acute phase: The vesicular rash develops, typically lasting 7–10 days before crusting over.
  • Resolution: The rash usually heals within 2–4 weeks, although postherpetic neuralgia can persist for months to years in some cases.
Herpes Zoster Symptoms [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="105" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Localized pain - Often the first symptom, pain occurs in the dermatome where the virus reactivates, caused by inflammation of sensory nerves.
  • Itching or tingling - Occurs due to nerve involvement before the rash appears, as the virus travels along sensory neurons.
  • Vesicular rash - Appears after the prodrome, with fluid-filled blisters along a dermatome due to viral replication in the skin.
  • Fatigue - Common due to the body's immune response to the viral reactivation.
  • Headache and fever - These systemic symptoms occur less frequently but are a response to the viral reactivation and immune response.
Herpes Zoster Signs [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="106" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Unilateral vesicular rash - Clusters of vesicles appear along a single dermatome, often thoracic or cranial, due to viral spread in sensory neurons.
  • Erythema - The skin surrounding the vesicles is inflamed as part of the immune response.
  • Crusting lesions - The vesicles eventually rupture and crust over, usually 7–10 days after onset.
  • Postherpetic neuralgia - Persistent pain in the same dermatome after the rash resolves, due to nerve damage from viral inflammation.
Herpes Zoster Red Flag Features [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="107" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus - Involvement of the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve, risking vision loss due to corneal involvement.
  • Disseminated herpes zoster - Widespread rash beyond the primary dermatome, indicating immunosuppression and risk of systemic involvement.
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome - Reactivation in the geniculate ganglion leading to facial paralysis, ear pain, and vesicles in the ear canal, often with hearing loss.
Herpes Zoster Atypical Presentations [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="108" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Zoster sine herpete - Reactivation of VZV without the characteristic rash. Presents only with neuropathic pain, making diagnosis difficult without confirmatory testing.
  • Multiple dermatomal involvement - Instead of the usual single dermatome, multiple adjacent dermatomes may be affected, indicating more extensive nerve involvement.
Herpes Zoster Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="109" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Viral PCR of vesicle fluid - Detects VZV DNA in vesicle fluid or skin swabs, confirming diagnosis.
  • Tzanck smear - Microscopic examination of vesicular fluid may show multinucleated giant cells, but this is non-specific for VZV.
  • Serology - Antibodies to VZV can be detected, but these are not typically required in a clinical diagnosis unless there is diagnostic uncertainty.

Herpes Zoster Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="111" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis - Often bilateral and involves itching more than pain, without a dermatomal distribution or vesicles.
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection - Can cause vesicular lesions but typically occurs around the mouth or genitals and lacks the dermatomal pattern seen in herpes zoster.
  • Cellulitis - Presents with erythema and swelling, but there are no vesicles or neuropathic pain, and it is not confined to a dermatome.
Herpes Zoster Treatment [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="112" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Antiviral therapy - Oral aciclovir, valaciclovir, or famciclovir for 7 days, ideally started within 72 hours of rash onset to reduce severity and duration.
  • Pain management - Paracetamol, NSAIDs, and in cases of postherpetic neuralgia, gabapentin or amitriptyline.
  • Shingles vaccine - Offered to people aged 70–79 years to reduce the risk of herpes zoster and postherpetic neuralgia.
Herpes Zoster Complications [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Herpes Zoster" select="115" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Herpes Zoster (Shingles)"][/sc]
  • Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) - Persistent neuropathic pain in the affected dermatome lasting months to years, caused by nerve damage.
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus - Can lead to corneal damage and vision loss.
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome - Facial paralysis and hearing loss due to VZV reactivation in the geniculate ganglion.
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Vesicular lesions may become superinfected, particularly with Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes.
