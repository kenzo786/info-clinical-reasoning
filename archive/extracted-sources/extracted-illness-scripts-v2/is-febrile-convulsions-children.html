Febrile Convulsions (Children)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Febrile convulsions are seizures that occur in children aged 6 months to 5 years, triggered by fever without evidence of intracranial infection or other defined causes. These are generalised tonic-clonic seizures and typically last less than 15 minutes (simple febrile seizures), though more complex presentations may occur.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Febrile convulsions affect 2-5% of children in the UK, with a peak incidence between 18 and 24 months of age. They are more common in boys and have a recurrence rate of about 30-40%. Globally, the incidence is similar across most populations, though it can be influenced by genetic and environmental factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Febrile convulsions are thought to result from an immature brain's response to fever, particularly in children with genetic predisposition. A rapid rise in body temperature can lead to a dysregulated neuronal firing pattern, precipitating a seizure. The underlying fever is typically caused by viral infections, such as those of the respiratory or gastrointestinal tract.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • The febrile seizure often coincides with the rapid onset of fever, usually within 24 hours of the fever’s onset. Simple febrile seizures typically last less than 15 minutes and do not recur within 24 hours, while complex febrile seizures last longer, occur more than once in 24 hours, or have focal features. The fever itself may last for 2-3 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Generalised tonic-clonic movements - This is the most common presentation, characterised by a sudden onset of unconsciousness followed by stiffening (tonic) and jerking (clonic) movements of the limbs due to widespread neuronal discharge.
  • Fever - Typically above 38°C and often the first sign of an underlying infection, usually viral in origin.
  • Drowsiness or confusion post-seizure - This postictal phase is common following the seizure and represents transient neuronal exhaustion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Hyperthermia - High body temperature (>38°C) due to fever, a trigger for the seizure.
  • Generalised muscle stiffness and jerking - Seen during the tonic and clonic phases of the seizure, respectively.
  • Altered consciousness - During and after the seizure, the child may be unconscious or exhibit postictal confusion or drowsiness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Seizure lasting >15 minutes - May suggest a complex febrile seizure or an underlying neurological issue like meningitis.
  • Focal neurological signs - Such as hemiparesis or unilateral jerking, raising concern for focal epilepsy or structural brain lesions.
  • Recurrent seizures within 24 hours - A feature of complex febrile seizures, associated with a higher risk of future epilepsy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Febrile seizures may rarely present with subtle focal features, such as twitching confined to one side of the body, or brief episodes of unresponsiveness without tonic-clonic movements, potentially leading to a misdiagnosis of absence seizures.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Temperature measurement - To confirm febrile status; fever is a prerequisite for diagnosis.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC) and inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) may be used to identify the underlying cause of the fever, usually infection.
  • Lumbar puncture - Performed if there is suspicion of meningitis or encephalitis (e.g., child is drowsy, or has neck stiffness or photophobia).
  • EEG - Rarely indicated for simple febrile seizures but may be used in complex cases to rule out epilepsy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • A previously healthy 18-month-old boy presents with a 39°C fever and a generalised tonic-clonic seizure lasting 2 minutes. The seizure occurs 12 hours after the onset of the fever, and he has a brief postictal period of confusion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy - Unlike febrile seizures, epilepsy may present without fever and often recurs without a clear trigger.
  • Meningitis - Associated with fever and seizure, but also neck stiffness, photophobia, and altered consciousness, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Encephalitis - Fever, seizures, and altered mental status with focal neurological signs or personality changes, often viral in origin (e.g., herpes simplex).
  • Breath-holding spells - Occur in young children but are typically triggered by emotional upset, not fever.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Initial management - Provide reassurance to parents. Simple febrile seizures do not require long-term anticonvulsant therapy.
  • Antipyretics - Paracetamol or ibuprofen to manage fever, though these do not prevent seizures.
  • Emergency care for prolonged seizures - Benzodiazepines (e.g., rectal diazepam or buccal midazolam) are used for seizures lasting longer than 5 minutes.
  • Referral - Children with complex febrile seizures should be referred for further evaluation, including possible neuroimaging and EEG.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Febrile Convulsions (Children)"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy - Around 2-4% of children with febrile seizures develop epilepsy later in life, particularly if they have complex seizures, a family history of epilepsy, or abnormal neurological development.
  • Recurrent febrile seizures - About 30-40% of children experience recurrence, especially if the first seizure occurred before 12 months of age.
  • Developmental delay - Rarely, recurrent complex febrile seizures may be associated with mild developmental delay.
