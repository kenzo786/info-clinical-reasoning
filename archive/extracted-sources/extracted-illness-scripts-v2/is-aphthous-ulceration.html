Aphthous Ulceration Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Aphthous ulceration, also known as canker sores or recurrent aphthous stomatitis (RAS), is a common condition characterised by recurrent, painful ulcers of the oral mucosa. The ulcers are typically small, shallow, round or oval with a grey or yellow base surrounded by a red halo. They commonly occur on the non-keratinised mucosal surfaces such as the inner lips, cheeks, and tongue. Aphthous ulcers are benign, non-infectious, and often self-limiting.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 10-20% of the population is affected by recurrent aphthous ulceration, making it one of the most common oral mucosal diseases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Rarely leads to hospitalisation, though severe or persistent cases may be seen in secondary care, particularly in patients with underlying systemic conditions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects around 2-25% of the global population. More common in developed countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of aphthous ulceration is unknown, but it is thought to involve a T-cell-mediated immune response that leads to the destruction of epithelial cells in the oral mucosa. Triggers may include local trauma, stress, hormonal changes, food sensitivities, and micronutrient deficiencies (e.g., iron, folate, vitamin B12). Genetic predisposition also plays a role, with family history increasing the likelihood of recurrence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Small painful lesions appear within 1-2 days after prodromal burning or tingling in the affected area.
  • Healing phase: Ulcers typically heal without scarring within 7-14 days in mild cases. Larger ulcers (major aphthae) may take up to 6 weeks to resolve.
  • Recurrent episodes: Many patients experience multiple recurrences, often with symptom-free intervals lasting from weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Painful oral ulcers - Typically, these ulcers are the main symptom and can cause significant discomfort when eating, drinking, or speaking. The pain arises due to nerve exposure in the ulcer bed.
  • Tingling or burning sensation - Preceding the formation of ulcers, this sensation occurs as an early sign of mucosal irritation and impending ulceration.
  • Difficulty eating and speaking - As the ulcer size increases or as multiple ulcers develop, pain can interfere with oral function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Round or oval ulcers - These are clearly demarcated lesions with a grey or yellow base and a surrounding red halo, typically seen on non-keratinised mucosa (e.g., inside cheeks, lips, or under the tongue).
  • Single or multiple lesions - The number can vary from a single ulcer to multiple ulcers scattered across different oral sites.
  • Non-involvement of keratinised tissue - Ulcers typically spare the hard palate, gingiva, and other keratinised surfaces, helping to distinguish aphthous ulcers from other ulcerative conditions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Ulcers persisting beyond 3 weeks - Could signify an underlying systemic condition such as Crohn’s disease or oral cancer. Chronic ulcers that fail to heal should be further investigated.
  • Large, deep ulcers (>1 cm) - Known as major aphthae, they may indicate an underlying immunodeficiency or systemic disorder like Behçet's disease.
  • Multiple recurrent ulcers with systemic symptoms - Such as fever, weight loss, or fatigue, which could suggest systemic autoimmune diseases or gastrointestinal conditions (e.g., coeliac disease, HIV).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Ulcers on keratinised surfaces - Although rare, aphthous ulcers can occasionally involve the gingiva or palate, especially in immunocompromised patients. This can lead to diagnostic confusion with other ulcerative conditions such as herpetic stomatitis.
  • Non-painful ulcers - In some immunocompromised patients or those with neurological disorders, aphthous ulcers may present with minimal pain, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on the appearance and recurrence pattern of the ulcers.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC) may reveal anaemia or neutropenia, and deficiencies in iron, folate, or vitamin B12 may also be investigated. Coeliac screening may be indicated in persistent cases.
  • Biopsy - Rarely required but may be performed if malignancy or other systemic conditions (e.g., oral lichen planus) are suspected. Biopsy typically shows nonspecific ulceration with infiltration of lymphocytes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old female presents with recurrent, painful mouth ulcers occurring for 7-10 days every few months over the past year. The ulcers are small, round, and located on the inside of her lips, making eating and speaking difficult.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Oral Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) infection - Typically presents with painful blisters or vesicles that rupture, forming ulcers. Unlike aphthous ulcers, these often involve keratinised mucosa (e.g., hard palate).
  • Oral lichen planus - Features white reticular patterns or erosive lesions on the oral mucosa, often more persistent and less painful than aphthous ulcers.
  • Behçet's disease - Recurrent aphthous ulcers associated with genital ulcers, ocular involvement, and systemic vasculitis.
  • Coeliac disease - May present with recurrent aphthous ulcers, often accompanied by gastrointestinal symptoms such as diarrhoea, malabsorption, or weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Topical corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone buccal tablets or triamcinolone paste) - First-line treatment to reduce inflammation and pain.
  • Topical analgesics (e.g., benzydamine mouthwash or lidocaine gel) - Can provide symptomatic relief of pain.
  • Severe cases: Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) or immunosuppressants may be required in severe or refractory cases.
  • Nutritional supplements - If micronutrient deficiencies are identified, supplements (e.g., iron, folate, vitamin B12) may reduce recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aphthous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Occasionally, ulcers may become secondarily infected, leading to increased pain and delayed healing.
  • Chronic or recurrent ulcers - Persistent or frequently recurring ulcers can significantly impact quality of life by interfering with nutrition, oral hygiene, and social interaction.
  • Association with systemic diseases - Recurrent ulcers may be a manifestation of conditions like coeliac disease, Behçet's disease, or inflammatory bowel disease, requiring further investigation.
