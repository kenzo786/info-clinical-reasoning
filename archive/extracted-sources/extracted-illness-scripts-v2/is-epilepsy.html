Epilepsy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterised by a predisposition to recurrent, unprovoked seizures. These seizures result from abnormal, excessive, or synchronous neuronal activity in the brain. To be diagnosed, a person typically needs to have had two or more unprovoked seizures separated by at least 24 hours.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1 in 100 people.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 600,000 people in the UK live with epilepsy, accounting for 1% of the UK population.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 50 million people worldwide are affected by epilepsy, making it one of the most common neurological conditions globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy results from abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Seizures occur when a large number of neurons fire synchronously. This can result from multiple factors, including genetic predisposition, structural abnormalities in the brain, or metabolic disturbances.
  • Neuronal hyperexcitability and hypersynchrony are key to the pathophysiology, with a reduced threshold for neuronal firing, often linked to imbalances between excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters like glutamate and GABA.
  • Focal seizures arise from a specific area in the brain, while generalised seizures involve more widespread abnormal activity from both hemispheres.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Some people experience warning signs (mood changes, headaches, etc.) before a seizure.
  • Ictal phase: The actual seizure, lasting from seconds to a few minutes, characterised by abnormal neuronal activity.
  • Postictal phase: Period following the seizure, often with confusion, weakness (Todd’s paresis), or somnolence, lasting from minutes to hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Seizures - The hallmark symptom of epilepsy. They can vary from subtle, like absence seizures (staring spells), to severe tonic-clonic seizures (loss of consciousness and violent muscle contractions). These occur due to the sudden abnormal electrical discharge in the brain.
  • Aura - Some individuals experience an aura before a seizure, which can manifest as a strange sensation, smell, or emotional change, caused by focal brain activity.
  • Postictal confusion - Following a seizure, the brain takes time to recover, leading to confusion, fatigue, or amnesia for the event.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Tonic-clonic movements - The common physical manifestation of generalised seizures, characterised by stiffening (tonic phase) and jerking movements (clonic phase), resulting from widespread neuronal discharge.
  • Automatisms - Repetitive, involuntary movements (e.g., lip-smacking, picking at clothes), often seen in focal seizures, reflecting localised brain involvement.
  • Todd’s paresis - Transient focal weakness following a seizure, usually indicating the involvement of the motor cortex.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Status epilepticus - Seizure lasting longer than 5 minutes or recurrent seizures without full recovery between episodes. This is a medical emergency due to the risk of neuronal damage, hypoxia, and metabolic complications.
  • Sudden onset in adulthood - May suggest a structural lesion (tumour, stroke) or metabolic disturbance, necessitating urgent evaluation.
  • Focal neurological deficits post-seizure - Prolonged weakness or sensory changes (beyond Todd’s paresis) may indicate a stroke or other neurological insult.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Absence seizures - Often missed as brief episodes of staring, these are more common in children and can be mistaken for daydreaming or inattentiveness.
  • Focal seizures with retained awareness - Manifesting as strange sensations (e.g., déjà vu, odd smells), these can be under-recognised, especially if motor symptoms are minimal.
  • Nocturnal seizures - Seizures occurring during sleep can be challenging to identify, sometimes only presenting as waking confusion or bedwetting.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • EEG (Electroencephalogram) - Shows abnormal electrical activity during or between seizures. Characteristic findings include generalised spike-and-wave discharges in absence epilepsy and focal abnormalities in focal epilepsy.
  • MRI brain - Often used to identify structural causes, such as tumours, vascular lesions, or cortical dysplasia, especially in focal epilepsy.
  • Serum electrolytes and glucose - Hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, and other metabolic disturbances can provoke seizures, so identifying and correcting these is crucial.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old woman presents with recurrent episodes of loss of consciousness, muscle stiffening, and rhythmic jerking, lasting 1-2 minutes, followed by confusion and exhaustion. Symptoms have occurred intermittently for the past year.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Brief loss of consciousness due to transient cerebral hypoperfusion. Unlike epilepsy, syncope is often preceded by lightheadedness and does not involve postictal confusion or tonic-clonic movements.
  • Pseudoseizures (Non-epileptic attacks) - Psychogenic in origin, these episodes often lack stereotypical features of epileptic seizures, such as tongue biting or incontinence, and may have prolonged duration without postictal confusion.
  • Migraine with aura - May present with sensory disturbances or altered consciousness but usually lacks the motor activity or postictal state of seizures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • First-line: Sodium valproate or carbamazepine for generalised seizures. Lamotrigine is also commonly used. Guidelines recommend monotherapy, titrated to effect.
  • Second-line: If seizures remain uncontrolled, add-on therapy with levetiracetam, topiramate, or other anti-epileptic drugs may be considered.
  • Surgery: In drug-resistant epilepsy, surgical resection of a focal lesion (e.g., temporal lobectomy) may be curative in some patients.
  • Lifestyle modifications: Patients should avoid known seizure triggers (e.g., sleep deprivation, alcohol) and be counselled on safety measures, including driving regulations in the UK.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Status epilepticus: A life-threatening emergency that can result in brain damage, metabolic acidosis, and hypoxia if untreated.
  • Sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP): Rare but serious complication where patients with epilepsy unexpectedly die, thought to be due to cardiac or respiratory dysfunction.
  • Injuries: People with epilepsy are at higher risk of fractures, head trauma, and burns during seizures.
