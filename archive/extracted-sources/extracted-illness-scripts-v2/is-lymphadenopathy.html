Lymphadenopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy refers to the abnormal enlargement of lymph nodes, which can occur due to infection, inflammation, malignancy, or other immune-related processes. Lymph nodes are small, bean-shaped structures that are part of the lymphatic system, and they play a key role in immune surveillance by filtering pathogens and presenting antigens to immune cells.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy is a common finding in primary care, with up to 0.6% of UK GP consultations involving lymph node enlargement. In hospital settings, it is often associated with systemic diseases like infections, autoimmune disorders, and malignancies. Worldwide, the prevalence of lymphadenopathy varies depending on the region and underlying aetiology, with infectious causes being more common in developing countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Enlargement of lymph nodes occurs due to immune cell proliferation, infiltration by malignant cells, or increased cellular activity in response to infection or inflammation. During an immune response, lymphocytes and macrophages proliferate within the lymph nodes, leading to swelling. In malignancies like lymphoma or metastases, cancerous cells infiltrate and replace normal lymphatic tissue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset, typically associated with infections (e.g., bacterial or viral). Lymph nodes are tender and may resolve after the infection subsides.
  • Subacute: Gradual enlargement over weeks, often seen in chronic infections or inflammatory conditions.
  • Chronic: Lymphadenopathy persisting for months, often linked to malignancies or autoimmune diseases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Painless swelling of lymph nodes - Common in malignancies (e.g., lymphoma) due to tumour cell infiltration causing gradual, non-inflammatory enlargement.
  • Tender lymph nodes - Typically associated with infections as a result of local inflammation and immune cell proliferation.
  • Fever - Occurs in infections or systemic inflammatory conditions due to the release of pyrogens from immune cells.
  • Night sweats - Seen in lymphoma, related to systemic immune responses to malignant cells.
  • Weight loss - Common in malignancies or chronic infections due to increased metabolic demands.
  • Fatigue - Results from the body’s immune response to infection or malignancy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Enlarged lymph nodes (>1 cm) - The most common sign, reflecting underlying immune or malignant processes.
  • Firm, rubbery texture - Suggestive of malignancy, such as lymphoma, due to the infiltration of abnormal cells into the lymph node.
  • Tenderness to palpation - Indicates inflammatory causes, such as infection, where the nodes are acutely inflamed.
  • Redness or warmth over lymph node - Suggests acute bacterial infection, often in cases of lymphadenitis.
  • Splenomegaly - Seen in systemic infections (e.g., EBV) or haematologic malignancies due to widespread immune activity or infiltration of abnormal cells.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Non-tender, hard lymph nodes - Can signify metastatic cancer, where tumour cells invade lymph nodes and cause nodal fibrosis without inflammation.
  • Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy - Often associated with malignancy, particularly abdominal or thoracic cancers, as lymphatic drainage from these areas leads to nodal involvement.
  • Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, and fever ("B symptoms") - These are systemic symptoms of lymphoma, indicating an aggressive or advanced disease stage.
  • Lymphadenopathy lasting more than 4-6 weeks - Persistent lymph node enlargement raises suspicion of malignancy or chronic infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy without localised symptoms - Can be missed as a sign of systemic diseases like HIV or haematologic malignancies, which may present insidiously.
  • Lymphadenopathy in children - Reactive lymphadenopathy is common and often overlooked, but underlying infections or rare malignancies (e.g., leukaemia) should be considered.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show leukocytosis in infections or abnormal white cell lines in haematological malignancies like leukaemia.
  • Blood film - Can detect abnormal or immature white cells in leukaemia or lymphoma.
  • Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) - Elevated in malignancies such as lymphoma due to rapid cell turnover.
  • Ultrasound of lymph nodes - Can help assess the size, structure, and vascularity of nodes, with irregular or hypoechoic nodes suggesting malignancy.
  • Biopsy - Essential for diagnosing malignancy, confirming the presence of cancer cells in the node tissue.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-week history of painless, progressively enlarging cervical lymph nodes, weight loss, and night sweats. Examination reveals multiple firm, rubbery nodes in the cervical and supraclavicular regions.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis (EBV) - Presents with fever, sore throat, and posterior cervical lymphadenopathy. Unlike malignancies, nodes are typically tender, and there may be splenomegaly.
  • Hodgkin lymphoma - Usually presents with painless, firm lymphadenopathy and "B symptoms". Diagnosis is confirmed via lymph node biopsy showing Reed-Sternberg cells.
  • Metastatic cancer - Firm, fixed, and non-tender lymph nodes, particularly in older adults with a history of cancer. Biopsy confirms the diagnosis.
  • HIV infection - Generalised lymphadenopathy in multiple regions, along with systemic symptoms. Diagnosis is confirmed via serology and CD4 counts.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Infection-related lymphadenopathy - Treated with appropriate antibiotics or antiviral therapy depending on the causative organism (e.g., penicillin for streptococcal infections).
  • Lymphoma - Managed with chemotherapy (e.g., R-CHOP for non-Hodgkin lymphoma) and possibly radiotherapy. Stem cell transplantation may be considered in refractory cases.
  • Metastatic cancer - Treatment depends on the primary cancer but often includes surgery, chemotherapy, or radiotherapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Compression of adjacent structures - Enlarged lymph nodes can compress airways, blood vessels, or nerves, leading to symptoms such as dyspnoea or neurological deficits.
  • Sepsis from untreated infections - Bacterial lymphadenitis may progress to systemic infection if not adequately managed.
  • Transformation to aggressive lymphoma - In cases of indolent lymphomas, there is a risk of transformation to more aggressive subtypes, which worsens prognosis.
