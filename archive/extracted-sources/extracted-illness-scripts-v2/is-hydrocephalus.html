Hydrocephalus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Hydrocephalus is a neurological condition caused by an accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) within the ventricles of the brain, leading to increased intracranial pressure. This can result from impaired CSF flow, absorption, or excessive production. Types include communicating, non-communicating, normal pressure, and ex vacuo hydrocephalus, each defined by the underlying pathophysiological mechanism.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • UK General Population: Hydrocephalus is relatively rare, with an estimated prevalence of 0.1%.
  • UK Hospital Setting: Hydrocephalus accounts for approximately 0.5% of all neurosurgical admissions.
  • Worldwide: Hydrocephalus incidence varies by type, but is most common in neonates (0.5-1 per 1,000 live births) and in the elderly with normal pressure hydrocephalus.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • CSF is normally produced by the choroid plexus, circulates through the ventricles, and is absorbed by arachnoid granulations. In hydrocephalus, blockage (non-communicating), impaired absorption (communicating), or overproduction disrupts this balance, leading to excess CSF, increased ventricular size, and raised intracranial pressure. Chronic hydrocephalus can cause brain atrophy and ventriculomegaly, impacting neurological function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset symptoms, often due to trauma, haemorrhage, or acute obstruction.
  • Subacute/Chronic: Progressive symptoms developing over weeks to months, commonly seen in congenital or idiopathic cases.
  • Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH): Gradual progression, typically in elderly patients, leading to chronic cognitive and motor impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Headache - Caused by increased intracranial pressure stretching pain-sensitive structures in the meninges.
  • Nausea and Vomiting - Resulting from raised intracranial pressure affecting the brainstem and area postrema.
  • Gait Disturbance - Common in NPH, with ventricular enlargement impacting white matter tracts responsible for motor control.
  • Urinary Incontinence - Often seen in NPH, due to disrupted neural control over the bladder as the ventricles enlarge.
  • Cognitive Impairment - Progressive memory and concentration difficulties, common in NPH due to fronto-subcortical dysfunction.
  • Visual Disturbances - May present as blurred vision or "setting-sun sign" in infants, from increased intracranial pressure on cranial nerves.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Increased Head Circumference (infants) - Due to skull expansion from elevated CSF pressure.
  • Papilloedema - Swelling of the optic disc from raised intracranial pressure, seen in acute cases.
  • Setting-Sun Sign (infants) - Downward gaze caused by pressure on cranial nerve pathways.
  • Positive Romberg's Test - Unsteadiness due to impaired cerebellar or vestibular function in advanced cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Severe Headache with Rapid Progression - Suggests acute obstruction or haemorrhage requiring urgent intervention.
  • Loss of Consciousness - Indicates critical intracranial pressure elevation, risking herniation and brainstem compression.
  • Severe Visual Changes or Papilloedema - May indicate optic nerve damage due to persistent intracranial pressure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Isolated Gait Disturbance - Common in elderly patients, may be mistaken for normal ageing or Parkinson’s disease.
  • Subtle Cognitive Decline - Can be gradual, resembling dementia, particularly in NPH.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • CT/MRI Brain - Shows ventricular enlargement and CSF flow obstruction. MRI is preferred for detail, especially to detect aqueductal stenosis or tumours.
  • Lumbar Puncture - CSF pressure measurement may be elevated in communicating hydrocephalus.
  • Gait Assessment (for NPH) - Assess gait speed and symmetry before and after CSF drainage to evaluate symptom improvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male presents with a six-month history of worsening gait disturbance, urinary incontinence, and mild cognitive decline, consistent with normal pressure hydrocephalus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Parkinson’s Disease: Tremor and rigidity more pronounced, slow progression, lacks ventricular enlargement on imaging.
  • Alzheimer’s Disease: Predominantly cognitive decline without gait disturbance, absence of ventricular dilatation.
  • Brain Tumour: Symptoms may localise based on tumour location, with focal neurological deficits and distinct imaging findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt: Standard treatment for symptomatic hydrocephalus to drain excess CSF. Requires monitoring for infection and shunt malfunction.
  • Endoscopic Third Ventriculostomy (ETV): Alternative for non-communicating hydrocephalus, creating a bypass around obstruction.
  • Periodic Monitoring: For patients with stable symptoms, particularly in elderly patients with NPH who may have marginal benefit from invasive intervention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Shunt Infection: Occurs in 10-15% of cases, leading to meningitis or abscess formation.
  • Shunt Malfunction: Can cause rapid symptom recurrence and requires urgent surgical correction.
  • Brain Herniation: Life-threatening condition caused by critically high intracranial pressure.
  • Prognosis: Generally favourable in treated cases, but untreated hydrocephalus can result in progressive neurological decline and death.
