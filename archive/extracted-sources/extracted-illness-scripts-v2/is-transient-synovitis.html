Transient Synovitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Transient synovitis, also called “irritable hip,” is a self-limiting inflammatory condition of the synovium, usually affecting children. It primarily causes hip pain and limping, with no known specific infectious or traumatic cause. This condition is characterised by synovial inflammation, often following viral infection, and typically resolves within 1-2 weeks without long-term sequelae.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Commonest cause of acute hip pain in children aged 3-8 years.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Accounts for up to 70% of cases presenting with hip pain in paediatric emergency departments.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Affects 1-4% of children, with a higher prevalence in boys, at a male-to-female ratio of approximately 4:1.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Transient synovitis is thought to result from a transient inflammatory response in the hip synovium, often secondary to viral illness or minor trauma. Viral infection triggers a local inflammatory reaction, resulting in increased synovial fluid production and mild joint effusion. This effusion leads to pain and restricted movement due to distension of the joint capsule. Unlike bacterial infections, there is no significant purulence or joint destruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Onset: Rapid, usually over 24-48 hours, often following a recent viral illness.
  • Peak Symptoms: Within the first 1-3 days, characterised by limping and hip discomfort.
  • Resolution: Symptoms typically resolve within 1-2 weeks with supportive care, although residual discomfort may last slightly longer in some cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Hip Pain: Localised hip pain, usually unilateral, due to inflammatory synovial effusion.
  • Limping: Often the presenting symptom, caused by discomfort and limited range of motion.
  • Limited Range of Motion: Discomfort, particularly on internal rotation, secondary to joint capsule distension.
  • Referred Knee Pain: Due to shared sensory innervation from the obturator nerve.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Antalgic Gait: Weight-bearing gait disturbance, with reluctance to put weight on the affected side due to pain.
  • Hip Joint Tenderness: Pain on palpation around the hip joint, indicating localised inflammation.
  • Restricted Internal Rotation: Pain with this movement due to synovial effusion and inflammation.
  • Afebrile Status: Unlike septic arthritis, transient synovitis typically presents without fever.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Fever >38°C: May indicate septic arthritis, a serious bacterial infection requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe Pain and Inability to Bear Weight: Suggests a possible hip fracture or septic arthritis.
  • Persistence Beyond 2 Weeks: Indicates the need to rule out other causes such as Perthes disease or juvenile idiopathic arthritis.
  • Night Pain: Atypical for transient synovitis, more suggestive of malignancy or osteomyelitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Hip Pain: Rare but possible, often leads to misdiagnosis; consider systemic conditions.
  • No Recent Viral Illness: Can occur without a preceding infection; may lead to consideration of other causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: May show joint effusion in the hip. Helps confirm diagnosis by visualising synovial fluid without structural changes.
  • Blood Tests: ESR and CRP are typically normal or mildly elevated, differentiating from bacterial causes.
  • Joint Aspiration: Rarely needed but would yield clear synovial fluid if performed, unlike septic arthritis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old boy presents with unilateral hip pain and a limp that started after a recent viral infection, without fever or signs of systemic illness. Symptoms have worsened over 48 hours but are mild and localised.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Septic Arthritis: Typically presents with fever and marked elevation in inflammatory markers, which are absent in transient synovitis.
  • Perthes Disease: Chronic condition with gradual onset; may show femoral head changes on imaging.
  • Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis: Persistent joint pain, morning stiffness, and swelling rather than self-limiting symptoms.
  • Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis: Common in adolescents; presents with hip pain and limping, often with visible displacement on X-ray.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive Care: Rest, limited weight-bearing until symptoms resolve.
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs like ibuprofen to reduce pain and inflammation.
  • Follow-Up: Reassessment in 1-2 weeks to ensure symptom resolution.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Transient Synovitis"][/sc]
  • Recurrent Episodes: Occurs in a minority of cases, though generally resolves fully without further intervention.
  • Misdiagnosis of More Serious Conditions: Failure to rule out septic arthritis or Perthes disease can lead to delayed treatment for these serious conditions.
  • Prognosis: Excellent, with spontaneous resolution in most cases within 1-2 weeks.
