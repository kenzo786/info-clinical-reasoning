Chlamydia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Chlamydia is a common bacterial sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by Chlamydia trachomatis. It primarily infects the urogenital tract but can also affect the rectum and pharynx. In women, untreated chlamydia can lead to complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), while in men, it may lead to epididymitis. It is often asymptomatic, making early detection and treatment critical to prevent complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population Prevalence: Chlamydia is the most common STI in the UK, with an estimated prevalence of around 2-3% in sexually active individuals aged 15-24.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Approximately 200,000 cases per year are diagnosed in genitourinary medicine clinics in the UK, with most cases among younger adults.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: WHO estimates that globally, around 131 million new cases occur annually, with significant underdiagnosis due to asymptomatic presentations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Pathogen: Chlamydia trachomatis is an obligate intracellular bacterium.
  • Cellular Infection: The bacterium enters epithelial cells in the urogenital tract, rectum, oropharynx, or conjunctiva, where it exists in an infectious form (elementary body) and a replicative form (reticulate body).
  • Immune Response: The body’s immune response causes inflammation and tissue damage, particularly in the cervix, urethra, and upper reproductive tract, which can result in scarring and complications if untreated.
  • Complications: Persistent infection can lead to chronic inflammation and fibrosis, potentially causing PID in women or epididymitis in men, with risks of infertility in severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: Typically 1-3 weeks from exposure to symptom onset if symptomatic.
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms, if present, can last several weeks and may involve dysuria, discharge, or pain. Many cases remain asymptomatic during this phase.
  • Chronic Phase: In untreated cases, chronic inflammation may develop, increasing the risk of PID or epididymitis, typically months to years after initial infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Commonly asymptomatic in up to 70% of women and 50% of men; lack of symptoms leads to high rates of undiagnosed cases.
  • Dysuria - Burning sensation during urination due to urethral inflammation.
  • Vaginal/penile discharge - Mucopurulent discharge from the urethra or cervix due to local infection and inflammatory exudate.
  • Lower abdominal pain - Often mild but can reflect pelvic inflammation in women.
  • Intermenstrual/postcoital bleeding - Cervicitis can cause fragile cervical tissue, leading to bleeding.
  • Rectal pain or discharge - In cases of rectal infection, often following anal intercourse.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Cervical motion tenderness - Tenderness on cervical movement, suggesting PID or cervicitis.
  • Mucopurulent cervical or urethral discharge - Thick, yellowish discharge on examination due to epithelial inflammation.
  • Erythema of the cervix or urethra - Local redness and inflammation due to immune response to infection.
  • Pelvic or abdominal tenderness - Palpable tenderness in the lower abdomen with PID.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate advanced PID or tubo-ovarian abscess formation, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Fever - Suggestive of systemic infection or severe PID; requires urgent management to prevent complications.
  • Pelvic mass - Could suggest a tubo-ovarian abscess, a serious complication of PID that may need surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Occurs due to autoinoculation or sexual contact; presents with redness, discharge, and irritation.
  • Rectal infection - Commonly asymptomatic but may present with proctitis, especially in men who have sex with men.
  • Pharyngitis - Rarely symptomatic; may follow orogenital contact and can present with sore throat.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Nucleic Acid Amplification Test (NAAT) - The preferred test; performed on urine samples, cervical, or urethral swabs. NAAT detects chlamydia DNA and has high sensitivity and specificity.
  • Urine Test - A first-void urine sample tested by NAAT can diagnose urogenital chlamydia in men and women.
  • Swab Test - Cervical or urethral swabs are tested by NAAT for direct detection of the organism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old woman presents with mild lower abdominal pain, dysuria, and intermenstrual bleeding for 2 weeks. Examination reveals cervical motion tenderness and mucopurulent discharge. NAAT confirms chlamydia infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Gonorrhoea - Similar presentation with urethral or cervical discharge but typically a thicker discharge and often presents with more intense dysuria.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - Dysuria and pelvic pain may mimic chlamydia, but usually lacks mucopurulent discharge and cervical tenderness.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - Can be secondary to untreated chlamydia but presents with more severe pain and fever.
  • Trichomoniasis - Presents with frothy, greenish discharge and may cause itching, differentiating it from chlamydia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • First-line: Doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 7 days is the recommended treatment.
  • Alternative: Azithromycin 1g single dose if adherence may be an issue, though recent data suggest lower efficacy in pharyngeal infections.
  • Sexual Partner Management: All sexual partners within the last 6 months should be tested and treated if positive.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - Occurs in up to 10-20% of untreated cases, potentially leading to infertility.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy - Increased risk due to tubal scarring from PID.
  • Epididymitis - In men, chlamydia can cause epididymitis, potentially leading to infertility.
  • Reiter’s Syndrome - A reactive arthritis affecting the joints, eyes, and urethra, although rare.
