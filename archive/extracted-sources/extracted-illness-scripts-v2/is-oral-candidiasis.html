Oral Candidiasis illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Oral candidiasis, also known as oral thrush, is a fungal infection of the mucous membranes of the mouth caused primarily by *Candida albicans*. It is characterised by the development of white or creamy lesions, often on the tongue or inner cheeks, and can extend to the gums, tonsils, and throat. This condition is more common in individuals with weakened immune systems or those using certain medications, such as antibiotics or corticosteroids.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK general practice (GP) population is low but increases significantly in individuals with immunocompromised conditions (e.g., HIV/AIDS, cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy, or organ transplant recipients).
  • In UK hospital settings, oral candidiasis is common among patients with prolonged hospitalisation, especially those on immunosuppressive therapies, antibiotics, or tube feeding.
  • Worldwide, oral candidiasis is a common condition, particularly in developing countries where HIV prevalence is high.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • *Candida albicans* is a commensal organism in the oral cavity. Under normal circumstances, it coexists with the host's immune system without causing disease.
  • When the immune system is compromised, or the normal bacterial flora is disrupted (e.g., by antibiotic use), *Candida* can overgrow, leading to oral thrush. Other risk factors include xerostomia (dry mouth), diabetes, dentures, and corticosteroid inhaler use.
  • Overgrowth of *Candida* leads to the formation of pseudohyphae, which invade the superficial layers of the epithelium, causing inflammation and white plaques on the oral mucosa.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Acute oral candidiasis can develop over a few days, especially after antibiotic or corticosteroid use, or in immunocompromised patients.
  • Chronic forms may develop over weeks or months, particularly in individuals with untreated underlying conditions like diabetes or in those who wear dentures.
  • Without treatment, the condition may worsen, leading to increased discomfort and extension to other areas, including the oesophagus (oesophageal candidiasis).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • White or creamy plaques - These are the hallmark of oral candidiasis, resulting from the accumulation of fungal hyphae, desquamated epithelial cells, and debris on the mucosal surface.
  • Sore, painful mouth - Inflammation caused by the invasion of pseudohyphae into the mucosal layers leads to discomfort, particularly when eating or swallowing.
  • Altered taste or dysgeusia - Fungal overgrowth can disrupt the taste buds, leading to altered or diminished taste perception.
  • Dry mouth (xerostomia) - Dryness can predispose individuals to fungal infections by reducing the protective barrier of saliva.
  • Asymptomatic - In some cases, particularly in chronic forms, oral candidiasis may present without obvious symptoms, especially in immunocompromised individuals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • White, creamy plaques on the mucosa - This is the most characteristic sign, typically found on the tongue, inner cheeks, gums, or tonsils.
  • Erythematous mucosa - Redness may be seen after the plaques are scraped off, indicating underlying inflammation of the mucosal tissue.
  • Bleeding on scraping - Attempting to remove the plaques may result in bleeding, a sign of mucosal invasion by *Candida*.
  • Angular cheilitis - Cracking and inflammation at the corners of the mouth may be associated with oral candidiasis, particularly in denture wearers or those with dry mouth.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal involvement (dysphagia) - When oral candidiasis spreads to the oesophagus, it can lead to difficulty swallowing, signifying more widespread immunosuppression or uncontrolled infection.
  • Recurrent candidiasis - Recurrence may indicate underlying immunodeficiency, such as HIV, or other systemic conditions like diabetes mellitus or malignancy.
  • Weight loss and fever - These systemic symptoms, alongside oral candidiasis, suggest a more serious underlying condition or systemic involvement of the fungal infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Chronic hyperplastic candidiasis - This is a form of oral candidiasis that presents with thick, adherent white plaques that do not easily scrape off, often found in smokers. It can be confused with oral leukoplakia.
  • Median rhomboid glossitis - A smooth, red, and flat lesion at the centre of the tongue caused by *Candida* overgrowth, often missed in early diagnosis.
  • Erythematous candidiasis - Instead of white plaques, this variant presents with red patches, often seen in denture wearers, and can be mistaken for other inflammatory conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Diagnosis is often clinical, based on the appearance of white plaques that can be scraped off, revealing an erythematous base.
  • Microscopy with KOH preparation - Scraping of lesions examined under a microscope with potassium hydroxide (KOH) reveals yeast cells and pseudohyphae, confirming *Candida* infection.
  • Fungal culture - In unclear cases, a swab of the lesion can be cultured to confirm the presence of *Candida* species, although this is rarely necessary.
  • HIV testing - If recurrent or severe oral candidiasis is present, HIV testing should be considered to rule out underlying immunosuppression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 1-week history of a sore mouth and difficulty swallowing, following a recent course of antibiotics. Examination reveals white plaques on the tongue and buccal mucosa, which bleed when scraped off.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Oral leukoplakia - Unlike oral candidiasis, leukoplakia presents with white plaques that do not scrape off and is usually non-painful. It may be premalignant, particularly in smokers.
  • Lichen planus - Presents with white, lace-like patches and associated redness. The lesions do not scrape off and may be painful.
  • Herpes simplex infection - Typically presents with painful vesicles or ulcers rather than white plaques, often accompanied by fever and lymphadenopathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Topical antifungals - First-line treatment is with topical nystatin or miconazole gel applied directly to the affected areas for 7-14 days.
  • Oral fluconazole - Systemic treatment is used in more severe or refractory cases, especially in immunocompromised patients. The typical dose is 50-100 mg daily for 7-14 days.
  • Address underlying causes - Optimising glycaemic control in diabetic patients, reducing steroid inhaler use, and improving denture hygiene are crucial for preventing recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oral Candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal candidiasis - Occurs when the infection spreads to the oesophagus, causing dysphagia and chest pain. This is more common in immunosuppressed patients.
  • Recurrent infection - In individuals with untreated underlying conditions, oral candidiasis may become chronic or recurrent, leading to long-term discomfort and potential systemic involvement.
  • Systemic candidiasis - Rare but serious, systemic spread of *Candida* can occur in severely immunocompromised patients, leading to sepsis and multisystem organ failure.
