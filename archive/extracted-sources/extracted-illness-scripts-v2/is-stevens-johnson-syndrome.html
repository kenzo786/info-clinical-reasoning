Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) is a severe mucocutaneous reaction, usually triggered by medications or infections, characterised by epidermal necrosis, widespread skin blistering, and mucous membrane involvement. It represents a spectrum of disease, with SJS affecting 30% of the BSA.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • SJS is rare, with an estimated incidence of 1–2 cases per million people annually worldwide. In the UK, the incidence of SJS in GP settings is extremely low. Hospitalised cases are rare, but occur more frequently in tertiary care centres with dermatology and intensive care units. The condition is more common in adults than in children and has a higher prevalence in certain genetic populations, particularly those with HLA-B*1502 and HLA-B*5801 alleles.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • SJS is primarily an immune-mediated hypersensitivity reaction, often drug-induced. The key mechanism involves cytotoxic T cells targeting keratinocytes that express foreign antigens, leading to widespread apoptosis of epidermal cells. This results in the separation of the epidermis from the dermis, leading to blistering, necrosis, and loss of the epidermal barrier. The condition also affects mucous membranes due to similar immune-mediated destruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • SJS typically begins within 1 to 3 weeks of exposure to a triggering agent. The early phase involves non-specific flu-like symptoms (fever, malaise, sore throat). Mucocutaneous lesions develop rapidly over the next few days, leading to widespread epidermal detachment. The disease can progress to involve multiple organ systems within 2-4 weeks. Recovery is prolonged, with full epithelial healing taking weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Fever - Common early symptom, due to systemic inflammation and cytokine release.
  • Malaise - Non-specific symptom related to the widespread immune response.
  • Sore throat - Early mucosal involvement as epithelial cells are targeted.
  • Blistering rash - Widespread painful erythematous macules that blister, due to epidermal detachment secondary to keratinocyte apoptosis.
  • Oral ulcers - A result of mucous membrane involvement, leading to painful erosions and difficulty eating/drinking.
  • Ocular irritation - Involvement of the conjunctiva, leading to redness, pain, and risk of corneal damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Erythematous macules - Flat, red lesions that eventually blister, due to inflammation and necrosis of the epidermis.
  • Positive Nikolsky sign - Gentle pressure on the skin causes the epidermis to peel away, indicating loss of epidermal adhesion.
  • Skin detachment - Patches of skin sloughing off, resulting from widespread apoptosis of keratinocytes.
  • Oropharyngeal erosions - Ulcerations on the mucous membranes, a hallmark of severe mucosal involvement.
  • Conjunctival injection - Redness and inflammation of the conjunctiva, secondary to ocular involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Extensive skin detachment (>10% BSA) - Indicates severe disease and high risk of complications such as infection and sepsis.
  • Ocular involvement - Can lead to long-term visual impairment if not treated promptly.
  • Difficulty swallowing or breathing - Suggests severe mucosal involvement, which can compromise airway integrity.
  • Fever >39°C - Indicates severe systemic inflammation, possibly heralding multi-organ involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Localised mucosal involvement only - Rarely, SJS may present with minimal skin involvement and only affect mucous membranes, leading to diagnostic confusion with other mucosal diseases.
  • Subacute onset - Some patients may develop symptoms more gradually over several weeks, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Skin biopsy - Confirms the diagnosis by showing necrosis of keratinocytes and a sparse lymphocytic infiltrate.
  • Blood tests (FBC, CRP) - Raised CRP and white cell count suggest systemic inflammation.
  • Liver function tests - May be elevated in cases with hepatic involvement.
  • Microbiological swabs - To rule out secondary infections in affected skin areas.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 4-day history of fever, malaise, sore throat, and a blistering rash. Over the past 24 hours, she developed painful oral ulcers and widespread skin blistering, with involvement of both eyes and lips. She recently started carbamazepine for trigeminal neuralgia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN) - Differs by affecting >30% of the BSA. Presents with more widespread skin detachment.
  • Erythema Multiforme - Less severe, with target lesions rather than widespread blistering. Minimal mucosal involvement.
  • Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS) - Typically occurs in children, caused by bacterial exotoxins. No mucosal involvement.
  • Acute Generalised Exanthematous Pustulosis (AGEP) - Pustular rash rather than blistering, usually triggered by medications.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate cessation of causative agent - First-line intervention.
  • Supportive care - Management in a burns or ICU unit with fluid and electrolyte balance, temperature regulation, and pain control.
  • Topical and systemic corticosteroids - Controversial but may be used in early stages.
  • Intravenous immunoglobulins (IVIG) - May help reduce the immune-mediated damage.
  • Ophthalmological care - Early intervention to prevent long-term ocular damage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Common due to loss of skin barrier, increasing risk of infections.
  • Ocular complications - Corneal scarring and blindness may occur.
  • Chronic pain - Resulting from scarring and nerve damage.
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation - May persist after skin healing.
