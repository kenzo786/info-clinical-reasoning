Pulmonary Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a pathophysiological disorder characterised by elevated pressure in the pulmonary arteries. It is diagnosed when the mean pulmonary artery pressure (mPAP) is ≥25 mmHg at rest, as assessed by right heart catheterisation. PH can be idiopathic or secondary to a variety of conditions, including left heart disease, lung diseases, or chronic thromboembolic disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare, with approximately 15-50 cases per million people.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: More common in specialist centres, with higher rates in patients with underlying conditions like heart failure or lung disease.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies based on the underlying cause, but idiopathic pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) has a global prevalence of 5-15 cases per million.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • PH occurs due to increased pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR), which raises pressure in the pulmonary arteries. This increase in PVR can be due to narrowing of the small pulmonary arteries, vascular remodelling, or obstruction (e.g., due to emboli in chronic thromboembolic PH). In group 2 PH (related to left heart disease), the elevated pressures are secondary to increased left atrial pressure.
  • Over time, elevated pressure in the pulmonary circulation leads to right ventricular hypertrophy and eventual right heart failure, as the right ventricle struggles to pump against the increased resistance.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Symptoms may be minimal or absent; patients might report mild shortness of breath or fatigue with exertion.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms like dyspnoea, chest pain, and syncope become more pronounced as right ventricular failure develops.
  • Late Phase: Severe right heart failure with symptoms such as oedema, ascites, and significant functional decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Exertional dyspnoea - Due to increased pulmonary pressures limiting cardiac output during physical activity.
  • Fatigue - Caused by reduced oxygen delivery due to decreased cardiac output.
  • Chest pain - Likely related to right ventricular ischaemia as it struggles to pump against increased pulmonary pressures.
  • Syncope - Occurs during exertion due to reduced cerebral perfusion secondary to low cardiac output.
  • Peripheral oedema - A consequence of right heart failure leading to venous congestion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Loud P2 (accentuated pulmonary component of the second heart sound) - Reflects increased pressure in the pulmonary artery.
  • Right ventricular heave - Palpable impulse on the left parasternal border, indicating right ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Jugular venous distension (JVD) - Due to elevated right atrial pressure from right heart failure.
  • Peripheral oedema - Caused by systemic venous congestion due to right ventricular failure.
  • Ascites - Also a result of systemic venous congestion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Indicates advanced disease with poor prognosis, as it reflects a severely compromised cardiac output.
  • Worsening dyspnoea - May suggest progression to right heart failure, which carries a high risk of mortality.
  • New onset chest pain - Could signal right ventricular ischaemia or acute worsening of PH due to pulmonary embolism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with vague symptoms such as generalised fatigue, which could be misattributed to other causes like deconditioning or depression. Dyspnoea might be subtle or occur only with significant exertion, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Right heart catheterisation - Gold standard for diagnosing PH, showing mPAP ≥25 mmHg. This reflects increased pressure in the pulmonary circulation.
  • Echocardiography - Often the first test to raise suspicion, showing signs like right ventricular hypertrophy or dilatation and elevated pulmonary artery systolic pressure.
  • CT pulmonary angiography - To exclude chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) by visualising pulmonary arteries.
  • Pulmonary function tests (PFTs) - Help differentiate PH from lung diseases by assessing lung volumes and diffusing capacity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with 6 months of worsening exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, and intermittent syncope. She has a history of connective tissue disease. Examination reveals a loud P2, right ventricular heave, and jugular venous distension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Left heart failure - Typically presents with crackles on lung auscultation and evidence of pulmonary oedema on imaging, which are absent in PH.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Usually has a prolonged smoking history, abnormal PFTs, and hyperinflated lungs on imaging, which are not features of isolated PH.
  • Interstitial lung disease - Diffuse lung crackles and reduced lung volumes on PFTs help differentiate from PH.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • General measures - Include optimising oxygenation, diuretics for right heart failure, and physical rehabilitation.
  • Pulmonary arterial hypertension-specific treatments - In patients with PAH, options include endothelin receptor antagonists (e.g., bosentan), phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors (e.g., sildenafil), and prostacyclin analogues (e.g., epoprostenol).
  • Chronic thromboembolic PH - Treatment includes anticoagulation and surgical intervention (pulmonary endarterectomy) if appropriate.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Right heart failure - Most common and life-threatening complication, occurring due to the increased pressure load on the right ventricle.
  • Arrhythmias - Atrial fibrillation and other arrhythmias can occur due to right atrial dilatation and strain.
  • Thromboembolism - Both venous and pulmonary thromboembolism can complicate PH, particularly in patients with underlying conditions like CTEPH.
