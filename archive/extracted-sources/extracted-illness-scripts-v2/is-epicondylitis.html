Epicondylitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Epicondylitis, commonly referred to as "tennis elbow" (lateral epicondylitis) or "golfer’s elbow" (medial epicondylitis), is a condition characterised by pain and tenderness at the lateral or medial epicondyles of the humerus. It is caused by overuse or repetitive strain of the tendons originating from these areas, leading to microtears and inflammation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 1-3% annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less than 1% of outpatient orthopaedic visits.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar rates globally, more common in individuals aged 35-54.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Repetitive microtrauma to the extensor carpi radialis brevis (lateral epicondylitis) or flexor-pronator muscles (medial epicondylitis).
  • Leads to collagen degeneration, angiofibroblastic hyperplasia, and inflammation.
  • Inadequate healing responses contribute to chronicity, with scar tissue formation further weakening the tendons.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Pain and tenderness develop over days to weeks with repetitive activity.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent symptoms lasting over 6 months; structural changes in tendons may occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Lateral or medial elbow pain - Typically worsens with gripping or lifting activities due to tendon strain.
  • Weak grip strength - Results from pain and tendon dysfunction affecting muscle performance.
  • Difficulty with daily tasks - Such as opening jars or shaking hands, due to pain exacerbated by forearm use.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Tenderness at the epicondyle - Indicates local inflammation and microtrauma.
  • Pain on resisted wrist extension or flexion - Reproduces symptoms by stressing the affected tendons.
  • Decreased range of motion - May occur due to pain and stiffness in severe cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Unrelenting night pain - Could indicate malignancy or infection.
  • Systemic symptoms - Fever, weight loss, or fatigue may suggest an alternative diagnosis like rheumatoid arthritis or infection.
  • Neurological symptoms - Numbness or tingling in the hand suggests nerve entrapment (e.g., ulnar nerve).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Pain radiating to the forearm or shoulder - May mimic cervical radiculopathy.
  • Bilateral symptoms - Suggests a systemic cause such as inflammatory arthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Tenderness over the epicondyle, pain on resisted wrist extension or flexion.
  • Ultrasound - May reveal tendon thickening or tears.
  • MRI - Reserved for persistent cases; shows detailed tendon degeneration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • A 42-year-old manual labourer presents with 3 months of worsening lateral elbow pain, particularly during gripping activities, with no systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Radial tunnel syndrome - Pain more distal to the epicondyle and with forearm supination.
  • Osteoarthritis - Associated with stiffness and crepitus rather than sharp pain on use.
  • Cervical radiculopathy - May have associated neck pain or paraesthesia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Rest, activity modification, and physiotherapy focusing on eccentric strengthening exercises.
  • Analgesia - NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) for pain relief.
  • Corticosteroid injections - For persistent pain, though limited to short-term relief.
  • Surgical intervention - Reserved for refractory cases after 6-12 months of conservative treatment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epicondylitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Results from inadequate tendon healing and recurrent microtrauma.
  • Loss of function - Due to tendon scarring or persistent weakness.
  • Tendon rupture - Rare but can occur if overuse continues without treatment.
