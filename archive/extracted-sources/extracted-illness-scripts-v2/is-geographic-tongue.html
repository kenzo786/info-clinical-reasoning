Geographic Tongue
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Geographic tongue (benign migratory glossitis) is a benign inflammatory condition of the oral mucosa, characterised by irregular, smooth, red patches on the dorsal and lateral aspects of the tongue, often surrounded by a white or yellow border. The lesions change location, size, and shape over time, giving the appearance of a "geographic map". It is usually asymptomatic but can sometimes cause discomfort, especially when consuming certain foods.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Around 1-3% of adults, with a higher prevalence in females.
  • Hospital prevalence: Rarely requires hospitalisation, typically managed in primary care.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 1-3%, varying slightly with geographic and ethnic factors. Higher in individuals with a family history of geographic tongue or associated conditions like psoriasis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Geographic tongue is thought to result from abnormal desquamation of the filiform papillae of the tongue. This leads to areas of depapillation (loss of papillae), which appear as red, atrophic patches. The lesions tend to heal and reappear in different locations, likely due to an immune-mediated process. Some researchers suggest it may be linked to other inflammatory conditions, such as psoriasis, due to shared genetic or immune pathways.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • The lesions of geographic tongue may appear rapidly, change location within days or weeks, and often resolve spontaneously. The course can be chronic with recurrent flare-ups, though some patients may go long periods without lesions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Most cases are asymptomatic, and patients may be unaware of the condition, which is often discovered incidentally during routine oral examinations.
  • Burning sensation - A minority of patients report discomfort or a burning sensation, particularly after consuming acidic, spicy, or salty foods. This is likely due to irritation of the exposed mucosa following loss of protective papillae.
  • Altered taste sensation - In some cases, patients report an altered taste in the affected areas, possibly due to inflammation disrupting normal taste bud function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Red patches on the tongue - The hallmark sign is the presence of well-defined, smooth, red patches of depapillated mucosa, typically on the dorsal or lateral aspects of the tongue. These areas may vary in size and migrate over time.
  • White or yellow borders - The red patches are often bordered by slightly raised, white or yellowish margins, representing areas of hyperkeratosis or regenerating mucosa.
  • Lesion migration - The appearance of the lesions changes over time, typically moving to different parts of the tongue within days to weeks, which is why it is referred to as "geographic" tongue.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Persistent ulceration - Could indicate an underlying malignancy or other systemic condition, such as oral lichen planus or squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Severe pain or bleeding - These are unusual for geographic tongue and may signify more serious conditions, such as erosive lichen planus or oral candidiasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Occasionally, geographic tongue can present with large, stable patches that do not migrate or cause atypical symptoms, leading to misdiagnosis as oral leukoplakia or other chronic inflammatory oral conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on the characteristic appearance of lesions. No specific diagnostic tests are required.
  • If the diagnosis is uncertain, biopsy may be performed. Histology typically shows desquamation of the filiform papillae, with neutrophilic infiltration in the superficial mucosa.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a burning sensation on her tongue that worsens with spicy foods. She is otherwise well, with no systemic symptoms. Examination reveals well-demarcated, red patches on the lateral borders of the tongue, consistent with geographic tongue.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Oral lichen planus - Typically presents with white, lace-like patterns (Wickham’s striae), often with associated mucosal erosions, unlike the smooth, migratory patches of geographic tongue.
  • Candidiasis - Characterised by white, curd-like plaques that can be wiped off, leaving a red base. Geographic tongue does not have this feature.
  • Leukoplakia - Presents as persistent white patches that cannot be scraped off and do not change in appearance or location, unlike geographic tongue’s migratory nature.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • No specific treatment is required for geographic tongue, as it is a benign and self-limiting condition.
  • If symptomatic, topical corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone buccal tablets) or topical anaesthetic gels may be used to alleviate discomfort.
  • Patients should be advised to avoid known triggers, such as spicy, acidic, or hot foods.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Geographic Tongue"][/sc]
  • Geographic tongue is generally benign and not associated with any long-term complications.
  • In rare cases, patients may experience persistent discomfort or secondary infections, but this is uncommon.
