Diabetic Retinopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a microvascular complication of diabetes mellitus characterised by progressive damage to the retina. It is caused by chronic hyperglycaemia leading to vascular changes, including capillary occlusion, leakage, and neovascularisation. DR is a leading cause of blindness in adults worldwide.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Affects approximately 10-15% of diabetic patients.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Severe cases requiring treatment account for about 2% of patients referred to ophthalmology.
  • Worldwide prevalence: 35% of all diabetic patients show some form of DR, with 10% experiencing vision-threatening complications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic hyperglycaemia induces oxidative stress and activates the polyol and protein kinase C pathways.
  • Microvascular damage leads to endothelial dysfunction, capillary occlusion, and leakage.
  • Hypoxia triggers the release of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), promoting neovascularisation.
  • Advanced cases feature haemorrhage and retinal detachment due to proliferative neovascularisation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Often asymptomatic; background changes include microaneurysms and minor haemorrhages.
  • Progressive stage: Development of macular oedema, capillary non-perfusion, and venous changes.
  • Advanced stage: Proliferative DR with neovascularisation, vitreous haemorrhage, and retinal detachment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Blurred vision - Due to macular oedema or ischaemia causing distortion of the central retina.
  • Floaters - Caused by vitreous haemorrhage from fragile new vessels.
  • Sudden loss of vision - May occur from vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment.
  • Asymptomatic in early stages - Background DR often lacks noticeable symptoms due to mild changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Microaneurysms - Small outpouchings in capillaries visible on fundoscopy due to weak vessel walls.
  • Dot and blot haemorrhages - Intraretinal bleeding from ruptured capillaries.
  • Hard exudates - Lipid deposits in the retina due to leakage from damaged vessels.
  • Neovascularisation - Fragile new vessels formed in response to ischaemia.
  • Macular oedema - Retinal thickening detected clinically or via optical coherence tomography (OCT).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - May indicate vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment, requiring urgent ophthalmic intervention.
  • Severe floaters or shadowing - Suggests significant vitreous haemorrhage.
  • Photopsias (flashes of light) - A sign of retinal traction or imminent detachment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Non-diabetic patients with DR-like findings may have conditions such as hypertensive retinopathy or retinal vein occlusion.
  • Slow progressive visual decline without dramatic symptoms could be overlooked in poorly monitored diabetics.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Fundoscopy - Visualises microaneurysms, haemorrhages, exudates, and neovascularisation.
  • Optical coherence tomography (OCT) - Assesses macular oedema and retinal thickness.
  • Fluorescein angiography - Identifies capillary non-perfusion and leakage.
  • HbA1c - Evaluates long-term glucose control contributing to DR.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • A 57-year-old man with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes presents with progressive blurred vision in both eyes over 6 months. Fundoscopy reveals microaneurysms, hard exudates, and macular oedema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive retinopathy - Differentiated by arteriolar narrowing and flame-shaped haemorrhages.
  • Retinal vein occlusion - Presents with sudden vision loss and "blood and thunder" fundus appearance.
  • Age-related macular degeneration - Features drusen and geographic atrophy, not microaneurysms or neovascularisation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Optimisation of glucose control - To prevent progression.
  • Anti-VEGF injections (e.g., ranibizumab) - For macular oedema and proliferative DR.
  • Pan-retinal photocoagulation - Reduces retinal ischaemia and neovascularisation.
  • Vitrectomy - Indicated in non-clearing vitreous haemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Blindness - Due to macular scarring or retinal detachment.
  • Recurrent vitreous haemorrhage - Impairs vision and requires surgical intervention.
  • Glaucoma - Secondary to neovascularisation of the iris.
