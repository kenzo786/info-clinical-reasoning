Haemochromatosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Haemochromatosis is a genetic disorder characterised by excessive absorption of dietary iron, leading to iron overload in organs. The most common form is hereditary haemochromatosis (HHC), associated with mutations in the HFE gene. This condition primarily affects the liver, heart, pancreas, and joints, potentially leading to cirrhosis, diabetes, and cardiomyopathy if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, HFE-related haemochromatosis affects approximately 1 in 150 people with Northern European ancestry, although many remain asymptomatic.
  • In UK hospitals, the prevalence is higher due to symptomatic patients presenting with organ damage, particularly in hepatology and cardiology settings.
  • Globally, haemochromatosis is most prevalent in populations of Northern European descent, with a worldwide prevalence estimated at 0.5%-1% among this group. It is rare in people of non-European descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Mutations in the HFE gene, typically C282Y homozygosity, disrupt normal hepcidin regulation, leading to increased intestinal iron absorption.
  • Excessive iron accumulates in the bloodstream and deposits in tissues, leading to oxidative stress and tissue damage.
  • Organ damage from iron overload is gradual, causing fibrosis and potential organ failure over time, particularly in the liver, pancreas, heart, and joints.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Haemochromatosis is typically a chronic condition. Iron accumulation often begins in young adulthood, though symptoms generally appear in middle age (40-60 years), after years of gradual iron deposition.
  • Initial asymptomatic phase: Most individuals remain asymptomatic for years despite iron accumulation.
  • Symptomatic phase: Symptoms develop as organs reach critical iron levels, often manifesting as fatigue, joint pain, or abdominal pain.
  • Advanced phase: Without intervention, organ damage becomes irreversible, leading to cirrhosis, diabetes, heart disease, or arthropathy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common due to chronic inflammation and organ dysfunction from iron toxicity.
  • Joint pain (arthralgia) - Primarily in the hands, related to iron deposition in joints, causing oxidative damage and inflammation.
  • Abdominal pain - Due to hepatomegaly or liver inflammation as iron accumulates in hepatic tissue.
  • Diabetes mellitus - Develops as iron deposits impair pancreatic beta cells, leading to insulin resistance and deficiency.
  • Loss of libido and impotence - Related to hypogonadism from pituitary iron overload affecting hormone production.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver due to iron deposition and inflammation.
  • Skin hyperpigmentation ("bronzing") - Iron deposition in skin melanocytes leads to increased melanin production.
  • Arthropathy - Typically affecting the second and third metacarpophalangeal joints, caused by iron deposits in cartilage.
  • Heart failure - From iron overload in myocardial tissue, leading to cardiomyopathy.
  • Splenomegaly - Due to congestion and haematopoiesis dysregulation as a secondary effect of liver disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Severe fatigue and heart failure - May indicate advanced heart disease with iron overload cardiomyopathy.
  • Jaundice - Suggests significant liver injury or cirrhosis; often a late sign indicating decompensation.
  • Diabetes with skin pigmentation - Combination of diabetes, skin changes, and hepatomegaly should prompt consideration of haemochromatosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Isolated arthritis - Particularly in the hands; may be misdiagnosed as osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Primary cardiac symptoms - Patients may present with cardiomyopathy or arrhythmias before other symptoms, especially in younger patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Serum ferritin - Elevated (>300 µg/L in men; >200 µg/L in women) due to iron overload.
  • Transferrin saturation - Elevated (>45% in men; >40% in women), reflecting iron excess.
  • HFE genetic testing - Identifies C282Y or H63D mutations; confirms hereditary haemochromatosis diagnosis.
  • Liver biopsy or MRI - Detects iron deposits in hepatic tissue, assesses degree of fibrosis and cirrhosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with fatigue, joint pain, and "bronzed" skin, with elevated liver enzymes and diabetes, suspected of haemochromatosis based on high serum ferritin and transferrin saturation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic liver disease - Differentiated by history of alcohol intake and absence of high ferritin/transferrin saturation.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Often seen with obesity; lacks the high transferrin saturation of haemochromatosis.
  • Chronic hepatitis C - Presents with liver fibrosis and cirrhosis, confirmed by HCV antibodies rather than iron overload markers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Phlebotomy - Regular blood removal (weekly initially) to reduce serum ferritin to <50 µg/L.
  • Iron chelation - For patients unable to undergo phlebotomy, deferasirox or deferoxamine may be used.
  • Lifestyle advice - Avoid iron supplements, high-iron foods, and alcohol; vaccinations for hepatitis A and B recommended.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Frequent in untreated cases; caused by hepatic fibrosis from chronic iron deposition.
  • Diabetes mellitus - From iron-induced pancreatic damage; common in long-standing disease.
  • Heart disease - Cardiomyopathy due to iron overload in myocardial tissue, potentially fatal if untreated.
  • Arthropathy - Common, especially in the hands, due to joint cartilage damage from iron deposits.
