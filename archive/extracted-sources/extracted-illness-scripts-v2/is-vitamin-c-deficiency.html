Vitamin C Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin C deficiency, or scurvy, is a condition resulting from inadequate intake or absorption of vitamin C, an essential nutrient involved in collagen synthesis, antioxidant functions, and immune defence. Its core characteristics include collagen instability leading to connective tissue weakness and systemic inflammation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Rare, but cases observed among populations with limited access to fresh produce (e.g., elderly, malnourished individuals).
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 10–15% of malnourished inpatients may have suboptimal vitamin C levels.
  • Worldwide prevalence: More common in developing regions and in populations with low socioeconomic status or restricted diets.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is crucial for hydroxylation of proline and lysine in collagen synthesis. Without it, unstable collagen is produced, resulting in impaired wound healing, blood vessel fragility, and connective tissue degradation.
  • Vitamin C also acts as an antioxidant, protecting cells from oxidative damage. Deficiency leads to increased oxidative stress and cellular injury.
  • The immune system relies on vitamin C for leukocyte function and oxidative burst; deficiency results in immunodeficiency, making patients prone to infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Early phase (weeks): Fatigue, malaise, and minor bruising may appear due to reduced collagen stability and capillary fragility.
  • Intermediate phase (1–3 months): Symptoms intensify, with gum swelling, joint pain, and petechiae as collagen further degrades.
  • Late phase (>3 months): Severe systemic involvement, bleeding complications, impaired wound healing, and potential fatality if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced carnitine synthesis affecting muscle metabolism.
  • Gum swelling and bleeding - Impaired collagen in gingival tissues leads to friability and bleeding.
  • Joint pain - Collagen breakdown in connective tissues results in joint stiffness and pain.
  • Easy bruising and petechiae - Capillary fragility from unstable collagen causes subcutaneous bleeding.
  • Poor wound healing - Insufficient collagen synthesis disrupts normal wound repair processes.
  • Mood changes - Vitamin C deficiency affects neurotransmitter synthesis, potentially causing irritability and depression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Swollen, bleeding gums - Friable gingival tissue due to lack of collagen.
  • Petechiae and ecchymoses - Visible subcutaneous bleeding from capillary fragility.
  • Coiled or corkscrew hair - Distorted hair follicles due to collagen instability.
  • Perifollicular haemorrhages - Microvascular bleeding around hair follicles as a result of weakened vessel walls.
  • Oedema of lower limbs - Poor collagen support in blood vessels and tissues leads to fluid leakage and swelling.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe bleeding (gastrointestinal, mucosal) - Indicates advanced deficiency; risk of significant blood loss and haemodynamic instability.
  • Shortness of breath and chest pain - May signify anaemia and cardiac involvement due to increased oxidative stress.
  • Profound fatigue and weakness - Severe vitamin C depletion, potential impact on cardiovascular and muscular systems.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Mild ecchymoses without classic gum symptoms - In partial deficiency, bleeding can present without severe gingival involvement.
  • Mood disturbances without physical signs - Neurological effects such as depression can appear in mild deficiency, potentially masking scurvy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Plasma vitamin C levels: <11 µmol/L is diagnostic of deficiency.
  • Complete blood count: May reveal anaemia (normocytic or macrocytic) due to impaired iron metabolism.
  • Imaging (X-rays of joints): In severe cases, may show subperiosteal haemorrhages or osteoporosis, especially in children.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with fatigue, gum bleeding, and easy bruising over three months. History reveals limited dietary intake of fruits and vegetables due to socioeconomic factors.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Presents with fatigue and pallor, but lacks bleeding or gum changes associated with vitamin C deficiency.
  • Immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP): Causes bruising and petechiae, but normal gum and hair changes; platelet count is low.
  • Connective tissue disorders (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos syndrome): Skin fragility and bruising are prominent, but genetic markers distinguish from scurvy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin C supplementation: Oral ascorbic acid (200 mg–1000 mg daily) for adults until symptoms resolve; taper dose thereafter.
  • Dietary counselling: Emphasis on fresh fruits and vegetables (e.g., citrus fruits, peppers) to prevent recurrence.
  • Supportive management: Iron supplements if anaemia is present, treat infections as indicated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin C Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe bleeding complications: High mortality risk if untreated, particularly from gastrointestinal haemorrhage.
  • Secondary infections: Due to immunosuppression, increasing the risk of pneumonia or other serious infections.
  • Osteoporosis and bone fractures: Especially in children, vitamin C deficiency can lead to permanent bone damage if prolonged.
