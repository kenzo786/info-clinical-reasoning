Measles
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Measles is an acute, highly contagious viral illness caused by the measles virus, a paramyxovirus. It is primarily spread through respiratory droplets, and patients typically present with a prodromal phase of fever, cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis, followed by a characteristic maculopapular rash.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Measles cases are uncommon in the UK due to high vaccination coverage, but outbreaks still occur, especially among unvaccinated groups.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hospitalisation is required in severe cases, and in outbreaks, hospitalisation rates may increase due to complications, particularly in children and immunocompromised patients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Measles remains a significant public health issue globally, with higher incidence in countries with low vaccination rates. In 2018, the WHO reported over 140,000 measles-related deaths worldwide, predominantly in low-resource settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • The measles virus infects the respiratory epithelium and initially multiplies locally.
  • The virus then spreads to lymphatic tissue, where it further replicates and disseminates through the bloodstream, leading to a widespread viral invasion.
  • This viral spread triggers a robust immune response, leading to the release of cytokines that cause systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, malaise) and the characteristic rash.
  • Immunosuppression occurs, making patients more susceptible to secondary infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 10–14 days from exposure to symptom onset.
  • Prodromal phase: 3–4 days, characterised by fever, cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis.
  • Rash phase: Occurs approximately 4 days after prodrome onset, lasting 5–6 days, typically starting on the face and spreading downwards.
  • Recovery phase: After the rash fades, patients usually experience resolution of symptoms over 1–2 weeks, though immunosuppression can persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Fever: Due to systemic inflammatory response, often one of the earliest signs.
  • Cough: Caused by respiratory tract involvement and irritation.
  • Coryza (nasal congestion and discharge): Reflects viral infection and inflammation of nasal mucosa.
  • Conjunctivitis: Viral spread to conjunctiva, causing redness and discomfort in the eyes.
  • Rash: Appears 3-4 days after initial symptoms, beginning at the face and spreading downwards; it is due to the immune response to the virus in skin capillaries.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Koplik spots: Small white lesions on the buccal mucosa, pathognomonic for measles and caused by viral infection of oral epithelium.
  • Maculopapular rash: Begins on the face and spreads downwards, due to immune response in capillaries.
  • Conjunctival injection: Redness and inflammation of the conjunctiva due to direct viral invasion.
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy: Immune response involving lymphoid tissue, often palpable in cervical and occipital regions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • High fever (>40°C): May indicate severe infection and increased risk for complications.
  • Altered mental status: Could indicate encephalitis, a serious complication of measles.
  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain: May signify secondary bacterial pneumonia, a common complication.
  • Severe abdominal pain: Can be associated with acute appendicitis or other gastrointestinal complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Atypical rash distribution: In immunocompromised patients, the rash may be less pronounced or absent.
  • Mild or absent prodromal symptoms: Often seen in partially vaccinated individuals or those with some immunity.
  • Prolonged course of illness: More common in patients with immunodeficiency, reflecting impaired immune response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Serology: IgM antibodies detectable within 3 days of rash onset; confirms acute infection.
  • RT-PCR: Detects measles RNA in blood, urine, or respiratory specimens, particularly useful in early infection.
  • Full blood count (FBC): Often reveals lymphopenia, a common finding in measles due to immunosuppression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old, unvaccinated child presents with a 4-day history of fever, cough, and conjunctivitis, followed by the onset of a maculopapular rash starting on the face and spreading downward.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Rubella: Rash appears similarly but spreads faster and is usually less intense. Often associated with mild fever and lymphadenopathy.
  • Scarlet fever: Typically associated with sore throat and a fine, sandpaper-like rash, not descending rash distribution as in measles.
  • Roseola: Rash appears after a sudden high fever, typically in infants, and is usually confined to the trunk.
  • Kawasaki disease: Has persistent high fever and erythema of palms and soles, often without a cough or coryza.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Supportive care: Includes hydration, antipyretics, and isolation to prevent spread.
  • Vitamin A supplementation: Recommended in children to reduce complications and mortality.
  • Antibiotics: Prescribed if secondary bacterial infections, like pneumonia, are suspected.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis: Rare but serious complication leading to neurological sequelae in some cases.
  • Pneumonia: Most common cause of death in measles, particularly among malnourished and immunocompromised individuals.
  • Otitis media: Middle ear infection that can result in hearing loss if not treated.
  • Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE): Rare, late-onset complication with progressive neurological deterioration, typically occurring years after infection.
