Liver Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Liver failure refers to the inability of the liver to perform its normal synthetic, metabolic, and detoxification functions. It can be classified as acute (developing within days to weeks) or chronic (progressing over months to years), with cirrhosis often underlying chronic liver failure. Key features include jaundice, coagulopathy, hepatic encephalopathy, and multi-systemic involvement.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Liver failure is relatively rare in primary care but is usually seen as a consequence of chronic liver diseases such as alcoholic liver disease and hepatitis B or C.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: In the UK, liver disease accounts for around 11,000 deaths annually. Acute liver failure (ALF) is less common than chronic, accounting for < 10% of liver-related hospital admissions.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, liver failure has significant geographical variation. In developing countries, viral hepatitis is a leading cause, while alcohol and fatty liver disease are prominent in high-income nations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Liver failure occurs when there is extensive hepatocyte injury, resulting in the inability to maintain essential liver functions. In acute liver failure (ALF), rapid necrosis of hepatocytes due to toxins (paracetamol overdose) or viral hepatitis leads to sudden dysfunction. In chronic liver failure, long-standing conditions such as cirrhosis cause gradual fibrosis, impairing liver architecture and blood flow. This results in portal hypertension, impaired synthetic function (e.g. clotting factor deficiency), and detoxification failure (e.g. hyperammonaemia).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute Liver Failure (ALF): Rapid onset over days to weeks, commonly associated with drug toxicity (paracetamol) or viral infections.
  • Chronic Liver Failure (CLF): Progressive over months to years, typically due to underlying cirrhosis from alcohol, NAFLD (Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease), or chronic hepatitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Fatigue – Caused by decreased metabolic function and systemic inflammation due to liver dysfunction.
  • Jaundice – Yellowing of the skin and sclera due to hyperbilirubinaemia from impaired conjugation and excretion of bilirubin.
  • Nausea and Vomiting – Often associated with toxin build-up in the bloodstream due to the liver’s inability to detoxify.
  • Abdominal pain – Particularly in acute liver failure, where liver enlargement or inflammation may lead to discomfort.
  • Confusion/Encephalopathy – Results from accumulation of neurotoxins, particularly ammonia, leading to altered mental status.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Jaundice – Visible yellow discolouration due to elevated bilirubin levels.
  • Spider Naevi – Superficial dilated capillaries often seen in cirrhosis due to hormonal imbalance, particularly increased oestrogen.
  • Palmar Erythema – Reddening of the palms due to increased vasodilation, often linked with chronic liver disease.
  • Ascites – Abdominal swelling due to the accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, secondary to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Asterixis (flapping tremor) – Caused by metabolic encephalopathy, particularly in the setting of hyperammonaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Severe Encephalopathy – Indicates a risk of cerebral oedema, a potentially fatal complication of liver failure.
  • Coagulopathy (INR > 1.5) – Suggests significant synthetic dysfunction, which increases the risk of spontaneous bleeding.
  • Hypoglycaemia – Liver failure reduces gluconeogenesis, and persistent hypoglycaemia can lead to seizures or coma.
  • Haematemesis or Melena – Indicates variceal bleeding from portal hypertension, a life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Subclinical hepatic encephalopathy – Mild cognitive impairments, such as difficulty with concentration or sleep disturbances, may be missed.
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) – Often presents with subtle signs like low-grade fever or confusion without marked abdominal pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) – Elevated bilirubin, ALT, and AST suggest hepatocellular damage. In chronic liver disease, albumin may be low, and INR prolonged.
  • Serum Ammonia – Elevated levels can confirm hepatic encephalopathy due to the liver’s inability to detoxify ammonia.
  • Ultrasound with Doppler – Identifies cirrhosis, ascites, or portal hypertension, and assesses liver vasculature for thrombus or flow issues.
  • CT/MRI – Useful for identifying liver lesions, masses, or detailed liver architecture in chronic liver failure.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with confusion, jaundice, and abdominal distension over a week, with a background of chronic alcohol use and recent onset of fatigue.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Hepatitis – Hepatitis may present similarly but usually lacks significant encephalopathy unless very advanced.
  • Biliary obstruction – Presents with jaundice, but ultrasound would show biliary dilation, distinguishing it from liver failure.
  • Alcoholic Hepatitis – Common in patients with heavy alcohol use, presents with hepatomegaly and systemic inflammatory response, differentiating it from non-alcohol-related liver failure.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute Liver Failure: Immediate hospital admission, supportive care in ICU, monitoring for cerebral oedema, coagulopathy correction, and potential liver transplant referral. Paracetamol-induced liver failure requires N-acetylcysteine.
  • Chronic Liver Failure: Manage underlying cause (e.g., antivirals for hepatitis, alcohol cessation, weight loss for NAFLD), manage complications (diuretics for ascites, lactulose for encephalopathy), and refer for liver transplantation in advanced cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Hepatorenal Syndrome: Renal failure due to reduced renal perfusion from systemic vasodilation and portal hypertension.
  • Variceal Bleeding: Rupture of dilated oesophageal or gastric veins due to portal hypertension, leading to life-threatening haemorrhage.
  • Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP): Infection of ascitic fluid, often associated with advanced cirrhosis, leading to sepsis if untreated.
