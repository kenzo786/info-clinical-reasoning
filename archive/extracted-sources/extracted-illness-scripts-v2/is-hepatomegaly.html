Hepatomegaly
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Hepatomegaly is defined as an abnormal enlargement of the liver, where the liver spans more than 12–15 cm in the midclavicular line in adults. This enlargement can be a result of various systemic or liver-specific conditions, such as infection, metabolic diseases, infiltrative processes, or vascular disorders. It is often discovered incidentally but may present with symptoms related to the underlying cause or due to mass effect from the enlarged liver.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • In the UK, hepatomegaly is relatively uncommon in general practice but is more frequently seen in hospitalised patients. Exact prevalence rates vary due to the wide range of underlying causes. Worldwide, hepatomegaly is more common in regions with a higher prevalence of infectious diseases like viral hepatitis, malaria, or schistosomiasis, or in populations with high rates of metabolic syndrome and alcohol-related liver disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of hepatomegaly depends on the underlying cause:
    • Infectious causes: Inflammation due to viral infections (e.g., hepatitis) or parasitic infections (e.g., schistosomiasis) leads to hepatocyte injury and immune cell infiltration, causing swelling of the liver tissue.
    • Metabolic causes: Accumulation of fat, iron, or glycogen (as in fatty liver disease, haemochromatosis, or glycogen storage disorders) results in hepatocyte enlargement.
    • Congestive causes: Increased venous pressure due to heart failure or Budd-Chiari syndrome leads to passive hepatic congestion and subsequent enlargement.
    • Neoplastic causes: Infiltration by malignant cells (e.g., primary hepatocellular carcinoma or metastases) increases the liver size as the tumour grows.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Hepatomegaly can develop acutely or chronically depending on the cause. Infective causes like viral hepatitis may result in a rapid onset of hepatomegaly, while metabolic and neoplastic causes generally result in a more gradual enlargement over weeks to months. Congestive causes may fluctuate in size based on changes in haemodynamic status.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Abdominal discomfort or fullness - Hepatomegaly can cause a sensation of fullness or discomfort in the right upper quadrant due to stretching of the liver capsule.
  • Fatigue - A non-specific symptom commonly associated with underlying liver pathology, such as viral hepatitis or cirrhosis, affecting overall metabolism and energy levels.
  • Jaundice - If hepatomegaly is related to cholestasis or significant hepatocellular damage, jaundice may occur due to impaired bilirubin clearance.
  • Weight loss - Often seen in malignant causes of hepatomegaly, such as primary or metastatic liver cancer, due to systemic effects of cancer and metabolic derangements.
  • Pruritus - If cholestasis is present, accumulation of bile salts can lead to itching.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Palpable liver edge - On physical examination, the liver edge may be palpable below the costal margin, often firm or irregular depending on the cause (e.g., cirrhosis vs. tumour).
  • Ascites - A sign of advanced liver disease or portal hypertension, where fluid accumulates in the peritoneal cavity.
  • Spider naevi - Dilated cutaneous blood vessels on the upper torso, seen in chronic liver disease due to altered oestrogen metabolism.
  • Scleral icterus - Yellowing of the sclera due to hyperbilirubinaemia in hepatocellular dysfunction or cholestasis.
  • Peripheral oedema - Often associated with hypoalbuminaemia or portal hypertension in advanced liver disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of hepatomegaly - May indicate acute liver failure or malignancy, both requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe jaundice - Suggests significant liver dysfunction or biliary obstruction, warranting immediate investigation.
  • Weight loss and cachexia - Raises concern for underlying malignancy, particularly hepatocellular carcinoma or metastatic disease.
  • Haematemesis or melaena - Indicates possible variceal bleeding due to portal hypertension, a life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Hepatomegaly is often found incidentally during routine examinations or imaging, particularly in metabolic causes like fatty liver disease.
  • Painless jaundice - In malignancies (e.g., pancreatic cancer with liver metastases), jaundice may develop without significant pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Abnormalities in ALT, AST, ALP, and bilirubin levels help differentiate between hepatocellular injury (elevated ALT, AST) and cholestasis (elevated ALP).
  • Ultrasound of the liver - First-line imaging to confirm liver size and assess for structural abnormalities like tumours, cysts, or fatty infiltration.
  • CT/MRI of the abdomen - Provides more detailed imaging, particularly useful in characterising masses, liver architecture, and vascular involvement.
  • Liver biopsy - Performed if imaging and blood tests are inconclusive, providing definitive diagnosis in cases of infiltrative or inflammatory liver disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old male presents with a two-month history of right upper quadrant discomfort and early satiety. He reports weight loss and mild fatigue. On examination, hepatomegaly and mild jaundice are noted. Blood tests show elevated ALT and ALP, and an abdominal ultrasound confirms an enlarged liver with fatty infiltration.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Fatty liver disease - Usually asymptomatic with normal or mildly elevated LFTs. Differentiated from other causes by imaging showing fatty infiltration.
  • Liver cirrhosis - Presents with signs of chronic liver disease (e.g., ascites, varices), and imaging shows a nodular liver with evidence of portal hypertension.
  • Liver metastases - Often accompanied by weight loss and systemic symptoms. Imaging typically shows multiple focal lesions.
  • Right heart failure - Hepatomegaly occurs due to passive congestion; clinical signs include raised JVP and peripheral oedema.
  • Haematological malignancies - Lymphomas or leukaemias can present with hepatosplenomegaly, fatigue, and systemic symptoms like fever or night sweats.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Treatment of underlying cause - Management of hepatomegaly revolves around addressing the underlying condition, whether it be antiviral therapy for hepatitis, lifestyle changes and weight loss for fatty liver disease, or chemotherapy for malignancies.
  • Symptomatic management - In cases of discomfort or ascites, diuretics (e.g., spironolactone, furosemide) and therapeutic paracentesis may be used.
  • Liver transplant - Considered in cases of end-stage liver disease or irresectable hepatocellular carcinoma.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hepatomegaly"][/sc]
  • Liver failure - Occurs in advanced liver disease, leading to jaundice, coagulopathy, and encephalopathy.
  • Portal hypertension - Resulting in ascites, variceal bleeding, and splenomegaly.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma - A potential complication of cirrhosis or chronic viral hepatitis.
