Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy refers to a group of disorders characterised by bilateral, symmetrical, and progressive visual loss due to damage to the optic nerves, caused by toxins, nutritional deficiencies, or a combination of both.
  • It involves preferential damage to the papillomacular bundle of retinal ganglion cells, leading to central or cecocentral scotomas and impaired colour vision.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is rare, estimated at <1 case per 100,000 individuals annually.
  • More prevalent in resource-poor settings or in populations with high alcohol use or poor nutrition.
  • Worldwide prevalence is not well-documented but is higher in areas with endemic malnutrition or exposure to toxic substances.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Damage occurs primarily to the optic nerve axons, specifically in the papillomacular bundle.
  • Nutritional deficiencies, particularly of vitamin B12, thiamine, or folate, impair myelin synthesis and axonal function.
  • Toxins, such as methanol or tobacco by-products, induce mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress, further damaging axonal integrity.
  • Ischaemia and apoptosis of retinal ganglion cells exacerbate optic nerve damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual onset of blurred vision and central scotomas over weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: Visual loss worsens, and colour vision impairment develops if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Stabilisation of visual deficits with potential for partial recovery if nutritional and toxic causes are corrected.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Blurred vision - Central or cecocentral vision affected due to papillomacular bundle damage.
  • Loss of colour vision - Often an early symptom, linked to retinal ganglion cell dysfunction.
  • Photophobia - Increased light sensitivity due to retinal stress.
  • Gradual onset bilateral vision loss - Symmetrical involvement typical of systemic causes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Central scotomas - Detected on visual field testing, indicative of papillomacular bundle involvement.
  • Reduced visual acuity - Bilateral impairment, reflecting optic nerve damage.
  • Optic disc pallor - Late finding, representing chronic optic nerve atrophy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive visual loss - May indicate methanol toxicity or other acute insults requiring emergency intervention.
  • Associated neurological symptoms - E.g., ataxia or paraesthesia, suggesting concurrent nutritional deficiencies like B12 deficiency.
  • Unilateral vision loss - Atypical and may suggest alternative diagnoses such as optic neuritis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Unilateral involvement - Rare and may delay diagnosis, seen in early-stage toxic exposure.
  • Minimal vision loss with prominent colour vision impairment - Seen in subtle cases of early nutritional deficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Visual field testing - Central or cecocentral scotomas.
  • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - Thinning of retinal nerve fibre layer in the papillomacular bundle.
  • Serum studies - Low vitamin B12, folate, or thiamine; elevated homocysteine or methylmalonic acid in B12 deficiency.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]

A 45-year-old male with a history of alcohol misuse presents with gradual bilateral blurred vision and difficulty distinguishing colours over three months, with central scotomas on visual field testing.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis - Typically unilateral, painful, and associated with multiple sclerosis.
  • Leber’s hereditary optic neuropathy - Mitochondrial inheritance, usually affecting younger males.
  • Glaucoma - Peripheral visual field loss first, elevated intraocular pressure.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Nutritional supplementation - Vitamin B12, thiamine, folate correction.
  • Avoidance of toxins - Cessation of alcohol and tobacco use.
  • Supportive care - Visual aids and rehabilitation for irreversible damage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss - From delayed treatment or severe exposure.
  • Neurological deficits - Peripheral neuropathy in untreated nutritional deficiencies.
