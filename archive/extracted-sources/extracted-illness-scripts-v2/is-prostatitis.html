Prostatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Prostatitis is an inflammatory condition of the prostate gland, often caused by bacterial infection or chronic inflammation. It is characterised by pelvic pain, urinary symptoms, and sometimes systemic symptoms depending on the underlying type (acute bacterial, chronic bacterial, chronic pelvic pain syndrome, or asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, prostatitis is estimated to affect approximately 8-10% of men, with higher rates among younger men and those with recurrent urinary tract infections.
  • Acute bacterial prostatitis represents about 2% of prostatitis cases seen in primary care, while chronic bacterial prostatitis and chronic pelvic pain syndrome account for most remaining cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence is comparable, with an estimated lifetime prevalence of 2-16% globally, influenced by geographical and lifestyle factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Prostatitis often begins with an infection in the urinary tract, which can ascend to the prostate, particularly in cases of acute bacterial prostatitis. Common pathogens include Escherichia coli, Klebsiella, and Proteus species.
  • In chronic bacterial prostatitis, recurrent infections lead to persistent inflammation within the prostate, sometimes due to biofilm formation by bacteria, which resists immune clearance.
  • In chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CPPS), no bacterial cause is identified, and pathophysiology involves inflammatory and neuropathic mechanisms, likely related to pelvic muscle dysfunction and nerve sensitisation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bacterial prostatitis: Sudden onset of symptoms, typically within days. Symptoms may resolve with treatment over 2-4 weeks.
  • Chronic bacterial prostatitis: Recurrent or persistent symptoms lasting >3 months, with remissions and exacerbations.
  • Chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CPPS): Persistent symptoms for >3 months, often fluctuating in intensity.
  • Asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis: Detected incidentally without symptoms; no acute time course.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic or perineal pain - Common in all types; due to inflammation of the prostate and surrounding structures.
  • Dysuria - Pain during urination, resulting from inflammation of the prostate and nearby urethra.
  • Urinary frequency and urgency - Increased sensitivity of the bladder and surrounding nerves, often with urinary irritation.
  • Fever and chills - In acute bacterial prostatitis, systemic inflammation causes these symptoms.
  • Lower back pain - Due to proximity of the prostate to pelvic and sacral nerves.
  • Sexual dysfunction - May include pain with ejaculation or erectile dysfunction, typically in chronic conditions due to local nerve sensitisation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Tender prostate on digital rectal examination (DRE) - In acute cases, the prostate may be swollen and tender; in chronic cases, tenderness may be milder.
  • Suprapubic tenderness - Reflects inflammation in the pelvic region.
  • Fever - Common in acute bacterial prostatitis, as systemic infection activates the immune response.
  • Reduced or interrupted urine flow - Occurs due to inflammation and swelling around the urethra.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Systemic infection can progress to sepsis in acute bacterial prostatitis; manifests as fever, tachycardia, hypotension, and confusion.
  • Urinary retention - Inflammation may obstruct urine flow, leading to acute retention and bladder distension.
  • Abscess formation - A complication in severe cases, indicated by fluctuating fevers and worsening pelvic pain.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis - Identified incidentally during investigations for other conditions, with no reported symptoms.
  • Isolated lower back pain - In chronic cases, may present without classic urinary symptoms, causing diagnostic challenges.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Urine culture - Identifies causative bacteria in bacterial prostatitis; likely pathogens include E. coli and Proteus species.
  • Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) - Elevated in prostatitis due to inflammation, though not specific.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show leukocytosis in acute bacterial cases.
  • Transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) - Used in chronic cases or to detect abscess formation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute onset pelvic pain, dysuria, fever, and chills lasting 2 days. Physical examination reveals a tender prostate on DRE, and urine culture is positive for E. coli.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Similar urinary symptoms but lacks prostate tenderness on DRE.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) - Common in older men with urinary symptoms but without pain or systemic symptoms.
  • Prostate cancer - Usually asymptomatic or with obstructive symptoms; often detected with elevated PSA and biopsy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bacterial prostatitis: Empirical antibiotics (e.g., ciprofloxacin or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) adjusted per culture results; analgesia; hydration.
  • Chronic bacterial prostatitis: Prolonged antibiotics (4-6 weeks); NSAIDs for pain; alpha-blockers in select cases for urinary symptoms.
  • Chronic pelvic pain syndrome: Multimodal approach with NSAIDs, alpha-blockers, and pelvic floor physiotherapy; consider referral to urology for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - More likely in acute bacterial prostatitis if untreated, with potential systemic impact.
  • Chronic prostatitis - Untreated acute prostatitis can progress to chronic symptoms and recurrent infections.
  • Prostate abscess - Develops in severe cases of bacterial infection, requiring drainage and prolonged antibiotics.
