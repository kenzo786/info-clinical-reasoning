Cystic Fibrosis (CF)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Cystic Fibrosis (CF) is an autosomal recessive genetic disorder caused by mutations in the CFTR gene, leading to dysfunctional chloride ion transport across epithelial cells. This results in thickened secretions affecting multiple organ systems, primarily the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and reproductive tracts.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 10,500 individuals live with CF in the UK, with most diagnosed through newborn screening.
  • UK hospital prevalence: CF is managed by specialised CF centres, affecting about 1 in 2,500 live births annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: CF prevalence varies widely, most common among Caucasians of European descent, with a carrier frequency of 1 in 25.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Mutations in the CFTR gene disrupt chloride and water transport across epithelial cells.
  • Reduced chloride secretion leads to viscous mucus in airways, gastrointestinal tract, and ducts.
  • Thickened secretions result in airway obstruction, chronic infections, pancreatic insufficiency, and malabsorption.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal phase: Meconium ileus or asymptomatic identification through screening.
  • Childhood: Respiratory symptoms and failure to thrive often emerge.
  • Adulthood: Progressive respiratory decline, increased complications, and reduced life expectancy without intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Chronic productive cough: Caused by mucus obstruction and recurrent infections in the airways.
  • Frequent respiratory infections: Resulting from thick mucus promoting bacterial colonisation.
  • Failure to thrive: Due to malabsorption from pancreatic insufficiency.
  • Steatorrhoea: Fatty stools caused by impaired fat digestion.
  • Infertility: Secondary to obstructive azoospermia in males or viscous cervical mucus in females.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Digital clubbing: Chronic hypoxia leads to changes in nailbed vasculature.
  • Crackles on auscultation: Reflect airway mucus plugging and inflammation.
  • Failure to gain weight: Visible in infants and children due to malnutrition.
  • Barrel chest: Indicative of chronic respiratory disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis: May indicate bronchiectasis or severe infection.
  • Sudden dyspnoea or chest pain: Suggests pneumothorax, a common complication.
  • Persistent fever: Reflects ongoing or severe infection such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Late-onset presentations with mild symptoms such as recurrent sinusitis or male infertility.
  • Atypical mutations may present with less severe pulmonary or pancreatic disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Newborn screening: Elevated immunoreactive trypsinogen (IRT) in blood.
  • Sweat chloride test: Levels ≥60 mmol/L confirm diagnosis; reflects defective chloride transport.
  • Genetic testing: Identification of CFTR mutations confirms the diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • A 3-month-old male identified via newborn screening, presenting with failure to thrive, steatorrhoea, and recurrent respiratory infections.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia: Recurrent respiratory infections but normal sweat chloride levels.
  • Coeliac disease: Malabsorption with weight loss but no chronic respiratory symptoms.
  • Asthma: Chronic cough with reversible airway obstruction and no malabsorption.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Airway clearance therapies: Regular physiotherapy and mucolytics like dornase alfa.
  • Antibiotics: Prophylactic or targeted treatment against common pathogens.
  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy: Improves digestion and absorption.
  • CFTR modulators: E.g., ivacaftor, tailored to specific mutations.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cystic Fibrosis (CF)"][/sc]
  • Bronchiectasis: Frequent infections cause structural airway damage.
  • Diabetes mellitus: Secondary to pancreatic damage.
  • Osteoporosis: Caused by malabsorption and chronic inflammation.
  • Prognosis: Life expectancy is improving with modern treatments, averaging over 40 years in the UK.
