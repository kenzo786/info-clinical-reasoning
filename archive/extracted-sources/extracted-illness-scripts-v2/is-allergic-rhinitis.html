Allergic Rhinitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis is a chronic inflammatory condition of the nasal mucosa triggered by an IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reaction to allergens. It is characterised by symptoms such as nasal congestion, sneezing, rhinorrhoea, and nasal itching. It can be classified as seasonal (hay fever) or perennial (year-round).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 10–30%.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less commonly managed in hospitals unless complications arise, but significant in ENT outpatient settings.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects 10–40% of the global population, with increasing rates in urbanised areas.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Exposure to allergen: Initial sensitisation occurs when allergens (e.g., pollen, dust mites) are inhaled.
  • IgE production: Allergen-specific IgE is produced by plasma cells and binds to receptors on mast cells in the nasal mucosa.
  • Subsequent exposure: Re-exposure leads to allergen cross-linking on mast cells, causing degranulation.
  • Release of mediators: Histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins cause vasodilation, increased permeability, and stimulation of sensory nerves.
  • Late-phase reaction: Recruitment of eosinophils and other inflammatory cells prolongs the response, leading to persistent symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset occurs minutes after allergen exposure, dominated by sneezing and itching.
  • Late phase: Begins 4–6 hours post-exposure, with sustained nasal congestion and eosinophilic infiltration.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent symptoms if exposure is ongoing or untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Nasal congestion: Caused by vasodilation and increased vascular permeability leading to mucosal oedema.
  • Sneezing: Triggered by histamine acting on sensory nerves.
  • Rhinorrhoea: Clear nasal discharge due to increased glandular secretion.
  • Nasal itching: Histamine-induced stimulation of nerve endings.
  • Postnasal drip: Excess mucus drainage into the throat, especially in perennial rhinitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Allergic salute: Repeated upward rubbing of the nose leading to a transverse nasal crease.
  • Pale, oedematous nasal mucosa: Caused by chronic inflammation and vasodilation.
  • Conjunctival injection: Hyperaemia of conjunctiva due to associated allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Dark circles under eyes (allergic shiners): Due to venous stasis from nasal congestion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Unilateral symptoms: May indicate a nasal polyp, foreign body, or tumour.
  • Persistent facial pain: Suggests sinusitis or malignancy.
  • Bloody nasal discharge: Raises suspicion of malignancy or granulomatosis with polyangiitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough: May be misdiagnosed as asthma or GERD due to postnasal drip.
  • Fatigue: Resulting from disrupted sleep due to nasal congestion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Skin prick test: Positive reaction to suspected allergens indicates sensitisation.
  • Specific IgE testing: Elevated IgE levels against allergens confirm diagnosis.
  • Nasal smear: Eosinophilia supports allergic aetiology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with recurrent sneezing, nasal congestion, and clear rhinorrhoea during spring, exacerbated by outdoor activities.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Non-allergic rhinitis: Absence of IgE-mediated mechanism and allergen sensitisation.
  • Sinusitis: Presence of purulent discharge and facial pain.
  • Nasal polyps: Associated with anosmia and visible on examination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Avoidance of allergens: Key preventive measure.
  • Intranasal corticosteroids: First-line for reducing inflammation (e.g., fluticasone).
  • Antihistamines: Second-generation agents (e.g., loratadine) for symptom relief.
  • Leukotriene receptor antagonists: May be beneficial in some cases.
  • Immunotherapy: Considered for severe cases unresponsive to pharmacotherapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Sinusitis: Due to obstruction of sinus drainage.
  • Eustachian tube dysfunction: Leading to otitis media.
  • Impaired quality of life: Poor sleep and daytime fatigue.
