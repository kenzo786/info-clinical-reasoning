Brain Tumour
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • A brain tumour is an abnormal growth of tissue within the brain or central spinal canal. It may be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). Tumours are categorised based on the cells they originate from and their behaviour. Common types include gliomas, meningiomas, and metastatic tumours. Primary brain tumours arise from brain tissue, while secondary brain tumours metastasise from cancers elsewhere in the body.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • In the UK, brain tumours account for approximately 1% of all GP consultations. They make up 3-4% of all primary tumours seen in UK hospitals, and the incidence rate is about 12 per 100,000 people globally per year. The risk of brain tumours increases with age, although they can occur at any age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Brain tumours result from uncontrolled cell proliferation. Genetic mutations disrupt the normal regulatory mechanisms that control cell division. As the tumour enlarges, it exerts pressure on surrounding brain structures, leading to localised neurological deficits. Malignant tumours may invade adjacent tissues and cause widespread destruction. Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) from tumour growth or associated oedema is a key contributor to many symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • The time course of brain tumours varies widely depending on the type and location. Benign tumours typically grow slowly over months to years, while malignant tumours can progress rapidly over weeks to months. Symptoms may worsen gradually or acutely if there is haemorrhage, oedema, or increased intracranial pressure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Headache - Often worse in the morning and may be accompanied by nausea/vomiting due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Seizures - Occur in about 30% of patients with brain tumours, caused by disruption of normal electrical activity in the brain.
  • Cognitive or personality changes - Tumours in the frontal lobe can cause disinhibition, memory problems, or personality shifts due to interference with executive functions.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Often due to increased intracranial pressure or direct irritation of brain structures.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Symptoms like weakness, visual field loss, or speech difficulties depend on the tumour's location.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Papilloedema - Swelling of the optic disc due to raised intracranial pressure.
  • Hemiparesis - Weakness on one side of the body, often seen with tumours affecting the motor cortex or corticospinal tracts.
  • Aphasia - Difficulty with speech or language, particularly with tumours in the dominant hemisphere’s temporal or frontal lobes.
  • Ataxia - Tumours in the cerebellum can disrupt coordination and balance.
  • Altered consciousness - Due to raised intracranial pressure or large tumours compressing brainstem structures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • New-onset seizures in an adult - Suggestive of intracranial pathology such as a tumour disrupting normal brain function.
  • Progressive focal neurological deficits - Indicates localised brain tissue damage or compression from tumour growth.
  • Headaches worsening in the morning or with Valsalva manoeuvres - Typical of increased intracranial pressure, which can be a sign of brain tumours.
  • Vomiting without nausea - A sign of raised intracranial pressure affecting the brainstem vomiting centre.
  • Altered consciousness - May signal brainstem involvement or raised intracranial pressure at a critical level.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Subtle personality changes - Can be easily mistaken for psychiatric conditions, particularly in frontal lobe tumours.
  • Chronic headaches with no neurological signs - Slow-growing tumours can present with non-specific symptoms, sometimes confused with migraines or tension headaches.
  • Intermittent visual disturbances - Tumours in the occipital lobe may cause visual aura-like symptoms, often misinterpreted as migraine.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • MRI of the brain - Preferred imaging modality to visualise brain tumours. Expected findings include mass effect, oedema, and contrast enhancement indicating a tumour.
  • CT of the brain - Useful for detecting large masses or calcified tumours but less sensitive than MRI for soft tissue detail.
  • Biopsy - Essential for histological diagnosis and grading of the tumour. Determines if the tumour is benign or malignant.
  • EEG (Electroencephalogram) - May be used if seizures are present to evaluate abnormal electrical activity in the brain.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of progressively worsening headaches, new-onset seizures, and subtle memory deficits. MRI reveals a left temporal lobe mass consistent with a glioma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Sudden onset compared to the gradual progression seen in brain tumours. Imaging will show infarction or haemorrhage without mass effect.
  • Brain abscess - More likely to present with fever and systemic infection markers. MRI shows a ring-enhancing lesion with central necrosis.
  • Migraine - Headaches may mimic those of brain tumours, but migraines often have a long history without focal neurological deficits.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Symptoms may resemble those of a tumour, but MRI will show demyelination rather than a mass lesion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Surgery - Surgical resection is often the first step for accessible tumours, aiming for maximal tumour removal.
  • Radiotherapy - Used post-operatively for malignant or incompletely resected tumours.
  • Chemotherapy - Temozolomide is often used in gliomas, especially when paired with radiotherapy.
  • Symptom control - Anticonvulsants for seizure control and steroids to reduce cerebral oedema and intracranial pressure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Raised intracranial pressure - Can lead to brain herniation and death if untreated. Frequent in large tumours or those causing significant oedema.
  • Neurological deficits - Permanent impairments such as hemiparesis, aphasia, or visual loss may occur depending on tumour location.
  • Seizures - Can become chronic and refractory to treatment, especially in cases of cortical involvement.
  • Recurrence - Malignant tumours, particularly glioblastomas, have a high risk of recurrence even after treatment.
