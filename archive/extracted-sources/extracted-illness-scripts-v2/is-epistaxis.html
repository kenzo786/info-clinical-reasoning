Epistaxis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Epistaxis, commonly known as a nosebleed, is bleeding from the nasal cavity, often originating from the anterior nasal septum (Kiesselbach's plexus) or, less frequently, from posterior sources. It ranges from minor self-limiting episodes to severe cases requiring medical intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Epistaxis accounts for approximately 1 in 200 GP consultations, affecting around 10% of the UK population annually.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospital admissions for epistaxis occur in 20–40 per 100,000 people per year, more common in elderly populations.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Epistaxis occurs in up to 60% of people globally, though only around 6–10% seek medical attention.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Epistaxis often results from rupture of blood vessels within the nasal mucosa. In anterior epistaxis, which is more common, rupture occurs in the Kiesselbach’s plexus, an area with dense capillary networks.
  • Common causes include local trauma (e.g., nose-picking, nasal injury), environmental dryness, and inflammation from infections.
  • Posterior epistaxis, more severe and likely in older adults, involves branches of the sphenopalatine artery and can result in substantial blood loss.
  • Systemic factors such as hypertension, coagulopathies, and medications (e.g., anticoagulants) increase bleeding risk by promoting vessel fragility or impairing clot formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Epistaxis may present acutely, often resolving within minutes. However, it can persist intermittently over weeks if predisposing factors like dry air or nasal picking remain.
  • Severe, recurrent episodes may indicate underlying systemic causes (e.g., hypertension, blood disorders) requiring longer-term management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Unilateral nasal bleeding - Commonly occurs with anterior epistaxis, as bleeding typically stems from one nostril due to rupture in the Kiesselbach plexus.
  • Bilateral nasal bleeding - May occur in posterior epistaxis when blood flows back, potentially indicating a more serious source.
  • Frequent bleeding episodes - Often suggestive of underlying factors like coagulopathies or hypertension, due to impaired clotting or increased vascular fragility.
  • Associated nasal congestion - Can result from inflammation or irritation, often seen in upper respiratory infections or allergic rhinitis.
  • Headache or dizziness - Significant blood loss or anaemia can lead to dizziness, especially in posterior epistaxis with large volume loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Visible nasal blood or clots - Common in anterior epistaxis, where blood is typically visible at the nostril opening.
  • Posterior pharyngeal blood flow - Blood seen in the oropharynx may suggest posterior epistaxis, particularly if blood drips down the throat.
  • Pallor - Pallor may result from significant blood loss in severe epistaxis episodes.
  • Increased blood pressure - Hypertension may be noted, either as a primary cause or as a response to blood loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Recurrent, heavy bleeding episodes - May indicate a bleeding disorder (e.g., haemophilia) or malignancy if prolonged and not responsive to standard treatment.
  • Posterior bleeding - Suggests potentially more severe causes, often seen in elderly patients and may lead to airway compromise or aspiration.
  • Signs of anaemia (pallor, fatigue) - Indicate significant or chronic blood loss, warranting full blood count and iron studies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Isolated recurrent unilateral epistaxis - Can indicate a local tumour (e.g., nasopharyngeal carcinoma) and should be investigated if other causes are excluded.
  • Epistaxis in younger children - Rarely suggests a bleeding disorder (e.g., von Willebrand disease) if there is a family history or concurrent bruising.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - To assess haemoglobin levels for anaemia from blood loss and evaluate platelet count for potential thrombocytopenia.
  • Coagulation profile - Includes PT, APTT, and INR to evaluate clotting abnormalities, which are relevant in patients on anticoagulants or with bleeding disorders.
  • Nasal endoscopy - Allows direct visualisation for identifying posterior bleeds or assessing for masses in persistent/recurrent cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • An elderly male with hypertension presents with recurrent unilateral epistaxis that is difficult to control and is associated with mild dizziness, suggesting potential posterior bleeding.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Often associated with recurrent epistaxis but lacks other signs of bleeding disorder.
  • Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia - Causes recurrent, spontaneous epistaxis with visible telangiectasias on mucous membranes.
  • Leukaemia - May present with epistaxis due to thrombocytopenia, with additional findings of bruising and fatigue.
  • Trauma or foreign body - Suggestive if recent trauma or nasal obstruction is present, usually in children.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Initial management - Involves pressure on the nasal septum, ice application, and leaning forward.
  • Topical vasoconstrictors and cauterisation - Used if initial measures fail, typically silver nitrate cautery for visible bleeds.
  • Nasal packing - Employed for posterior epistaxis or if bleeding persists after cautery.
  • Address underlying conditions - Control hypertension and discontinue blood thinners if appropriate.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability - Occurs in severe cases, especially posterior epistaxis, due to significant blood loss.
  • Anaemia - From recurrent or heavy bleeding episodes, particularly in older adults or those with underlying disorders.
  • Aspiration - Posterior bleeds can lead to blood aspiration, risking airway compromise or aspiration pneumonia.
