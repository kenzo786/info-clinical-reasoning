Otitis Externa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa is an inflammation of the external auditory canal, which may involve the auricle and outer ear. It is often associated with bacterial infection, though fungal infections and irritant causes are also possible. The condition typically presents with pain, itching, and sometimes discharge from the ear.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa is common, with an incidence of around 1% of GP consultations in the UK annually. It is most frequent in adults, though children can be affected, particularly those who swim frequently. Hospitalisation is rare, but severe cases or complications may require admission.
  • Globally, it is prevalent in warm, humid climates and among individuals who engage in water activities ("swimmer's ear").
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • The external auditory canal is lined with skin, which contains glands that produce cerumen (earwax). Cerumen protects the canal by creating an acidic, hydrophobic barrier. When this barrier is disrupted—by moisture, trauma, or ear cleaning—the environment becomes more susceptible to infection. Bacteria such as *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and *Staphylococcus aureus* are common pathogens. Inflammatory responses lead to swelling, pain, and sometimes purulent discharge.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis externa typically develops over a few days to a week. The symptoms may worsen rapidly if untreated. In chronic cases, symptoms last longer than three months and may be more persistent but less intense than acute presentations. Exacerbations may occur intermittently.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Otalgia (ear pain) - The most common presenting symptom, due to inflammation and pressure on sensory nerves in the external auditory canal.
  • Pruritus (itching) - Caused by irritation of the skin and inflammatory mediators, often preceding the onset of pain.
  • Discharge - Can be serous or purulent, indicating a breach in the integrity of the epithelial barrier due to infection or inflammation.
  • Hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss may occur when the canal is occluded by swelling or debris.
  • Aural fullness - The sensation of ear blockage, caused by swelling of the external ear canal or discharge buildup.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Tenderness on tragal pressure - Pain when pressing on the tragus or pulling the pinna is a hallmark of otitis externa, caused by inflammation of the canal's outer structures.
  • Canal oedema and erythema - Visible swelling and redness due to the inflammatory process.
  • Otorrhoea - Presence of discharge, typically seen in bacterial infection.
  • Debris in the ear canal - Dead skin, wax, or purulent material may accumulate within the canal.
  • Conductive hearing loss - Due to occlusion of the canal, not damage to the middle or inner ear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Fever - Suggests a more severe infection, potentially extending beyond the ear canal.
  • Severe pain extending to the face or neck - May indicate the spread of infection to surrounding tissues, including cellulitis or necrotising otitis externa (malignant otitis externa).
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Could signify the involvement of the cranial nerves in necrotising otitis externa, a serious complication often seen in immunocompromised patients.
  • Diabetes or immunocompromised state - Increased risk of necrotising otitis externa, a life-threatening infection of the bone (osteomyelitis) around the ear canal.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Chronic otitis externa - Presents with mild, persistent symptoms such as itching, discomfort, and flaking skin rather than severe pain or discharge.
  • Fungal otitis externa - Characterised by less pain but more intense pruritus, and the presence of "fluffy" fungal debris in the ear canal.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Otoscopy - Reveals canal swelling, erythema, and discharge. This is the primary diagnostic tool for visualising the external ear.
  • Ear swab and culture - Indicated if the infection is chronic or not responding to treatment. Bacterial culture may identify *Pseudomonas* or *Staphylococcus* species, while fungal culture can confirm fungal aetiologies.
  • CT scan - In cases of suspected necrotising otitis externa, a CT scan can detect bone involvement and help assess the extent of the infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old male presents with a 5-day history of worsening right ear pain and pruritus after swimming. He reports mild hearing loss and a feeling of fullness in the affected ear, with purulent discharge observed during examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media - Typically involves the middle ear and is more associated with systemic symptoms like fever and a bulging tympanic membrane.
  • Eustachian tube dysfunction - Causes aural fullness and hearing loss but lacks the pain and discharge typical of otitis externa.
  • Foreign body in the ear - May present with pain or discharge, but otoscopy will reveal the presence of the object.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Topical antibiotics - First-line treatment, often with a combination of ciprofloxacin and dexamethasone ear drops.
  • Ear cleaning - Removal of debris from the ear canal enhances the efficacy of topical treatments.
  • Analgesia - Paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain relief.
  • Antifungal drops - Indicated for fungal otitis externa.
  • Systemic antibiotics - Reserved for severe infections or when there is suspicion of necrotising otitis externa.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Necrotising otitis externa - A rare but serious complication, where the infection spreads to the skull base, more common in diabetics or immunocompromised patients.
  • Chronic otitis externa - Prolonged inflammation can lead to persistent discomfort and hearing loss.
