Metabolic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Metabolic neuropathy refers to peripheral nerve damage caused by underlying metabolic disorders, such as diabetes mellitus, renal failure, and hypothyroidism. It is characterised by progressive loss of nerve function leading to sensory, motor, and autonomic symptoms, depending on the severity and type of metabolic insult.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Diabetic neuropathy is seen in approximately 10-20% of patients with diabetes.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Diabetic neuropathy accounts for the majority of metabolic neuropathies seen in hospitalised patients.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Diabetic neuropathy affects around 50% of patients with long-term diabetes mellitus.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Metabolic neuropathy arises due to chronic exposure of peripheral nerves to metabolic derangements. In diabetes, chronic hyperglycaemia leads to oxidative stress, glycation of nerve proteins, and microvascular damage. In renal failure, the accumulation of uraemic toxins impairs nerve function. In hypothyroidism, axonal swelling occurs due to deposition of mucopolysaccharides, leading to demyelination and nerve dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: In conditions like diabetes, symptoms may be absent or mild, with only small fibre neuropathy (burning, tingling) initially present.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms worsen over months to years, with a transition from sensory to motor involvement in severe cases.
  • Late Phase: Motor weakness and autonomic dysfunction (e.g., postural hypotension) may appear in advanced cases, particularly in poorly controlled diabetes or longstanding hypothyroidism.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Distal sensory loss (stocking-glove distribution) - Due to length-dependent axonal degeneration, sensory fibres in the extremities are commonly affected first.
  • Paresthesia (tingling, burning) - Small-fibre involvement leads to abnormal sensations due to nerve fibre hyperexcitability.
  • Muscle weakness - Prolonged damage leads to motor nerve involvement, particularly in severe or uncontrolled metabolic states.
  • Autonomic symptoms (e.g., postural hypotension, gastroparesis) - Autonomic fibres are affected in more advanced disease, disrupting sympathetic and parasympathetic function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Reduced vibration and proprioception - Large-fibre involvement leads to decreased sensation in the lower limbs, assessed via tuning fork tests.
  • Absent ankle reflexes - Loss of large afferent fibres and motor involvement leads to diminished or absent deep tendon reflexes.
  • Muscle wasting - Chronic neuropathy results in denervation and atrophy, especially in the distal muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of weakness - May indicate Guillain-Barré syndrome or other acute neuropathies, which require urgent intervention.
  • Severe autonomic dysfunction - Potentially life-threatening and indicates advanced disease, such as cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy.
  • Severe, unremitting pain - Could suggest an underlying compressive or neoplastic process rather than purely metabolic causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Focal mononeuropathy - While metabolic neuropathies are typically symmetric, focal mononeuropathies such as carpal tunnel syndrome may occur in diabetes.
  • Proximal muscle weakness - Diabetic amyotrophy may present with thigh pain and proximal muscle weakness, differing from the usual distal pattern of involvement.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction studies - Reveal reduced conduction velocity in large fibres and axonal loss. These tests confirm the presence and extent of neuropathy.
  • Blood glucose and HbA1c - Elevated levels confirm diabetes as the metabolic cause. HbA1c > 6.5% suggests poor glycaemic control.
  • Renal function tests (e.g., urea, creatinine) - Elevated levels indicate uraemia as a potential cause.
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Low T4 and elevated TSH in hypothyroidism can lead to metabolic neuropathy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a 20-year history of poorly controlled diabetes presents with burning pain and numbness in his feet, along with progressive weakness over the past year. Examination reveals absent ankle reflexes and reduced vibration sense in both lower limbs.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome - Rapid onset of ascending weakness and areflexia, distinguished by acute presentation and CSF findings of elevated protein.
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP) - More prolonged course of progressive weakness, but may overlap with diabetic neuropathy; confirmed with electrophysiological testing.
  • Alcoholic neuropathy - History of alcohol abuse, with similar sensory and motor symptoms, but often accompanied by evidence of liver disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Glycaemic control - Tight glucose control (HbA1c target <7%) is essential to slow progression in diabetic neuropathy.
  • Pain management - Neuropathic pain may be treated with gabapentinoids (e.g., gabapentin, pregabalin) or tricyclic antidepressants.
  • Treat underlying cause - Address underlying metabolic derangements such as hypothyroidism or renal insufficiency.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Foot ulcers and amputations - Reduced sensation increases the risk of injury, leading to chronic ulcers and, in severe cases, amputations.
  • Autonomic neuropathy - Can lead to severe complications like gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension, and arrhythmias.
  • Permanent motor dysfunction - Longstanding disease can cause irreversible motor deficits and atrophy, particularly in the lower limbs.
