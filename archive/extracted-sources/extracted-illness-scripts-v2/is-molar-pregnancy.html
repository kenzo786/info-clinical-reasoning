Molar Pregnancy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A molar pregnancy, or hydatidiform mole, is a gestational trophoblastic disease (GTD) that results from abnormal fertilisation, leading to the growth of abnormal placental tissue. It can be classified into two types: complete and partial moles. A complete mole occurs when an empty egg is fertilised by one or two sperm, leading to no foetal development. A partial mole arises when a normal egg is fertilised by two sperm, resulting in abnormal foetal tissue.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • The incidence of molar pregnancy is approximately 1 in 714 live births in the UK. It is more common in East Asian populations, with global incidence rates varying from 1 in 100 to 1 in 1000 pregnancies. The condition is most common in women under 20 or over 40 years of age and in those with previous molar pregnancies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • In a molar pregnancy, abnormal fertilisation results in an imbalance in the genetic material from the egg and sperm. In complete moles, there is no maternal DNA, and only paternal chromosomes exist, causing trophoblastic hyperplasia without foetal development. In partial moles, both maternal and paternal chromosomes are present, but the extra paternal material causes abnormal placental development and potential foetal abnormalities. Both types lead to excessive growth of trophoblastic tissue, which secretes high levels of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), contributing to the symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Molar pregnancies usually present within the first trimester, typically between 6 and 12 weeks of gestation. Symptoms may escalate quickly due to rapid trophoblastic growth, and if untreated, can lead to complications such as preeclampsia or progression to gestational trophoblastic neoplasia (GTN). After treatment, hCG levels are monitored for several months to ensure resolution and to detect possible malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Vaginal bleeding - The most common symptom, occurring due to abnormal trophoblastic growth causing haemorrhage into the uterine cavity.
  • Excessive nausea and vomiting - Hyperemesis gravidarum is more pronounced due to the high levels of hCG produced by trophoblastic tissue.
  • Pelvic discomfort or pain - Uterine enlargement due to the proliferating molar tissue can cause pressure or pain.
  • Absence of foetal movement - In complete moles, there is no viable foetus, resulting in lack of expected foetal activity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Uterine size larger than gestational age - Due to rapid trophoblastic proliferation, the uterus may be significantly larger than expected for the gestational age.
  • Absent foetal heart sounds - Complete moles have no foetal tissue, leading to an absence of heart tones on Doppler.
  • Grapelike vesicles on ultrasound - Ultrasound reveals a “snowstorm” or “grape-like” appearance of the molar tissue due to swollen villi.
  • Severe hypertension - Pre-eclampsia may develop early (before 20 weeks), which is unusual in normal pregnancies but is a potential complication of molar pregnancy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Severe, early-onset pre-eclampsia - Pre-eclampsia before 20 weeks is a major red flag, suggesting trophoblastic disease.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Elevated hCG levels can mimic TSH, leading to signs of hyperthyroidism like palpitations, heat intolerance, and tremors.
  • Respiratory distress - This can occur if trophoblastic tissue spreads to the lungs, indicating metastatic gestational trophoblastic neoplasia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Minimal symptoms - In some cases, patients may have only mild bleeding or nausea, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • Presenting as a miscarriage - A molar pregnancy may be misdiagnosed as a miscarriage if not properly evaluated with imaging and hCG levels.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Serum hCG - Levels are usually markedly elevated (often >100,000 mIU/mL) compared to normal pregnancy due to the trophoblastic hyperplasia.
  • Pelvic ultrasound - Ultrasound findings include a “snowstorm” appearance in complete moles, with no detectable foetal parts.
  • Histopathological examination - Following evacuation of the uterus, the tissue is examined for trophoblastic hyperplasia and villous oedema to confirm the diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents at 10 weeks gestation with vaginal bleeding and excessive vomiting. Examination reveals a uterine size larger than expected for gestational age, and ultrasound shows a “snowstorm” pattern with no foetal heart sounds. Serum hCG is significantly elevated.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Missed miscarriage - Presents with vaginal bleeding and absent foetal heart sounds, but ultrasound reveals retained foetal parts and lower hCG levels.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Can present with vaginal bleeding and pelvic pain, but ultrasound often shows an empty uterus or a gestational sac outside the uterus, with lower hCG levels.
  • Choriocarcinoma - A type of gestational trophoblastic neoplasia that can occur after molar pregnancy, presenting with similar symptoms but more aggressive behaviour and potential metastases.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Suction curettage - The primary treatment for molar pregnancy is uterine evacuation via suction curettage to remove molar tissue.
  • hCG monitoring - Serial hCG measurements are required after treatment to ensure complete resolution. Persistent hCG elevation suggests GTN.
  • Contraception - Effective contraception is recommended for at least 6-12 months post-treatment to avoid confusion with hCG monitoring.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Molar Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Gestational trophoblastic neoplasia (GTN) - Approximately 15-20% of complete moles and 0.5-5% of partial moles can progress to GTN, a malignant form of GTD.
  • Metastasis - GTN can metastasise, particularly to the lungs, leading to respiratory complications.
  • Uterine perforation - Uterine evacuation may lead to perforation in some cases.
