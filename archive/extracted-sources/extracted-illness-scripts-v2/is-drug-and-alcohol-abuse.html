Drug and Alcohol Abuse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Drug and alcohol abuse refers to the excessive and harmful use of substances, including illicit drugs, prescription medications, and alcohol. It is characterised by a pattern of consumption leading to physical, psychological, or social impairment. This often progresses to addiction, marked by an inability to control use, cravings, and continued use despite negative consequences.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 1 in 11 adults aged 16-59 have used an illicit drug in the past year (2021 data), with alcohol misuse affecting about 27% of men and 13% of women annually.
  • In UK general practice, around 10-20% of patients may present with substance abuse issues.
  • Hospital admissions for drug-related conditions were around 7,500 in 2021-2022, while alcohol-related admissions totalled 976,425.
  • Worldwide, the Global Burden of Disease study reports that alcohol and drug use disorders contribute to 5.4% of the global disease burden.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Substance use impacts brain reward pathways, particularly involving dopamine and other neurotransmitters. Prolonged use leads to neuroplastic changes, resulting in tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal symptoms.
  • Alcohol primarily affects the GABA and NMDA receptors, leading to central nervous system depression. Chronic use can cause liver disease (via oxidative stress and inflammation), cardiovascular issues, and neurotoxicity.
  • Drugs like opioids bind to mu-receptors, altering pain perception and inducing euphoria, while stimulants like cocaine increase dopamine in synapses, leading to intense cravings and psychological dependence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Sporadic use with limited impact on life, possibly socially motivated.
  • Progression Phase: Increasing frequency and dosage, development of tolerance, withdrawal, and psychological dependence.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent use despite harmful effects on physical health, relationships, employment, and legal status. High risk of overdose and significant physical/mental health deterioration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Cravings - An intense desire to use substances due to dopamine pathway dysregulation.
  • Withdrawal symptoms - Physical and psychological discomfort (e.g., nausea, sweating, anxiety) caused by the body's dependence on the substance.
  • Memory lapses - Alcohol and drugs impair the hippocampus, leading to blackouts or memory loss.
  • Social or occupational dysfunction - Persistent substance use interferes with responsibilities, stemming from impaired judgement and prioritisation of substance use.
  • Insomnia - Disruption in sleep-wake cycles due to drug/alcohol interference with neurotransmitter balance (GABA, glutamate, etc.).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Slurred speech - Alcohol and sedatives depress central nervous system function.
  • Pupil changes (e.g., pinpoint pupils with opioids) - Drug-specific signs due to effects on autonomic control.
  • Tremors (alcohol withdrawal) - Autonomic hyperactivity in response to withdrawal from central nervous system depressants.
  • Jaundice - Indicates alcohol-induced liver damage, leading to hyperbilirubinaemia.
  • Track marks - Signs of intravenous drug use, associated with injection sites and potential infections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Seizures - May indicate severe alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal, or opioid overdose.
  • Respiratory depression - Particularly associated with opioid overdose, leading to hypoxia and potential death.
  • Confusion or altered mental state - Suggests potential alcohol poisoning, Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, or severe liver dysfunction.
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting blood due to oesophageal varices or gastritis in chronic alcoholics.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - May present with falls, confusion, or worsening chronic illness, masking underlying alcohol or prescription drug misuse.
  • Teenagers - Substance use may manifest as behavioural problems, poor academic performance, or mental health crises rather than direct substance-related complaints.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Raised MCV may indicate chronic alcohol use, as alcohol impairs red blood cell production.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Raised AST and ALT, with a higher AST/ALT ratio, suggests alcohol-related liver disease.
  • Urine drug screen - Detects recent drug use (e.g., opioids, cocaine) and assists in identifying substance-related causes of acute symptoms.
  • Gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) - Often elevated in chronic alcohol use due to its induction in the liver.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a history of escalating alcohol consumption over 10 years, daily use of opioids, recent job loss, and withdrawal symptoms including tremors, sweating, and anxiety.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Delirium Tremens (severe alcohol withdrawal) - Presents with tremors, confusion, agitation, but typically accompanied by hallucinations and fever.
  • Wernicke's Encephalopathy - Confusion, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, often in malnourished alcoholics.
  • Depression or anxiety disorders - May co-exist or mimic symptoms of drug and alcohol misuse, but without clear substance-related patterns.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Alcohol misuse - Managed with psychosocial support, withdrawal management (e.g., chlordiazepoxide), and long-term relapse prevention (e.g., acamprosate).
  • Opioid misuse - Substitution therapy with buprenorphine or methadone, alongside psychosocial support.
  • Harm reduction - Naloxone kits for opioid users and needle exchange programmes to prevent infections.
  • Psychotherapy - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) or motivational interviewing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Drug and Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Liver cirrhosis - Progressive liver failure due to chronic alcohol use, leading to portal hypertension and varices.
  • Overdose - Particularly with opioids, leading to respiratory failure and potential death.
  • Infectious complications - Increased risk of HIV, hepatitis B/C in intravenous drug users.
