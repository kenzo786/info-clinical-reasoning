Spinal Tuberculosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Spinal Tuberculosis, also known as Pott's disease, is a form of extrapulmonary tuberculosis that affects the vertebral column. It is characterised by infection with *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, typically involving the anterior part of the vertebrae and intervertebral discs, leading to vertebral destruction, spinal instability, and potential spinal cord compression.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Spinal tuberculosis is rare in the UK, constituting less than 1% of tuberculosis cases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 2–3% of patients with TB in the UK present with spinal involvement.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Common in regions with high TB incidence, particularly in Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. Spinal TB accounts for around 10% of extrapulmonary TB cases worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Primary TB infection usually occurs in the lungs, and the bacteria can spread haematogenously or via lymphatics to the vertebrae.
  • The infection tends to localise in the highly vascular cancellous bone of the vertebral bodies, leading to granuloma formation.
  • Progressive inflammation and caseous necrosis cause destruction of intervertebral discs and vertebrae, resulting in kyphotic deformity and potential spinal cord compression.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Incubation: Several months to years post-primary TB infection before spinal symptoms appear.
  • Early Phase: Vague back pain and general malaise lasting weeks to months.
  • Progressive Phase: Pain intensifies, neurological symptoms appear as spinal cord compression progresses.
  • Chronic Phase: Advanced deformities, chronic pain, and significant functional impairment if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Back pain - Most common symptom, often persistent and worsening. It results from inflammation and vertebral destruction.
  • Fever, night sweats - Systemic TB symptoms due to cytokine release from infection.
  • Weight loss - A chronic systemic effect of tuberculosis.
  • Lower limb weakness or numbness - Indicates neurological involvement from spinal cord compression.
  • Reduced mobility - Pain and spinal deformity limit movement, particularly in advanced stages.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Localized tenderness - Tenderness over affected vertebrae due to bone inflammation.
  • Spinal deformity (kyphosis) - Kyphotic deformity from vertebral collapse and disc space narrowing.
  • Neurological deficits - Weakness, numbness, or paralysis in lower limbs, indicating spinal cord compression.
  • Cold abscess - A fluctuant mass near the spine, often in the psoas region, due to the spread of infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Progressive lower limb weakness or numbness - Indicates potential spinal cord compression.
  • Incontinence - Loss of bladder or bowel control is a late sign of spinal cord involvement.
  • Severe, unremitting back pain - May indicate extensive spinal destruction and potential instability.
  • Spinal deformity (kyphosis) - Can lead to respiratory complications in severe cases.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Isolated neurological symptoms - Without significant back pain, especially in older adults.
  • Low-grade fever without other systemic symptoms - Especially in immunocompromised patients.
  • Spinal deformity without acute symptoms - In cases with gradual vertebral collapse without acute inflammation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Spine MRI - Shows vertebral destruction, soft tissue abscesses, and spinal cord compression; essential for detailed spinal imaging.
  • Tuberculin skin test or IGRA - Identifies TB exposure, though not specific to spinal TB.
  • ESR/CRP - Raised inflammatory markers, typically elevated due to chronic infection.
  • Tissue biopsy with culture - Confirms *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* infection, definitive diagnostic test.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old male with a 6-month history of progressive back pain, night sweats, weight loss, and recent lower limb weakness. Examination reveals kyphotic spinal deformity and tenderness over the thoracic spine.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Pyogenic vertebral osteomyelitis - Typically acute onset with fever; MRI shows abscesses but blood cultures may identify bacterial cause.
  • Spinal metastases - More common in older adults with known malignancy; MRI reveals tumour without typical TB vertebral collapse.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Chronic inflammatory back pain in younger patients, but MRI reveals characteristic sacroiliitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Antitubercular therapy (ATT) - Standard 6-12 months with rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol for 2 months, then continuation phase with rifampicin and isoniazid for 4-10 months.
  • Surgical decompression and stabilisation - For patients with severe spinal cord compression, deformity, or failure of medical therapy alone.
  • Pain management and physiotherapy - Adjuncts to improve pain and mobility.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Paralysis - From untreated or progressive spinal cord compression.
  • Kyphotic deformity - Leads to chronic pain, respiratory compromise, and reduced mobility.
  • Chronic pain and disability - Due to vertebral damage and deformity, impacting quality of life.
