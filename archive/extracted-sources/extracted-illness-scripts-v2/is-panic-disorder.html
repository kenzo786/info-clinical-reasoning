Panic Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Panic disorder is a mental health condition characterised by recurring, unexpected panic attacks. These are intense episodes of sudden fear, discomfort, or sense of impending doom, which peak within minutes and are often accompanied by physical symptoms such as palpitations, sweating, and shortness of breath. Panic disorder involves persistent concern about additional attacks, worry about the implications of attacks, or significant behavioural changes to avoid future attacks.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • In the UK, panic disorder has a prevalence of approximately 1.7% in the GP population.
  • Hospital prevalence for panic disorder is lower as many cases are managed in primary care, but individuals may present in emergency settings during acute panic attacks.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence of panic disorder varies but is generally estimated to be around 1-2% of the population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Panic disorder is thought to arise from a combination of genetic, neurobiological, and environmental factors.
  • Dysregulation in the brain’s limbic system, particularly the amygdala, leads to exaggerated fear responses.
  • Alterations in neurotransmitter systems (e.g., GABA, serotonin, norepinephrine) affect the brain’s ability to regulate anxiety and stress.
  • Heightened sensitivity to physiological changes and a tendency to misinterpret benign bodily sensations as dangerous trigger the ‘fight or flight’ response, resulting in panic attacks.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Onset: Panic disorder often begins in late adolescence to early adulthood, with a sudden and intense onset of panic attacks.
  • Course: Without treatment, the disorder can be chronic, with panic attacks occurring frequently or sporadically.
  • Duration of Attacks: Panic attacks typically peak within 10 minutes and last about 20-30 minutes, though the aftereffects may persist longer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sudden intense fear - Represents the ‘fight or flight’ response due to overactivity of the limbic system and amygdala.
  • Palpitations - Caused by sympathetic nervous system activation, increasing heart rate in response to perceived danger.
  • Sweating - Part of the autonomic response to stress, aiding in temperature regulation during perceived threats.
  • Shortness of breath - Hyperventilation due to anxiety can cause dyspnoea and a sensation of breathlessness.
  • Dizziness - Due to hyperventilation-induced changes in blood gas levels, reducing cerebral blood flow.
  • Nausea - The body diverts resources from the digestive system as part of the ‘fight or flight’ response.
  • Fear of losing control or dying - Stemming from the misinterpretation of physical symptoms as catastrophic.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Increased heart rate due to sympathetic nervous system activation.
  • Tachypnoea - Rapid breathing as part of the hyperventilation that often occurs during panic attacks.
  • Diaphoresis - Excessive sweating due to the autonomic stress response.
  • Muscle tension - Due to increased adrenaline levels, preparing muscles for action.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Chest pain - Could indicate myocardial infarction; important to distinguish from panic-related symptoms.
  • Syncope - Could signify arrhythmias or other serious cardiovascular issues.
  • Severe dyspnoea - May suggest respiratory or cardiac causes rather than panic.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset of fear - Some cases may lack the typical ‘out of the blue’ panic attack onset.
  • Absence of obvious physical symptoms - In some individuals, panic is more cognitive (e.g., fear of going mad) than physical.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • ECG - To rule out cardiac causes; normal in panic disorder but essential for excluding other diagnoses.
  • Blood tests (e.g., thyroid function) - Thyroid dysfunction can mimic or exacerbate anxiety symptoms.
  • Oxygen saturation and blood gases - Helpful in cases of hyperventilation to assess respiratory alkalosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman with recurring episodes of intense fear, palpitations, sweating, and dyspnoea, occurring unpredictably over the past six months, with avoidance of social settings to prevent further attacks.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD) - More constant worry rather than episodic panic.
  • Myocardial infarction - Presents with chest pain and may show abnormal ECG findings.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Anxiety and palpitations are present; thyroid function tests are abnormal.
  • Asthma - Shortness of breath with wheezing, often triggered by environmental factors.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) - First-line therapy, focusing on challenging and changing thoughts and behaviours associated with panic attacks.
  • SSRIs (e.g., sertraline) - Pharmacotherapy option, especially if CBT is not fully effective alone.
  • Short-term benzodiazepines - For acute management; used with caution due to dependency risks.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Panic Disorder"][/sc]
  • Agoraphobia - Fear of leaving safe places to avoid panic attacks, potentially leading to severe functional limitations.
  • Social isolation and depression - From avoidance behaviours and impact on daily life; higher prevalence in untreated individuals.
