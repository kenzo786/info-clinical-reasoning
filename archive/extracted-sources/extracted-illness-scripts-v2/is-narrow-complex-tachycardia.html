Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT) refers to a group of arrhythmias characterised by a rapid heart rate (>100 bpm) with a QRS complex duration of less than 120 ms on an electrocardiogram (ECG). This indicates that the electrical impulse is travelling through the normal His-Purkinje conduction system. Common subtypes include sinus tachycardia, atrioventricular nodal re-entrant tachycardia (AVNRT), atrioventricular re-entrant tachycardia (AVRT), and atrial flutter with rapid ventricular response.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of NCT varies with the specific subtype. Paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT) is seen in approximately 2 per 1,000 people in the general population. Hospital admissions for NCT are more common in adults and increase with age. Globally, NCT prevalence is higher in females and often occurs in structurally normal hearts.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • NCT results from abnormal electrical conduction or re-entry circuits within the heart, often involving the atria and AV node. In AVNRT, a dual pathway re-entry circuit occurs within the AV node itself. AVRT is characterised by the presence of an accessory pathway (e.g., Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome), leading to re-entrant tachycardia. Atrial flutter results from a macro-reentrant circuit typically in the right atrium. These circuits cause rapid ventricular rates, though conduction through the His-Purkinje system remains intact, resulting in a narrow QRS complex.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • NCT can present acutely, often with sudden onset and termination (paroxysmal), lasting minutes to hours. Chronic or recurrent episodes may occur, particularly in conditions like AVNRT or AVRT. Symptom duration and frequency vary by underlying cause.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - The sensation of a rapid or irregular heartbeat due to the rapid rate of atrial and ventricular contractions.
  • Dizziness or light-headedness - Due to reduced cardiac output from the decreased diastolic filling time, causing transient cerebral hypoperfusion.
  • Shortness of breath - A result of poor cardiac output and increased work of breathing during periods of tachycardia.
  • Chest discomfort - Can occur due to myocardial ischaemia, particularly in those with coronary artery disease, as the rapid heart rate increases oxygen demand.
  • Fatigue - Caused by reduced perfusion to tissues during prolonged episodes of tachycardia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Rapid heart rate - The most prominent sign, typically >100 bpm, as a direct result of the tachycardia.
  • Hypotension - In severe cases due to poor cardiac output, particularly if the tachycardia is sustained.
  • Jugular venous distension - May be present if there is right heart strain, often seen in atrial flutter or AVNRT with high rates.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Suggests significant haemodynamic compromise, potentially leading to sudden cardiac death if untreated.
  • Severe chest pain - Can indicate myocardial ischaemia or impending infarction, especially in patients with underlying coronary artery disease.
  • Marked hypotension - Reflects poor perfusion, signalling possible cardiogenic shock.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic episodes - Some patients, particularly younger or otherwise healthy individuals, may have brief or intermittent episodes that go unnoticed, despite the presence of tachycardia on an ECG.
  • Non-specific symptoms - Older adults may present with generalised fatigue, weakness, or confusion, especially if comorbidities mask the typical cardiovascular symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) - NCT is diagnosed by the presence of a QRS complex duration of less than 120 ms with a rapid ventricular rate. P waves may be absent or abnormal, depending on the subtype (e.g., retrograde P waves in AVNRT).
  • Holter monitoring - For patients with intermittent symptoms, a 24-hour Holter monitor can capture transient episodes of NCT.
  • Electrophysiological studies - Used in cases of recurrent or refractory NCT to determine the specific arrhythmia mechanism and guide management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with intermittent episodes of rapid heart rate, dizziness, and palpitations lasting 10–15 minutes. Symptoms began suddenly three months ago and occur weekly. ECG reveals a narrow complex tachycardia with a rate of 160 bpm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation - Differentiated by the presence of irregularly irregular rhythm and absence of P waves on ECG.
  • Ventricular tachycardia - Identified by a wide QRS complex (>120 ms), often associated with structural heart disease.
  • Sinus tachycardia - Usually caused by physiological stress (e.g., fever, dehydration) with a gradual onset and offset, and visible normal P waves on ECG.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Vagal manoeuvres - First-line treatment for acute episodes, such as carotid sinus massage or Valsalva manoeuvre, to slow conduction through the AV node.
  • Adenosine - Administered intravenously in acute settings to terminate AVNRT or AVRT by blocking AV node conduction.
  • Beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers - Used to prevent recurrent episodes by reducing AV node conduction.
  • Catheter ablation - Considered for recurrent or refractory NCT, particularly in cases of AVNRT or AVRT, to destroy the re-entry circuit or accessory pathway.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Narrow Complex Tachycardia (NCT)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Prolonged or recurrent episodes of NCT can lead to tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy.
  • Stroke - Atrial flutter or atrial fibrillation associated with NCT increases the risk of thromboembolism.
  • Sudden cardiac death - Rare but possible in cases of poorly controlled or severe tachycardia, especially in patients with underlying heart disease.
