Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Temporal arteritis, also known as Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA), is a systemic vasculitis primarily affecting medium and large arteries, particularly the temporal arteries. It is characterised by granulomatous inflammation, leading to vessel wall thickening, narrowing, or occlusion. The condition is a medical emergency due to its potential to cause irreversible vision loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 20 per 100,000 people over the age of 50.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common in rheumatology and ophthalmology departments, with higher incidence in individuals of northern European descent.
  • Worldwide prevalence: More common in populations of European ancestry, with rare incidence in non-Caucasian populations. The overall worldwide prevalence is around 10–30 per 100,000 people aged over 50.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • GCA involves chronic granulomatous inflammation in the walls of medium and large arteries, particularly the extracranial branches of the carotid artery. T-cell and macrophage activation leads to the formation of multinucleated giant cells and inflammatory cytokine release. This results in intimal hyperplasia and luminal narrowing, reducing blood flow and increasing the risk of ischaemia in affected areas, particularly the eyes, resulting in visual loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Onset: The onset is often subacute, with symptoms evolving over weeks to months, although it can occasionally present acutely.
  • Phases: Initial symptoms may be non-specific (e.g., fatigue, fever), followed by more specific signs like headache, jaw claudication, and vision changes. If untreated, it can lead to serious complications such as permanent vision loss, typically within days to weeks of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Headache - Localised to the temporal region, often severe and throbbing. This occurs due to inflammation of the temporal arteries.
  • Jaw claudication - Pain or fatigue in the jaw muscles during chewing, resulting from ischaemia of the maxillary artery branches.
  • Vision loss - Partial or complete vision loss can occur due to ischaemia of the optic nerve (anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy) resulting from artery occlusion.
  • Scalp tenderness - Sensitivity when combing hair or touching the scalp, caused by inflamed temporal arteries.
  • Constitutional symptoms - Fatigue, low-grade fever, weight loss, and night sweats are common, reflecting systemic inflammation.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) - Associated muscle pain and stiffness, particularly in the shoulders and hips, occur in up to 50% of patients with GCA.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Temporal artery abnormalities - The temporal arteries may be tender, thickened, or have reduced pulsation due to inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Visual field defects - Partial or complete loss of vision is a result of optic nerve ischaemia.
  • Fever - Low-grade fever is common and reflects the systemic inflammatory response.
  • Scalp tenderness - Visible scalp tenderness on palpation, especially in the temporal region, indicates localised artery inflammation.
  • Reduced shoulder/hip movement - If associated with PMR, restricted mobility due to pain and stiffness is common.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - May indicate impending permanent blindness due to occlusion of the central retinal artery or optic nerve ischaemia.
  • Diplopia - This may signify cranial nerve involvement and could precede vision loss.
  • Stroke symptoms - GCA can involve the vertebrobasilar system or carotid arteries, leading to transient ischaemic attacks or strokes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Isolated PMR - Some patients present with only PMR symptoms (proximal muscle pain and stiffness) without overt GCA signs, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • Fever of unknown origin - Occurs without headache or jaw claudication and can delay diagnosis if the vasculitic origin is not suspected.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) - Typically elevated (>50 mm/hr), reflecting systemic inflammation.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Markedly raised, another marker of acute inflammation.
  • Temporal artery biopsy - Gold standard diagnostic test showing giant cells and granulomatous inflammation. A negative biopsy does not exclude GCA due to skip lesions.
  • Ultrasound of temporal arteries - Shows a "halo sign," indicating oedema in the vessel wall due to inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old woman presents with a 4-week history of worsening headache localised to the right temporal region, associated with jaw claudication and scalp tenderness. She reports fatigue and a 3-day history of partial vision loss in the right eye.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Tension headache - Lacks the systemic features (e.g., fatigue, jaw claudication) and visual symptoms of GCA.
  • Primary open-angle glaucoma - Gradual vision loss with optic nerve damage, but no inflammatory markers or systemic symptoms.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) - Can overlap with GCA but without headache or vision loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • High-dose corticosteroids - Initiate immediately (e.g., prednisolone 40–60 mg daily) to prevent vision loss. Taper based on clinical response and inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP).
  • Aspirin - Low-dose aspirin (75 mg daily) is often recommended to reduce thrombotic risk.
  • Tocilizumab - IL-6 receptor antagonist used in refractory cases or as a steroid-sparing agent.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss - Occurs in up to 20% of untreated patients due to optic nerve ischaemia or central retinal artery occlusion.
  • Aortic aneurysm - GCA can involve the aorta, leading to aneurysms that may rupture if untreated.
  • Stroke - Cerebral ischaemia due to vertebrobasilar or carotid artery involvement.
