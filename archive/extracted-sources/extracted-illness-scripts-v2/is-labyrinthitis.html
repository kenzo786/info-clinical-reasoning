Labyrinthitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Labyrinthitis is an inflammatory condition affecting the labyrinth of the inner ear, typically involving both the cochlea and the vestibular system. It is characterised by the sudden onset of vertigo, hearing loss, and balance disturbance, usually following a viral or bacterial infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approx. 3.5 cases per 1,000 person-years.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less common, hospitalised cases often reflect more severe forms or complications.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar rates globally, more common following upper respiratory tract infections.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Labyrinthitis results from inflammation of the labyrinth, which contains both the cochlea (responsible for hearing) and the vestibular apparatus (responsible for balance). The inflammation disrupts signal transmission, leading to vertigo and hearing loss. Infections, typically viral (e.g., herpes simplex or respiratory viruses), are the most common cause. Bacterial labyrinthitis is rarer but can occur following otitis media or meningitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe vertigo, nausea, and vomiting, lasting several days.
  • Subacute phase: Gradual improvement in vertigo over weeks, but balance and hearing disturbances may persist.
  • Chronic phase: Residual balance dysfunction or hearing loss may continue for months or, in some cases, become permanent.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Vertigo - Sudden onset of spinning sensation, resulting from disruption in the vestibular system.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Occurs due to severe vertigo and the brain’s difficulty in interpreting disordered vestibular signals.
  • Hearing loss - Partial or total, typically in the affected ear, due to inflammation of the cochlea.
  • Tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing sound, explained by damage to the inner ear’s hair cells.
  • Imbalance - Persistent difficulty in maintaining balance, resulting from disrupted vestibular input.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Positive head impulse test - Inability to maintain visual fixation during rapid head movements, indicating vestibular dysfunction.
  • Spontaneous nystagmus - Rapid, involuntary eye movements observed during physical examination, reflecting imbalance in the vestibular input to the brain.
  • Hearing impairment - Detected through audiometry or whisper tests, indicating cochlear involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., facial weakness, dysarthria) - May indicate a central cause, such as stroke, rather than labyrinthitis.
  • Severe headache, neck stiffness - Suggestive of meningitis or a CNS cause.
  • Persistent hearing loss - May indicate permanent damage or a more severe infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Subtle balance issues without vertigo - In some cases, particularly in older adults, the primary complaint may be unsteadiness rather than overt vertigo.
  • Absence of hearing loss - Viral labyrinthitis can sometimes present without noticeable hearing impairment, making diagnosis more challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Audiometry - Shows sensorineural hearing loss, indicating cochlear involvement.
  • Head impulse test - Positive test indicates vestibular hypofunction.
  • Electronystagmography (ENG) - Can confirm vestibular dysfunction by measuring eye movements.
  • MRI scan - Performed if central causes (e.g., stroke or tumour) are suspected. In labyrinthitis, MRI may show enhancement of the labyrinth.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with the sudden onset of severe vertigo, nausea, and hearing loss in the right ear, following a recent upper respiratory infection. Physical examination reveals spontaneous nystagmus and a positive head impulse test.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) - Presents with brief episodes of vertigo triggered by head movements, unlike the continuous vertigo in labyrinthitis. No hearing loss.
  • Vestibular neuritis - Similar vestibular symptoms but without hearing loss, as it only affects the vestibular nerve, not the cochlea.
  • Meniere's disease - Recurrent episodes of vertigo, hearing loss, and tinnitus, typically with fluctuating hearing, unlike the single acute episode of labyrinthitis.
  • Stroke - Central vertigo often presents with other neurological deficits such as weakness or sensory loss, not seen in labyrinthitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Symptomatic relief - Vestibular suppressants (e.g., prochlorperazine, betahistine) to reduce vertigo.
  • Corticosteroids - May be used to reduce inflammation, particularly if hearing loss is present.
  • Antibiotics - Indicated in cases of bacterial labyrinthitis, especially following otitis media.
  • Rehabilitation - Vestibular rehabilitation exercises may help in long-term balance recovery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss - Occurs in some cases, especially with bacterial labyrinthitis.
  • Chronic imbalance - Some patients may continue to experience balance issues due to persistent vestibular dysfunction.
  • Meningitis - A rare but serious complication of bacterial labyrinthitis if infection spreads to the meninges.
