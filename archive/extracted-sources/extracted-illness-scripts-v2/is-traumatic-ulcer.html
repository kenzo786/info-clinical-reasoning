Traumatic Ulcer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • A traumatic ulcer is a localised injury to the oral mucosa or skin caused by mechanical, thermal, chemical, or physical trauma. It presents as a painful lesion with a yellow-grey base and erythematous borders, often resulting from biting, irritation from dental appliances, or accidental trauma. Typically, these ulcers heal within 1-2 weeks, though persistent ulcers require further investigation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Oral traumatic ulcers are common, affecting approximately 1-2% of patients in general practice.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Incidence rises in hospital settings, especially in patients with trauma, oral surgery, or dental procedures.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Estimated 5-10% of people experience traumatic ulcers at some point, with varying prevalence depending on oral hygiene practices and access to dental care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Traumatic ulcers result from direct damage to the epithelial lining of the mucosa or skin, typically due to mechanical trauma like biting or irritation from rough dental surfaces. The epithelial disruption leads to inflammation and exposure of the underlying connective tissue. A fibrinous exudate covers the ulcer, forming a yellow-grey base, while surrounding tissues become erythematous due to vascular dilation and immune response. Healing usually occurs within 1-2 weeks as epithelial regeneration closes the lesion, provided the traumatic source is removed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Initial Injury: Trauma to the mucosa or skin triggers the formation of an ulcer. Pain and inflammation appear within 24 hours.
  • Acute Phase (Days 1-7): The ulcer is most painful during this time, with visible erythema and a yellow-grey base.
  • Healing Phase (Days 7-14): Inflammation subsides, and epithelialisation begins. The ulcer heals unless recurrent trauma occurs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Pain - The most common symptom, due to inflammation and exposure of underlying nerve endings. Pain is usually exacerbated by eating or speaking.
  • Swelling - Localised swelling due to the inflammatory response surrounding the ulcer site.
  • Burning sensation - Some patients report a burning or stinging feeling due to the irritation of exposed mucosa.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing may occur if the ulcer is located in areas like the oropharynx or tongue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Ulceration with yellow-grey base - The hallmark sign, caused by a fibrinous covering over exposed connective tissue.
  • Erythematous margins - The redness around the ulcer is due to vasodilation and inflammation.
  • Localised oedema - Swelling in the tissues surrounding the ulcer due to the inflammatory process.
  • Palpable tenderness - Tenderness upon palpation, reflecting underlying inflammation and tissue damage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Non-healing ulcer >3 weeks - May indicate malignancy such as squamous cell carcinoma. Persistent ulcers require biopsy for diagnosis.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Can suggest underlying malignancy or chronic systemic disease.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, night sweats) - Suggest a possible infectious or malignant aetiology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Multiple ulcers - Trauma usually causes a single ulcer, so multiple lesions should prompt investigation for systemic conditions like Crohn's disease or Behçet's disease.
  • Painless ulcers - While pain is a typical feature, some patients, especially immunocompromised individuals, may present with painless ulcers.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Clinical Examination - Diagnosis is often clinical, based on the characteristic appearance of the ulcer and history of trauma.
  • Biopsy - Indicated if the ulcer persists for >3 weeks, to rule out malignancy. Histology would show non-specific inflammation or neoplastic changes if cancerous.
  • Blood tests - FBC and inflammatory markers may be performed to rule out systemic causes if multiple or unusual ulcers are present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a painful ulcer on the inside of his cheek, lasting for 5 days following accidental biting. He reports significant discomfort when eating, with no systemic symptoms or weight loss. Examination reveals a 5mm ulcer with erythematous margins and a yellow-grey base.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Aphthous ulcer - Recurrent and unrelated to trauma, often smaller and multiple.
  • Herpetic ulcer - Often multiple, associated with systemic symptoms (fever, malaise), and caused by HSV.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma - Non-healing, painless, with indurated margins, more common in older patients with risk factors (e.g., smoking).
  • Syphilitic ulcer (chancre) - Typically painless and indurated, with associated systemic signs of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Removal of the traumatic source (e.g., sharp dental appliance), good oral hygiene, and avoidance of irritants.
  • Topical anaesthetics - Lidocaine gel to alleviate pain during eating.
  • Antiseptic mouthwash - Chlorhexidine mouthwash to prevent secondary infection.
  • Referral - If no healing within 3 weeks, referral to oral medicine or ENT for biopsy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Traumatic Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Secondary infection - Bacterial colonisation of the ulcer can lead to delayed healing and increased pain.
  • Malignant transformation - Though rare, chronic non-healing ulcers should be biopsied to rule out squamous cell carcinoma.
