Stomatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Stomatitis is a general term for inflammation of the oral mucosa, which can involve the lips, tongue, cheeks, palate, and floor of the mouth. It may present with pain, erythema, ulcers, and difficulty in eating or speaking, and can result from infections, irritants, systemic diseases, or idiopathic causes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, stomatitis is a common presentation in primary care, affecting approximately 2-3% of patients annually.
  • It is more prevalent in hospitalised or immunocompromised patients, with higher rates in oncology wards and intensive care units.
  • Globally, the prevalence of specific forms of stomatitis, such as recurrent aphthous stomatitis (RAS), ranges from 5-25% depending on population and risk factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Stomatitis results from disruption of the mucosal barrier due to various triggers:
    • Infectious agents: Viral (e.g., HSV-1, Coxsackievirus), bacterial, or fungal infections directly damage the epithelium.
    • Immune-mediated mechanisms: Autoimmune or hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., RAS or Behçet’s disease) lead to localised inflammation and ulceration.
    • Chemical or physical irritants: Tobacco, alcohol, or chemotherapy agents can disrupt cellular repair mechanisms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute stomatitis: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over hours to days, as seen in herpetic stomatitis or after exposure to irritants.
  • Chronic stomatitis: Persistent or recurrent symptoms over weeks to months, as seen in autoimmune or systemic diseases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Painful oral ulcers: Common in RAS and herpetic stomatitis; results from localised tissue necrosis and inflammation.
  • Erythema: Diffuse redness due to increased blood flow and inflammatory mediators.
  • Burning sensation: Often precedes ulcer formation, particularly in chemical-induced stomatitis.
  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing due to pain and swelling.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Ulcers with erythematous halos: Characteristic of RAS; result from immune-mediated epithelial disruption.
  • White plaques: Indicative of oral candidiasis; caused by fungal overgrowth.
  • Blisters or vesicles: Seen in herpetic stomatitis; result from viral replication and keratinocyte lysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Non-healing ulcers: May indicate malignancy, particularly oral squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Severe pain and fever: Suggests systemic infection or herpetic stomatitis.
  • Dysphagia with weight loss: Could indicate oesophageal involvement or systemic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Painless oral lesions: May occur in immunocompromised patients (e.g., HIV-associated stomatitis).
  • Isolated erythema: Seen in early stages of stomatitis without ulceration.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Swabs for microscopy and culture: Identifies bacterial, fungal, or viral pathogens.
  • Serology for HSV or Coxsackievirus: Confirms viral aetiology.
  • Full blood count: May reveal neutropenia or anaemia as contributory factors.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 1-week history of painful oral ulcers, difficulty eating, and low-grade fever. She has a history of recurrent aphthous stomatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Herpes simplex virus infection: Differentiated by grouped vesicles and systemic symptoms.
  • Oral candidiasis: White plaques that can be scraped off.
  • Behçet’s disease: Includes genital ulcers and systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Topical corticosteroids: For inflammatory stomatitis (e.g., RAS).
  • Antiviral therapy: Acyclovir for herpetic stomatitis.
  • Antifungal agents: Nystatin for oral candidiasis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Secondary infection: Bacterial superinfection of ulcers can prolong healing.
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Difficulty eating may lead to weight loss.
  • Chronic pain: Can impair quality of life.
