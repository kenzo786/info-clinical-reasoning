Pelvic Mass
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • A pelvic mass refers to an abnormal growth or enlargement of tissue or organs in the pelvic region. It may arise from reproductive organs (ovaries, uterus), urinary system (bladder), or gastrointestinal system (colon). Pelvic masses can be benign (e.g., ovarian cysts, fibroids) or malignant (e.g., ovarian cancer) and often present with varied symptoms depending on the cause and size of the mass.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK General Practice: Pelvic masses are relatively common presentations in women of reproductive age, with benign conditions like ovarian cysts being frequent.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Up to 10% of women may undergo surgery for a pelvic mass during their lifetime, with a substantial portion being for benign causes such as fibroids.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Similar to the UK, benign pelvic masses are common globally, while ovarian cancer, a malignant cause, has a worldwide incidence of 6.6 per 100,000 women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Pelvic masses develop due to abnormal tissue growth or organ enlargement. In benign cases, this may involve hormonal stimulation (e.g., functional ovarian cysts), smooth muscle hyperplasia (e.g., fibroids), or fluid accumulation. Malignant masses, such as ovarian or endometrial cancer, occur from dysregulated cell proliferation, invasion of surrounding tissues, and potential metastasis. Other causes include infectious or inflammatory processes (e.g., tubo-ovarian abscesses).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset of symptoms such as pain or bloating may indicate ovarian torsion, rupture of a cyst, or an infectious process like a tubo-ovarian abscess.
  • Subacute to Chronic: Slowly growing masses, such as fibroids or benign ovarian cysts, may be asymptomatic for months or years before causing noticeable symptoms.
  • Progressive: Malignant pelvic masses tend to grow progressively, with worsening symptoms over weeks to months, often without a clear acute onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Pelvic pain - Common, caused by stretching of the pelvic organs or irritation of nearby tissues. In acute cases, may indicate ovarian torsion or rupture.
  • Abdominal bloating - Due to mass effect or accumulation of fluid (ascites) in malignancies like ovarian cancer.
  • Menstrual irregularities - Associated with masses of the reproductive system, like fibroids or ovarian tumours, that disrupt normal hormonal signalling.
  • Urinary frequency/urgency - Caused by compression of the bladder from an enlarging mass.
  • Constipation - Resulting from compression of the bowel by larger pelvic masses.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Abdominal or pelvic mass on palpation - A palpable mass may indicate a large growth, often found during bimanual examination or abdominal palpation.
  • Abdominal distension - Visible swelling due to ascites or large masses, often seen in ovarian malignancy.
  • Tenderness - Localised or diffuse tenderness on examination may suggest an inflammatory or infectious cause like a tubo-ovarian abscess.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May suggest malignancy, particularly ovarian cancer, due to systemic metabolic effects.
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - A concerning sign for endometrial cancer or other malignant pelvic masses.
  • Severe acute pain with nausea/vomiting - Could indicate ovarian torsion, a surgical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Many benign pelvic masses, especially smaller fibroids or ovarian cysts, may present without symptoms and are often found incidentally.
  • Mimicking urinary or gastrointestinal conditions - Large masses may cause symptoms related to bladder or bowel compression, leading to misdiagnosis as urinary tract infections or irritable bowel syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Transvaginal ultrasound - The first-line imaging modality to visualise ovarian cysts, fibroids, and other pelvic masses. It helps in assessing size, structure (solid or cystic), and vascularity.
  • CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis - Useful for staging in suspected malignancies and for visualising masses that extend beyond the pelvis.
  • Serum CA-125 - A biomarker elevated in ovarian cancer, though not specific and can be raised in benign conditions like endometriosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old premenopausal woman presents with 3 months of progressive lower abdominal bloating, pelvic discomfort, and urinary frequency. Ultrasound reveals a 10 cm left ovarian mass, and serum CA-125 is mildly elevated.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cyst - Benign cysts are often asymptomatic or cause mild pain, and are usually smaller on imaging compared to malignancies.
  • Uterine fibroids - Typically associated with menstrual irregularities, they are solid masses found on ultrasound with a characteristic "whorled" appearance.
  • Endometriosis - Can present with chronic pelvic pain and dysmenorrhoea, with masses being endometriomas seen on ultrasound.
  • Ovarian cancer - May present with a combination of bloating, weight loss, and elevated CA-125 levels, often seen in postmenopausal women.
  • Tubo-ovarian abscess - Presents with fever, acute pain, and a complex mass on imaging, often in association with a history of pelvic inflammatory disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Benign masses: For asymptomatic cysts, expectant management or hormonal treatment may be advised. Large or symptomatic fibroids may require myomectomy or hysterectomy.
  • Malignant masses: Ovarian cancer is treated with surgery (debulking) and chemotherapy, following UK guidelines. Referral to a gynaecological oncologist is crucial for staging and management.
  • Infectious causes: Tubo-ovarian abscesses require antibiotics, with drainage or surgery if unresponsive.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pelvic Mass"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion - A surgical emergency, where a twisted ovary leads to ischaemia. It causes acute severe pain and vomiting.
  • Malignancy progression - Ovarian and endometrial cancers may metastasise to other organs, worsening the prognosis.
  • Rupture of a mass - Can lead to internal bleeding and peritonitis, requiring urgent intervention.
