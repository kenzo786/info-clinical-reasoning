Perthes' Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Perthes’ disease, also known as Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease, is an idiopathic condition characterised by avascular necrosis of the femoral head in children. It primarily affects children aged 4–10 years and results in the disruption of blood supply to the femoral head, leading to bone necrosis and eventual revascularisation and remodelling over time. If left untreated, it can lead to hip deformity and early-onset osteoarthritis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, Perthes’ disease occurs in approximately 5–15 per 100,000 children annually, with a male-to-female ratio of around 4:1.
  • It is more common among children of northern European descent and is rare in African, Asian, and Native American populations.
  • Perthes’ disease constitutes a small percentage of paediatric hip pathology cases seen in GP practices and hospitals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • The disease begins with compromised blood supply to the femoral head due to unclear mechanisms, leading to bone ischaemia.
  • Bone necrosis (avascular necrosis) of the femoral head occurs as a result, causing bone weakening.
  • Subsequently, revascularisation occurs, stimulating new bone growth and remodelling. This phase can last for several years and varies in success, affecting the femoral head's shape.
  • If the femoral head becomes deformed due to improper remodelling, it may lead to altered biomechanics, hip instability, and increased risk of osteoarthritis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Blood supply disruption and early bone necrosis; usually asymptomatic.
  • Fragmentation Phase: Necrotic bone begins to break down, with increased pain and limited hip mobility.
  • Reossification Phase: New bone formation occurs, often accompanied by improved symptoms.
  • Remodelling Phase: Bone shape solidifies; residual deformity may lead to functional limitations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Hip pain: The primary symptom due to necrosis and fragmentation, which causes inflammatory pain in the hip joint.
  • Knee pain: Referred pain is common due to shared nerve supply from the femoral nerve.
  • Limping: Occurs as a result of pain and weakened hip muscles, often worsening with activity.
  • Stiffness and restricted movement: Due to joint inflammation and structural changes in the femoral head.
  • Asymptomatic: Early stages may lack symptoms, especially in younger children, until more significant changes develop.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Antalgic gait (limping): Limping gait due to pain and muscle weakness around the hip.
  • Decreased hip range of motion: Particularly in abduction and internal rotation, indicating hip joint involvement.
  • Thigh muscle atrophy: Disuse atrophy due to chronic pain and reduced activity, leading to muscle wasting.
  • Positive Trendelenburg sign: Weakness of hip abductors may cause hip drop when standing on the affected side.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden-onset pain: Suggests a pathological fracture or more aggressive pathology such as slipped capital femoral epiphysis.
  • Significant fever or systemic symptoms: These are not typical of Perthes’ disease and may suggest infection (e.g., septic arthritis).
  • Progressive worsening of symptoms: May indicate poor remodelling or a secondary osteochondritis dissecans.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Pain limited to the knee: Without hip symptoms, this may be misdiagnosed as a primary knee pathology.
  • Bilateral presentation: Though uncommon, around 10% of cases may present bilaterally, complicating diagnosis.
  • Asymptomatic: Some cases remain symptom-free in early stages, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Pelvic X-ray: Shows femoral head flattening, fragmentation, or sclerosis in affected phases. In early stages, signs may be minimal.
  • MRI of hip: Can reveal early necrosis and changes in bone marrow, even before X-ray changes, by showing decreased signal intensity in the femoral head.
  • Bone scan: Less commonly used but may show reduced uptake in the femoral head in avascular necrosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Example: A 6-year-old male presenting with a painless limp, intermittent knee pain, and restricted hip motion over the past month. No fever or trauma history. X-ray reveals femoral head fragmentation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE): Presents with hip or knee pain and restricted movement, usually in adolescents; X-ray shows femoral head displacement.
  • Transient Synovitis: Sudden onset hip pain and limp, often post-viral; resolves spontaneously, and X-rays are typically normal.
  • Septic Arthritis: Presents with acute hip pain, fever, and restricted movement; elevated inflammatory markers and fluid on joint aspiration.
  • Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH): Often seen in infants and young children, may present with limited abduction; diagnosed with ultrasound or X-ray.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Conservative management: Observation and physiotherapy in early stages or mild cases; aims to maintain hip function.
  • Non-weight-bearing protocols: Crutches or bracing to reduce stress on the femoral head during healing phases.
  • Surgical intervention: Procedures such as femoral or pelvic osteotomies may be indicated in severe cases to improve hip congruency and prevent deformity.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Perthes' Disease"][/sc]
  • Hip deformity: Permanent structural changes can lead to early-onset osteoarthritis due to joint incongruity.
  • Reduced hip mobility: Long-term limited range of motion due to improper femoral head remodelling.
  • Early osteoarthritis: Deformity and abnormal biomechanics increase the likelihood of early degenerative changes.
