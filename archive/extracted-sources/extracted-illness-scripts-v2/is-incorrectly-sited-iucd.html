Incorrectly Sited IUCD
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • An incorrectly sited intrauterine contraceptive device (IUCD) refers to an IUCD that has migrated or is positioned outside of its intended location within the uterine cavity, often due to expulsion, perforation, or displacement into adjacent structures like the cervix or abdominal cavity.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Approximately 1 in 1,000 IUCD insertions are complicated by uterine perforation.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Incorrectly sited IUCDs are a rare but notable presentation in emergency gynaecology departments, often requiring radiographic imaging for assessment.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Incidence varies widely due to IUCD type and insertion technique; global rates of IUCD perforation are around 1.6 per 1,000 insertions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Insertion Process: IUCD insertion involves placing the device in the uterus; if excessive force or incorrect angle is applied, uterine wall perforation can occur.
  • Migration: Over time, an IUCD may migrate due to uterine contractions, incorrect initial placement, or structural changes, leading it to move within or outside the uterine cavity.
  • Complications of Incorrect Placement: If the IUCD is incorrectly sited, it may be ineffective for contraception, cause local inflammation, and increase the risk of infection or injury to adjacent organs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Immediate Post-Insertion: Displacement may cause immediate symptoms or may be asymptomatic initially.
  • Early Weeks: Symptoms of migration or perforation may emerge within a few weeks post-insertion, particularly if the patient experiences pain or abnormal bleeding.
  • Long Term: Some displacements are detected incidentally months or years later, often during routine imaging or examination due to asymptomatic presentations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Pain: Commonly caused by local irritation or inflammation if the IUCD is improperly positioned.
  • Abnormal Vaginal Bleeding: May occur due to uterine irritation or partial expulsion of the IUCD.
  • Dyspareunia: Discomfort or pain during intercourse can occur if the device is pressing against sensitive structures.
  • Asymptomatic: Many patients remain symptom-free and are diagnosed incidentally.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • String Non-visibility on Cervical Examination: The most common finding, indicating potential displacement of the IUCD.
  • Palpable IUCD in Cervix: An expelled or low-lying IUCD can sometimes be felt in the cervical canal on bimanual examination.
  • Local Tenderness: Often present during pelvic examination if inflammation or irritation is present due to an incorrectly sited IUCD.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Severe Abdominal Pain: May indicate perforation or migration into the abdominal cavity, potentially requiring urgent intervention.
  • Fever and Purulent Discharge: Suggests possible pelvic infection, which may occur secondary to IUCD displacement and increase the risk of pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Haemodynamic Instability: Rare but may occur with significant haemorrhage due to uterine perforation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic with Absent Strings: Often detected during routine check-ups when IUCD strings are not visible.
  • Lower Back Pain: Rarely, displacement into the pelvis or abdomen can refer pain to the lower back.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Ultrasound: Preferred imaging modality to determine IUCD position; may show IUCD outside the uterus or embedded in the uterine wall.
  • X-ray of the Abdomen and Pelvis: Used when IUCD is not visualised on ultrasound, as IUCDs are typically radiopaque.
  • Speculum Examination: Allows direct visualisation of IUCD strings; absence may suggest displacement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with pelvic pain and abnormal bleeding three weeks after IUCD insertion. Examination reveals absent IUCD strings on cervical inspection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Ectopic Pregnancy: Presents with abdominal pain and bleeding but is often accompanied by a positive pregnancy test.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Can cause pain and discharge but is associated with fever and tenderness upon examination.
  • Uterine Fibroids: May cause bleeding and pelvic discomfort but is typically associated with an enlarged, irregular uterus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Removal of IUCD: Removal of a displaced IUCD is recommended, typically under ultrasound guidance if embedded in the myometrium.
  • Antibiotic Therapy: If infection is suspected, broad-spectrum antibiotics may be prescribed as per UK guidelines.
  • Reinsertion or Alternative Contraception: Following recovery, reinsertion may be considered or alternative contraception discussed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Incorrectly Sited IUCD"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Infection: Increases with IUCD displacement, leading to potential PID or abscess formation.
  • Perforation of Adjacent Organs: Migration can rarely cause damage to structures such as the bladder or bowel.
  • Infertility: Chronic pelvic infection can lead to tubal damage and potential infertility.
