Electrolyte Disturbance
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbance refers to an imbalance in the body's essential electrolytes, such as sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride, and magnesium. These ions are crucial for nerve function, muscle contraction, and fluid balance. Electrolyte disturbances can range from mild and asymptomatic to severe and life-threatening conditions, depending on the electrolyte involved and the degree of imbalance.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • In the UK, electrolyte disturbances are common in both primary and secondary care. For instance, hyponatraemia affects 15-30% of hospitalised patients. Worldwide, the incidence varies depending on the healthcare setting, with ICU patients having a higher risk due to the nature of their illness and treatment interventions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbances occur due to an imbalance in intake, absorption, excretion, or cellular distribution of electrolytes. For example, hypokalaemia may result from increased renal losses (e.g., diuretics), inadequate intake, or intracellular shifting (e.g., insulin). Each electrolyte has specific roles in maintaining cellular membrane potential, neuromuscular excitability, and acid-base balance, and disturbances can lead to systemic effects depending on the electrolyte involved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbances can develop acutely, as in the case of fluid shifts or drug effects, or chronically, often seen in long-term conditions like chronic kidney disease or heart failure. The time course can influence symptoms, with rapid changes often producing more severe clinical effects.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Muscle weakness - Seen in hypokalaemia or hyperkalaemia due to impaired neuromuscular transmission.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Common in severe hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia due to cerebral oedema or dehydration.
  • Fatigue - General symptom in most electrolyte disturbances, linked to disrupted cellular energy metabolism.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Common in disturbances like hypokalaemia and hyperkalaemia, where changes in membrane potential affect cardiac conduction.
  • Seizures - May occur in severe hyponatraemia or hypocalcaemia due to altered neuronal excitability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Muscle cramps or spasms - Indicative of hypocalcaemia or hypomagnesaemia due to increased neuromuscular excitability.
  • Postural hypotension - May be seen in hypovolaemic hyponatraemia, reflecting fluid and electrolyte losses.
  • Tachycardia - A compensatory response to hypotension or dehydration, often seen in hypernatraemia.
  • Chvostek’s and Trousseau’s signs - Signs of latent tetany in hypocalcaemia due to increased neuromuscular irritability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Sign of severe potassium or calcium imbalance, potentially leading to sudden cardiac death.
  • Seizures - Reflects critical hyponatraemia or hypocalcaemia and may signal impending brain herniation.
  • Severe muscle weakness - Suggests marked electrolyte imbalance, particularly in potassium disturbances, with the risk of respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Subtle cognitive changes - Particularly in elderly patients with chronic hyponatraemia, presenting as mild confusion or reduced concentration.
  • Nocturia or polydipsia - These may be the only presenting features in patients with early stages of sodium disturbances.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Serum electrolyte levels - Abnormal levels of sodium, potassium, calcium, and magnesium confirm the diagnosis. For example, potassium 145 mmol/L suggests hypernatraemia.
  • ECG - To assess for arrhythmias in cases of potassium or calcium imbalances. Hypokalaemia may show flattened T waves, while hyperkalaemia can cause peaked T waves.
  • Urine electrolyte analysis - Useful in differentiating renal from extrarenal causes of electrolyte loss, especially in hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with confusion and muscle weakness over the past 2 days. Blood tests reveal hyponatraemia (Na+ 120 mmol/L) and hypokalaemia (K+ 3.0 mmol/L). The patient is on long-term diuretics for heart failure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - While it can cause electrolyte abnormalities, dehydration typically presents with dry mucous membranes and hypotension, often with a concentrated urine.
  • Acid-base disorders - Metabolic acidosis or alkalosis can lead to shifts in electrolyte levels, but also features abnormal blood gas results and specific underlying causes like renal failure or respiratory disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Correction of underlying cause - For example, stopping diuretics or treating gastrointestinal losses.
  • Electrolyte replacement - Potassium and sodium repletion via oral or IV routes based on the severity of the disturbance.
  • Fluid management - Hypernatraemia is often treated with slow fluid replacement to avoid rapid osmotic shifts, following UK guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Especially with severe hypokalaemia or hyperkalaemia, due to disrupted cardiac conduction.
  • Seizures or coma - May result from severe electrolyte disturbances, such as hyponatraemia, leading to cerebral oedema or brain herniation.
  • Rhabdomyolysis - Seen in severe hypokalaemia or hypernatraemia due to muscle breakdown, leading to acute kidney injury.
