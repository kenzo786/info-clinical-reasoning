Lichen Simplex Chronicus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Lichen Simplex Chronicus (LSC) is a skin disorder characterised by thickened, lichenified plaques that result from chronic scratching or rubbing of the skin. It often arises in areas easily accessible to scratching, such as the nape of the neck, wrists, forearms, lower legs, and anogenital regions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated at approximately 2-5%, with a higher prevalence among patients with chronic skin conditions such as eczema.
  • Hospital-based prevalence data are limited, but presentations are common in dermatology settings due to the need for symptom relief and management.
  • Globally, the prevalence is uncertain but believed to be similar across various populations, affecting adults predominantly, with a higher incidence in women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • The primary pathophysiology of LSC involves the "itch-scratch cycle," where persistent scratching or rubbing leads to epidermal thickening and increased skin sensitivity, which then perpetuates the urge to scratch.
  • Initial itch may be due to underlying skin disorders, stress, or idiopathic factors. Scratching results in inflammation and hyperkeratosis, increasing skin thickness.
  • The skin becomes lichenified with visible accentuation of skin lines, resulting in pruritus and heightened irritation.
  • Chronic inflammation and local trauma lead to altered skin sensation and possibly neural sensitisation, which further drives the itch-scratch cycle.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Localised itching prompts scratching and initial skin irritation.
  • Intermediate phase: Continuous scratching causes epidermal thickening and lichenification, typically over weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: Long-standing lichenified plaques may persist for years without effective intervention, often leading to frequent recurrences.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Intense itching - This is usually the primary symptom and worsens in response to stress. It occurs due to neural sensitisation and repetitive scratching.
  • Localised discomfort or burning - Often reported due to continuous irritation and skin damage from scratching.
  • Disturbed sleep - Itching may intensify at night, disrupting sleep and leading to secondary effects like fatigue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Lichenified plaques - Thickened, roughened areas of skin with prominent skin lines, indicative of chronic scratching and hyperkeratosis.
  • Excoriations - Evidence of self-inflicted scratches, often seen in easily accessible areas, showing active scratching behaviour.
  • Hyperpigmentation or hypopigmentation - Due to chronic inflammation and healing, resulting in skin discolouration over affected areas.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Ulceration or infection - Deep scratching can cause skin breakdown, leading to bacterial infection, often marked by pain, erythema, and pus.
  • Progressive expansion of lesions - May suggest secondary factors or other dermatologic conditions complicating LSC.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with generalised itching without overt plaques, especially if lesions are hidden or recently scratched.
  • Rarely, patients may present with excoriations only, lacking classic lichenification, which may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Diagnosis is primarily clinical based on the appearance of thickened, lichenified plaques.
  • Skin biopsy - Rarely needed, but may confirm LSC by showing hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, and chronic inflammatory infiltrate if diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Dermatology referral - Recommended if lesions are atypical or unresponsive to treatment, to exclude other dermatoses or underlying skin disorders.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with a 6-month history of severe pruritus on the wrists, now with thickened, hyperpigmented plaques due to chronic scratching, affecting sleep and quality of life.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Atopic dermatitis - Diffuse, generalised eczema-like distribution with more variable pruritus and absence of thick lichenification in early stages.
  • Psoriasis - Sharply demarcated plaques with silvery scales, usually non-pruritic, and affecting different areas such as the elbows and scalp.
  • Prurigo nodularis - Raised nodules with intense pruritus and more localised nodular lesions rather than plaques.
  • Chronic contact dermatitis - Related to exposure to an irritant or allergen, with initial erythema and oedema rather than lichenification.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Topical corticosteroids - High-potency corticosteroids (e.g., clobetasol propionate) to reduce inflammation and pruritus.
  • Antihistamines - Sedating antihistamines like hydroxyzine for nocturnal itching and sleep improvement.
  • Behavioural therapy - Psychological support to break the itch-scratch cycle; cognitive behavioural therapy may be helpful for habit reversal.
  • Moisturisers and emollients - To soothe and repair skin barrier function, which may reduce itching and improve overall skin texture.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lichen Simplex Chronicus"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Increased susceptibility to infection due to skin breakdown; may require antibiotics.
  • Scarring and pigmentation changes - Permanent changes in skin pigmentation, especially if scratching is prolonged.
  • Chronic pain and neural sensitisation - Persistent itching may result in chronic discomfort and increased skin sensitivity.
