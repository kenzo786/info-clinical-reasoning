Haemorrhoids
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids, commonly known as piles, are vascular structures in the anal canal that act as cushions to aid stool control. When these vascular structures become swollen or inflamed, they are referred to as haemorrhoidal disease. Haemorrhoids can be internal (originating above the dentate line) or external (below the dentate line), and they are classified into different grades based on their degree of prolapse.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids are common in the general population, with a prevalence of around 4-5% in the UK GP population. Hospital prevalence may vary, but it is a common complaint among patients presenting with anal symptoms. Globally, the prevalence of haemorrhoids ranges between 2-20%, depending on the population studied and the diagnostic criteria used.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids result from increased pressure within the rectal venous plexus, leading to the dilation and engorgement of the vascular cushions in the anal canal. Contributing factors include chronic straining during defecation, constipation, prolonged sitting, pregnancy, and advancing age. These lead to the weakening of the supportive tissues, allowing the vascular cushions to prolapse. Internal haemorrhoids are located above the dentate line and are covered by mucosa, while external haemorrhoids are below the dentate line and are covered by skin.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids may develop gradually over time, especially with chronic factors such as constipation or pregnancy. They often present acutely when thrombosis or strangulation occurs. Internal haemorrhoids are graded from I to IV based on prolapse severity. Grade I haemorrhoids do not prolapse, while Grade IV haemorrhoids are irreducibly prolapsed. Symptoms may wax and wane depending on exacerbating factors such as bowel habits and diet.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Rectal bleeding – Commonly bright red and associated with defecation. It is due to the rupture of engorged vessels during bowel movements, particularly with internal haemorrhoids.
  • Anal itching – Caused by mucous discharge from prolapsed internal haemorrhoids, irritating the perianal skin.
  • Perianal discomfort or pain – External haemorrhoids can cause pain, especially when thrombosed. Internal haemorrhoids typically do not cause pain unless strangulated.
  • Prolapse – A sensation of tissue protruding from the anus is common in more advanced cases (Grades III and IV).
  • Soiling – Mucous secretion and incomplete closure of the anal sphincter due to prolapsed haemorrhoids may lead to minor faecal soiling.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Visible prolapse – Inspection may reveal prolapsed internal haemorrhoids during straining or standing.
  • Thrombosed external haemorrhoid – Presents as a firm, tender, bluish lump around the anal verge.
  • Rectal bleeding – Bright red blood may be seen on rectal examination or during defecation.
  • Mucosal changes – Chronic haemorrhoids may lead to excoriation or maceration of the perianal skin due to irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Significant weight loss – May indicate malignancy rather than haemorrhoids.
  • Persistent or worsening bleeding – Could signify more serious conditions like colorectal cancer.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia – This suggests chronic blood loss, which is unusual for uncomplicated haemorrhoids.
  • Change in bowel habits – Unexplained changes, especially with rectal bleeding, should raise suspicion of malignancy or other gastrointestinal conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Absence of bleeding – Haemorrhoids can be present without overt bleeding, especially in external thrombosed haemorrhoids, where pain may be the primary symptom.
  • Chronic pain – Internal haemorrhoids are typically painless, but in rare cases of strangulation, they may cause severe pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Digital rectal examination – May reveal internal haemorrhoids or rule out other pathologies such as masses.
  • Proctoscopy – Allows direct visualisation of the anal canal to confirm the presence of haemorrhoids and assess their grade.
  • Colonoscopy – Performed when there are red flag features to exclude other causes of rectal bleeding, such as colorectal cancer.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with painless bright red rectal bleeding during defecation for the past three months. He also reports intermittent anal itching and mild discomfort, with occasional prolapse after bowel movements, which he can manually reduce.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Anal fissure – Typically causes sharp pain during and after defecation, with possible blood on toilet paper. Bleeding tends to be less pronounced compared to haemorrhoids.
  • Colorectal cancer – May present with rectal bleeding, weight loss, and changes in bowel habits. Digital rectal exam or colonoscopy is needed to differentiate.
  • Rectal prolapse – Complete prolapse of the rectum through the anus is more extensive than haemorrhoidal prolapse and is associated with a palpable mass during rectal examination.
  • Diverticular disease – May cause painless rectal bleeding, but typically associated with changes in bowel habits or abdominal pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications – Increasing fibre intake, avoiding straining, and improving hydration help to prevent constipation and reduce haemorrhoid symptoms.
  • Topical treatments – Over-the-counter creams or suppositories containing local anaesthetics, corticosteroids, or vasoconstrictors to relieve symptoms.
  • Rubber band ligation – A common outpatient procedure for Grade II and III internal haemorrhoids, causing them to shrink and fall off.
  • Haemorrhoidectomy – Surgical removal is recommended for Grade IV haemorrhoids or when other treatments fail. Minimally invasive procedures, such as stapled haemorrhoidopexy, may also be considered.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Thrombosis – External haemorrhoids may become acutely thrombosed, causing significant pain and swelling.
  • Strangulation – Prolapsed internal haemorrhoids may become trapped by the anal sphincter, leading to impaired blood flow, necrosis, and ulceration.
  • Infection – Rare but possible, especially after procedures such as rubber band ligation or haemorrhoidectomy.
