Tension Type Headache (TTH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Tension-type headache (TTH) is the most common primary headache disorder, characterised by a bilateral, pressing or tightening quality of pain, typically mild to moderate in intensity, and not aggravated by routine physical activity. It is not associated with nausea or vomiting, but may involve light or sound sensitivity. It is often episodic but can become chronic.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK general population: Approximately 40% annually.
  • Prevalence of chronic TTH in UK primary care: 2-3%.
  • Global prevalence: 38% of the population reports experiencing TTH at least once yearly.
  • More common in women than men (female-to-male ratio: 5:4).
  • Peak prevalence: 30-39 years of age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Though the exact mechanism is not fully understood, TTH is thought to result from increased sensitivity of pain pathways in the central nervous system, particularly involving the pericranial muscles. Prolonged muscle tension and stress are believed to activate nociceptors, leading to pain perception. Altered serotonin and endogenous opioid systems may also play a role in pain regulation and perception.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Episodic TTH – Attacks last from 30 minutes to 7 days, occurring infrequently or up to 15 days per month.
  • Chronic TTH – Headache occurs on more than 15 days per month for at least 3 months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Bilateral headache – Most commonly described as a dull, non-pulsating pain on both sides of the head. Pathophysiology: Thought to be related to muscle tension in the scalp and neck.
  • Pressure or tightening sensation – Patients often describe the pain as feeling like a tight band around the head. Pathophysiology: Linked to sustained contraction of pericranial muscles.
  • Absence of nausea or vomiting – A key distinguishing feature from migraine. Pathophysiology: Lack of central sensitisation typical of migraines.
  • Photophobia or phonophobia (mild) – Some patients may experience light or sound sensitivity, but these are less pronounced than in migraine. Pathophysiology: Mild hyperexcitability of sensory processing centres.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Tenderness of pericranial muscles – On palpation, patients often report tenderness over the scalp, neck, or shoulders. Pathophysiology: Muscle tension and myofascial trigger points.
  • No neurological deficits – Unlike migraines or cluster headaches, there are no focal neurological signs. Pathophysiology: Lack of involvement of central nervous system pathways beyond pain processing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset ("thunderclap" headache) – May indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage or other serious intracranial event. Pathophysiology: Rapid intracranial pressure change.
  • Progressive or worsening headaches – Could suggest a space-occupying lesion or increased intracranial pressure. Pathophysiology: Mass effect in the brain.
  • Focal neurological signs – Suggests a structural lesion or stroke. Pathophysiology: Localised disruption of brain function.
  • Headache triggered by cough, exertion, or Valsalva manoeuvre – Can indicate increased intracranial pressure (ICP) or Chiari malformation. Pathophysiology: Increased ICP or structural abnormalities at the base of the skull.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Unilateral pain – While TTH is typically bilateral, some cases may present unilaterally, which could lead to confusion with migraine.
  • Shorter duration – Tension headaches lasting less than 30 minutes may be mistaken for other headache types.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis – Based on history and physical examination. No specific imaging or tests are required unless red flag features are present.
  • CT/MRI head – Indicated only if red flags are present. Expected findings: Normal imaging, unless a secondary cause (e.g., mass lesion) is identified.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 2-month history of bilateral, dull headache, described as a tight band around the head, lasting for several hours daily. No associated nausea, vomiting, or photophobia. Examination reveals mild tenderness over the scalp muscles, and no neurological deficits.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Migraine – Typically unilateral, pulsating pain, associated with nausea, vomiting, and moderate to severe photophobia/phonophobia.
  • Cluster headache – Severe unilateral pain around the eye, accompanied by autonomic symptoms (tearing, nasal congestion).
  • Medication overuse headache – Chronic daily headache, often worsened by the use of analgesics, unlike TTH which is usually episodic and non-progressive.
  • Cervicogenic headache – Pain originating from the neck, often radiating to the head, usually associated with neck movement or position.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Acute treatment – Simple analgesics (paracetamol or ibuprofen). Avoid opioids.
  • Preventive treatment – For chronic TTH, tricyclic antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline) are recommended.
  • Non-pharmacological interventions – Stress management, relaxation techniques, and regular physical activity.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Chronic TTH – Can develop if untreated or inadequately managed, leading to a substantial reduction in quality of life.
  • Medication overuse headache – Can result from frequent use of analgesics, complicating the clinical picture.
