Fever
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Fever is defined as a temporary elevation in body temperature, typically above 38°C, as a result of an underlying infection, inflammation, or other pathological processes. It represents a systemic response to various stimuli such as infection (viral, bacterial, or fungal), tissue injury, malignancies, or autoimmune conditions. Fever is a protective mechanism aimed at enhancing the immune response to infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Fever is one of the most common presenting complaints in both primary and secondary care. In the UK, around 10-20% of GP visits involve a complaint of fever, especially in children. Hospital prevalence is higher, particularly in infectious diseases, where fever is a key symptom in approximately 60-70% of cases. Worldwide, fever remains a major health issue, particularly in tropical regions where malaria, dengue, and other febrile illnesses are endemic.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Fever is caused by the release of pyrogens, substances that induce fever, typically from immune cells such as macrophages or pathogens themselves (e.g., bacterial endotoxins). These pyrogens act on the hypothalamus, the body's thermoregulatory centre, to increase the set-point temperature. This leads to autonomic responses, including vasoconstriction and shivering, to raise the body temperature. The increase in temperature enhances immune function by improving neutrophil activity and inhibiting pathogen replication.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Acute: Fever typically develops over hours to days and is often self-limiting, especially in viral infections, resolving within 3-7 days.
  • Subacute: Lasts from 1 to 2 weeks, seen in conditions such as typhoid fever or endocarditis.
  • Chronic: Persistent fever for more than 3 weeks can suggest more serious conditions like malignancy or chronic infections (e.g., tuberculosis).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • High Temperature (≥38°C) - The hallmark of fever, due to the hypothalamic reset triggered by pyrogens.
  • Chills/Shivering - Occur as a compensatory mechanism to generate heat during the early stages of fever.
  • Malaise - A general feeling of discomfort or unease, often due to systemic immune activation and cytokine release.
  • Headache - Caused by increased prostaglandin production, which affects the pain pathways.
  • Muscle Aches (Myalgia) - Result from cytokine-induced inflammation affecting muscle fibres.
  • Sweating - A response to cooling the body when the fever 'breaks' as the hypothalamic set-point returns to normal.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Elevated Body Temperature (≥38°C) - Measured via oral, tympanic, or rectal methods; a direct result of hypothalamic set-point change.
  • Flushed Skin - Caused by peripheral vasodilation as the body attempts to lose heat.
  • Tachycardia - Elevated heart rate due to increased metabolic demands and sympathetic activation.
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate compensates for increased oxygen demand and metabolic rate.
  • Dehydration - Results from insensible fluid loss via sweating and increased respiratory rate.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Altered Mental Status - Could signify sepsis, meningitis, or encephalitis due to cerebral involvement or metabolic disturbances.
  • Hypotension - A sign of septic shock, where widespread infection leads to vasodilation and reduced cardiac output.
  • Rash (Petechiae or Purpura) - Suggests a severe infection like meningococcal septicaemia, indicating microvascular damage.
  • Severe Abdominal Pain - May suggest an intra-abdominal infection such as appendicitis, peritonitis, or pancreatitis.
  • Dyspnoea - Suggests pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, or heart failure as underlying causes of fever.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Afebrile Infection - In elderly or immunocompromised patients, infections may not trigger a significant febrile response due to impaired immune function.
  • Isolated Tachycardia - In some patients, particularly older adults, fever may manifest primarily as an elevated heart rate without overt temperature rise.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Elevated white cell count (leukocytosis) suggests infection or inflammation. Neutrophilia points towards bacterial infection, while lymphocytosis suggests viral infections.
  • C-Reactive Protein (CRP) - An acute-phase reactant elevated in response to inflammation, used to assess the severity of infection.
  • Blood Cultures - Necessary to identify bacteraemia in cases of suspected sepsis.
  • Urinalysis - Detects urinary tract infections by identifying pyuria, haematuria, or nitrites.
  • Chest X-ray - Helps in diagnosing pneumonia or other chest infections, especially in febrile patients with respiratory symptoms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with a 3-day history of fever (39°C), chills, headache, and generalised muscle aches. No significant past medical history. He reports mild cough but no shortness of breath. On examination, he has tachycardia and flushed skin, but his chest is clear on auscultation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Viral Infection (e.g., Influenza) - Typically presents with rapid onset of fever, cough, and myalgia. Lymphocytosis on FBC. Unlike bacterial infections, viral illnesses are usually self-limiting without the need for antibiotics.
  • Bacterial Pneumonia - Fever with productive cough and dyspnoea, crackles on lung auscultation, and consolidation on chest X-ray. Leukocytosis with neutrophilia on FBC.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - Fever with dysuria, urgency, and frequency of urination. Positive urine dipstick for nitrites and leucocytes.
  • Malaria - Particularly relevant in returning travellers. Presents with cyclical fever, rigors, and headache. Positive blood film for Plasmodium parasites.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Symptomatic Treatment: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for temperature control and symptomatic relief.
  • Antibiotics: Indicated if a bacterial cause is suspected or confirmed (e.g., pneumonia, UTI). Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics may be used initially in cases of sepsis, with de-escalation based on culture results.
  • Antiviral Treatment: If a viral cause is identified (e.g., oseltamivir for influenza).
  • Hydration and Monitoring: Ensure adequate hydration and monitor vital signs closely in hospitalised patients.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fever"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - A severe, life-threatening response to infection leading to multi-organ failure. Characterised by fever, hypotension, and altered mental status.
  • Dehydration - Prolonged fever leads to fluid losses, potentially causing hypovolaemia and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Delirium - Fever, especially in older adults, can cause confusion or delirium due to metabolic or infectious causes.
