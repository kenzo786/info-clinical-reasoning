Tonsillitis Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Tonsillitis is an inflammation of the palatine tonsils, typically caused by viral or bacterial infections. It is characterised by sore throat, painful swallowing, and erythema of the tonsils, sometimes accompanied by purulent exudate. Most cases are viral, with group A streptococcus (GAS) being the most common bacterial cause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, tonsillitis accounts for approximately 1.5% of all GP consultations. Most cases occur in children and young adults, though it can affect individuals of all ages. Bacterial tonsillitis, primarily caused by GAS, is responsible for about 20-30% of cases in children and 5-15% in adults.
  • Globally, tonsillitis is common, with incidence highest in temperate climates and during the winter months.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • In tonsillitis, the tonsils are infected and inflamed, often due to pathogens like viruses (adenovirus, rhinovirus) or bacteria (e.g., GAS). The immune response to infection leads to recruitment of inflammatory cells to the tonsils, resulting in swelling, erythema, and sometimes exudate formation. The inflammatory mediators also cause the characteristic pain and fever associated with the condition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Acute tonsillitis typically lasts 5-7 days, with peak symptom severity on days 2-3. Viral cases resolve more quickly, while bacterial tonsillitis may linger without treatment. Complications, such as peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), can develop if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Sore throat – Caused by tonsillar inflammation and irritation of local nerves.
  • Dysphagia (painful swallowing) – Due to tonsillar and surrounding tissue swelling, making swallowing difficult.
  • Fever – A systemic inflammatory response triggered by infection.
  • Ear pain (referred otalgia) – Pain referred to the ears via the glossopharyngeal nerve.
  • Headache – Caused by systemic inflammation and dehydration.
  • Fatigue – A common systemic symptom due to immune activation and inflammation.
  • Voice changes (muffled voice) – Swelling of the tonsils may alter vocal resonance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Tonsillar erythema – Due to local inflammation.
  • Tonsillar exudates – Often seen in bacterial infections like GAS, reflecting pus formation.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy – Enlargement of lymph nodes as they drain the infection in the tonsils.
  • Halitosis – Resulting from bacterial colonisation and pus in the oropharynx.
  • Fever – Indicative of systemic infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Difficulty breathing – Suggests airway compromise due to severe swelling or a peritonsillar abscess.
  • Severe neck stiffness – Could indicate extension of infection into the deep neck spaces (e.g., retropharyngeal abscess).
  • Trismus – Inability to open the mouth may signal a peritonsillar abscess.
  • Unilateral tonsillar swelling – Concerning for peritonsillar abscess formation.
  • Drooling – Can suggest airway compromise or significant pharyngeal swelling.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation – Some viral causes may result in mild or no symptoms, especially in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Chronic tonsillitis – Persistent mild sore throat and enlarged tonsils without acute symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Throat swab – A throat culture or rapid antigen detection test (RADT) for GAS; a positive result confirms bacterial aetiology.
  • FBC – May show elevated white blood cell count, particularly neutrophilia in bacterial infections.
  • Monospot test – If infectious mononucleosis is suspected, this can detect Epstein-Barr virus infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) – Elevated in bacterial infections but non-specific.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • A 16-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of sore throat, fever, and painful swallowing. On examination, she has tonsillar erythema with purulent exudates and tender cervical lymphadenopathy. No difficulty breathing is noted.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Viral pharyngitis – Typically lacks purulent exudate, and symptoms are less severe than bacterial tonsillitis.
  • Infectious mononucleosis – Similar presentation but often associated with fatigue, splenomegaly, and a positive Monospot test.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) – Presents with trismus, unilateral swelling, and difficulty opening the mouth.
  • Epiglottitis – Acute onset, drooling, and muffled voice, with rapid airway compromise risk.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care – Hydration, analgesia (e.g., paracetamol or ibuprofen).
  • Antibiotics – Phenoxymethylpenicillin or amoxicillin for 10 days if bacterial tonsillitis (GAS) is confirmed.
  • Corticosteroids – May be used in severe cases to reduce inflammation and swelling.
  • Tonsillectomy – Considered in recurrent or chronic cases, especially with complications.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Peritonsillar abscess – Localised infection that can cause airway compromise.
  • Rheumatic fever – A systemic immune response to untreated GAS infection, affecting the heart, joints, and skin.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis – Immune complex deposition in the kidneys following GAS infection.
