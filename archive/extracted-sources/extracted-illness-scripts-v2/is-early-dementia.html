Early Dementia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Dementia is a clinical syndrome characterised by a progressive decline in cognitive function, memory, language, executive function, and other mental abilities severe enough to interfere with daily life. Early dementia refers to the initial stage, where impairment is mild and may not be immediately obvious to the individual or others.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: ~1% in people aged 65–69, increasing with age to over 20% in those over 85.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Dementia is seen in up to 25% of older adult inpatients, though many are undiagnosed.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Over 50 million people have dementia, projected to triple by 2050 due to ageing populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Most cases of dementia result from neurodegenerative processes, commonly Alzheimer’s disease (characterised by amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles) and vascular changes (small vessel disease leading to white matter damage). Neurodegeneration leads to cortical and subcortical brain atrophy, neurotransmitter deficits (e.g., acetylcholine), and progressive loss of cognitive function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild symptoms affecting memory, often mistaken for normal ageing. Progresses slowly over months to years.
  • Moderate phase: Cognitive and functional decline becomes more noticeable, interfering with daily tasks and social interactions.
  • Advanced phase: Severe cognitive impairment, with major dependence on caregivers for personal care, lasting several years before terminal decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Memory Loss - Often the earliest symptom, especially for recent events (short-term memory). Pathophysiology involves hippocampal atrophy in Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Language Impairment - Difficulty finding words or forming coherent speech; linked to left hemisphere atrophy.
  • Disorientation - Confusion with time and place due to disruption in spatial awareness and processing.
  • Loss of Executive Function - Problems with planning, judgement, and complex tasks, due to frontal lobe involvement.
  • Reduced Insight - Patients may not recognise their own impairments, associated with frontal lobe atrophy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Impaired Recall on Testing - Difficulty recalling information after a delay, indicating memory dysfunction.
  • Disorientation - Poor orientation to time, place, or situation; worsens as dementia progresses.
  • Poor Verbal Fluency - Difficulty with word-finding during speech assessments, such as naming or fluency tasks.
  • Constructional Apraxia - Inability to copy complex shapes or draw, indicating visuospatial impairment.
  • Apathy - Lack of motivation or interest, common in neurodegenerative dementias.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Rapid Cognitive Decline - Suggests potentially reversible causes such as infection, metabolic disorder, or neoplasm.
  • Focal Neurological Deficits - Indicates possible cerebrovascular events or other neurological conditions (e.g., stroke, tumour).
  • Severe Mood or Behaviour Changes - Significant changes may suggest psychiatric conditions or atypical dementias like frontotemporal dementia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Predominant Language Impairment - May be primary progressive aphasia, an atypical variant.
  • Behavioural Changes as Primary Feature - May suggest frontotemporal dementia, often misdiagnosed as a psychiatric disorder.
  • Fluctuating Cognition with Visual Hallucinations - Characteristic of Lewy body dementia, commonly misdiagnosed.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • MMSE or MoCA - Screening tools to assess cognitive function; a score below standard cut-offs suggests cognitive impairment.
  • MRI Brain - To assess for cortical and hippocampal atrophy in Alzheimer’s or white matter lesions in vascular dementia.
  • Blood Tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, TSH, Vitamin B12) - To exclude reversible causes like vitamin deficiencies, thyroid dysfunction, or metabolic issues.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • An 80-year-old woman with progressive short-term memory loss over 2 years, difficulty recalling recent events, mild language impairment, and difficulty managing daily tasks.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Depression - Can cause "pseudo-dementia" with cognitive symptoms, but responds to antidepressants, and usually lacks progressive memory loss.
  • Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) - Memory issues without significant impact on daily function; may progress to dementia over time.
  • Delirium - Acute cognitive changes with fluctuating course, often reversible if the underlying cause is addressed.
  • Vascular Dementia - Stepwise progression with evidence of stroke or cerebrovascular risk factors.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Acetylcholinesterase Inhibitors (e.g., donepezil) - First-line for mild to moderate Alzheimer’s disease; improves cognition and function.
  • Memantine - NMDA receptor antagonist used in moderate to severe Alzheimer’s.
  • Non-Pharmacological Interventions - Cognitive stimulation therapy, structured activities, caregiver support and education.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Early Dementia"][/sc]
  • Functional Decline - Loss of independence in daily activities, requiring caregiver support.
  • Increased Risk of Falls - Due to cognitive and physical impairment; common in moderate to advanced stages.
  • Behavioural and Psychological Symptoms - Agitation, aggression, and psychosis are frequent, increasing with disease severity.
  • Malnutrition and Dehydration - Common due to neglect or inability to care for oneself in advanced dementia.
