Goitre
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Goitre is an enlargement of the thyroid gland, which can be diffuse or nodular, and is often visible as a swelling at the base of the neck. It is not a diagnosis in itself but can be associated with various thyroid dysfunctions, including euthyroid, hyperthyroid, or hypothyroid states. Goitres may arise from iodine deficiency, autoimmune thyroid disease, or nodular thyroid conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare (approximately 1-5% of adults).
  • Prevalence in UK hospital population: Common in endocrinology clinics due to related thyroid disorders.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Goitre is highly prevalent in regions with iodine deficiency, affecting approximately 30% of the global population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Goitre results from thyroid hypertrophy and/or hyperplasia due to various stimuli. In iodine-deficient regions, decreased iodine availability leads to reduced thyroid hormone production, prompting increased TSH secretion and thyroid gland growth. In autoimmune thyroid diseases like Graves’ disease or Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, either stimulating or damaging immune processes lead to glandular enlargement. Nodular goitre occurs due to focal areas of thyroid cell hyperplasia or cyst formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Goitre can develop gradually over months to years. In the case of iodine deficiency, the enlargement occurs slowly as thyroid hormone levels gradually decline. Autoimmune goitre, such as in Hashimoto’s thyroiditis or Graves’ disease, may also have an insidious onset but can sometimes occur more rapidly in cases of acute inflammatory thyroiditis. Nodular goitre may develop over years with periods of stability and gradual increase in size.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Neck swelling - The most common presenting symptom, usually painless, caused by the enlarged thyroid gland.
  • Pressure symptoms - These include difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), a sensation of choking, or shortness of breath due to compression of the trachea or oesophagus.
  • Hypothyroid symptoms - Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, and dry skin may be present in goitre associated with hypothyroidism, due to decreased thyroid hormone production.
  • Hyperthyroid symptoms - Anxiety, weight loss, palpitations, heat intolerance, and tremors may occur if the goitre is associated with hyperthyroidism, such as in Graves’ disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Visible neck swelling - Enlargement at the base of the neck, typically asymmetrical in nodular goitre.
  • Thyroid bruits - Audible with a stethoscope in cases of hyperactive thyroid tissue, especially in Graves' disease, due to increased vascularity.
  • Hoarseness - A result of recurrent laryngeal nerve compression by an enlarging goitre.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Rapidly enlarging goitre - May signify malignancy or acute haemorrhage into a thyroid nodule.
  • Hoarseness or vocal cord palsy - Suggests compression or invasion of the recurrent laryngeal nerve, possibly indicating thyroid cancer.
  • Tracheal deviation or compression - Severe compression can lead to airway compromise, signalling urgent need for intervention.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy - Suspicious for metastatic spread from thyroid cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Small, non-visible goitres can present with isolated symptoms of thyroid dysfunction, such as weight changes or fatigue, without apparent neck swelling. This may delay recognition of the goitre.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Thyroid function tests - TSH, free T4, and free T3 levels to assess for hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism. Low TSH with high T3/T4 suggests hyperthyroidism, while high TSH with low T4 indicates hypothyroidism.
  • Ultrasound - To assess the size, nature (solid or cystic), and vascularity of the goitre. Useful for differentiating between diffuse and nodular goitre.
  • Fine needle aspiration (FNA) - Indicated for suspicious nodules on ultrasound to rule out malignancy.
  • Thyroid autoantibodies - Elevated in autoimmune thyroid diseases like Hashimoto’s thyroiditis (anti-TPO antibodies) or Graves' disease (TSH receptor antibodies).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of neck swelling and mild dysphagia. She reports fatigue, cold intolerance, and weight gain. Examination reveals an enlarged, firm thyroid with no cervical lymphadenopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Thyroid cancer - Typically presents with a hard, irregular, rapidly enlarging nodule; associated with lymphadenopathy or vocal cord palsy, unlike benign goitre.
  • Thyroiditis - Presents with pain (especially in subacute thyroiditis) and tenderness, unlike painless goitre.
  • Multinodular goitre - Typically presents with asymmetrical enlargement and multiple palpable nodules, while diffuse goitre is smooth and symmetrical.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Iodine supplementation - In iodine-deficient areas, dietary iodine supplementation can reduce goitre size and prevent further growth.
  • Levothyroxine - For hypothyroid goitre, replacing thyroid hormone can reduce TSH stimulation and potentially reduce goitre size.
  • Radioactive iodine or surgery - Indicated in cases of large, symptomatic goitres or in hyperthyroid goitre that fails medical management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Goitre"][/sc]
  • Thyroid malignancy - A small percentage of goitres harbour thyroid cancer, particularly if there are suspicious features like rapid growth or lymphadenopathy.
  • Airway obstruction - Large goitres can cause tracheal compression, leading to dyspnoea and requiring urgent intervention.
  • Hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism - Depending on the underlying cause, untreated goitre can lead to significant thyroid dysfunction.
