Smokers Laryngitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Smokers laryngitis is an inflammatory condition of the larynx resulting from prolonged exposure to tobacco smoke. It is characterised by chronic irritation, vocal cord damage, and voice hoarseness due to repeated chemical injury. The condition is typically associated with heavy and prolonged smoking, and may lead to chronic laryngitis or precancerous changes if smoking continues.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, smokers laryngitis is commonly seen in long-term smokers, with a prevalence of around 10-20% in general practice populations. Hospital-based prevalence is less common unless complications such as malignancy arise. Globally, smokers laryngitis is more common in countries with high smoking rates, particularly in populations with limited access to tobacco cessation services.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • 1. Chronic exposure to tobacco smoke introduces irritants that cause repeated inflammation of the laryngeal mucosa.
  • 2. Over time, this leads to epithelial changes, including squamous metaplasia, which is the body's adaptive response to the toxic stimuli.
  • 3. Persistent inflammation also damages the vocal cords, resulting in thickening and loss of normal vibratory function, contributing to hoarseness.
  • 4. If untreated, chronic laryngitis may lead to precancerous changes, such as dysplasia, and eventually malignant transformation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase – Mild laryngitis may present after only a few months of heavy smoking, with intermittent hoarseness that worsens with further exposure.
  • Chronic Phase – After years of exposure, patients develop persistent hoarseness, chronic cough, and an inability to project their voice. Symptoms tend to fluctuate depending on smoking habits.
  • Long-term Phase – Untreated smokers laryngitis can progress to chronic laryngitis, dysplastic changes, or even laryngeal cancer over decades.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Hoarseness - Caused by chronic inflammation and damage to the vocal cords, leading to reduced vibratory function.
  • Chronic dry cough - Irritation of the laryngeal mucosa triggers coughing in an attempt to clear secretions or irritants.
  • Throat irritation - Due to chronic exposure to smoke and particulate matter, causing persistent discomfort.
  • Frequent throat clearing - As a result of increased mucus production and irritation of the throat and larynx.
  • Dysphonia - Changes in voice quality, pitch, and volume due to the inflammation and structural changes in the vocal cords.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Hoarse voice - The most obvious sign, caused by thickening of the vocal cords due to chronic inflammation.
  • Laryngoscopic findings - Reddened and swollen vocal cords, thickened laryngeal mucosa, sometimes with visible leukoplakia (white patches), indicating squamous changes.
  • Decreased vocal cord mobility - Visualised during laryngoscopy due to scarring and thickening of vocal fold tissue.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Persistent hoarseness lasting more than 3 weeks - May indicate malignancy, particularly laryngeal cancer, due to prolonged inflammation and potential dysplasia.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Suggests possible cancer or systemic disease linked to chronic smoking.
  • Dysphagia or odynophagia - Difficulty or pain when swallowing may be indicative of tumour growth, obstructing the pharynx or oesophagus.
  • Neck masses - May signal metastasis from laryngeal cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Intermittent voice changes - Smokers who do not smoke regularly may only experience symptoms sporadically, which could delay diagnosis.
  • Minimal voice symptoms with cough - Some patients may present primarily with a cough and minimal hoarseness, especially in the early stages.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Laryngoscopy - Visualisation of the larynx is the primary diagnostic test. Findings include erythema, oedema, and vocal cord thickening.
  • CT or MRI - May be ordered to evaluate for structural abnormalities, especially if malignancy is suspected.
  • Biopsy - If dysplastic changes or leukoplakia are noted, a biopsy is performed to rule out malignancy. Histological examination reveals squamous metaplasia or carcinoma.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male smoker presents with a 6-month history of progressive hoarseness, dry cough, and throat discomfort, worsening over the last 2 weeks. He has smoked 20 cigarettes daily for 30 years.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Vocal cord nodules - More common in those with vocal strain, such as singers, without the history of smoking. Typically bilateral lesions.
  • Reflux laryngitis - Caused by acid reflux irritating the larynx, associated with heartburn and acid regurgitation. No direct link to smoking.
  • Laryngeal cancer - More severe symptoms, including persistent hoarseness, difficulty swallowing, and neck masses. Strong association with smoking.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Smoking cessation - The most critical intervention to halt progression. Referrals to smoking cessation services, nicotine replacement therapy, and counselling.
  • Voice rest - Reducing vocal strain can aid in recovery, especially if hoarseness is prominent.
  • Hydration and humidification - Keeping the throat moist may reduce irritation and aid in healing the inflamed mucosa.
  • Corticosteroids (occasionally) - In cases of severe inflammation, short courses of oral corticosteroids may be used to reduce inflammation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Smokers Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Laryngeal cancer - Chronic inflammation and squamous metaplasia from smoking can lead to malignant transformation over time.
  • Permanent voice changes - Continued smoking and laryngitis can lead to irreversible damage to the vocal cords, resulting in chronic hoarseness or dysphonia.
  • Respiratory complications - Chronic cough and mucous hypersecretion can worsen or be complicated by chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).
