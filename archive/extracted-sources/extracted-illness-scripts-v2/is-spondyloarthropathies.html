Spondyloarthropathies
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Spondyloarthropathies (SpA) are a group of inflammatory rheumatic diseases that share clinical, genetic, and radiological features. Core characteristics include axial inflammation (affecting the spine and sacroiliac joints), peripheral arthritis, enthesitis (inflammation at tendon or ligament attachment points), and a strong association with HLA-B27 genetic markers. Subtypes include ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis, reactive arthritis, and enteropathic arthritis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • In the UK general practice population, the prevalence of ankylosing spondylitis, the prototypical SpA, is approximately 0.1–0.2%. Psoriatic arthritis prevalence ranges from 0.1–0.3% in GP settings.
  • UK hospital prevalence of SpA is higher due to referral bias and is reported to range from 1–3% among rheumatology clinic patients.
  • Globally, SpA prevalence varies by ethnicity, with higher rates in populations with significant HLA-B27 positivity, ranging from 0.1–1.4% depending on the region.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • HLA-B27 misfolding leads to endoplasmic reticulum stress and an aberrant immune response.
  • Inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-17 promote synovitis and enthesitis, leading to bone erosion and abnormal bone repair.
  • Chronic inflammation results in ankylosis (fusion of joints), particularly in axial SpA, reducing mobility over time.
  • Enthesitis at peripheral sites contributes to pain and stiffness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Initial presentation often includes insidious onset of inflammatory back pain or enthesitis.
  • Progression varies but may include episodic peripheral arthritis or axial stiffness over months to years.
  • Chronic disease can lead to permanent structural damage, such as spinal fusion, in later stages.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory back pain - Insidious onset of pain, worse at night and improving with activity; due to sacroiliac joint and vertebral inflammation.
  • Peripheral arthritis - Asymmetric oligoarthritis affecting large joints, caused by synovial inflammation.
  • Morning stiffness - Lasts >30 minutes; reflects synovitis and enthesitis-driven stiffness.
  • Enthesitis - Pain and tenderness at tendon insertion sites like the Achilles tendon; due to localised inflammation.
  • Fatigue - Related to systemic inflammation and cytokine-mediated effects.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Reduced spinal mobility - Loss of lumbar flexion, tested by Schober's test; reflects ankylosis.
  • Sacroiliac joint tenderness - Detected on palpation or compression tests; due to inflammation.
  • Peripheral joint swelling - Indicative of arthritis.
  • Dactylitis - "Sausage digits," caused by diffuse inflammation of a digit.
  • Psoriatic skin lesions - Seen in psoriatic arthritis; result from hyperproliferative keratinocytes and inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe back pain - Could indicate a vertebral fracture, especially in advanced disease with osteoporosis.
  • Neurological deficits - Suggest cauda equina syndrome from spinal stenosis or nerve compression.
  • Systemic symptoms - Fever or weight loss may signify malignancy or systemic infection mimicking SpA.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Isolated enthesitis - Without axial or peripheral symptoms; may be mistaken for mechanical injury.
  • Unilateral sacroiliitis - Inflammatory back pain confined to one side, delaying diagnosis.
  • Extra-articular features - Uveitis, inflammatory bowel disease, or psoriasis as the presenting symptom.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Imaging - X-rays show sacroiliitis (sclerosis, erosion), while MRI detects early bone marrow oedema.
  • HLA-B27 testing - Present in 90% of ankylosing spondylitis cases but lacks specificity.
  • CRP/ESR - Elevated in active disease due to systemic inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male with a 6-month history of worsening inflammatory back pain, morning stiffness lasting >1 hour, and fatigue. MRI shows bilateral sacroiliitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Mechanical back pain - Lacks inflammatory features and morning stiffness.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Predominantly affects small joints symmetrically, unlike SpA.
  • Fibromyalgia - Pain is widespread, lacks inflammatory or imaging findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • First-line - NSAIDs (e.g., naproxen) for pain and inflammation.
  • Second-line - Biologic DMARDs like TNF inhibitors (adalimumab) or IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab).
  • Physiotherapy - Regular exercise to maintain spinal mobility.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spondyloarthropathies"][/sc]
  • Spinal fusion - Leading to reduced mobility and disability.
  • Fractures - Osteoporotic fractures due to chronic inflammation and immobility.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Increased risk from chronic systemic inflammation.
