Autonomic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Autonomic neuropathy refers to damage to the autonomic nervous system (ANS), which controls involuntary functions like heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, and temperature regulation. This condition can affect multiple organs and is commonly associated with systemic diseases like diabetes, causing a wide range of symptoms that impair daily life. It can result from a variety of causes including chronic illnesses, infections, or toxins.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 5-10% of diabetic patients are diagnosed with autonomic neuropathy, particularly those with long-standing diabetes.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Autonomic neuropathy is more commonly diagnosed in tertiary care centres, especially in diabetic patients and those with systemic illnesses such as Parkinson’s or amyloidosis.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, diabetic autonomic neuropathy is one of the most common forms, affecting up to 20% of diabetics depending on disease duration and control.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • The autonomic nervous system has two components: the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems. Autonomic neuropathy occurs when these systems are damaged, usually due to chronic hyperglycaemia in diabetes, inflammatory processes, or toxins. This leads to impaired neural transmission and dysregulation of involuntary functions. In diabetes, prolonged hyperglycaemia causes microvascular damage, affecting the small nerve fibres that supply autonomic organs. Other causes include autoimmune disorders, amyloidosis, and chronic alcoholism, which can lead to direct nerve damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Subclinical autonomic dysfunction may occur without overt symptoms.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms gradually appear and worsen as the neuropathy progresses. This may take months to years, depending on the underlying cause.
  • Chronic Phase: Long-standing autonomic neuropathy leads to severe, irreversible organ dysfunction (e.g., gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension), significantly impacting quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Orthostatic hypotension - Dizziness or light-headedness when standing due to impaired sympathetic regulation of blood pressure.
  • Gastroparesis - Nausea, vomiting, and bloating caused by delayed gastric emptying from vagus nerve damage.
  • Urinary retention - Difficulty initiating urination and poor bladder emptying due to autonomic dysfunction of the bladder.
  • Constipation or diarrhoea - Gastrointestinal dysmotility, especially in diabetes, results from enteric nervous system impairment.
  • Anhidrosis - Lack of sweating due to disruption of sympathetic nerve control of sweat glands.
  • Sexual dysfunction - Erectile dysfunction or loss of vaginal lubrication as a result of parasympathetic dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Postural hypotension - A drop in blood pressure upon standing due to impaired baroreceptor reflexes.
  • Loss of heart rate variability - A blunted response in heart rate during deep breathing, indicative of parasympathetic dysfunction.
  • Absent sweating - A localised or generalised lack of sweating, reflecting sympathetic denervation of sweat glands.
  • Bladder distension - Enlarged bladder due to impaired bladder emptying and urinary retention.
  • Skin changes - Dry or cracked skin due to impaired sweating and autonomic control of skin hydration.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Severe orthostatic hypotension - May lead to syncope or falls, indicating widespread autonomic failure.
  • Cardiac autonomic dysfunction - Unexplained resting tachycardia or bradycardia can lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • Intractable gastroparesis - Severe nausea, vomiting, and malnutrition, indicating progressive autonomic dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - In early stages, patients may have no overt symptoms despite significant autonomic dysfunction, often detected during routine diabetic follow-up.
  • Fluctuating symptoms - Some patients may have intermittent symptoms such as occasional dizziness or gastrointestinal discomfort, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Autonomic function tests - Tilt table test, Valsalva manoeuvre, and deep breathing tests assess the autonomic reflexes. Pathologically, these reveal diminished heart rate variability and impaired blood pressure response.
  • 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring - Detects non-dipping or reverse dipping patterns, which are signs of autonomic dysfunction.
  • Gastric emptying study - Used to confirm gastroparesis by showing delayed gastric emptying.
  • Urodynamic testing - Assesses bladder function and identifies abnormalities like urinary retention.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a 20-year history of poorly controlled type 2 diabetes presents with dizziness on standing, nausea, bloating, and urinary retention over the past 6 months, indicative of progressive autonomic neuropathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease - Can present with autonomic dysfunction, but would be distinguished by motor symptoms like tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia.
  • Amyloidosis - Presents with autonomic dysfunction but may include additional features such as peripheral neuropathy, heart failure, or hepatomegaly.
  • Multiple system atrophy - Features early autonomic failure but is associated with rapid progression and Parkinsonism.
  • Chronic alcoholism - May cause autonomic dysfunction but usually with clear history of alcohol abuse and peripheral neuropathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Non-pharmacological: Management of underlying cause (e.g., optimising glycaemic control in diabetes), compression stockings for orthostatic hypotension, and increased fluid and salt intake.
  • Pharmacological: Midodrine or fludrocortisone for orthostatic hypotension, metoclopramide for gastroparesis, and medications such as sildenafil for erectile dysfunction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures - Due to severe orthostatic hypotension leading to syncope.
  • Cardiovascular death - Sudden death may result from cardiac autonomic dysfunction, including arrhythmias.
  • Malnutrition - Due to severe gastroparesis leading to poor oral intake.
