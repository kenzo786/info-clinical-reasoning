Immunisation Reaction
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • An immunisation reaction is an adverse event following vaccination, ranging from mild local symptoms to systemic reactions. It occurs due to the immune system's response to the vaccine components, including antigens or adjuvants.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Immunisation reactions are reported in approximately 10–20% of vaccinated individuals, primarily mild local reactions.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Severe reactions requiring hospitalisation are rare, occurring in less than 1 per 1,000,000 doses.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: The prevalence of immunisation reactions varies but remains predominantly mild and self-limiting.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Vaccination introduces antigens, leading to activation of the innate immune response.
  • Antigen-presenting cells process the antigen and activate T cells, promoting antibody production and memory cell formation.
  • Adjuvants may amplify the immune response, causing local inflammation and systemic effects.
  • Rarely, hypersensitivity reactions occur due to IgE-mediated mechanisms or complement activation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Immediate Phase: Within minutes to hours (e.g., anaphylaxis).
  • Early Phase: 1–3 days post-vaccination (e.g., fever, local redness).
  • Late Phase: 5–14 days post-vaccination (e.g., rash from live attenuated vaccines).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Local Pain and Redness - Due to inflammation at the injection site, mediated by cytokines like IL-1 and TNF-α.
  • Fever - A systemic response from pyrogenic cytokines such as IL-6 acting on the hypothalamus.
  • Fatigue - Related to systemic inflammatory response and cytokine release.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Injection Site Swelling - Localised oedema due to capillary permeability from histamine release.
  • Warmth - Indicates increased blood flow from the inflammatory response.
  • Generalised Rash - Often associated with live vaccines, mediated by immune complexes or hypersensitivity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Rapid onset, difficulty breathing, hypotension, and urticaria. Indicates IgE-mediated hypersensitivity, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe Neurological Symptoms - Such as seizures or encephalopathy. Could signify a rare complication like vaccine-induced encephalitis.
  • Persistent High Fever - Suggestive of an underlying infection or severe systemic response.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Delayed Hypersensitivity - Presents with erythema multiforme days to weeks later, often missed.
  • Vaccine-Associated Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) - Rare, presenting with ascending paralysis weeks post-vaccination.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count - May show leukocytosis indicating an immune response.
  • Serum IgE Levels - Elevated in cases of suspected anaphylaxis.
  • Skin Testing - For hypersensitivity, particularly in repeated vaccine reactions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with fever, injection site swelling, and fatigue 24 hours after receiving the influenza vaccine.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - Distinguished by progressive redness, warmth, and systemic symptoms.
  • Vaccine-Preventable Disease - Symptoms occurring due to vaccine failure or underlying infection.
  • Drug Reaction - Systemic symptoms, often with multi-drug exposure.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Mild Reactions - Symptomatic treatment with analgesics (e.g., paracetamol) and antihistamines for itching or mild rash.
  • Anaphylaxis - Immediate administration of intramuscular adrenaline (0.5 mg in adults), followed by supportive care.
  • Persistent Symptoms - Further evaluation for underlying conditions or secondary infections.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Immunisation Reaction"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Rare but life-threatening, requiring rapid treatment.
  • Neurological Complications - Such as encephalopathy or GBS, though very rare.
  • Severe Local Reactions - May lead to abscess formation or prolonged discomfort.
