Angina
Definition [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="101" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Angina is a clinical syndrome characterised by chest pain or discomfort due to myocardial ischaemia, typically triggered by exertion or emotional stress, and relieved by rest or nitrates. It is most commonly caused by atherosclerotic narrowing of the coronary arteries, resulting in reduced blood flow and oxygen supply to the heart muscle.
Epidemiology, Risk Factors & Prevention [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="102" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • [sc name="perplexity-link-epidemiology-icon" URL="https://www.perplexity.ai/search/summarise-the-epidemiology-of-OaUDhHgLTiGxp39zuurmAg" edit="890"][/sc] Angina affects approximately 3-4% of adults in the UK, with a prevalence of 10-14% in men and 10-12% in women aged 65-84. It accounts for around 22,600 new cases annually in the UK. Globally, angina is responsible for 18 million hospital admissions in Europe, with a higher burden in socioeconomically deprived groups.
  • Risk Factors: The main risk factors for angina include hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, smoking, diabetes mellitus, obesity, and a sedentary lifestyle. A family history of coronary artery disease (CAD) and increased age also contribute significantly to risk.
  • Prevention: Lifestyle modifications such as smoking cessation, a healthy diet (rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains), regular physical activity, and weight management can reduce the risk of developing angina. Managing comorbid conditions like hypertension and diabetes through medical therapy and regular monitoring is also crucial.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="103" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerosis: The underlying cause of most cases of angina is atherosclerosis, where lipid-laden plaques develop in the coronary arteries, leading to progressive narrowing.
  • Myocardial ischaemia: Reduced coronary artery blood flow decreases oxygen delivery to myocardial cells, particularly during exertion, where oxygen demand increases.
  • Lactate production and pain: Inadequate oxygen supply leads to anaerobic metabolism and lactic acid production, which stimulates pain receptors in the heart.
  • Endothelial dysfunction: Impaired vasodilation of coronary arteries exacerbates the mismatch between myocardial oxygen supply and demand.
Time Course [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="104" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Stable angina: Symptoms typically follow a chronic, predictable pattern, lasting for months or years, with episodes triggered by exertion and relieved by rest.
  • Unstable angina: Symptoms may occur more frequently, at rest, or with minimal exertion, indicating a higher risk of acute coronary events.
  • Progression: If untreated, angina can progress to myocardial infarction or heart failure over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="105" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Chest pain or discomfort: The most common symptom, typically described as pressure, tightness, or heaviness. It occurs due to myocardial ischaemia and may radiate to the left arm, neck, jaw, or back.
  • Dyspnoea: Shortness of breath is common due to impaired myocardial function and subsequent left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Fatigue: Patients may experience fatigue, especially during exertion, as the heart struggles to meet the body’s oxygen demands.
  • Nausea or sweating: These can accompany chest pain, reflecting the autonomic response to myocardial ischaemia.
Signs [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="106" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • [sc name="definition-of-term-search" Q="Levine’s sign"][/sc]: Patients often clutch their chest to describe their pain, indicative of myocardial ischaemia.
  • Tachycardia: The heart rate may increase as the body attempts to compensate for reduced cardiac output.
  • Hypertension or hypotension: Blood pressure changes can reflect the severity of ischaemia or heart function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="107" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe chest pain: This could indicate myocardial infarction, which requires immediate intervention to prevent further cardiac damage.
  • Chest pain at rest: This suggests unstable angina, increasing the risk of an acute coronary event.
  • Syncope or near-syncope: These symptoms may suggest significant ischaemia or arrhythmias, increasing the risk of sudden cardiac death.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="108" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Atypical chest pain: Some patients, especially women, older adults, and those with diabetes, may experience angina without classic chest pain, presenting instead with fatigue, dyspnoea, or gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Silent ischaemia: In some cases, patients may have myocardial ischaemia without experiencing any pain, which can lead to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="109" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • ECG (Electrocardiogram): Resting ECG may show ST-segment depression or T-wave inversion during an episode of angina. These changes reflect myocardial ischaemia.
  • Exercise stress test: This test involves monitoring ECG while the patient exercises. Ischaemic changes (e.g., ST depression) during exertion help diagnose angina.
  • Coronary angiography: This imaging test visualises coronary artery blockages and is typically reserved for high-risk patients or those with unstable symptoms.
  • CT coronary angiography: A non-invasive test used to visualise coronary arteries and assess for atherosclerotic plaques.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="111" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction (MI): Unlike angina, MI presents with prolonged chest pain unrelieved by rest or nitrates, often accompanied by diaphoresis, nausea, and significant ECG changes (ST-elevation or new Q waves).
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): GORD-related chest pain is typically burning, associated with meals, and relieved by antacids, not exertion.
  • Pulmonary embolism (PE): PE presents with sudden onset pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea, and sometimes haemoptysis, with risk factors like recent surgery or prolonged immobility.
Treatment [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="112" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Anti-anginal medications: First-line therapy includes nitrates (e.g., GTN spray), beta-blockers (e.g., bisoprolol), and calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine) to reduce myocardial oxygen demand and improve blood flow.
  • Antiplatelet therapy: Low-dose aspirin is recommended to reduce the risk of thrombotic events.
  • Lifestyle modification: Risk factor control through smoking cessation, dietary changes, and regular exercise is crucial.
  • Coronary interventions: In severe cases, percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) may be indicated to restore blood flow.
Complications [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Angina" select="115" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction: Progressive coronary artery narrowing can lead to plaque rupture and complete occlusion, causing an infarction.
  • Heart failure: Chronic ischaemia can impair myocardial function, leading to systolic dysfunction and heart failure.
  • Arrhythmias: Myocardial ischaemia can disrupt the heart’s electrical system, resulting in arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation or ventricular tachycardia.
