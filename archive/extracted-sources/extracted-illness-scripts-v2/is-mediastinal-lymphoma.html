Mediastinal Lymphoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Mediastinal lymphoma is a type of malignant lymphoma that primarily affects the mediastinum, the central compartment of the thoracic cavity. It most commonly involves two types: primary mediastinal (thymic) large B-cell lymphoma (PMBCL), an aggressive form of non-Hodgkin lymphoma, and Hodgkin lymphoma, especially nodular sclerosis subtype.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Rare, estimated prevalence is <1% of all lymphomas.
  • UK hospital prevalence: More common in specialised oncology centres, particularly in young adults and adolescents.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Represents about 2-3% of all non-Hodgkin lymphomas and up to 10-20% of lymphomas in young adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Primary mediastinal lymphomas arise from B cells in the thymus, resulting in rapid proliferation of malignant lymphocytes. This mass compresses surrounding structures such as the trachea, superior vena cava (SVC), and lungs.
  • SVC compression can lead to superior vena cava syndrome (SVCS), while tracheal compression results in respiratory symptoms. In Hodgkin lymphoma, Reed-Sternberg cells are the characteristic malignant cells.
  • As the tumour grows, systemic symptoms (e.g., fevers, night sweats, weight loss) result from cytokine release by the tumour and the immune system's inflammatory response.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Often asymptomatic or mild symptoms, including cough or chest pain.
  • Progressive stage: Rapid increase in mass size leading to compression symptoms over weeks to months, including respiratory distress and SVCS.
  • Advanced stage: Systemic symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) and possible spread to other lymph nodes or organs if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Cough - Common due to tracheal compression by the mediastinal mass.
  • Chest pain - From compression of structures in the thorax, often dull and non-specific.
  • Dyspnoea - Resulting from tracheal or bronchial compression; worsens as the tumour enlarges.
  • Facial swelling - Due to SVCS, where blood flow from the head is impeded, causing venous congestion.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) - Caused by cytokine release and metabolic demand of the growing tumour, indicating B symptoms of lymphoma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Swollen neck or face - From SVCS due to mediastinal mass obstructing venous return.
  • Engorged neck veins - Observed in SVCS as venous flow is impeded.
  • Reduced breath sounds - May indicate lung compression or pleural effusion from tumour growth.
  • Pallor and fatigue - If the tumour’s metabolic demand leads to anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea - Indicates possible airway compression, an emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • Rapid onset facial/neck swelling - Suggestive of SVCS, which may lead to cerebral oedema or respiratory failure.
  • Weight loss >10% body weight in 6 months - A B symptom indicating advanced disease, linked to poorer prognosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic mediastinal mass - Detected incidentally on imaging, especially in early stages.
  • Unexplained fever or night sweats without other respiratory symptoms - May lead to initial misdiagnosis as an infectious or inflammatory condition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - Shows widened mediastinum or mass; initial diagnostic tool.
  • CT Chest with contrast - Defines size, extent, and relationship of the mass with other structures.
  • PET-CT - Used to assess metabolic activity and stage the disease.
  • Biopsy (mediastinoscopy or CT-guided) - Confirms diagnosis, with pathology showing lymphoid tissue and characteristic cells (e.g., Reed-Sternberg cells in Hodgkin lymphoma).
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC) may show anaemia; raised LDH levels often indicate tumour activity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 2-month history of persistent cough, progressive dyspnoea, and recent night sweats. Examination reveals facial swelling and engorged neck veins. Chest X-ray shows a widened mediastinum.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Thymoma - Lacks systemic B symptoms; often slower growth compared to lymphoma.
  • Germ cell tumour - May present with similar mass effect symptoms, but beta-hCG and AFP levels are typically elevated.
  • Bronchogenic carcinoma - More common in older patients and often associated with smoking history; may cause haemoptysis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Primary treatment - For PMBCL, R-CHOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisolone) chemotherapy regimen, followed by radiotherapy if needed.
  • Hodgkin lymphoma - ABVD (adriamycin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) protocol, with radiotherapy for residual disease.
  • Supportive care - Includes corticosteroids to reduce mass effect symptoms and airway obstruction risks.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mediastinal Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Superior vena cava syndrome - Occurs frequently due to compression, leading to venous congestion.
  • Respiratory failure - Can result from tracheal compression; life-threatening if unrelieved.
  • Disseminated disease - Risk of spreading to extranodal sites if untreated; poorer prognosis in advanced stages.
