Medication-Induced Pruritus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced pruritus is a distressing skin symptom characterised by itching as a direct result of drug therapy. Unlike primary dermatological causes of pruritus, this form is a secondary adverse reaction to systemic or topical medications. It typically presents without visible skin changes, although scratching may cause secondary lesions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Medication-induced pruritus is estimated to affect up to 5% of patients prescribed common medications such as opioids and certain antibiotics.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: In hospitalised patients, particularly in oncology and post-operative settings, the prevalence of pruritus due to medication can rise to 10-20%.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, the prevalence of drug-induced pruritus varies by drug type and region but is estimated at around 2-10% among patients taking implicated medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Pruritus arises due to a complex interplay of immune and neural mechanisms, triggered by specific drugs. Some drugs, like opioids, directly activate μ-opioid receptors, which can modulate itch perception. Others may trigger histamine release or act via serotonergic or cholinergic pathways, which indirectly stimulate itch receptors (nociceptors) in the skin.
  • For example, antibiotics may provoke an immunological response leading to mast cell degranulation and histamine release, whereas chemotherapeutic agents can cause cellular damage that activates pro-inflammatory pathways. The release of cytokines (e.g., IL-31) further activates peripheral itch receptors, creating a sensation of itching.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Onset: Medication-induced pruritus may develop within hours to a few days of initiating the causative drug, although some may present after weeks due to cumulative effects.
  • Progression: Itching may intensify with continued drug use. Stopping the drug usually leads to gradual resolution over days to weeks, though in some cases, pruritus may persist if tissue damage has occurred.
  • Resolution: Complete resolution can take from a few days to several weeks, depending on the half-life of the drug and any secondary skin changes from scratching.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Itching (Pruritus) - The primary symptom is diffuse itching, usually without rash initially. This sensation is often due to direct receptor activation or cytokine release.
  • Burning Sensation - Some patients may report a burning or tingling sensation, particularly with opioids or certain chemotherapeutics, due to neuropathic stimulation.
  • Dry Skin - While not directly caused by medication, frequent scratching can lead to xerosis, intensifying pruritus.
  • Anxiety or Restlessness - Itch may cause significant psychological distress, leading to secondary symptoms such as anxiety, particularly when persistent.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Excoriations - Secondary skin changes such as excoriations are common due to repeated scratching and indicate chronicity.
  • Erythema - Reddening of the skin may appear in affected areas due to prolonged scratching and inflammation.
  • Lichenification - Chronic scratching can lead to thickened skin, particularly in chronic cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Systemic Symptoms (e.g., Fever, Jaundice) - May suggest severe drug reaction (e.g., drug-induced liver injury) and requires urgent evaluation.
  • Widespread Rash - Can indicate a more severe allergic reaction, such as Stevens-Johnson syndrome, which is a medical emergency.
  • Swelling or Angioedema - Associated with anaphylaxis, especially in cases involving new or recent medication use.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Pruritus limited to unusual sites (e.g., palms or soles) may be missed and attributed to other conditions. Some individuals may present with localised itching or minimal scratching, masking the severity of symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May reveal eosinophilia in cases of hypersensitivity reaction.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) - Used to exclude drug-induced liver injury, which may present with pruritus.
  • Skin Patch Testing - Can help identify potential allergens for topical medications, although not commonly done for systemic drugs.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with a history of chronic pain presents with generalised pruritus and excoriations two days after initiating a new opioid analgesic, with no visible rash.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Chronic Urticaria - Differs by presence of wheals; pruritus in urticaria is more transient and resolves with antihistamines.
  • Atopic Dermatitis - Often has a childhood onset and associated with eczematous patches.
  • Scabies - Presents with intense itching, particularly at night, and often includes burrow marks.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation of Causative Drug - Where possible, this is the primary intervention.
  • Antihistamines - Non-sedating antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine) are first-line for histaminergic itching, though they are less effective for opioid-induced pruritus.
  • Opioid Receptor Antagonists - In cases of opioid-induced pruritus, low-dose naloxone or naltrexone can be used under supervision.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Pruritus"][/sc]
  • Secondary Skin Infections - Repeated scratching can introduce bacteria, leading to cellulitis.
  • Psychological Distress - Persistent pruritus may lead to anxiety, sleep disturbance, and reduced quality of life.
