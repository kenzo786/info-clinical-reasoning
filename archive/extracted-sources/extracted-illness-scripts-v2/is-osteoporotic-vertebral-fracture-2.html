Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • A fracture is a structural break in a bone resulting from trauma or pathological processes. Osteoporotic vertebral fractures (OVFs) specifically occur due to reduced bone strength, often with minimal trauma, and are characterised by deformity or collapse of the vertebral body.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men over the age of 50 in the UK will experience an osteoporotic fracture in their lifetime. OVFs account for around 40% of all osteoporotic fractures.
  • In UK general practice, an estimated 10 per 1,000 women over 50 are diagnosed annually with OVFs. Worldwide, the prevalence is increasing with an ageing population, particularly in regions with longer life expectancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Bone remodelling imbalance: In osteoporosis, increased bone resorption by osteoclasts exceeds bone formation by osteoblasts, leading to reduced bone density and structural integrity.
  • Decreased mechanical strength: Loss of trabecular microarchitecture and cortical thinning make vertebrae prone to collapse under normal axial loads.
  • Microfractures: Accumulated microdamage can coalesce into a full vertebral body fracture, often occurring with minimal or no trauma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of pain following minimal trauma or spontaneous collapse.
  • Subacute phase: Pain gradually diminishes but may persist for weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: Deformity and functional impairment, potentially with persistent pain and height loss.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Acute back pain - Sudden, severe pain often localised to the mid- or lower back due to vertebral collapse.
  • Chronic pain - Persistent or intermittent back pain secondary to deformity or nerve irritation.
  • Height loss - Progressive shortening caused by cumulative vertebral fractures leading to spine shortening.
  • Functional limitation - Difficulty bending, lifting, or prolonged sitting due to pain and spine deformity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Kyphosis - Excessive forward curvature of the spine (dowager’s hump) due to multiple collapsed vertebrae.
  • Tenderness over vertebrae - Localised tenderness over the affected level due to inflammation and microinstability.
  • Loss of spinal height - Observable shortening of stature due to vertebral compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Neurological deficits - Weakness, numbness, or bladder/bowel dysfunction indicating spinal cord or nerve root compression.
  • Night pain or systemic symptoms - Suggests malignancy or infection as underlying causes of fracture.
  • Severe or progressive deformity - Indicative of multiple fractures and potential instability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic fractures discovered incidentally on imaging.
  • Chronic back pain misattributed to musculoskeletal causes.
  • Acute abdominal pain in thoracolumbar fractures due to referred pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Plain X-ray - Demonstrates vertebral height loss, endplate deformities, or wedge-shaped vertebrae.
  • DEXA scan - Confirms osteoporosis with T-score ≤ -2.5.
  • MRI - Identifies acute fractures with bone marrow oedema and excludes malignancy or infection.
  • Blood tests - Rule out secondary causes (e.g., calcium, vitamin D, PTH levels).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman presents with sudden onset of mid-back pain after bending forward, with a history of height loss and kyphosis. Imaging confirms a T12 vertebral fracture.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Metastatic spinal lesions - Persistent pain, systemic symptoms, lytic or sclerotic lesions on imaging.
  • Spinal infection - Fever, elevated inflammatory markers, paravertebral abscess on MRI.
  • Muscle strain - Localised tenderness without vertebral deformity on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Analgesia, physiotherapy, bracing.
  • Osteoporosis treatment - Bisphosphonates, calcium/vitamin D supplementation, or denosumab.
  • Surgical intervention - Kyphoplasty or vertebroplasty for severe cases with instability or unmanageable pain.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fracture (including Osteoporotic Vertebral Fracture)"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Due to spinal deformity and altered biomechanics.
  • Spinal cord compression - Neurological deficits from severe collapse.
  • Recurrent fractures - Increased risk due to underlying osteoporosis.
