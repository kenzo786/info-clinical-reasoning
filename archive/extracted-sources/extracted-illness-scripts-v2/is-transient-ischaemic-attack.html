Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • A transient ischaemic attack (TIA) is a brief, reversible neurological deficit caused by a temporary reduction in cerebral blood flow without infarction. Symptoms resolve completely within 24 hours, typically within minutes to a few hours, and there is no evidence of acute tissue damage on imaging.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Approximately 0.2% annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: TIA accounts for around 20–25% of minor stroke admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: TIA incidence is estimated at 50 per 100,000 person-years globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Transient ischaemia occurs due to a brief interruption of blood flow to the brain, often from emboli, thrombi, or arterial stenosis.
  • Restoration of blood flow occurs spontaneously or through collateral circulation, preventing permanent infarction.
  • Key mechanisms include atherosclerosis, cardioembolism (e.g., atrial fibrillation), and small vessel disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Abrupt onset of focal neurological symptoms.
  • Resolution: Symptoms typically resolve within minutes to a few hours (always <24 hours).
  • Recurrent phase: Increased risk of subsequent TIA or stroke within days to weeks if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Unilateral weakness or numbness - Often in the face, arm, or leg, due to ischaemia in the motor or sensory cortex.
  • Speech difficulties - Dysphasia or slurred speech if Broca’s or Wernicke’s area is affected.
  • Visual disturbance - Monocular vision loss or hemianopia caused by ischaemia in the retinal or occipital artery.
  • Dizziness or ataxia - Results from posterior circulation ischaemia affecting the cerebellum or brainstem.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • No persistent neurological deficits - By definition, symptoms resolve completely within 24 hours.
  • Hypertension - Common during the acute phase as a compensatory mechanism.
  • Carotid bruit - Suggestive of underlying carotid artery stenosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Prolonged symptoms (>1 hour) - May indicate evolving stroke.
  • Severe headache or neck pain - Suggests dissection or haemorrhage.
  • Recurrent episodes - High risk of impending stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Amnesia (transient global amnesia) - Rarely, isolated memory loss occurs without other deficits.
  • Non-specific dizziness - Can lead to underdiagnosis in posterior circulation TIAs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • CT/MRI brain - Normal findings; MRI DWI may show no infarction.
  • Carotid Doppler - Identifies stenosis (>50%).
  • ECG - Detects atrial fibrillation.
  • Blood tests - Includes FBC, U&E, cholesterol, and glucose for underlying risks.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with sudden onset left arm weakness and slurred speech lasting 15 minutes, resolving completely by presentation. No history of head trauma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic stroke - Symptoms persist >24 hours or evidence of infarction on imaging.
  • Seizure with postictal weakness (Todd's paresis) - History of seizure activity.
  • Migraine with aura - Gradual onset symptoms and often accompanied by headache.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Antiplatelet therapy - Aspirin 300 mg immediately.
  • Carotid endarterectomy - Indicated for symptomatic carotid stenosis >70%.
  • Risk factor modification - Blood pressure control, statins, and anticoagulation if atrial fibrillation present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic stroke - Occurs in 10–20% within 90 days without treatment.
  • Vascular dementia - Chronic cerebrovascular insufficiency increases risk.
