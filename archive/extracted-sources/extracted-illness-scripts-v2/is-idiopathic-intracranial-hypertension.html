Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH) is a disorder characterised by elevated intracranial pressure (ICP) without an identifiable cause such as a mass lesion or hydrocephalus. It predominantly affects overweight women of childbearing age and presents with symptoms of increased ICP, most commonly headache, visual disturbances, and papilloedema. The condition can lead to permanent vision loss if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: approximately 1–2 per 100,000 people.
  • Prevalence in the UK hospital population: up to 20 per 100,000 in tertiary care centres due to referral bias.
  • Worldwide prevalence: higher in populations with increased obesity rates, particularly in developed countries; around 1–4 per 100,000 overall.
  • IIH is most commonly diagnosed in women aged 20–40 years, especially those with a BMI > 30.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • IIH results from impaired cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) absorption or increased resistance to CSF outflow, leading to elevated ICP.
  • Increased ICP compresses the optic nerves, causing optic disc swelling (papilloedema) and subsequent visual disturbances.
  • Obesity, hormonal factors (e.g., oestrogen), and metabolic changes are implicated in the pathogenesis, but the exact mechanism remains unclear.
  • Other proposed mechanisms include increased venous sinus pressure and alterations in CSF dynamics.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • IIH often develops insidiously over weeks to months, with symptoms gradually worsening over time.
  • Initial symptoms typically include headaches and visual disturbances, which can progress to more severe visual impairment if untreated.
  • Chronic cases may exhibit fluctuating symptoms, with periods of exacerbation and remission.
  • Without treatment, permanent visual loss may occur within months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Headache - Typically the most common symptom, often described as a pulsatile or pressure-like pain, worse in the morning and exacerbated by Valsalva manoeuvres (e.g., coughing or bending). This is due to increased ICP compressing pain-sensitive structures such as the dura.
  • Transient Visual Obscurations - Brief episodes of visual blurring, often bilateral and lasting seconds, caused by increased ICP affecting the optic nerve's function.
  • Pulsatile Tinnitus - A "whooshing" sound in time with the heartbeat, due to turbulence in the venous system caused by increased ICP.
  • Nausea and Vomiting - These symptoms arise from direct pressure on the vomiting centre (area postrema) in the brainstem.
  • Photophobia and Neck Pain - Both may occur due to diffuse increased ICP and meningeal irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Papilloedema - Swelling of the optic discs seen on fundoscopic exam, caused by raised ICP obstructing axoplasmic flow in the optic nerve.
  • Visual Field Defects - Most commonly an enlarged blind spot or peripheral vision loss due to optic nerve dysfunction from prolonged elevated ICP.
  • Cranial Nerve VI Palsy - Results in horizontal diplopia (double vision) and is caused by increased ICP compressing the abducens nerve.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Sudden Vision Loss - May indicate optic nerve atrophy and irreversible damage due to sustained elevated ICP, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe, Refractory Headache - Worsening headache despite treatment may indicate a secondary cause of raised ICP, such as venous sinus thrombosis.
  • Confusion or Altered Consciousness - Suggests an acute rise in ICP or associated complication like cerebral venous thrombosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Normal Weight Patients - While obesity is a strong risk factor, IIH can occur in patients of normal weight, making diagnosis more challenging.
  • Chronic or Subtle Symptoms - Patients may present with non-specific symptoms like mild, chronic headaches or visual disturbances, which could delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Ophthalmoscopy - Examination for papilloedema, a hallmark of IIH, showing optic disc swelling.
  • Visual Field Testing - Commonly reveals enlarged blind spots or peripheral vision loss due to optic nerve damage.
  • Brain MRI with MR Venography - Normal brain MRI with exclusion of mass lesions or venous sinus thrombosis. MR venography is essential to rule out cerebral venous sinus thrombosis.
  • Lumbar Puncture - Confirms elevated opening pressure (> 25 cmH₂O) in the absence of other abnormalities. CSF analysis is typically normal (no infection, malignancy).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old obese female presents with chronic daily headaches, transient visual obscurations, and pulsatile tinnitus. On examination, papilloedema and a left-sided cranial nerve VI palsy are noted. Brain MRI is normal, and lumbar puncture reveals elevated opening pressure with normal CSF analysis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Migraine - Unlike IIH, migraine typically presents with episodic headaches, often unilateral, associated with nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia but without papilloedema or visual field defects.
  • Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis - Can present similarly to IIH with headaches and papilloedema, but MRI venography will reveal thrombosis in the venous sinuses.
  • Brain Tumour - Raised ICP can occur, but MRI would reveal a mass lesion, differentiating it from IIH.
  • Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus - Presents with gait disturbance, dementia, and urinary incontinence, but lumbar puncture reveals normal or mildly elevated opening pressure.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Weight Loss - The first-line treatment for overweight or obese patients. A 5-10% reduction in body weight can significantly reduce ICP.
  • Acetazolamide - A carbonic anhydrase inhibitor that reduces CSF production, thereby lowering ICP. Initial dose: 250–500 mg twice daily.
  • Topiramate - An alternative to acetazolamide, also has the added benefit of weight loss.
  • Surgical Options - Reserved for refractory cases, includes CSF shunting or optic nerve sheath fenestration to prevent visual loss.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)"][/sc]
  • Permanent Vision Loss - Occurs in 10-25% of untreated cases due to chronic optic nerve damage from sustained raised ICP.
  • Chronic Daily Headache - Persistent headaches can affect quality of life even after ICP is controlled.
  • Shunt Malfunction - In patients requiring CSF shunts, complications such as infection or malfunction can occur, necessitating further surgery.
