Achilles Tendon Rupture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • An Achilles tendon rupture is a complete or partial tear of the Achilles tendon, which connects the calf muscles (gastrocnemius and soleus) to the calcaneus (heel bone). It commonly results from sudden, forceful dorsiflexion or loading of the tendon during activities such as running, jumping, or sudden directional changes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 18 per 100,000 person-years.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher in sports medicine or orthopaedic settings, particularly in active adults.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar rates globally, with higher incidence in athletes and physically active individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Overuse or acute excessive loading leads to microtears in the Achilles tendon.
  • Repetitive stress can weaken the tendon structure, particularly in hypovascular areas of the tendon.
  • When subjected to a sudden, high tensile force, the tendon fails, resulting in partial or complete rupture.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of symptoms following injury.
  • Subacute phase: Pain and functional impairment over weeks, particularly if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent weakness, difficulty with activities involving plantar flexion.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Sudden pain in the heel or calf: Described as a "pop" or sharp pain; occurs due to acute tendon rupture and associated tissue damage.
  • Inability to push off: Difficulty in plantar flexion from loss of tendon continuity.
  • Swelling and bruising: Localised inflammation and haemorrhage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Palpable gap in the tendon: Indicates physical discontinuity in the tendon fibres.
  • Positive Thompson’s test: Absence of plantar flexion when the calf is squeezed, due to a lack of tension transmission.
  • Swelling and ecchymosis: Due to inflammatory response and bleeding into the surrounding tissue.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Persistent inability to weight bear: May indicate a complete rupture or associated injury.
  • Severe swelling or erythema: Potential signs of compartment syndrome or infection if associated with trauma.
  • Neurovascular compromise: Rare but critical to identify; could signify severe associated trauma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset of weakness: Partial tears or chronic degeneration may lack acute symptoms.
  • Mild pain with functional limitations: May occur in sedentary individuals.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: Shows tendon discontinuity or hypoechoic areas; key for confirming rupture.
  • MRI: Provides detailed images of tendon integrity and associated injuries.
  • X-ray: May show soft tissue swelling or associated calcaneal avulsion fractures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old male athlete presents with acute onset of severe heel pain after a sudden jump during a basketball game, followed by inability to walk or push off the affected foot.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Calf muscle strain: Localised pain but retains ability to push off; no palpable gap.
  • Posterior tibial tendon dysfunction: Chronic presentation; affects foot alignment.
  • Ankle sprain: Lateral ligament involvement with instability, rather than Achilles-specific findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Non-surgical: Functional rehabilitation with a boot and physiotherapy for partial ruptures.
  • Surgical: Open or minimally invasive repair for complete ruptures, particularly in active individuals.
  • Post-operative care: Includes physiotherapy and gradual weight-bearing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Re-rupture: Occurs in poorly rehabilitated patients.
  • Chronic weakness: Persistent deficit in plantar flexion strength.
  • Infection: Post-surgical risk, especially with open repair.
