Pericarditis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Pericarditis is the inflammation of the pericardium, the thin sac surrounding the heart. It can be acute, subacute, or chronic and is often characterised by sharp, pleuritic chest pain, pericardial friction rub, and changes on ECG, such as widespread ST-elevation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Viral infections (e.g., coxsackievirus, influenza, HIV), autoimmune diseases (e.g., systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis), post-myocardial infarction (Dressler’s syndrome), uraemia (chronic kidney disease), malignancies (e.g., lung or breast cancer metastasis), and recent cardiac surgery increase the likelihood of pericarditis.
  • Prevention: Prevention strategies include prompt treatment of infections, optimal management of autoimmune diseases, regular monitoring of renal function in patients with chronic kidney disease, and vaccination against influenza and other common viral pathogens. Avoidance of excessive anticoagulation in at-risk individuals may also reduce haemorrhagic pericarditis risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Pericarditis occurs when the pericardial layers become inflamed, often due to an underlying infection (viral, bacterial), autoimmune disease, or trauma. The inflammation results in increased pericardial fluid (pericardial effusion) or fibrin deposition. This irritation triggers chest pain and leads to characteristic ECG changes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Acute pericarditis typically develops over hours to days, with a duration of less than 4-6 weeks. Subacute cases progress more gradually over weeks to months. Chronic pericarditis lasts longer than three months and may result in complications like constrictive pericarditis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Sharp, pleuritic chest pain – Caused by inflammation of the pericardium, exacerbated by inspiration or coughing.
  • Pain relieved by sitting forward – Leaning forward reduces pressure on the pericardium, easing discomfort.
  • Dyspnoea – Often due to associated pericardial effusion or irritation causing restricted cardiac filling.
  • Fever – Associated with infectious or inflammatory aetiologies.
  • Palpitations – Due to inflammatory irritation affecting the heart's electrical activity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Pericardial friction rub – A scratching or squeaking sound heard on auscultation due to the inflamed pericardial layers rubbing against each other.
  • Tachycardia – The body's compensatory response to pain or pericardial effusion reducing stroke volume.
  • Pulsus paradoxus – An exaggerated drop in systolic blood pressure during inspiration, seen in severe cases with tamponade.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension – May indicate cardiac tamponade, where excessive fluid in the pericardium restricts heart function.
  • Jugular venous distension – Signifies increased central venous pressure, often due to tamponade or effusion.
  • Muffled heart sounds – Can suggest pericardial effusion or impending tamponade.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Pericarditis may present atypically with minimal or no chest pain in patients who are immunocompromised or elderly, where inflammation may be less pronounced, or the clinical signs may be subtle.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • ECG – Diffuse ST-segment elevation and PR depression in acute pericarditis.
  • Chest X-ray – Normal in early cases; may show an enlarged cardiac silhouette if effusion is present.
  • Echocardiogram – Used to assess for pericardial effusion or cardiac tamponade.
  • Blood tests – Raised inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), elevated cardiac enzymes (troponin) if myocarditis is also present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with sharp, pleuritic chest pain, worse on lying down, relieved by leaning forward, associated with low-grade fever. ECG shows diffuse ST-elevation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome – Typically presents with crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw, with localised ECG changes and raised cardiac enzymes.
  • Pulmonary Embolism – Sudden onset pleuritic pain, dyspnoea, and tachycardia, with evidence of a clot on imaging and a positive D-dimer.
  • Myocarditis – Can mimic pericarditis with chest pain and elevated troponins, but ECG changes are typically focal or absent.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • NSAIDs – First-line treatment to reduce inflammation, such as ibuprofen.
  • Colchicine – Used alongside NSAIDs to prevent recurrence and reduce inflammation.
  • Glucocorticoids – Reserved for cases unresponsive to NSAIDs or in autoimmune aetiologies.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pericarditis"][/sc]
  • Cardiac Tamponade – Life-threatening compression of the heart due to pericardial effusion, leading to haemodynamic instability.
  • Constrictive Pericarditis – Chronic inflammation can cause scarring and thickening of the pericardium, restricting heart function.
