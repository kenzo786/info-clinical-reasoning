Oral Cancer Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Oral cancer refers to malignant tumours that arise in the tissues of the mouth or oropharynx, including the lips, tongue, gums, floor of the mouth, and palate. The majority (over 90%) are squamous cell carcinomas, which originate in the epithelial cells. Oral cancer often presents late, making early diagnosis essential for improving outcomes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • In the UK, oral cancer accounts for about 2% of all cancers diagnosed, with approximately 8,300 new cases annually. It is more common in males than females, with a ratio of about 2:1. Globally, oral cancer is more prevalent in regions with high tobacco and alcohol use, such as South and Southeast Asia. It is the 11th most common cancer worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • The development of oral cancer is typically related to chronic exposure to carcinogens, such as tobacco smoke, betel quid chewing, or heavy alcohol consumption, leading to DNA mutations in the epithelial cells of the oral mucosa. These mutations result in uncontrolled cell growth, invasion of local structures, and eventual metastasis to regional lymph nodes or distant organs.
  • Human papillomavirus (HPV), particularly HPV-16, is also associated with the development of oropharyngeal cancers, contributing to an increasing number of cases in younger populations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Oral cancer typically progresses over months to years. Initial lesions may appear as painless white or red patches (leukoplakia or erythroplakia). As the disease progresses, the lesion may ulcerate, become painful, and infiltrate deeper structures, leading to symptoms like dysphagia, odynophagia, or speech difficulties. Advanced stages may involve metastasis to lymph nodes or distant sites.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless ulcer or lump - The most common presentation is a persistent, non-healing ulcer or lump, often painless initially due to limited nerve involvement.
  • White or red patches (leukoplakia or erythroplakia) - These lesions are premalignant and can develop into cancer; erythroplakia has a higher risk of malignancy.
  • Oral pain - Pain develops as the tumour grows, invades surrounding tissues, and affects local nerves.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing occurs when the tumour invades deeper structures like the tongue or throat, interfering with the swallowing mechanism.
  • Speech difficulties - Tumour growth affecting the tongue, palate, or floor of the mouth can impair articulation.
  • Weight loss - Advanced disease, especially with difficulty eating, can lead to unintentional weight loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Non-healing ulcer - A common early sign, typically with raised, indurated edges.
  • Exophytic mass - A visible or palpable lump may develop, often firm and irregular.
  • Regional lymphadenopathy - Enlarged, firm, and possibly fixed cervical lymph nodes suggest metastasis.
  • Fixation of tissues - Advanced tumours may cause fixation of the tongue or other oral tissues due to infiltration of local structures.
  • Trismus - Limited mouth opening can result from tumour invasion into the muscles of mastication or surrounding tissues.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Non-healing ulcer >3 weeks - Indicates potential malignant transformation, as benign ulcers typically resolve within this time frame.
  • Unexplained persistent pain - Suggests local invasion into nerves or other structures.
  • Progressive dysphagia or odynophagia - Signifies advanced disease involving deeper tissues.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Often a marker of systemic involvement or advanced disease.
  • Enlarged cervical lymph nodes - Suggests metastatic spread, particularly if nodes are firm or fixed.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic lesions - Many early cancers, especially leukoplakia or erythroplakia, are painless and found incidentally during routine dental or medical examinations.
  • Misdiagnosed as benign ulcers - Early cancerous lesions may be mistaken for aphthous ulcers or trauma-related ulcers, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Biopsy - Histological examination of a tissue sample confirms the diagnosis of squamous cell carcinoma, with findings of dysplastic epithelial cells and invasion of underlying tissues.
  • Imaging (CT, MRI, PET scans) - Used to assess tumour extent, involvement of local structures, and distant metastasis.
  • Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) - Used for evaluating suspicious cervical lymphadenopathy to check for metastatic spread.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old male with a 3-month history of a non-healing ulcer on the lateral tongue, associated with mild oral discomfort, progressive difficulty swallowing, and a 5kg unintentional weight loss. Examination reveals a firm, irregular ulcer with ipsilateral cervical lymphadenopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Aphthous ulcers - Typically painful, heal within 1-2 weeks, and do not present with weight loss or lymphadenopathy.
  • Traumatic ulcer - Often associated with a known history of trauma (e.g., biting) and heals once the trauma is eliminated.
  • Oral candidiasis - White, curd-like patches that scrape off, often with an underlying immunosuppressive condition (e.g., diabetes, HIV).
  • Oral lichen planus - White, reticular lesions on the buccal mucosa, typically bilateral, with associated discomfort.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early-stage (I-II) - Surgery is the mainstay of treatment, with wide local excision to achieve clear margins. Radiotherapy may be used as an adjunct.
  • Advanced-stage (III-IV) - Combined modality treatment with surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy. For unresectable tumours, chemoradiotherapy is often used.
  • HPV-positive tumours - May have a better prognosis and could be more responsive to treatment.
  • Palliative care - For metastatic or terminal cases, focus on symptom management and improving quality of life.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oral Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Most commonly to the cervical lymph nodes and, less frequently, to the lungs, liver, or bones.
  • Local invasion - Can affect critical structures such as the jaw, resulting in difficulty chewing or speaking.
  • Recurrence - Even after treatment, oral cancer has a high risk of recurrence, especially within the first 2 years.
  • Dysphagia and speech impairment - Result from extensive surgical resection or tumour invasion of local tissues.
