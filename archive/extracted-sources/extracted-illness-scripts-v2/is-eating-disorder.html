Eating Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders are a group of psychological conditions characterised by abnormal eating habits that negatively affect a person's physical and mental health. The most common types include anorexia nervosa (AN), bulimia nervosa (BN), binge eating disorder (BED), and other specified feeding or eating disorders (OSFED). Core features include an excessive preoccupation with weight, body image, and food, often leading to unhealthy weight control behaviours.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 1.25 million people suffer from an eating disorder, with around 10% being anorexia nervosa, 40% bulimia nervosa, and 50% binge eating disorder or OSFED. Eating disorders are more prevalent in women (around 75%), but rates in men have increased.
  • Worldwide prevalence is estimated at 8-10% of the population. They are common in adolescents and young adults, peaking in those aged 15-25 years.
  • Hospital admission rates for eating disorders in the UK have risen significantly, with around 20,000 hospitalisations annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders develop through a complex interplay of genetic, psychological, and environmental factors.
  • Genetics: Predisposing factors include heritability, with genetic influences on appetite regulation, impulsivity, and mood.
  • Neurobiology: Dysregulation of neurotransmitters (serotonin, dopamine) affecting mood, reward pathways, and appetite.
  • Psychosocial: Perfectionism, low self-esteem, and trauma often contribute to disordered eating. Social pressures regarding thinness, as well as a distorted body image, reinforce unhealthy eating patterns.
  • Hormonal: Starvation affects the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, leading to decreased leptin, ghrelin, and thyroid hormones, which further dysregulate appetite and metabolism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders can develop insidiously over months to years. Initial phases often involve subtle changes in eating habits, followed by an increasing preoccupation with weight and food, leading to more restrictive or binge-purge behaviours. Without intervention, chronicity is common, leading to severe physical and mental health deterioration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Preoccupation with food, weight, and body image - Persistent thoughts and anxiety about weight gain, linked to societal and personal standards of thinness.
  • Restriction of food intake - Voluntary starvation in anorexia nervosa due to fear of weight gain.
  • Binge eating episodes - Recurrent episodes of overeating in bulimia nervosa and binge eating disorder, caused by poor impulse control or emotional distress.
  • Purging behaviours - Vomiting, laxative misuse, or diuretic abuse, common in bulimia nervosa, serve to relieve guilt after binge eating or to avoid weight gain.
  • Amenorrhoea or menstrual irregularities - Chronic malnutrition suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis.
  • Fatigue and weakness - Secondary to caloric restriction and electrolyte imbalances, reducing muscle strength.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Low body weight (BMI <17.5 kg/m²) - Often seen in anorexia nervosa due to severe caloric restriction.
  • Bradycardia and hypotension - Due to the body's compensatory slowing of metabolism in response to starvation.
  • Lanugo (fine body hair) - Body's attempt to insulate itself due to fat loss.
  • Parotid gland enlargement - Found in bulimia nervosa, due to recurrent vomiting.
  • Dental erosion - Chronic exposure of teeth to stomach acid in bulimics who purge via vomiting.
  • Peripheral oedema - Hypoproteinaemia and electrolyte imbalances can cause oedema in severe anorexia nervosa.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Severe electrolyte disturbances (hypokalaemia) - Can cause arrhythmias, seizures, and sudden cardiac death.
  • Prolonged QT interval - A marker of severe malnutrition and electrolyte imbalance, increasing risk of fatal arrhythmias.
  • Severe bradycardia (<40 bpm) - Reflects profound energy deficit and can lead to heart failure.
  • Refusal to eat or drink - Signals imminent risk of life-threatening complications, including refeeding syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Atypical anorexia nervosa - Normal-weight or overweight individuals who still engage in significant food restriction and have distorted body image.
  • Males with eating disorders - May present with muscle dysmorphia and an intense focus on building muscle rather than losing weight.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia or leukopenia due to malnutrition.
  • Electrolyte panel - Hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, and hypochloraemia are common in bulimia and severe anorexia nervosa.
  • ECG - May show bradycardia, prolonged QT interval, or arrhythmias.
  • Bone density scan (DEXA) - May show osteopenia or osteoporosis due to chronic malnutrition.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • An 18-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of weight loss, amenorrhoea, and restrictive eating behaviours. She has developed an intense fear of gaining weight despite being underweight and reports daily fatigue.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism - Characterised by weight loss and fatigue, but accompanied by symptoms such as tremors, heat intolerance, and palpitations, with high thyroid hormones on blood tests.
  • Major depressive disorder - May present with reduced appetite and weight loss but lacks the intense fear of weight gain or body image distortion seen in eating disorders.
  • Coeliac disease - Causes weight loss and malnutrition, but symptoms often include gastrointestinal disturbances such as diarrhoea and bloating.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Psychological therapy - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) for eating disorders is first-line, aiming to normalise eating patterns and address cognitive distortions.
  • Nutritional rehabilitation - Supervised refeeding with gradual caloric increases to avoid refeeding syndrome.
  • Pharmacotherapy - Antidepressants (SSRIs) such as fluoxetine may be used in bulimia nervosa to reduce binge-purge cycles, although they are not first-line for anorexia nervosa.
  • Medical monitoring - Regular monitoring of weight, electrolytes, and ECG to prevent complications such as electrolyte imbalance and cardiac arrhythmias.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - Low oestrogen levels and chronic malnutrition lead to decreased bone density, increasing fracture risk.
  • Cardiac complications - Electrolyte imbalances can cause arrhythmias, while prolonged malnutrition can result in heart failure.
  • Refeeding syndrome - A potentially fatal condition caused by rapid refeeding after prolonged starvation, leading to severe electrolyte shifts (especially hypophosphataemia).
  • Psychological distress - Chronic anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation are common in untreated or severe eating disorders.
