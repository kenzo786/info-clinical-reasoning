Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction is the retention of a large amount of stool in the rectum or colon, leading to partial obstruction. It can result in overflow diarrhoea, where liquid stool leaks around the impacted mass. This condition is common in older adults, especially those with decreased mobility, chronic constipation, or neurological conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Faecal impaction is more common in elderly patients, particularly those over 65 years, with a prevalence of up to 27% in institutionalised older adults.
  • Hospital prevalence: Faecal impaction is reported in approximately 7–20% of patients admitted to hospital, particularly in those with mobility issues or post-surgery.
  • Worldwide prevalence: It is a global issue in older adults, with similar prevalence rates across developed countries due to common risk factors such as immobility and poor diet.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction occurs when hard, dry stool accumulates in the rectum or colon, usually due to prolonged constipation. As stool becomes impacted, it forms a large, firm mass that is difficult to pass.
  • Over time, liquid stool from the proximal colon leaks around the impaction, resulting in overflow diarrhoea. This leakage occurs because the liquid stool bypasses the obstruction but cannot fully empty the bowel.
  • The hard stool mass may exert pressure on the rectal mucosa, potentially leading to ischaemia, ulceration, and further complications such as bowel perforation if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual accumulation of stool over days to weeks, often unnoticed, leading to chronic constipation.
  • Established phase: Progressive hardening of stool mass, development of overflow diarrhoea, and abdominal discomfort.
  • Late phase: If untreated, complications such as bowel obstruction, rectal ulceration, or perforation may develop, accompanied by systemic signs like fever and sepsis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Overflow diarrhoea – Liquid stool leaks around the faecal mass due to incomplete evacuation.
  • Constipation – The patient may report a prolonged period of no bowel movements preceding the diarrhoea, due to stool retention.
  • Abdominal discomfort – The impacted stool causes distension and pain, often cramping in nature.
  • Inability to pass stool – Patients may feel the urge to defecate but are unable to pass anything significant.
  • Nausea and vomiting – These may occur as a result of bowel obstruction and distension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal distension – Visible or palpable bloating due to gas and stool accumulation.
  • Palpable faecal mass – Digital rectal examination (DRE) may reveal a hard stool mass.
  • Reduced bowel sounds – A sign of bowel obstruction or decreased intestinal motility.
  • Tender abdomen – Diffuse or localised tenderness, especially over the left lower quadrant, where the sigmoid colon is usually located.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Fever – May indicate infection secondary to bowel perforation or ischaemic damage.
  • Severe, sharp abdominal pain – Could signify bowel perforation or ischaemia.
  • Peritonitis – Generalised abdominal tenderness with guarding and rigidity, a sign of bowel perforation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Non-specific confusion or delirium – Especially in older adults, faecal impaction may present as delirium without overt gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Urinary symptoms – A large faecal mass may compress the bladder, leading to urinary frequency, retention, or incontinence.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal X-ray – May show distended loops of bowel and faecal loading in the colon. This helps confirm the diagnosis of faecal impaction.
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE) – Directly assesses for the presence of impacted stool in the rectum.
  • Full blood count (FBC) – May reveal a raised white cell count if infection or bowel perforation is present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman presents with a 2-week history of abdominal distension, cramping pain, and overflow diarrhoea following a period of chronic constipation. On examination, a palpable faecal mass is detected on digital rectal examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer – May present with altered bowel habits and abdominal distension, but often has blood in the stool or weight loss, which is uncommon in faecal impaction.
  • Bowel obstruction – Acute onset of severe abdominal pain, vomiting, and absolute constipation, without the overflow diarrhoea seen in faecal impaction.
  • Diverticulitis – Left-sided abdominal pain with fever and possible diarrhoea, but typically with systemic signs of infection and inflammation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Manual disimpaction – For immediate relief, especially in severe cases with significant rectal faecal loading.
  • Osmotic laxatives (e.g., macrogol) – First-line treatment to soften the stool and promote bowel movement.
  • Enemas – Used when oral treatments are ineffective or not tolerated.
  • Dietary fibre and hydration – Long-term management to prevent recurrence by ensuring regular bowel movements.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Faecal Impaction (Overflow Diarrhoea)"][/sc]
  • Bowel perforation – A rare but life-threatening complication due to increased intraluminal pressure and ischaemia.
  • Rectal ulceration – Chronic pressure from the faecal mass can lead to mucosal damage and ulceration.
  • Faecal incontinence – Persistent impaction can lead to a loss of rectal tone and incontinence.
