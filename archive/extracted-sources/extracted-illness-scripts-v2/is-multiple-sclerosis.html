Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, autoimmune, inflammatory disorder affecting the central nervous system (CNS), characterised by the formation of sclerotic plaques in the brain and spinal cord. These plaques disrupt the myelin sheath covering nerve fibres, leading to a variety of neurological symptoms. MS typically follows a relapsing-remitting course but can progress into a more chronic and progressive phase.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK: 150-200 per 100,000 people.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 2.8 million people affected globally.
  • Gender: MS is more common in women than men (3:1 ratio).
  • Age: Typically diagnosed between 20-40 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • MS is characterised by immune-mediated demyelination of the CNS. T cells and B cells abnormally target myelin antigens, triggering inflammation. This leads to myelin destruction and axonal damage. Over time, astrocyte proliferation causes sclerotic plaques. The demyelination slows or blocks nerve conduction, leading to the clinical manifestations of MS.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS): Characterised by periods of acute attacks (relapses) followed by recovery (remissions). The majority of patients initially present with this form.
  • Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS): RRMS can transition to SPMS, where patients experience steady progression of symptoms with or without relapses.
  • Primary Progressive MS (PPMS): Around 10-15% of patients present with steadily worsening symptoms without relapses or remissions from the onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue: One of the most common symptoms. Caused by central nervous system inflammation and demyelination, leading to inefficient nerve conduction.
  • Visual disturbances (e.g., optic neuritis): Frequently an early symptom. Inflammation of the optic nerve leads to unilateral vision loss, pain on eye movement, and blurred vision.
  • Muscle weakness: Demyelination affecting motor pathways leads to weakness, commonly in the legs, causing walking difficulties.
  • Numbness or tingling (paraesthesia): Due to sensory pathway disruption, presenting as abnormal sensations, usually in the limbs.
  • Bladder dysfunction: Spinal cord involvement affects the bladder, leading to urgency, frequency, or retention.
  • Cognitive impairment: Memory issues and slow processing, often due to frontal and temporal lobe involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Optic disc pallor: Seen in optic neuritis, indicating past or present inflammation of the optic nerve.
  • Hyperreflexia: Due to upper motor neuron involvement, patients may demonstrate brisk reflexes.
  • Spasticity: Chronic demyelination of motor pathways leads to muscle stiffness and spasticity, especially in the legs.
  • Ataxia: Demyelination in the cerebellum or its connections results in uncoordinated movements, causing ataxia and poor balance.
  • Uhthoff’s phenomenon: Temporary worsening of neurological symptoms, particularly visual symptoms, triggered by increased body temperature.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Rapid neurological deterioration: May indicate a severe MS relapse or progressive form requiring urgent intervention.
  • Bilateral optic neuritis: While optic neuritis is common in MS, bilateral involvement could suggest other conditions, such as neuromyelitis optica (NMO).
  • Respiratory failure: Can occur in severe, advanced MS due to diaphragmatic weakness, requiring urgent management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia in younger patients: Although trigeminal neuralgia is more common in older adults, its presence in young patients could be a sign of MS.
  • Unilateral hearing loss: Rare in MS, but can occur due to involvement of the auditory pathways.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • MRI of brain and spine: Typically shows demyelinating plaques, most commonly in the periventricular areas. Lesions in the spinal cord are also common.
  • Lumbar puncture: Oligoclonal bands in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) suggest intrathecal antibody production, supporting the diagnosis of MS.
  • Evoked potentials: Measures the speed of nerve conduction in response to stimuli. Slowed conduction in visual, auditory, or somatosensory pathways supports a diagnosis of MS.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 3-week history of unilateral vision loss and pain on eye movement. She reports previous episodes of leg weakness and numbness that improved spontaneously. MRI of the brain shows periventricular white matter lesions consistent with demyelination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Neuromyelitis optica (NMO): Bilateral optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis are more characteristic of NMO.
  • Cerebrovascular disease: Stroke can present with focal neurological deficits, but typically the onset is more acute, without the relapsing-remitting pattern seen in MS.
  • Lupus or other connective tissue diseases: Can mimic MS with neurological symptoms, but typically associated with systemic signs like arthritis or rash.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Relapses: High-dose corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone) are used to shorten the duration of relapses.
  • Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs): First-line treatments include beta-interferons and glatiramer acetate. More potent agents like ocrelizumab or alemtuzumab are used in aggressive or progressive MS.
  • Symptom management: Spasticity is managed with baclofen or gabapentin. Fatigue can be addressed with energy conservation techniques and medications like amantadine.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Secondary progressive MS: Many patients with relapsing-remitting MS eventually develop progressive disability.
  • Immobility: Chronic disease can lead to severe motor impairment, contributing to deconditioning, pressure sores, and infections.
  • Cognitive impairment: Progressive MS may cause significant memory and cognitive difficulties, impacting daily functioning.
