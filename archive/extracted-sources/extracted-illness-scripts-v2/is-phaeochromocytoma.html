Phaeochromocytoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytoma is a rare neuroendocrine tumour originating from chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla. These tumours secrete excessive catecholamines (adrenaline, noradrenaline, and sometimes dopamine), leading to episodic or sustained hypertension and other systemic symptoms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • The prevalence in the UK general practice population is very low, around 2-8 cases per million annually.
  • In UK hospital populations, approximately 0.1% of hypertensive patients are found to have a phaeochromocytoma.
  • Worldwide prevalence follows similar trends, with increased incidence in certain hereditary syndromes like Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia type 2 (MEN2).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytomas arise from chromaffin cells, which are responsible for synthesising and secreting catecholamines. In normal physiology, catecholamine release is tightly regulated by the autonomic nervous system.
  • These tumours produce excess catecholamines autonomously, leading to overstimulation of adrenergic receptors. The β-adrenergic stimulation increases heart rate, contractility, and vasodilation, while α-adrenergic stimulation causes vasoconstriction, both contributing to hypertension and systemic effects.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytomas may present acutely with sudden hypertensive crises or more chronically with intermittent episodes of symptoms such as headaches and palpitations. Over time, tumour growth can lead to more consistent symptomatology, although progression rates vary between individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Hypertension (paroxysmal or sustained) - Due to excessive catecholamine secretion, leading to increased vascular resistance (α-adrenergic) and cardiac output (β-adrenergic).
  • Headaches - Caused by vasoconstriction and hypertensive surges.
  • Palpitations - Catecholamine excess increases heart rate and contractility via β1-adrenergic receptors.
  • Excessive sweating - Due to sympathetic overactivation and increased thermogenesis.
  • Anxiety or panic attacks - Catecholamine surges mimic adrenergic ‘fight or flight’ responses, resulting in acute anxiety episodes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Persistent or episodic, due to increased catecholamine-mediated vasoconstriction.
  • Tachycardia - Results from catecholamine excess acting on β1-adrenergic receptors in the heart.
  • Pallor or flushing - Alternating sympathetic vasoconstriction and secondary vasodilation from β-adrenergic effects.
  • Weight loss - Increased basal metabolic rate from prolonged adrenergic stimulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive crisis - Sudden, severe hypertension can result in end-organ damage, such as myocardial infarction, stroke, or acute renal failure.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Prolonged catecholamine excess can lead to life-threatening arrhythmias, including ventricular tachycardia and fibrillation.
  • Severe anxiety or agitation - Episodes may escalate to confusion or delirium in extreme cases of catecholamine crisis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic hypertension - Some patients may present with sustained hypertension without typical adrenergic symptoms, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Episodic gastrointestinal complaints - Nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain may be the primary symptoms due to catecholamine effects on gut motility.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Plasma free metanephrines - Elevated due to increased catecholamine metabolism (sensitivity ~97%).
  • 24-hour urinary catecholamines and metanephrines - Higher levels confirm excessive catecholamine secretion.
  • Imaging (CT or MRI) - To localise the tumour, most commonly in the adrenal glands.
  • MIBG scintigraphy - Specific imaging for detecting extra-adrenal or metastatic tumours.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with episodic headaches, palpitations, and sweating over the past 6 months. He has a history of poorly controlled hypertension and recent weight loss.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - Typically sustained hypertension without episodic symptoms or associated catecholamine effects.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Can cause palpitations and anxiety but usually includes other signs like weight loss, tremor, and heat intolerance without paroxysmal hypertension.
  • Panic disorder - May mimic episodic catecholamine surges but lacks sustained hypertension and objective catecholamine elevation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Pre-operative medical management - Alpha-blockers (e.g., phenoxybenzamine) to control hypertension, followed by beta-blockers for tachycardia if needed.
  • Surgical resection - Definitive treatment via adrenalectomy for localised tumours.
  • Follow-up - Regular monitoring of catecholamine levels post-surgery to detect recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Cardiac complications - Prolonged catecholamine excess can lead to cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, or heart failure.
  • Stroke - Hypertensive crisis may result in haemorrhagic or ischaemic stroke.
  • Chronic hypertension - Even after tumour removal, long-term vascular changes may sustain hypertension.
