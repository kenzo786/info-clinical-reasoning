Otosclerosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Otosclerosis is a primary bone disorder of the otic capsule, characterised by abnormal bone remodelling in the middle and inner ear. This process affects the stapes bone, causing it to become fixed to the oval window, which impairs sound transmission and leads to conductive hearing loss. In some cases, the cochlea can also be involved, resulting in sensorineural hearing loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, otosclerosis affects approximately 0.3-0.4% of the general population. It accounts for 5-10% of cases of hearing loss seen in general practice and is a common cause of conductive hearing loss in adults.
  • Globally, the prevalence is estimated at 0.5-1%, with higher rates among people of Caucasian descent, particularly in women.
  • The condition is rare in individuals of African or Asian descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Otosclerosis begins with abnormal bone resorption in the otic capsule, specifically around the stapes footplate and the oval window.
  • This bone resorption is followed by disorganised bone remodelling, with replacement by sclerotic bone. This process leads to fixation of the stapes, impairing its ability to transmit sound vibrations to the inner ear.
  • In cochlear otosclerosis, the process spreads to the cochlea, resulting in damage to the sensory cells and nerve pathways, causing sensorineural hearing loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Slow, progressive hearing loss that may be noticed over several months to years. Often starts in the third or fourth decade of life.
  • Progressive stage: The hearing loss worsens over time, potentially becoming bilateral. Some patients may develop tinnitus or vertigo as the disease progresses.
  • Advanced stage: Complete conductive hearing loss may occur, with possible involvement of the cochlea, leading to mixed or sensorineural hearing loss.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Conductive hearing loss - Most common symptom, often starts in one ear and may become bilateral. Caused by stapes fixation, preventing sound transmission.
  • Tinnitus - Perceived as a buzzing or ringing sound, occurs in up to 75% of patients, likely due to inner ear involvement.
  • Vertigo or imbalance - Uncommon, but may occur in advanced cases when the cochlea is affected.
  • Paracusis of Willis - Patients may hear better in noisy environments, due to conductive hearing loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Rinne test negative - Bone conduction is better than air conduction, indicating conductive hearing loss.
  • Weber test lateralises to affected ear - Indicates conductive hearing loss on the side of lateralisation.
  • Normal tympanic membrane - Otosclerosis typically does not cause visible abnormalities of the eardrum.
  • Schwartz's sign - Reddish hue visible on the promontory behind the tympanic membrane, seen in some cases due to increased vascularisation in active disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive hearing loss - May indicate cochlear otosclerosis or other underlying pathologies, such as tumours or autoimmune disorders.
  • Severe vertigo or imbalance - Could suggest involvement of the vestibular system, or an alternative diagnosis like Ménière's disease.
  • Sudden hearing loss - Uncommon in otosclerosis, but warrants urgent evaluation for potential alternative causes, such as sensorineural hearing loss from other aetiologies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Otosclerosis may present with predominantly sensorineural hearing loss, which can obscure the diagnosis, particularly in cases with cochlear involvement (cochlear otosclerosis).
  • In rare instances, it may present as unilateral hearing loss with no significant progression over time, leading to confusion with other causes of conductive loss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Audiometry - Typically reveals conductive hearing loss with an air-bone gap, especially in the lower frequencies. Sensorineural loss may be evident if the cochlea is involved.
  • CT scan of temporal bone - Can confirm the diagnosis by visualising the sclerotic changes around the stapes and otic capsule.
  • Tympanometry - Usually normal, as the tympanic membrane is not involved in otosclerosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with slowly progressive hearing loss in her right ear over the past year. She reports mild tinnitus but denies vertigo or pain. Physical examination shows a negative Rinne test and Weber lateralising to the right ear.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic otitis media - Unlike otosclerosis, this presents with a history of recurrent ear infections, perforated tympanic membrane, and possible discharge.
  • Ossicular chain discontinuity - May present similarly but usually follows trauma or infection, and tympanometry often shows abnormal findings.
  • Cholesteatoma - Typically presents with conductive hearing loss, but is associated with chronic infection, discharge, and a retracted tympanic membrane or visible mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Hearing aids - First-line treatment for mild to moderate conductive hearing loss, helping to amplify sound and improve hearing function.
  • Stapedectomy or stapedotomy - Surgical options to replace or bypass the stapes with a prosthesis, effectively improving sound transmission to the inner ear.
  • Fluoride treatment - Sometimes used to slow the progression of cochlear otosclerosis, though evidence is limited.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Sensorineural hearing loss - Occurs in advanced cases with cochlear involvement, leading to a mixed hearing loss that may be more difficult to treat.
  • Complete deafness - Rare, but may occur if the disease severely affects both cochlear and conductive pathways.
  • Vestibular symptoms - Although uncommon, involvement of the balance organs can lead to vertigo and chronic imbalance.
