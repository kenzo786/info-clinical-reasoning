Diabetic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy is a progressive nerve disorder caused by prolonged hyperglycaemia, leading to nerve damage throughout the body. It is a complication of both type 1 and type 2 diabetes and most commonly affects the peripheral nerves, presenting as distal symmetrical polyneuropathy. The condition can manifest in various forms, including sensory, motor, and autonomic neuropathies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: 16-30% of people with diabetes.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: Diabetic neuropathy affects approximately 10-20% of hospitalised diabetic patients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: 50% of people with diabetes develop some form of neuropathy during their lifetime.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Hyperglycaemia leads to the accumulation of advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) and sorbitol within nerve tissues, causing oxidative stress and microvascular damage. This results in axonal degeneration, demyelination, and impaired nerve conduction. The small nerve fibres are often affected first, leading to sensory loss, followed by damage to large fibres, contributing to motor and proprioceptive deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy typically develops gradually over months to years. In early stages, it may be asymptomatic or mild, progressing to more severe symptoms such as pain and motor deficits. Without intervention, nerve damage worsens, leading to chronic complications like foot ulcers and autonomic dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Distal numbness – Sensory loss, typically starting in the feet and hands, follows a "glove and stocking" distribution due to the early involvement of small peripheral nerves.
  • Tingling or burning sensations – These are common initial symptoms of small fibre neuropathy, caused by hyperexcitability of damaged nerves.
  • Sharp, shooting pain – Occurs due to damage to sensory fibres, often worse at night.
  • Muscle weakness – Motor nerve involvement may cause weakness, especially in the lower limbs.
  • Loss of coordination – Damage to proprioceptive fibres results in difficulty with balance and gait.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Reduced or absent ankle reflexes – Early sign of large-fibre neuropathy due to demyelination and loss of nerve conduction.
  • Decreased vibration sense – Indicates involvement of large sensory fibres, detected through tuning fork tests.
  • Impaired light touch and pain sensation – Resulting from small fibre degeneration, tested with a monofilament or pinprick.
  • Muscle atrophy – Chronic motor neuropathy can lead to wasting of intrinsic foot muscles.
  • Foot ulcers – Result from a combination of sensory loss, motor dysfunction, and poor circulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe pain – Could indicate an alternative diagnosis like vasculitis or lumbar disc herniation.
  • Foot ulcers with infection – Signifies a complication of diabetic neuropathy, leading to risk of osteomyelitis and amputation.
  • Autonomic dysfunction – Including gastroparesis, erectile dysfunction, or orthostatic hypotension, may indicate severe or widespread neuropathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Proximal muscle weakness – Seen in diabetic amyotrophy, where motor neuropathy affects larger, more proximal muscles rather than the typical distal involvement.
  • Asymmetric sensory loss – May be mistaken for a mononeuropathy or entrapment syndrome like carpal tunnel syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Monofilament test – Assesses light touch sensation, with failure indicating significant sensory neuropathy.
  • Electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction studies (NCS) – Evaluate the extent of nerve damage and differentiate between demyelination and axonal loss.
  • HbA1c – Used to assess glycaemic control, which correlates with the severity of neuropathy. UK normal value: 48 mmol/mol or below.
  • Vibration perception threshold (VPT) – A tool to detect large fibre dysfunction using a tuning fork or biothesiometer.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old male with a 10-year history of type 2 diabetes presents with 6 months of burning sensations in the feet, numbness, and occasional shooting pains, worse at night. He has noticed reduced balance and intermittent muscle cramps.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic neuropathy – Similar sensory and motor deficits, but history of heavy alcohol use and vitamin deficiency differentiates it.
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP) – More rapid progression, often with proximal weakness and elevated CSF protein levels.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency – Presents with sensory neuropathy, but often accompanied by macrocytic anaemia and glossitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Glycaemic control – Optimisation of blood glucose levels is the cornerstone of management, aiming for an HbA1c target of 48 mmol/mol or lower, as per NICE guidelines.
  • Pain management – First-line treatments include amitriptyline or duloxetine. Gabapentin or pregabalin may be considered in refractory cases.
  • Foot care – Regular foot inspections and podiatry referrals to prevent ulcers and infections.
  • Lifestyle modifications – Smoking cessation and exercise to improve overall vascular health.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Foot ulcers – Affect 15% of diabetic patients with neuropathy, often leading to infections and, in severe cases, amputations.
  • Autonomic dysfunction – Can cause arrhythmias, postural hypotension, and gastrointestinal motility issues, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Charcot joint – Progressive joint destruction due to lack of protective sensation, particularly in the feet.
