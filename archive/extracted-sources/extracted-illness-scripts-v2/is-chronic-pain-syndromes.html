Chronic Pain Syndromes
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain syndromes refer to a group of conditions characterised by pain that persists for longer than 3-6 months, extending beyond the normal healing time. It may arise from an identifiable injury, but often has no clear origin. Chronic pain is often associated with psychological, social, and functional implications and can manifest as generalised or localised pain (e.g., fibromyalgia, chronic back pain).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice population: Chronic pain affects approximately 43% of the UK adult population, with higher prevalence among females and older adults.
  • UK hospital prevalence: In UK hospital settings, chronic pain syndromes account for a significant proportion of outpatient visits, particularly in pain and rheumatology clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Chronic pain affects about 20% of adults globally, with significant variations in prevalence across regions due to lifestyle, occupational, and genetic factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Initial injury or insult can trigger peripheral sensitisation, wherein pain receptors (nociceptors) become hyperresponsive due to inflammatory mediators.
  • Central sensitisation develops over time, as repeated pain signals lead to hyperexcitability in the spinal cord and brain, resulting in amplified pain perception.
  • Changes in pain processing pathways involve altered neurotransmitter levels, receptor changes, and structural brain changes (e.g., in the thalamus, cortex) contributing to persistent pain even without clear peripheral input.
  • Psychological factors, such as anxiety and depression, can further enhance pain perception through mechanisms involving the limbic system and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis dysregulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (0-3 months): Initial injury or insult, characterised by pain related to tissue damage, often with an inflammatory component.
  • Subacute phase (3-6 months): Pain persists despite resolution of the initial injury, often due to central sensitisation.
  • Chronic phase (>6 months): Pain becomes a persistent condition, with psychosocial effects, disability, and typically no clear pathological origin.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain (aching or throbbing) - Continuous or recurrent pain, often without a clear cause, due to central sensitisation and altered pain processing.
  • Fatigue - Commonly reported due to sleep disturbances, chronic stress, and the body’s response to prolonged pain.
  • Sleep disturbances - Difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep due to pain, leading to increased fatigue and exacerbated pain perception.
  • Anxiety and depression - Psychological distress that both exacerbates and results from chronic pain, often due to neurotransmitter imbalances and social implications of the condition.
  • Cognitive difficulties ("brain fog") - Reduced concentration and memory issues, often due to stress, sleep disruption, and chronic pain impact on cognitive centres.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Tenderness at affected sites - Pain sensitivity due to hyperresponsive nociceptors, especially in fibromyalgia and localised pain syndromes.
  • Allodynia (pain from non-painful stimuli) - A characteristic of central sensitisation where even light touch can induce pain.
  • Hyperalgesia - Increased pain response to painful stimuli, indicative of central and peripheral sensitisation.
  • Reduced range of motion - Often seen in chronic back pain due to muscular guarding, stiffness, and pain avoidance behaviours.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May indicate malignancy or infection as a secondary cause of pain.
  • Night sweats or fever - Could suggest an underlying infection or systemic inflammatory condition.
  • Neurological deficits - Weakness, numbness, or incontinence may indicate nerve root compression or spinal cord involvement.
  • Severe, unremitting pain - Potential sign of a serious underlying pathology, such as malignancy or bone metastasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Localised pain without clear cause - Patients may present with pain confined to a specific area without evidence of injury, reflecting central sensitisation.
  • Pain in absence of clear physical findings - Chronic pain may lack obvious signs of tissue damage, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - Normal inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) rule out underlying inflammatory or infectious conditions.
  • MRI or CT scans - Often unremarkable but used to exclude structural abnormalities when red flags are present.
  • Quantitative sensory testing (QST) - Demonstrates central sensitisation by assessing pain thresholds, useful in fibromyalgia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • 45-year-old female with 8-month history of persistent, widespread musculoskeletal pain, associated with fatigue, sleep disturbances, and no identifiable underlying cause.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Joint pain with symmetrical inflammation and elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Osteoarthritis - Localised joint pain with joint space narrowing and osteophytes on imaging.
  • Myofascial pain syndrome - Trigger points in specific muscles rather than generalised pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Medications - Antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline, duloxetine) and anticonvulsants (e.g., pregabalin) per NICE guidelines for neuropathic pain.
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) - Recommended for addressing the psychological impact of chronic pain.
  • Physical therapy - Exercise-based approaches to improve function and reduce pain sensitisation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Pain Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Depression and anxiety - Common complications due to chronic stress, limited function, and social isolation.
  • Reduced quality of life - Ongoing pain limits daily activities, work, and social interactions.
  • Dependency on pain medications - Increased risk of opioid dependence due to ineffective pain control.
