Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • An ankle or foot bone fracture refers to a break or crack in one or more bones of the foot or ankle, including the talus, calcaneus, navicular, cuboid, metatarsals, and phalanges in the foot, or the tibia and fibula at the ankle. Fractures can range from stress fractures (tiny cracks due to overuse) to complex breaks from high-impact trauma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP settings: Approximately 1-2% of GP visits for musculoskeletal injuries involve ankle or foot fractures.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Ankle fractures account for about 10% of fractures seen in A&E, with foot fractures making up an additional 5-7%.
  • Worldwide: Ankle fractures are estimated at 107 per 100,000 person-years, with a higher prevalence in active and elderly populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Direct trauma or excessive mechanical force results in failure of the bone's structural integrity.
  • Stress fractures occur due to repetitive microtrauma, leading to cumulative damage exceeding the bone's reparative capacity.
  • Osteoporosis and other bone-weakening conditions increase fracture risk by reducing bone density.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Pain and swelling develop immediately following injury. Deformity may be apparent in displaced fractures.
  • Subacute phase: Bruising and reduced mobility persist over days to weeks.
  • Healing phase: Bone union typically occurs over 6-12 weeks depending on fracture severity and location.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Pain - Localised, often severe, pain at the site of the fracture due to periosteal irritation and nerve involvement.
  • Swelling - Caused by vascular leakage and inflammatory mediators at the site of injury.
  • Difficulty bearing weight - Results from pain and mechanical instability at the fracture site.
  • Bruising - Occurs due to subcutaneous blood vessel rupture, often extending distally due to gravity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Tenderness on palpation - Localised pain when touching the fracture site, indicating bone disruption.
  • Deformity - Visible or palpable abnormal bone alignment in displaced fractures.
  • Reduced range of motion - Due to pain and mechanical disruption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Neurovascular compromise - Numbness, pallor, or absent pulses may indicate compartment syndrome or vascular injury.
  • Open fractures - A break in the skin overlying the fracture increases the risk of infection and requires urgent surgical intervention.
  • Severe swelling or blistering - May signify compartment syndrome, requiring emergency decompression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Stress fractures - Gradual onset of pain without acute trauma, often in runners or military personnel.
  • Painless fractures - May occur in neuropathic conditions like diabetes, where sensory loss masks symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • X-ray - Reveals fracture lines, displacement, or joint involvement. Standard views include AP, lateral, and oblique.
  • CT scan - Useful for complex fractures or pre-surgical planning to assess the extent of comminution.
  • MRI - Sensitive for detecting stress fractures, especially when X-rays are inconclusive.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old male presents with acute onset of severe right ankle pain and inability to bear weight after a twisting injury during football. Examination reveals swelling, deformity, and tenderness over the lateral malleolus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Ligament sprain - Differs by the absence of bony tenderness or fracture on imaging.
  • Dislocation - Joint misalignment without fracture, confirmed on imaging.
  • Arthritis flare - Presents with joint pain and swelling but lacks trauma history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Immobilisation - Cast or boot for stable fractures.
  • Reduction - Closed or open reduction for displaced fractures.
  • Surgery - Internal fixation for unstable or complex fractures.
  • Rehabilitation - Physiotherapy to restore strength and mobility post-immobilisation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ankle or Foot Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Non-union or malunion - Improper healing due to inadequate stabilisation.
  • Osteoarthritis - Joint involvement in fractures increases long-term arthritis risk.
  • Chronic pain - Persistent discomfort from nerve or soft tissue damage.
