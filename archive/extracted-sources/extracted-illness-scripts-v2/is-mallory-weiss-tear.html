Mallory-Weiss Tear
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • A Mallory-Weiss tear is a longitudinal mucosal laceration typically located at the gastro-oesophageal junction. It is commonly associated with episodes of severe retching, vomiting, or straining that lead to a sudden increase in intra-abdominal pressure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Exact rates are unclear; however, it is relatively uncommon in primary care due to most cases presenting acutely and requiring hospitalisation.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Estimated to account for approximately 5–10% of upper gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar rates globally, with higher incidences in populations with high alcohol consumption, as alcohol is a common risk factor.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Repeated vomiting, retching, or physical strain increases intra-abdominal pressure, which is transmitted to the gastro-oesophageal junction.
  • The mucosa at this junction undergoes sudden distension and shearing forces, leading to a tear.
  • This laceration may involve submucosal vessels, resulting in bleeding. Blood loss can vary from minimal to severe, depending on the extent and depth of the tear.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: The condition presents abruptly after an episode of vomiting, retching, or other strenuous activity.
  • Resolution: With appropriate management, minor tears often heal spontaneously within a few days. Severe cases with significant bleeding may require urgent intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - The most common symptom, presenting as fresh blood or “coffee-ground” emesis due to bleeding at the tear site.
  • Epigastric pain - Typically occurs due to the tear in the gastro-oesophageal junction.
  • Retching or vomiting preceding the haematemesis - Often reported as the triggering event leading to the tear.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis on inspection - Visible blood in vomit or “coffee-ground” appearance, indicating an upper GI bleed.
  • Tachycardia - Reflects blood loss and compensatory response to maintain cardiac output.
  • Hypotension in severe cases - Indicates significant blood loss, potentially signalling shock.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Hypotension or syncope - Suggests significant blood loss and risk of haemorrhagic shock.
  • Melena or haemodynamic instability - Indicates a potential ongoing or more severe bleed.
  • Failure to respond to conservative treatment - May signal a more severe tear or need for endoscopic intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Minimal or no haematemesis - Occurs if bleeding is minor or the tear is limited to the mucosal layer without vascular involvement.
  • Isolated syncope or weakness - Can occur in older patients with severe bleeding but without overt haematemesis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Upper GI endoscopy - Identifies the location and severity of the tear; a visible mucosal laceration with or without active bleeding is typical.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show anaemia in cases of significant blood loss.
  • Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) - Elevated levels suggest upper GI bleeding due to increased absorption of nitrogenous compounds from digested blood.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with sudden haematemesis after multiple episodes of vomiting following alcohol intake. He reports epigastric pain, and examination reveals tachycardia and mild hypotension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Typically presents with melena and epigastric pain; bleeding is often slower than in Mallory-Weiss tears.
  • Oesophageal varices - Common in patients with liver cirrhosis and manifests with massive haematemesis without preceding vomiting.
  • Gastric erosions - Minor bleeding with a history of NSAID use, but without the characteristic retching or vomiting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Initial stabilisation - Includes IV fluids and blood transfusion if necessary, to manage hypotension and hypovolaemia.
  • Endoscopic haemostasis - Utilised for active bleeding; techniques may include band ligation, epinephrine injection, or thermal coagulation.
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) - Reduces acid and promotes mucosal healing post-stabilisation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Recurrent bleeding - Occurs in approximately 5-10% of cases; typically within the first 48 hours of initial presentation.
  • Anaemia - Due to blood loss; may develop if the bleeding is recurrent or significant.
  • Haemorrhagic shock - Seen in severe cases with substantial blood loss; requires urgent intervention for stabilisation.
