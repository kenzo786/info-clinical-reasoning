ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • ACE-Inhibitor induced cough is a persistent, dry cough caused by the use of angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACE-Is), a class of medications commonly used to treat hypertension, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease. It is thought to result from the accumulation of bradykinin and substance P in the respiratory tract, which are normally degraded by ACE.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: 5–20% of patients prescribed ACE-Is.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Similar to primary care prevalence; seen in patients initiated on ACE-Is post-admission.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Ranges from 5–30% depending on ethnic background and genetic predisposition (higher in East Asians).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • ACE-Is inhibit the enzyme responsible for degrading bradykinin and substance P.
  • Accumulation of bradykinin leads to increased activation of sensory nerve endings in the respiratory tract.
  • Substance P enhances airway inflammation and neurogenic reflexes, contributing to cough.
  • No significant pulmonary pathology is involved; the cough resolves upon discontinuation of the drug.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically within days to weeks of starting an ACE-I, though delayed onset up to several months has been reported.
  • Duration: Persists as long as the ACE-I is continued; resolves within 1–4 weeks after discontinuation in most cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Dry, persistent cough - Most common symptom due to bradykinin accumulation and sensory nerve irritation.
  • Tickling sensation in the throat - Caused by bradykinin and substance P stimulating upper respiratory tract nerves.
  • Worsening at night - Often exacerbated in the supine position due to increased airway sensitivity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Absence of abnormal physical findings - No wheezing, crackles, or other respiratory signs; physical exam is often unremarkable.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea or stridor - Suggests potential angioedema, a rare but serious complication of ACE-I use.
  • Progressive worsening of cough despite cessation - May indicate an alternative diagnosis, such as interstitial lung disease or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Productive cough - Rare but can occur if coexisting infection or inflammation is present.
  • Minimal cough with throat clearing - More common in older adults and may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Clinical history - Strong temporal association between ACE-I initiation and cough onset.
  • Trial discontinuation - Resolution of cough within 1–4 weeks confirms diagnosis.
  • Chest X-ray - Typically normal; used to exclude other causes of cough.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old female with a 2-week history of persistent dry cough following initiation of ramipril for hypertension, worsening at night, with no other respiratory or systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Associated with wheezing, productive cough, and smoking history.
  • Asthma - Episodic cough with wheezing, often triggered by allergens or exercise.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Cough with symptoms of heartburn or regurgitation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Discontinue ACE-I - Substitute with an angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB) such as losartan, which does not affect bradykinin levels.
  • Symptomatic relief - Use of antitussives (if necessary) during the washout period.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Angioedema - Rare but serious; results from excessive bradykinin-mediated vasodilation, leading to airway obstruction.
  • Medication non-compliance - Due to unresolved symptoms if not promptly recognised and treated.
