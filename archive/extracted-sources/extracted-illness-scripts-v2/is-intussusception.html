Intussusception
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Intussusception is a medical condition in which a part of the intestine telescopes into an adjacent segment, leading to bowel obstruction. It most commonly occurs in children, particularly between 6 months and 3 years of age. If untreated, it can lead to bowel ischemia, necrosis, and perforation, making it a surgical emergency.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • UK Prevalence (GP): Intussusception is relatively rare, with an estimated annual incidence of 1-4 per 1,000 live births.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: It is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in children between 6 months and 3 years, with around 2,000 cases annually in the UK.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Similar incidence rates are seen globally, with slight regional variations in presentation age and underlying causes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Step 1: A portion of the bowel (usually the ileum) invaginates into a neighbouring segment (often the caecum or colon).
  • Step 2: This telescoping results in obstruction of the bowel lumen, compromising blood flow.
  • Step 3: Ischemia and venous congestion occur, leading to bowel wall oedema and eventual necrosis if untreated.
  • Step 4: The reduced blood flow causes the mucosal lining to slough off, leading to bleeding and the classic "red currant jelly" stools.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin suddenly, with episodes of colicky abdominal pain lasting for a few minutes and recurring every 15-20 minutes.
  • Mid phase: As the obstruction worsens, vomiting (often bilious) develops, and the child may become lethargic between episodes of pain.
  • Late phase: If untreated, the child may develop signs of peritonitis, shock, and sepsis due to bowel necrosis or perforation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Colicky abdominal pain - The most common symptom, due to bowel obstruction and intermittent peristalsis.
  • Vomiting - Initially non-bilious, but bilious vomiting indicates complete obstruction of the bowel.
  • Lethargy - Episodes of pallor and lethargy between pain episodes are common due to shock and poor perfusion.
  • Red currant jelly stools - Stools mixed with blood and mucus are a late sign due to bowel wall ischemia and mucosal sloughing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Sausage-shaped mass in the abdomen - Often palpable in the right upper quadrant or epigastrium, representing the intussuscepted bowel.
  • Abdominal distension - Occurs as bowel obstruction worsens, leading to trapped gas and fluid.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Late sign caused by dehydration, bowel ischemia, and sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Bile-stained vomiting - Indicates complete bowel obstruction, requiring urgent medical intervention.
  • Signs of peritonitis (rigid abdomen, rebound tenderness) - Suggest bowel perforation and necrosis.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - May indicate advanced disease with ischemia, necrosis, or sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Chronic intussusception - May present with intermittent abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and failure to thrive without typical acute symptoms.
  • Presentation in older children or adults - Often due to a pathological lead point (e.g., tumour, Meckel's diverticulum) and may present with more insidious symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Abdominal ultrasound - Preferred initial investigation. A "target" or "doughnut" sign indicates a cross-section of the intussuscepted bowel.
  • Contrast enema (air or barium) - Both diagnostic and therapeutic, showing a characteristic "coiled spring" appearance and often reducing the intussusception.
  • Abdominal X-ray - May show signs of bowel obstruction (air-fluid levels) or a soft tissue mass, but less sensitive than ultrasound.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • A 9-month-old male presents with sudden onset colicky abdominal pain, bilious vomiting, and lethargy, followed by red currant jelly stools. A palpable sausage-shaped mass is felt in the right upper quadrant.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis: Presents with diarrhoea and vomiting, but lacks the colicky abdominal pain or palpable mass typical of intussusception.
  • Appendicitis: Right lower quadrant pain is common but does not cause colicky pain or red currant jelly stools.
  • Volvulus: Both conditions cause bowel obstruction and bilious vomiting, but volvulus presents with more acute abdominal pain and peritonitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Non-surgical reduction: Air or barium enema is the first-line treatment, which is both diagnostic and therapeutic in most cases.
  • Surgical intervention: Required if enema reduction is unsuccessful, if there is a pathological lead point, or if complications like perforation have occurred.
  • Supportive care: IV fluids, analgesia, and monitoring for complications such as sepsis or bowel necrosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intussusception"][/sc]
  • Bowel perforation: Occurs if ischemia leads to necrosis; requires emergency surgery and increases mortality risk.
  • Sepsis: A result of bowel necrosis and perforation, leading to bacterial translocation and systemic infection.
  • Recurrence: Up to 10% of cases may recur, typically within 48 hours of successful reduction.
