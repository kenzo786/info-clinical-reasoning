Croup
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Croup, or laryngotracheobronchitis, is a common paediatric respiratory condition characterised by inflammation of the larynx, trachea, and bronchi, usually triggered by viral infections. It primarily affects children aged 6 months to 6 years and is typified by a barking cough, stridor, and respiratory distress due to airway narrowing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • In the UK, croup accounts for approximately 5% of acute respiratory presentations in primary care for children under 6 years.
  • Hospitalisation rates are around 1–2% of these cases, with severe croup being uncommon.
  • Globally, it is most prevalent in autumn and early winter and occurs most frequently in children aged 6 months to 3 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Viral infection (commonly parainfluenza virus types 1 and 2) causes inflammation and oedema in the subglottic region.
  • Narrowing of the airway increases resistance to airflow, especially during inspiration, leading to stridor and respiratory distress.
  • Mucosal swelling and increased secretions further exacerbate airway obstruction.
  • The condition is self-limiting, with immune-mediated resolution typically occurring within a few days.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Prodromal phase: Non-specific symptoms such as rhinorrhoea and mild fever for 1–2 days.
  • Acute phase: Barking cough, stridor, and respiratory distress lasting 3–7 days.
  • Resolution: Symptoms gradually improve, with a return to normal over 1–2 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Barking cough - Caused by turbulent airflow through the narrowed subglottic airway.
  • Inspiratory stridor - Results from partial airway obstruction and increased airflow resistance during inspiration.
  • Hoarseness - Inflammation of the vocal cords contributes to altered phonation.
  • Fever - A systemic response to viral infection.
  • Nasal congestion - Common in the prodromal phase, reflecting viral involvement of the upper respiratory tract.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Inspiratory stridor - Audible, high-pitched sound due to turbulent airflow in a narrowed airway.
  • Use of accessory muscles - Indicative of increased work of breathing.
  • Suprasternal retractions - Reflects increased intrathoracic pressure during inspiration.
  • Tachypnoea - A compensatory response to hypoxaemia and increased respiratory effort.
  • Mild cyanosis - Rare in mild to moderate cases, but may signify severe airway obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Severe respiratory distress - May indicate impending respiratory failure.
  • Cyanosis - Suggests hypoxaemia and requires urgent intervention.
  • Lethargy or altered consciousness - May be due to hypercarbia or hypoxaemia.
  • Rapid progression - Could indicate bacterial tracheitis or epiglottitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Atypical age group (e.g., older child or adult) - May raise suspicion for an alternative diagnosis.
  • Recurrent or prolonged symptoms - Could suggest underlying structural abnormalities or immunodeficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on history and examination; imaging is not routinely needed.
  • Neck X-ray - "Steeple sign" may be visible in severe cases due to subglottic narrowing.
  • Pulse oximetry - May reveal hypoxaemia in severe cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • A 2-year-old boy presents with a 2-day history of fever, barking cough, and inspiratory stridor, worsened at night. Examination shows tachypnoea, mild suprasternal retractions, and stridor at rest.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Epiglottitis - Sudden onset, high fever, drooling, and absence of cough differentiate it from croup.
  • Bacterial tracheitis - More toxic appearance and purulent secretions on suctioning.
  • Foreign body aspiration - Acute onset, asymmetry on auscultation, and lack of prodrome.
  • Allergic reaction - Associated with urticaria or angio-oedema.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Mild cases - Managed with reassurance and single-dose oral dexamethasone (0.15 mg/kg).
  • Moderate to severe cases - Require nebulised adrenaline and oxygen supplementation as needed.
  • Severe cases - May necessitate admission, continuous monitoring, and intubation in extreme cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Croup"][/sc]
  • Hypoxaemia - From significant airway obstruction.
  • Bacterial tracheitis - Rare but serious secondary bacterial infection.
  • Respiratory failure - Uncommon, usually in severe or untreated cases.
