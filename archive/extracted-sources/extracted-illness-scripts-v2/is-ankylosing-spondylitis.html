Ankylosing Spondylitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is a chronic, inflammatory autoimmune disease primarily affecting the spine and sacroiliac (SI) joints, leading to pain and stiffness. Over time, it can result in fusion (ankylosis) of the vertebrae, causing a progressive loss of spinal mobility. AS is classified under spondyloarthropathies, a group of disorders associated with HLA-B27 genetic markers.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 0.1–0.2% of the general population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher in specialist rheumatology settings due to case clustering in secondary care.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies based on HLA-B27 prevalence, from 0.1–1.4% globally; more common in northern European populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Inflammatory Phase: Driven by an abnormal immune response, likely in those with the HLA-B27 gene, leading to inflammation of the entheses (sites where ligaments and tendons attach to bone).
  • Chronic Inflammation: Persistent immune activation damages bone and cartilage, stimulating osteoblasts and resulting in new bone formation.
  • Ankylosis Development: Over time, inflammation and new bone formation cause fusion of vertebrae, leading to reduced spinal flexibility.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Insidious onset in late teens to early adulthood, often with intermittent lower back pain and stiffness.
  • Progressive Stage: Persistent, progressive pain and stiffness with reduced spinal flexibility.
  • Advanced Stage: Potential for ankylosis, or spinal fusion, reducing mobility and possibly causing a "bamboo spine" appearance on imaging.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Lower back pain: Common early symptom, typically worse in the morning and improves with activity due to inflammatory enthesitis.
  • Morning stiffness: Prolonged stiffness lasting over 30 minutes, a hallmark of inflammatory back pain linked to synovial inflammation.
  • Fatigue: Often reported, related to chronic inflammation and systemic cytokine release.
  • Limited spinal flexibility: Due to fusion of vertebrae, contributing to progressive reduction in range of motion.
  • Peripheral joint pain: Sometimes present, especially in shoulders, hips, and knees from similar enthesitic processes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Reduced lumbar flexion (Schober’s test): Indicates reduced spinal flexibility due to enthesitis and early ankylosis.
  • Loss of spinal curvature (thoracic kyphosis): Progressive fusion can lead to an exaggerated thoracic curve.
  • SI joint tenderness: Reflects sacroiliac joint inflammation, common in early disease.
  • Enthesitis: Inflammation at tendon insertion sites, often in the Achilles tendon or plantar fascia.
  • Reduced chest expansion: Due to costovertebral joint involvement, limiting lung expansion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Sudden worsening of back pain: May indicate a fracture due to osteoporosis, common in advanced AS.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, numbness): Suggests spinal cord compression from fracture or ankylosis.
  • Severe fatigue and fever: Could indicate secondary infection or malignancy; systemic review warranted.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Predominant peripheral joint pain: In some cases, peripheral arthritis may overshadow back pain.
  • Asymptomatic or mild symptoms: Some patients show minimal symptoms despite advanced imaging findings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • HLA-B27 Testing: Positive in around 90% of AS patients in European populations; however, not diagnostic alone due to variability in populations.
  • Pelvic X-Ray (SI Joints): Shows sacroiliitis, with characteristic erosions and sclerosis at later stages.
  • MRI of spine and SI joints: Early inflammatory changes, including bone marrow oedema, even before X-ray abnormalities.
  • CRP/ESR: Raised inflammatory markers correlate with disease activity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with chronic lower back pain and morning stiffness lasting over 30 minutes, improved with activity over the past 3 years. He has limited lumbar flexion and positive SI joint tenderness.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Mechanical back pain: Typically worsens with activity and improves with rest, unlike AS.
  • Psoriatic arthritis: Can involve the spine and SI joints but often has associated skin and nail changes.
  • Reactive arthritis: May present with joint inflammation post-infection, often with a more acute onset.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Peripheral joint involvement predominates; rarely affects the spine directly.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • NSAIDs: First-line treatment to control pain and inflammation.
  • Biologic agents (e.g., TNF inhibitors): For patients with inadequate response to NSAIDs, following NICE guidelines.
  • Physiotherapy: Essential for maintaining spinal mobility and preventing deformity.
  • Surgical intervention: Rarely indicated, but may be required for severe spinal deformities.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ankylosing Spondylitis"][/sc]
  • Spinal fractures: Increased risk due to osteoporosis in advanced AS, potentially causing spinal cord injury.
  • Cardiovascular disease: AS-associated chronic inflammation can increase cardiovascular risk.
  • Restrictive lung disease: Due to limited chest expansion from costovertebral joint involvement.
