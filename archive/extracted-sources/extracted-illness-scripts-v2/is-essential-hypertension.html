Essential Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension, also known as primary hypertension, is a chronic condition characterised by elevated blood pressure without a known secondary cause. It is defined as a systolic blood pressure (SBP) ≥140 mmHg and/or diastolic blood pressure (DBP) ≥90 mmHg, measured on multiple occasions. It accounts for approximately 90-95% of all hypertension cases and is a major risk factor for cardiovascular diseases such as stroke, heart failure, and myocardial infarction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Around 30% of adults aged 16 and over have hypertension.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Hypertension is a frequent diagnosis among inpatients, particularly those with cardiovascular or renal conditions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: An estimated 1.28 billion adults aged 30-79 have hypertension globally, with higher rates in lower-income regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension arises due to a combination of genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors. These lead to increased peripheral vascular resistance and cardiac output, driving elevated blood pressure. Key mechanisms include:
    • Sympathetic nervous system hyperactivity: Increased sympathetic tone leads to vasoconstriction, raising systemic vascular resistance.
    • Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) dysregulation: Overactivity of the RAAS system increases sodium retention and vasoconstriction, elevating blood volume and pressure.
    • Endothelial dysfunction: Reduced production of nitric oxide and increased endothelin levels contribute to impaired vasodilation.
    • Genetic predisposition: Family history and specific gene mutations play a significant role in blood pressure regulation.
    • Obesity and insulin resistance: Both conditions stimulate RAAS activity and sympathetic tone, increasing the risk of hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Hypertension typically develops over many years. Initially, blood pressure may be only slightly elevated, but without treatment, it progressively worsens. Many patients remain asymptomatic for years, making early detection challenging. Complications such as left ventricular hypertrophy, chronic kidney disease, or stroke often signal more advanced stages.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Most individuals with essential hypertension do not experience symptoms, especially in early stages, which is why it is often termed the "silent killer."
  • Headache: More common in those with very high blood pressure. It is thought to result from increased intracranial pressure due to vasoconstriction.
  • Dizziness: May occur due to fluctuations in blood pressure, particularly during rapid position changes (e.g., standing up quickly).
  • Blurred vision: Hypertension can cause retinopathy or optic disc oedema, resulting in transient visual disturbances.
  • Palpitations: Increased sympathetic tone can lead to episodes of tachycardia or irregular heartbeats.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Elevated blood pressure: A consistent finding in essential hypertension, with systolic BP ≥140 mmHg and/or diastolic BP ≥90 mmHg.
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH): Chronic pressure overload leads to LVH, which may be detected by ECG or echocardiography.
  • Arteriovenous nicking (retinal changes): Chronic hypertension damages retinal vessels, causing characteristic changes visible on fundoscopy.
  • S4 heart sound: A sign of a stiffened left ventricle, often present in long-standing hypertension due to LVH.
  • Peripheral oedema: May result from chronic hypertension leading to heart failure, affecting venous return.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Severe headache, visual disturbances, or confusion: These may indicate hypertensive encephalopathy, a medical emergency caused by critically elevated blood pressure.
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath: Could suggest acute coronary syndrome or heart failure, complications of prolonged, untreated hypertension.
  • Severe, sudden onset of hypertension: May signal secondary causes, such as phaeochromocytoma or renal artery stenosis.
  • Proteinuria or haematuria: Indicative of hypertensive nephropathy or underlying renal pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Young adults: Hypertension in younger individuals may be overlooked or attributed to secondary causes like renal artery stenosis or coarctation of the aorta.
  • Elderly patients: May present with isolated systolic hypertension, where only systolic pressure is elevated, often linked to reduced arterial compliance.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM): Confirms diagnosis, particularly to rule out white-coat hypertension. Hypertension is diagnosed if the mean 24-hour BP is ≥130/80 mmHg.
  • ECG: May show left ventricular hypertrophy or evidence of previous myocardial infarction.
  • Serum creatinine and eGFR: Assesses kidney function. Elevated creatinine or reduced eGFR suggests hypertensive nephropathy.
  • Urinalysis: Looks for proteinuria or haematuria, indicative of renal damage.
  • Lipid profile and fasting glucose: Assesses cardiovascular risk and identifies metabolic syndrome components, which often coexist with hypertension.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a routine BP check showing consistently elevated readings over 6 months, with a blood pressure of 160/95 mmHg, and no significant symptoms. He has a family history of hypertension and reports occasional headaches but no other symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Secondary hypertension: Caused by conditions such as renal artery stenosis or hyperaldosteronism. Differentiated by rapid onset, severe BP, and signs of underlying causes (e.g., abnormal kidney function).
  • White-coat hypertension: Blood pressure elevated in clinical settings but normal outside. Confirmed by ABPM or home BP monitoring.
  • Phaeochromocytoma: Episodic hypertension with palpitations, headaches, and sweating. Confirmed by elevated catecholamines.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications: These include dietary changes (reducing salt intake), increased physical activity, weight loss, smoking cessation, and reducing alcohol consumption.
  • First-line pharmacological treatment (NICE guidelines): ACE inhibitors (e.g., ramipril) for patients under 55, or calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine) for patients over 55 or of African/Caribbean descent.
  • Second-line treatments: Addition of thiazide-like diuretics (e.g., indapamide) or beta-blockers if necessary.
  • Monitoring: Regular blood pressure checks and management of cardiovascular risk factors (e.g., lipids, glucose).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Essential Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction: Due to increased cardiac workload and coronary artery disease.
  • Stroke: Caused by long-standing high blood pressure leading to cerebrovascular damage.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Due to hypertensive damage to renal vasculature, leading to impaired kidney function.
  • Heart failure: Long-term hypertension can result in left ventricular hypertrophy and eventual heart failure.
  • Aortic dissection: High blood pressure is a major risk factor for dissection of the aorta.
