Ulnar Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ulnar neuropathy is a peripheral nerve disorder caused by compression or injury to the ulnar nerve, most commonly at the elbow (cubital tunnel syndrome) or less frequently at the wrist (Guyon's canal syndrome). It results in sensory and motor deficits in the ulnar nerve distribution, including the fourth and fifth fingers and intrinsic hand muscles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, ulnar neuropathy is one of the most common entrapment neuropathies, second only to carpal tunnel syndrome. It affects approximately 25 per 100,000 people annually.
  • It is more prevalent in males and typically presents in the fourth to sixth decades of life.
  • Worldwide prevalence mirrors UK trends, with higher rates among populations engaged in repetitive elbow flexion or occupational vibration exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Compression or entrapment of the ulnar nerve impairs axonal transport and blood flow, causing nerve ischemia and dysfunction.
  • In cubital tunnel syndrome, repeated elbow flexion narrows the cubital tunnel, increasing pressure on the nerve.
  • In Guyon's canal syndrome, compression occurs due to repetitive wrist extension or direct trauma.
  • Prolonged compression may lead to demyelination or axonal loss, exacerbating motor and sensory deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Occurs with sudden trauma or injury.
  • Subacute onset: Gradual progression over weeks to months due to repetitive strain or postural compression.
  • Chronic: May result in permanent motor and sensory deficits if untreated or inadequately managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Paraesthesia in the ring and little fingers - Due to sensory nerve compression in the ulnar distribution.
  • Hand weakness - Affects grip strength and fine motor skills due to motor nerve involvement.
  • Elbow or wrist pain - Often a result of nerve compression exacerbated by joint movement.
  • Clumsiness - Difficulty with manual tasks due to muscle weakness and sensory deficits.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Positive Tinel's sign at the elbow - Percussion over the cubital tunnel elicits tingling due to nerve irritation.
  • Wasting of intrinsic hand muscles - Particularly hypothenar and interossei muscles due to chronic denervation.
  • Claw hand deformity - Hyperextension of the metacarpophalangeal joints and flexion of the interphalangeal joints in advanced cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent weakness - May indicate significant axonal loss requiring urgent intervention.
  • Rapidly progressive symptoms - Suggests an alternative diagnosis such as a mass lesion or severe trauma.
  • Neuropathic pain - Burning or shooting pain could indicate nerve entrapment with significant ischemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated wrist pain without sensory deficits may occur in early Guyon’s canal syndrome.
  • Diffuse upper limb pain without clear localisation may lead to misdiagnosis as cervical radiculopathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Nerve conduction studies - Reveal delayed conduction velocity at the elbow or wrist, indicating site of compression.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Identifies denervation in the ulnar-innervated muscles.
  • Ultrasound or MRI - May show nerve swelling or compression sites, particularly for surgical planning.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with 3 months of tingling in the ring and little fingers, worsened by elbow flexion, and progressive hand weakness. Examination reveals hypothenar wasting and a positive Tinel’s sign at the elbow.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy (C8/T1) - Differentiated by neck pain and dermatomal distribution.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome - Involves median nerve with sensory changes in the first three fingers.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Often bilateral and associated with systemic conditions like diabetes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Non-surgical - Activity modification, splinting in elbow extension, and physiotherapy.
  • Pharmacological - NSAIDs for pain relief.
  • Surgical - Ulnar nerve decompression or transposition if conservative measures fail or severe deficits are present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ulnar Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Permanent motor and sensory loss - Due to prolonged untreated compression.
  • Hand deformities - Such as claw hand, significantly impacting function.
  • Chronic neuropathic pain - Persistent pain even after decompression surgery.
