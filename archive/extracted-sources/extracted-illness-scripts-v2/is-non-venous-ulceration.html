Non-venous Ulceration
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Non-venous ulceration refers to chronic ulcers of the skin that are not primarily caused by venous insufficiency. These ulcers may arise due to arterial insufficiency, neuropathy, pressure damage, autoimmune conditions, or malignancy. They are characterised by prolonged failure to heal, often persisting for more than 6 weeks, and are typically painful, with variable characteristics depending on the underlying aetiology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK general practice population: ~1–2% of the adult population suffer from chronic wounds, of which a significant proportion are non-venous ulcers.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Found in 5–10% of inpatients with chronic wounds.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 1–3%, varying by region and healthcare access.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Arterial ulcers result from ischaemia due to peripheral arterial disease (PAD), leading to tissue necrosis from insufficient oxygen supply.
  • Neuropathic ulcers are caused by sensory loss, typically in diabetes, allowing repetitive trauma to go unnoticed.
  • Pressure ulcers develop when prolonged pressure impairs perfusion, leading to localised tissue necrosis.
  • Autoimmune ulcers (e.g., pyoderma gangrenosum) are driven by inflammatory responses and tissue damage.
  • Malignant ulcers occur from direct tumour invasion or metastases causing local tissue destruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Ulcer formation with progressive tissue breakdown over days to weeks.
  • Chronic Phase: Failure to heal despite standard wound care over 6 weeks, often with cycles of exacerbation and improvement.
  • Complications: Secondary infections, systemic manifestations such as sepsis, and surrounding tissue inflammation (cellulitis).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Pain - Common in arterial ulcers, exacerbated by elevation (ischaemic rest pain) due to reduced perfusion.
  • Slow Healing - A hallmark feature due to inadequate vascular supply or underlying neuropathy.
  • Drainage or Odour - Often signifies infection or necrotic tissue breakdown.
  • Numbness - Seen in neuropathic ulcers due to sensory loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Punched-Out Appearance - Typical of arterial ulcers, with well-demarcated edges.
  • Dry, Atrophic Skin - Indicative of poor perfusion in arterial ulcers.
  • Neuropathic Calluses - Surrounding neuropathic ulcers from repeated unnoticed trauma.
  • Signs of Infection - Erythema, purulent discharge, warmth, and swelling.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Rapid Enlargement - Suggestive of malignancy or severe infection.
  • Systemic Signs (Fever, Malaise) - Indicate potential sepsis or systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS).
  • Severe Pain Unresponsive to Management - May reflect critical limb ischaemia or deep infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Ulcers with indistinct margins or violaceous borders may indicate autoimmune aetiology like pyoderma gangrenosum.
  • Ulcers in unusual locations, such as the trunk, can suggest malignancy or vasculitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • ABPI (Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index) - Reduced in arterial ulcers (<0.9); normal or increased in venous or neuropathic ulcers.
  • Wound Swab and Culture - Identifies pathogenic organisms in suspected infection.
  • Skin Biopsy - Essential for suspected malignancy or autoimmune aetiology.
  • Blood Tests - FBC (infection or anaemia), HbA1c (diabetic control), CRP/ESR (inflammation).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old man with diabetes presents with a non-healing, painless ulcer on the plantar aspect of the foot for 8 weeks, associated with numbness and callused margins.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Venous Ulcer - Typically on the medial malleolus, associated with oedema and haemosiderin staining.
  • Arterial Ulcer - Painful, distal, with well-defined edges; ABPI usually <0.9.
  • Pressure Ulcer - Over bony prominences, caused by prolonged immobility.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Arterial Ulcers - Vascular referral for revascularisation, smoking cessation, optimised control of hypertension, and lipid management.
  • Neuropathic Ulcers - Offloading pressure using specialised footwear, glycaemic control, regular wound care.
  • Infected Ulcers - Antibiotics based on culture results, with debridement as necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Non-venous Ulceration"][/sc]
  • Infection - Cellulitis, osteomyelitis, and sepsis.
  • Amputation - Secondary to critical limb ischaemia or severe infection.
  • Malignant Transformation - Development of squamous cell carcinoma in chronic ulcers (Marjolin’s ulcer).
