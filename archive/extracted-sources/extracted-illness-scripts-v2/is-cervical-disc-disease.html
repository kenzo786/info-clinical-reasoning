Cervical Disc Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Cervical disc disease refers to degenerative changes affecting the intervertebral discs in the cervical spine, which can lead to disc herniation, spinal canal narrowing (stenosis), and nerve root compression. It manifests primarily as neck pain, radiculopathy, or myelopathy depending on the structures involved.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, cervical disc disease is prevalent in approximately 2% of the population, with hospital prevalence higher due to more severe cases presenting for surgery.
  • Worldwide, degenerative cervical spine conditions affect 10% of the population, more common in older adults (age > 50) and more frequently in men.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Degenerative changes start with disc desiccation, where the nucleus pulposus loses water content, leading to reduced disc height and increased pressure on the annulus fibrosus. Microtears in the annulus can lead to herniation of disc material.
  • As the disc degenerates, osteophyte formation (bone spurs) occurs around the vertebral bodies. These bony changes can narrow the spinal canal (stenosis) or compress adjacent nerve roots (radiculopathy).
  • Chronic compression of the spinal cord may lead to cervical myelopathy, causing motor and sensory deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • The condition typically progresses over years with an insidious onset. Initial symptoms may be mild and intermittent neck pain, which worsens over time as nerve or spinal cord involvement increases.
  • Acute exacerbations can occur with disc herniation or trauma, leading to sudden onset radiculopathy or myelopathy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Neck pain - Caused by inflammation or mechanical pressure on cervical discs and surrounding structures due to degeneration.
  • Radicular pain - Sharp, shooting pain radiating down the arm (cervical radiculopathy) due to nerve root compression.
  • Numbness/paraesthesia - Resulting from nerve root irritation or compression, leading to sensory disturbance in the distribution of the affected nerve root.
  • Weakness - Muscle weakness in the arm or hand, often due to chronic nerve compression affecting motor function.
  • Gait instability - In cases of cervical myelopathy, spinal cord compression can cause lower extremity weakness and difficulty walking.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Reduced cervical range of motion - Stiffness due to degeneration and pain from disc involvement.
  • Positive Spurling's test - Neck extension and lateral bending may reproduce radicular symptoms, suggesting nerve root compression.
  • Weakness in upper limb muscles - Correlates with nerve root compression (e.g., C5-C6 disc herniation may cause deltoid weakness).
  • Hyperreflexia - Increased reflexes in the legs due to upper motor neuron involvement in cervical myelopathy.
  • Hoffman’s sign - A positive test indicates spinal cord involvement in myelopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Progressive limb weakness - May indicate severe nerve compression or cord damage (cervical myelopathy), requiring urgent intervention to prevent permanent disability.
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction - Can suggest severe spinal cord compression, an emergency (cauda equina syndrome in lumbar spine but similarly critical in cervical myelopathy).
  • Unrelenting pain, especially at night - Could indicate malignancy, infection, or fracture, necessitating further urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases - Some individuals may have cervical disc degeneration without any symptoms, discovered incidentally on imaging.
  • Isolated shoulder pain - May be mistaken for a shoulder pathology but is caused by referred pain from cervical nerve root compression.
  • Chronic headache - Cervicogenic headaches can be caused by degenerative changes in the upper cervical spine.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Plain radiographs (X-ray) - Can show disc space narrowing, osteophyte formation, and vertebral alignment abnormalities.
  • MRI - Gold standard for visualising soft tissue structures including disc herniation, nerve root compression, and spinal cord involvement.
  • CT myelography - Used if MRI is contraindicated; can identify spinal canal narrowing and nerve impingement.
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG) - Helpful to assess the extent of nerve root compression and differentiate from peripheral nerve disorders.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old man presents with 3 months of neck pain radiating to the left arm, associated with paraesthesia in the thumb and index finger, and worsening arm weakness. Symptoms worsen with neck movement and are partially relieved with rest.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Shoulder impingement syndrome - Pain is localised to the shoulder and does not follow a dermatomal pattern or involve paraesthesia.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome - Compression of the median nerve causes hand symptoms but not arm or neck pain.
  • Multiple sclerosis - May present with similar neurological findings but is usually associated with other systemic signs and has a different time course.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Conservative treatment - Includes physiotherapy, analgesia (NSAIDs, paracetamol), and activity modification.
  • Steroid injections - For severe radiculopathy unresponsive to conservative management.
  • Surgical intervention - Reserved for cases of significant myelopathy, severe radiculopathy, or failure of conservative treatments. Procedures include anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Can develop due to ongoing nerve compression or central sensitisation.
  • Permanent neurological deficits - Occur in cases of untreated or severe myelopathy, leading to long-term disability.
