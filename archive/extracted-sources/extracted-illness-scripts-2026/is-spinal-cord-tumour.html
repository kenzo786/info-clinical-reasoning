Spinal Cord Tumour
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • A spinal cord tumour is an abnormal mass of tissue within or surrounding the spinal cord. It can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous), and classified based on its location as either intramedullary (within the spinal cord), intradural-extramedullary (inside the dura but outside the cord), or extradural (outside the dura, often involving vertebrae). These tumours can compress neural structures, leading to neurological deficits.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare, accounting for less than 1% of tumours affecting the central nervous system.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Approximately 1–2 cases per 100,000 people annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: The overall prevalence of primary spinal cord tumours is about 3–10 per 100,000 people. Extradural tumours (often metastases) are more common than primary tumours.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of spinal cord tumours depends on their location. Intramedullary tumours (e.g., ependymomas, astrocytomas) arise from glial cells, leading to direct cord compression and destruction of neural tissue. Intradural-extramedullary tumours (e.g., meningiomas, schwannomas) compress the cord from outside, causing progressive neurological impairment. Extradural tumours are often metastatic, spreading from primary cancers such as breast, prostate, or lung. Tumour growth results in spinal cord compression, disrupting motor, sensory, and autonomic pathways, causing pain, weakness, and sensory disturbances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • The time course varies depending on the tumour type and location. Benign tumours (e.g., meningiomas) may grow slowly over years, causing gradual symptoms. Malignant tumours, including metastatic lesions, can progress rapidly over weeks to months, leading to severe neurological deficits. The time course can be divided into phases of mild discomfort, followed by progressive neurological decline, and eventually severe motor and sensory impairment if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Back Pain - Often the first symptom, caused by tumour invasion or compression of surrounding tissues, including vertebral structures and nerve roots.
  • Weakness - Motor weakness due to compression of descending motor pathways, particularly in corticospinal tracts.
  • Sensory Loss - Numbness, tingling, or paraesthesia due to disruption of ascending sensory pathways (e.g., dorsal columns).
  • Bladder/Bowel Dysfunction - Autonomic pathway disruption, particularly in cases of conus medullaris or cauda equina involvement.
  • Lhermitte’s Sign - Electrical sensation down the spine with neck flexion, indicative of spinal cord compression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Motor Weakness - Loss of muscle strength, often in a segmental pattern corresponding to the level of cord compression.
  • Sensory Level - A defined area where sensory loss begins, corresponding to the level of spinal cord involvement.
  • Hyperreflexia - Increased reflexes due to upper motor neuron involvement in the corticospinal tract.
  • Spasticity - Increased muscle tone resulting from disruption of descending motor pathways.
  • Ataxia - Impaired coordination, particularly with intramedullary lesions affecting proprioceptive pathways.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Rapidly Progressive Weakness - Indicates severe spinal cord compression requiring urgent intervention to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Bladder/Bowel Incontinence - Suggests involvement of autonomic pathways, often seen in cauda equina syndrome, requiring urgent decompression.
  • Bilateral Symptoms - Neurological deficits on both sides suggest central spinal cord involvement, often seen with larger or more aggressive tumours.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Isolated Pain Without Neurological Deficits - Some patients may present with isolated back or radicular pain for extended periods before neurological deficits emerge.
  • Non-Specific Systemic Symptoms - Malaise or unintentional weight loss can occur in cases of malignant tumours or metastases, without overt neurological symptoms initially.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • MRI Spine with Contrast - The gold standard for diagnosis, revealing the exact location, size, and type of tumour. Tumours typically enhance with contrast due to disrupted blood-brain barriers.
  • CT Myelography - Used if MRI is contraindicated. It involves contrast injection into the spinal canal, highlighting blockages caused by tumours.
  • CSF Analysis - In cases of intramedullary tumours, lumbar puncture may show elevated protein without pleocytosis (albuminocytologic dissociation), due to disruption of the blood-brain barrier.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of progressive back pain, followed by weakness and numbness in her legs. She now reports difficulty walking and bladder incontinence. MRI reveals an intradural-extramedullary tumour compressing the thoracic spinal cord.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Degenerative Disc Disease - Often presents with back pain and radiculopathy but lacks the progressive neurological deficits seen in tumours. MRI shows degenerative changes without mass lesions.
  • Multiple Sclerosis - Causes demyelination of the spinal cord, but typically has a relapsing-remitting course, with lesions seen on MRI without a distinct mass.
  • Spinal Abscess - Presents with fever and back pain, and MRI shows a ring-enhancing lesion, typically with systemic signs of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Surgery - For most tumours, particularly intradural-extramedullary and intramedullary tumours, surgical resection is the treatment of choice. Urgent decompression may be required for significant neurological deficits.
  • Radiotherapy - Often used for malignant or metastatic tumours, or if surgery is not possible. Stereotactic radiotherapy can be used for small tumours.
  • Chemotherapy - Indicated for certain types of malignant tumours (e.g., lymphoma or metastatic cancers), often in combination with radiotherapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Permanent Neurological Deficits - Delayed diagnosis and treatment can lead to irreversible damage, including paraplegia or quadriplegia.
  • Spinal Instability - In cases of extradural tumours involving vertebrae, surgical intervention can lead to instability, requiring spinal fusion.
  • Recurrence - Some tumours, particularly malignant or incompletely resected ones, have a high risk of recurrence.
