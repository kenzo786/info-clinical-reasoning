COPD (Severe)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progressive, inflammatory lung disease characterised by irreversible airflow limitation. It results from chronic inflammation caused by exposure to irritants like cigarette smoke or pollution, leading to structural changes in the lungs, airways narrowing, and destruction of lung tissue (emphysema) or increased mucus production (chronic bronchitis).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: 2% of the adult population (estimated 1.2 million people).
  • UK hospital prevalence: 10-20% of respiratory admissions involve COPD exacerbations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects around 10% of people aged 40 and over globally, with 3 million annual deaths attributed to COPD.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to irritants leads to inflammation of the airways and alveoli.
  • In emphysema, protease-antiprotease imbalance results in alveolar destruction, reducing gas exchange surface area and causing hyperinflation.
  • Chronic bronchitis is marked by goblet cell hyperplasia and excessive mucus production, obstructing airways and impairing mucociliary clearance.
  • Both conditions result in airflow limitation, air trapping, and a mismatch between ventilation and perfusion, causing hypoxaemia and hypercapnia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Often asymptomatic or mild dyspnoea.
  • Progression: Worsening dyspnoea, chronic cough, increased sputum production over years.
  • Late phase: Frequent exacerbations, severe airflow obstruction, respiratory failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (breathlessness) - Most common presenting symptom. Occurs due to airflow limitation and air trapping, leading to increased effort of breathing.
  • Chronic cough - Caused by airway irritation and mucus hypersecretion, especially in chronic bronchitis.
  • Sputum production - Increased goblet cells and impaired clearance cause excess mucus, often worse in the morning.
  • Wheezing - Caused by turbulent airflow through narrowed airways.
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced oxygenation and increased effort in breathing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Barrel chest - Hyperinflation causes the chest to take on a rounded shape.
  • Use of accessory muscles - Patients rely on neck and shoulder muscles to assist with breathing due to diaphragm inefficiency.
  • Pursed-lip breathing - A compensatory mechanism to maintain airway pressure and improve oxygenation.
  • Cyanosis - Bluish skin or lips due to hypoxaemia, especially in advanced disease.
  • Decreased breath sounds - Due to hyperinflation and reduced airflow.
  • Wheezes or crackles - Due to airflow through narrowed or mucus-filled airways.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Increased dyspnoea - May indicate an acute exacerbation or impending respiratory failure.
  • Altered mental state - Suggestive of hypercapnic respiratory failure.
  • Peripheral oedema - May signify cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure).
  • Haemoptysis - Could indicate lung cancer or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue - Can be an early or under-recognised sign in milder disease.
  • No cough or sputum production - Emphysema-dominant COPD may lack these symptoms but still present with dyspnoea.
  • Progressive dyspnoea without significant smoking history - Consider alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Spirometry - FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.7 confirms obstructive lung disease. Severity is based on % predicted FEV1.
  • Chest X-ray - Hyperinflated lungs, flattened diaphragm, increased retrosternal airspace, and possible bullae in severe emphysema.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Hypoxaemia (low PaO2), hypercapnia (elevated PaCO2) in advanced disease or exacerbations.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show secondary polycythaemia due to chronic hypoxia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents with progressively worsening dyspnoea, productive cough, and frequent exacerbations over the last 5 years. He reports increasing fatigue and occasional cyanosis, especially during exertion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Asthma - Differs from COPD with its episodic nature, reversible airflow obstruction, and younger age of onset.
  • Heart failure - May present with dyspnoea and cough, but with additional features like bilateral pitting oedema and raised JVP.
  • Bronchiectasis - Chronic cough with copious sputum production, often associated with recurrent infections; may show bronchial dilation on imaging.
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD) - Progressive dyspnoea, often with dry cough; spirometry shows restrictive pattern (reduced FEV1 and FVC).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Smoking cessation - Key intervention to slow disease progression.
  • Bronchodilators - Long-acting beta-agonists (LABAs) and antimuscarinics (LAMA) improve airflow and symptoms.
  • Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) - Used in patients with frequent exacerbations to reduce inflammation.
  • Oxygen therapy - For those with chronic hypoxaemia (PaO2 < 7.3 kPa).
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation - Exercise training and education to improve quality of life.
  • Management of exacerbations - Short courses of oral steroids (prednisolone) and antibiotics when indicated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Acute exacerbations - Frequent in advanced disease, often triggered by infections.
  • Cor pulmonale - Right heart failure due to chronic pulmonary hypertension.
  • Pneumothorax - More common in severe emphysema due to ruptured bullae.
  • Respiratory failure - Hypercapnic or hypoxaemic respiratory failure in advanced stages.
