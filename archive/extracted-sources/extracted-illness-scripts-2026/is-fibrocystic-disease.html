Fibrocystic Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Fibrocystic disease, also known as fibrocystic breast changes, is a benign condition characterised by non-cancerous changes in breast tissue. It often presents as lumpy or rope-like breast tissue and is primarily associated with cyclic mastalgia (breast pain), breast lumpiness, and tenderness, particularly in the upper outer quadrants of the breasts. The changes are typically hormone-related, fluctuating with the menstrual cycle, and often stabilise after menopause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Approximately 50–60% of women experience fibrocystic changes, with many cases remaining asymptomatic.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Fibrocystic changes are one of the most common reasons for referral to breast clinics.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Similar prevalence rates globally, affecting roughly 60% of women, especially those aged 30–50 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Fibrocystic disease involves hormonally-driven changes in breast tissue, primarily due to fluctuating oestrogen and progesterone levels during the menstrual cycle. Cyclic hormone variation stimulates proliferation of the ductal and stromal components of the breast, leading to formation of cysts, fibrosis, and ductal hyperplasia. Over time, repetitive hormonal stimulation results in benign fibrous tissue and cyst formation, which can contribute to pain, lumpiness, and tenderness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Commonly starts in reproductive years and is linked to menstrual cycles.
  • Progression: Symptoms may intensify with each cycle, often peaking just before menstruation.
  • Stabilisation/Resolution: Tends to resolve or significantly reduce post-menopause when hormone levels decrease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Breast Pain (Mastalgia) - Cyclic and often worsens before menstruation due to oestrogen-related ductal stimulation and fluid accumulation within cysts.
  • Breast Lumpiness - Fluctuating, rope-like texture, particularly in the upper outer breast quadrants; related to fibrosis and cystic changes.
  • Breast Tenderness - Localised or generalised pain due to increased fibrous tissue and cyst formation, which stretches the surrounding tissue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Palpable Lumps - Soft to firm and mobile lumps, reflecting cystic and fibrotic changes; often disappear post-menstruation.
  • Breast Tenderness on Palpation - General or localised tenderness, typically correlating with the areas of fibrocystic change.
  • Nodularity - Bilateral nodular texture, especially in the upper outer quadrants, due to the localised fibrosis and cyst formation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Unilateral Persistent Lump - May indicate a malignancy; requires further assessment if it does not fluctuate with the menstrual cycle.
  • Bloody or Serous Nipple Discharge - Uncommon in fibrocystic disease, potentially indicative of intraductal papilloma or malignancy.
  • Skin Retraction or Dimpling - Suggests malignancy due to tissue infiltration; fibrocystic changes do not usually cause retraction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Non-Cyclic Pain - Occasional presentation that might be mistaken for non-hormonal causes; however, it can still be related to fibrocystic changes.
  • Unilateral Symptoms - While fibrocystic changes are typically bilateral, unilateral presentation can occur, which may lead to suspicion of malignancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - Useful for distinguishing cystic from solid masses, often showing fluid-filled cysts characteristic of fibrocystic disease.
  • Mammogram - Can reveal dense breast tissue and cystic structures, but dense breast tissue may limit visualisation.
  • Fine-Needle Aspiration - Performed if a cyst is identified, yielding non-bloody fluid in benign cases, which can relieve symptoms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with bilateral, cyclic breast pain and lumpiness that intensifies pre-menstrually, with symptom relief post-menstruation. Examination reveals soft, mobile nodules in the upper outer quadrants.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Breast Cancer - Typically presents as a hard, immobile, painless lump with possible skin changes, unlike the soft, mobile lumps of fibrocystic disease.
  • Fibroadenoma - Usually a solitary, rubbery, mobile lump in younger women, lacks the cyclic pain and bilateral nodularity of fibrocystic changes.
  • Mastitis - Presents with erythema, warmth, and tenderness, often associated with lactation; fibrocystic disease does not cause inflammatory signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Supportive Bras - Provides relief by reducing breast movement and strain.
  • Analgesics (e.g., Paracetamol, NSAIDs) - Used to manage pain, especially during the premenstrual phase.
  • Hormonal Therapy - Considered for severe cases; options may include oral contraceptives to stabilise hormonal fluctuations.
  • Fine-Needle Aspiration - Drains painful cysts when present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Cyst Rupture - Rare but can cause localised inflammation, which may mimic infection.
  • Psychological Distress - Ongoing pain and fear of cancer may cause anxiety, especially in symptomatic women requiring recurrent imaging.
  • Prognosis - Generally favourable; symptoms typically decrease after menopause, with no increased cancer risk associated with fibrocystic disease alone.
