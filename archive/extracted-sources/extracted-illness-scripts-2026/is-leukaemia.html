Leukaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Leukaemia is a malignant disorder of the blood and bone marrow characterised by an abnormal proliferation of immature white blood cells (blasts). These cells accumulate in the bone marrow, disrupting the production of normal blood cells. Leukaemia is classified into acute or chronic forms and further subdivided based on the lineage of the affected cells, such as lymphoid (acute lymphoblastic leukaemia, ALL) or myeloid (acute myeloid leukaemia, AML).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, leukaemia accounts for approximately 9% of all childhood cancers. Among adults, it is more prevalent in those aged 60 and older. The incidence is about 9-10 per 100,000 annually in the UK.
  • ALL is most common in children, while AML and chronic forms (CML and CLL) are more common in older adults.
  • Worldwide, the annual incidence of leukaemia is around 2.3 million, with significant regional variations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Leukaemia arises from genetic mutations in haematopoietic stem cells, causing a block in normal differentiation and uncontrolled proliferation of immature cells (blasts). These blasts replace normal bone marrow elements, leading to cytopenias (anaemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia).
  • In acute leukaemias, the rapid accumulation of blasts results in symptoms related to bone marrow failure and infiltration of tissues. Chronic leukaemias progress more slowly, with an initial phase of excessive production of mature but dysfunctional cells.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute leukaemia presents rapidly over days to weeks with symptoms related to bone marrow failure and organ infiltration.
  • Chronic leukaemia progresses slowly, often over years, with an initial asymptomatic phase. Symptoms emerge as the disease accelerates or transforms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Caused by anaemia due to reduced red blood cell production as the bone marrow is replaced by leukaemic blasts.
  • Fever - Associated with infection due to neutropenia or the release of inflammatory cytokines from the malignant cells.
  • Easy bruising or bleeding - Due to thrombocytopenia caused by insufficient platelet production.
  • Bone pain - Caused by the expansion of the bone marrow as leukaemic cells proliferate.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Common in malignancies due to increased metabolic activity and cytokine release.
  • Recurrent infections - Result of neutropenia and dysfunctional white blood cells.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Indicative of anaemia due to reduced erythropoiesis.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly - Caused by infiltration of leukaemic cells into the liver and spleen, particularly in acute leukaemias.
  • Petechiae - Small pinpoint haemorrhages on the skin due to thrombocytopenia.
  • Lymphadenopathy - More common in ALL or CLL, due to the accumulation of leukaemic cells in lymphoid tissues.
  • Gingival hyperplasia - A rare sign, seen in AML due to infiltration of the gums by leukaemic blasts.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe or sudden-onset bleeding - May indicate disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), a life-threatening complication in acute leukaemia.
  • Neurological symptoms - Suggests central nervous system (CNS) involvement, particularly in ALL, where leukaemic cells can infiltrate the meninges.
  • Unexplained weight loss with fever and night sweats - B symptoms, particularly suggestive of aggressive forms of leukaemia or transformation of chronic leukaemia into an acute form.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Chronic leukaemia (CML, CLL) may present with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue or be entirely asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on routine blood tests.
  • Acute leukaemia in the elderly - May present with vague constitutional symptoms like fatigue, making it easy to miss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Typically shows anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and either leucocytosis or leucopenia, depending on the stage of leukaemia. Blast cells may be seen on a blood film.
  • Bone marrow biopsy - Demonstrates hypercellularity with a predominance of blasts (>20% in acute leukaemia).
  • Cytogenetic analysis - Identifies chromosomal abnormalities, such as the Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)) in CML.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • An 8-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of fatigue, bruising, and recurrent infections. Full blood count reveals anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and leucocytosis with circulating blasts, suggesting acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic anaemia - Typically presents with pancytopenia but without the presence of blast cells on a blood film.
  • Lymphoma - May present similarly with lymphadenopathy and systemic symptoms but shows a different pattern of lymphocyte proliferation and does not have circulating blasts.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes - Present with cytopenias and dysplastic blood cells but do not typically progress as rapidly as acute leukaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute Leukaemia (ALL/AML) - Managed with chemotherapy (induction, consolidation, and maintenance phases). Haematopoietic stem cell transplantation may be considered in high-risk cases.
  • Chronic Leukaemia (CML) - Treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) such as imatinib. Stem cell transplantation is an option in cases of treatment resistance.
  • Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia (CLL) - Early-stage disease may be monitored without treatment. Advanced disease is treated with chemotherapy, targeted agents (e.g., ibrutinib), or immunotherapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Infection - A leading cause of morbidity and mortality in leukaemia due to neutropenia and immunosuppression.
  • Tumour lysis syndrome - Results from rapid destruction of malignant cells during treatment, leading to electrolyte disturbances and renal failure.
  • CNS infiltration - More common in ALL, causing headaches, seizures, or focal neurological deficits.
