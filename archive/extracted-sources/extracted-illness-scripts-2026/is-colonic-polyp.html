Colonic Polyp
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colonic polyps are abnormal growths of tissue that protrude from the mucosal surface of the colon. They can be benign (non-cancerous) or have malignant potential (precancerous), particularly adenomatous polyps. Polyps vary in size, shape (pedunculated or sessile), and histology, with adenomatous, hyperplastic, and inflammatory types being most common.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colonic polyps are commonly detected during screening colonoscopies, particularly in older adults. In the UK, approximately 20-30% of adults aged over 50 have colonic polyps. They are more prevalent in men and increase with age. Worldwide, the prevalence of adenomatous polyps is estimated to be 10-15%, with a higher prevalence in Western countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colonic polyps develop from the mucosal layer of the colon due to dysregulated epithelial cell proliferation. In adenomatous polyps, mutations in genes like APC, KRAS, and TP53 drive abnormal growth. Hyperplastic polyps result from increased cell turnover without dysplasia. Inflammatory polyps are associated with conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), arising from chronic inflammation and repair processes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colonic polyps are often slow-growing and may remain asymptomatic for years. Adenomatous polyps can progress to colorectal cancer over 10-15 years if left untreated. Hyperplastic and inflammatory polyps typically remain benign.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Most colonic polyps are asymptomatic and are discovered incidentally during screening colonoscopy.
  • Rectal bleeding - Bleeding may occur when larger polyps ulcerate or disrupt the mucosal surface, leading to occult or visible blood in stool.
  • Changes in bowel habits - Larger polyps can cause constipation or diarrhoea by obstructing the lumen or altering bowel motility.
  • Abdominal pain - Rarely, large polyps can cause intermittent colicky abdominal pain due to partial obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Pallor - May be present in cases of chronic blood loss from polyps, leading to anaemia.
  • Occult blood in stool - Detected via faecal immunochemical test (FIT) or guaiac-based faecal occult blood test (gFOBT), indicating mucosal disruption or ulceration of the polyp.
  • Palpable mass - Rarely, large polyps or cancerous growths can be palpated on digital rectal examination (DRE), especially if located distally.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May suggest malignancy transformation in a polyp or colorectal cancer.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Indicates chronic blood loss, which could signal a malignant polyp or early-stage colorectal cancer.
  • Persistent change in bowel habits - A persistent alteration, especially to looser stools or increased frequency, is concerning for malignancy or large polyps causing luminal obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Isolated iron-deficiency anaemia - May be the only sign of a bleeding polyp, particularly in older adults, without overt gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Non-specific abdominal discomfort - Some patients may report vague abdominal discomfort without significant changes in bowel habits, especially with sessile polyps.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colonoscopy - The gold standard for diagnosing and removing colonic polyps. Polyps appear as protrusions from the mucosa, which can be sessile or pedunculated. Histological analysis helps classify polyps (e.g., adenomatous, hyperplastic).
  • Faecal immunochemical test (FIT) - Detects occult blood in stool, indicating potential bleeding polyps or colorectal cancer.
  • CT colonography - A non-invasive imaging alternative to colonoscopy, useful in detecting larger polyps (>6 mm).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with rectal bleeding and recent changes in bowel habits, including intermittent constipation and diarrhoea. No significant weight loss is reported, and he has no family history of colorectal cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer - Unlike benign polyps, colorectal cancer typically presents with more severe symptoms such as unexplained weight loss, more frequent and persistent changes in bowel habits, and anaemia.
  • Diverticulosis - Can cause intermittent rectal bleeding but often without changes in bowel habits unless complicated by diverticulitis.
  • Haemorrhoids - Typically cause painless rectal bleeding but without changes in bowel habits or anaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Polypectomy - Endoscopic removal of polyps via colonoscopy, which is both diagnostic and therapeutic.
  • Surveillance - Regular colonoscopy based on the size, number, and histology of polyps (e.g., every 3-5 years for adenomatous polyps).
  • Lifestyle modifications - Increasing dietary fibre intake, smoking cessation, and maintaining a healthy weight may reduce polyp formation and progression.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Colonic Polyp"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer - The most significant complication of adenomatous polyps, with a 1-10% risk of progression depending on polyp size, histology, and dysplasia.
  • Bleeding - Large polyps may ulcerate and bleed, leading to iron deficiency anaemia or overt rectal bleeding.
  • Obstruction - Rarely, very large polyps can cause partial bowel obstruction, leading to symptoms such as abdominal pain and constipation.
