Endophthalmitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Endophthalmitis is a severe intraocular inflammatory condition involving the aqueous humour or vitreous humour, commonly caused by infection (bacterial or fungal) or, less frequently, by non-infectious inflammatory processes. It is a medical emergency due to its potential to cause permanent vision loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, post-cataract surgery endophthalmitis occurs in approximately 0.05-0.3% of cases.
  • Hospital prevalence of post-traumatic endophthalmitis is lower but significant in ocular trauma cases (up to 0.9%).
  • Worldwide prevalence varies, being higher in regions with limited access to aseptic surgical care and prophylactic measures.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Pathogens (bacteria or fungi) gain entry to the eye via surgical procedures, trauma, or endogenous spread.
  • The infection leads to rapid replication of microorganisms in the intraocular space.
  • This triggers a severe inflammatory response with release of cytokines, leukocyte infiltration, and increased vascular permeability, causing tissue damage and impaired vision.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Endophthalmitis: Symptoms develop within hours to days after exposure (e.g., post-surgery or trauma).
  • Chronic Endophthalmitis: Symptoms evolve slowly over weeks to months, often due to less virulent organisms such as Propionibacterium acnes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Severe eye pain - Due to inflammatory mediators and elevated intraocular pressure.
  • Decreased vision - Caused by vitreous opacities and inflammation obscuring the visual axis.
  • Photophobia - Resulting from ciliary body inflammation.
  • Redness - Conjunctival injection due to vascular response to inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Hypopyon - Accumulation of inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber due to severe inflammation.
  • Corneal oedema - Secondary to endothelial dysfunction caused by inflammation.
  • Vitreous haze - Indicative of inflammation in the vitreous body.
  • Loss of red reflex - Caused by vitreous opacities obstructing the passage of light to the retina.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Rapid vision loss - May signify aggressive infection threatening permanent damage.
  • Hypopyon - Suggests severe intraocular inflammation and risk of complications like retinal detachment.
  • Severe pain unresponsive to standard measures - Indicates significant intraocular pressure or uncontrolled infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic, low-grade symptoms with intermittent blurred vision and minimal pain, often due to low-virulence pathogens.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Aqueous or vitreous sample culture - To identify causative microorganisms.
  • Ocular ultrasound - Shows vitreous opacities and possible retinal detachment.
  • Slit-lamp examination - Reveals hypopyon, anterior chamber reaction, and keratic precipitates.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents 48 hours after cataract surgery with severe eye pain, marked vision loss, and redness in the operated eye. Examination reveals hypopyon and corneal oedema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Uveitis - Lacks hypopyon and typically less pain.
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - Often includes systemic symptoms like nausea and has elevated intraocular pressure.
  • Keratitis - Localised corneal infiltrates rather than intraocular inflammation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Intravitreal antibiotics (e.g., vancomycin and ceftazidime) - Directly targets infection.
  • Systemic antibiotics - Reserved for cases of endogenous endophthalmitis or extensive infection.
  • Steroids - To reduce inflammation once infection is controlled.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endophthalmitis"][/sc]
  • Retinal detachment - Due to inflammatory and infectious damage to retinal layers.
  • Permanent vision loss - From structural damage or optic nerve involvement.
  • Phthisis bulbi - Atrophy of the eyeball following severe uncontrolled infection.
