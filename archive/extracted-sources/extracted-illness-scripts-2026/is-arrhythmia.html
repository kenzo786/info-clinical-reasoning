Arrhythmia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmia refers to an abnormal heart rhythm resulting from improper electrical signalling within the heart. It encompasses various types of irregular heartbeats, including tachycardia (fast heart rate), bradycardia (slow heart rate), and irregular rhythms such as atrial fibrillation (AF). Arrhythmias can be asymptomatic or cause significant morbidity, particularly in patients with underlying cardiovascular disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Atrial fibrillation (AF), the most common arrhythmia, affects about 2-3% of the general population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: In hospitalised patients, arrhythmias are common, especially in those with heart failure or post-myocardial infarction (MI), with AF affecting 5-15% of older adults.
  • Worldwide prevalence: The global prevalence of AF is estimated at 33 million, with significant regional variations depending on risk factors such as hypertension and obesity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias occur due to disturbances in the heart's electrical conduction system, which includes the sinoatrial (SA) node, atrioventricular (AV) node, and conduction pathways. These disturbances may result from structural heart disease, electrolyte imbalances, ischaemia, or autonomic influences.
  • In atrial fibrillation, rapid and disorganised electrical signals originate from the atria, leading to ineffective atrial contraction and an irregularly irregular ventricular response. Tachycardia may arise from re-entry circuits (e.g., atrioventricular nodal re-entrant tachycardia, AVNRT), enhanced automaticity, or triggered activity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias may present acutely, such as with paroxysmal AF, or have a chronic, persistent course requiring long-term management. Paroxysmal episodes can last from seconds to days, while persistent arrhythmias persist longer and require intervention to restore sinus rhythm. Long-term complications like heart failure or thromboembolic events (e.g., stroke) may develop in chronic cases, particularly AF.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Common symptom due to rapid or irregular beating of the heart. This occurs as the heart struggles to maintain a normal rhythm.
  • Fatigue - Results from decreased cardiac output, particularly in AF where the irregular rhythm reduces the efficiency of atrial contraction.
  • Dizziness or Light-headedness - Occurs due to transient cerebral hypoperfusion, especially in cases of bradycardia or sustained tachyarrhythmias.
  • Shortness of Breath - Can occur due to the reduced cardiac output, leading to pulmonary congestion or inadequate systemic oxygen delivery.
  • Chest Pain - May occur in individuals with underlying coronary artery disease (CAD) due to increased oxygen demand from a rapid heart rate or due to poor perfusion during prolonged tachycardia.
  • Asymptomatic - Many arrhythmias, especially atrial fibrillation, may be incidentally discovered on routine ECG.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Irregular Pulse - Typically seen in AF, characterised by an irregularly irregular rhythm due to disorganised atrial activity.
  • Tachycardia - May be present in supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) or ventricular tachycardia (VT) where the heart rate exceeds 100 bpm.
  • Hypotension - Due to reduced cardiac output, particularly in arrhythmias that impair effective ventricular filling, such as AF with rapid ventricular response (RVR).
  • Jugular Venous Distension (JVD) - May be seen in cases of heart failure due to long-standing arrhythmias, indicating increased central venous pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Suggests severe arrhythmias like ventricular tachycardia (VT) or complete heart block, which may lead to transient or complete loss of consciousness due to inadequate cerebral perfusion.
  • Chest Pain with Arrhythmia - May indicate underlying ischaemia or myocardial infarction, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Heart Failure Symptoms - Such as peripheral oedema or worsening shortness of breath, indicating poor ventricular function secondary to chronic arrhythmia.
  • Severe Bradycardia or Tachycardia (>150 bpm) - Can lead to haemodynamic instability and requires immediate management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Silent Atrial Fibrillation - Some patients may have AF without typical symptoms like palpitations, instead presenting with complications like stroke or heart failure.
  • Atypical Chest Discomfort - Rather than classic angina, patients with arrhythmia may report vague chest discomfort, which can be easily overlooked.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) - The gold standard for diagnosing arrhythmias. AF shows absence of P waves with an irregularly irregular rhythm, while tachyarrhythmias may display narrow or wide QRS complexes depending on the origin.
  • Holter Monitor - Useful for capturing intermittent arrhythmias over a 24-48 hour period. Can confirm paroxysmal events that might not appear on a single ECG.
  • Echocardiography - Assesses structural heart disease, such as left atrial enlargement or ventricular dysfunction, which often accompany chronic arrhythmias like AF.
  • Electrolyte Panel - Used to identify precipitating factors like hypokalaemia or hypomagnesaemia, which can predispose to arrhythmias.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male with a history of hypertension and diabetes presents with intermittent palpitations and shortness of breath for 2 weeks. Examination reveals an irregularly irregular pulse, and ECG confirms atrial fibrillation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) - Differs by presenting with wide QRS complexes on ECG and often occurs in patients with structural heart disease or previous MI.
  • Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) - Usually presents with a regular, rapid heartbeat, and can be distinguished from AF by the absence of irregularity on ECG.
  • Sinus Tachycardia - Physiological response to stress or illness, with normal P waves preceding every QRS complex on ECG, differentiating it from AF or other arrhythmias.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Rate Control - Beta-blockers (e.g., bisoprolol) or calcium channel blockers (e.g., diltiazem) are first-line agents for AF to control ventricular rate.
  • Rhythm Control - Anti-arrhythmic drugs (e.g., amiodarone) or electrical cardioversion may be employed for patients where rhythm restoration is indicated.
  • Anticoagulation - Long-term anticoagulation (e.g., warfarin or DOACs) is recommended in AF patients with a CHA2DS2-VASc score of 2 or more to reduce stroke risk.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Due to thromboembolism from stasis of blood in the fibrillating atria, particularly in AF.
  • Heart Failure - Arrhythmias, especially when sustained, can lead to left ventricular dysfunction and exacerbation of heart failure symptoms.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - A risk in severe arrhythmias such as VT or VF, which can lead to cardiac arrest without immediate intervention.
