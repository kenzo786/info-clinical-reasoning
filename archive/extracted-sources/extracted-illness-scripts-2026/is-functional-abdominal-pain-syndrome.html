Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS) is a chronic pain disorder characterised by persistent or recurrent abdominal pain with no identifiable organic or structural cause. It falls under the broader category of functional gastrointestinal disorders (FGIDs), where symptoms arise from altered gut-brain interactions rather than anatomical abnormalities. Diagnosis is based on the absence of identifiable pathology, with pain lasting more than six months, unrelated to changes in bowel habits.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 2-5% of the population presents with FAPS, though accurate data is challenging due to overlapping FGID symptoms.
  • UK hospital prevalence: FGIDs, including FAPS, account for about 40% of gastroenterology outpatient visits.
  • Worldwide prevalence: FAPS and similar FGIDs affect roughly 10-20% of adults worldwide, with regional variations based on lifestyle, diet, and healthcare access.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • FAPS involves complex, multifactorial pathophysiology linked to altered gut-brain axis signalling. Key mechanisms include:
  • Visceral hypersensitivity: Heightened pain perception in the gastrointestinal tract due to altered central pain processing.
  • Central sensitisation: Long-standing pain leads to increased sensitivity of the central nervous system, resulting in amplified pain signals from normally non-painful stimuli.
  • Psychosocial factors: Anxiety, depression, and stress are highly correlated with FAPS, likely influencing pain perception through neuroendocrine and immune changes.
  • Impaired autonomic regulation: Dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system (ANS) affects gut motility and pain processing, contributing to symptom persistence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Symptoms may start with mild, intermittent abdominal pain, often with no clear trigger.
  • Chronic Phase: Pain becomes more consistent, typically lasting over six months, without association to bowel movements or food intake.
  • Persistent/Recurrent Phase: Patients may experience periods of increased pain intensity, often correlated with stress or life events, leading to long-term quality of life impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain: Persistent or recurrent, poorly localised pain often described as dull or cramp-like; results from altered visceral sensitivity and central sensitisation.
  • Bloating: Sensation of fullness or pressure in the abdomen, possibly due to dysregulated autonomic control affecting gut motility.
  • Nausea: Often co-occurs with pain, possibly linked to autonomic nervous system involvement and altered central pain processing.
  • Sleep disturbances: Due to chronic pain; pain may disrupt sleep quality, worsening fatigue and overall well-being.
  • Anxiety and depression: Commonly associated, likely both as a cause and result of FAPS, contributing to heightened pain perception through stress pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Normal abdominal examination: Despite significant pain, no signs of tenderness, guarding, or organomegaly, supporting a functional rather than organic cause.
  • Non-specific tenderness: Mild, diffuse tenderness on palpation without localisation, likely due to visceral hypersensitivity rather than inflammation.
  • Psychological signs: Signs of anxiety or stress may be present; patients may appear distressed or tense, reflecting the psychophysiological link.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss: May suggest malignancy, chronic infection, or inflammatory conditions.
  • Blood in stool: Indicative of potential gastrointestinal bleeding, ulceration, or malignancy.
  • Night sweats or fever: Could indicate infection or inflammatory pathology such as inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Onset after age 50: Higher suspicion for organic causes in older patients, including colorectal cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Presentation with primarily nausea or dizziness, without significant pain, can occasionally occur due to autonomic dysregulation.
  • Some may experience episodic, sharp pain rather than a dull ache, leading to initial misdiagnosis as other conditions like IBS.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Complete Blood Count (CBC): Typically normal; abnormal findings (e.g., anaemia) would prompt investigation for organic causes.
  • Faecal calprotectin: Normal, helping to exclude inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Endoscopy/colonoscopy: Reserved for patients with red flag signs; normal findings support a functional diagnosis.
  • Psychological evaluation: Screening for anxiety and depression, as these are common comorbidities impacting pain perception.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with a 1-year history of persistent, poorly localised abdominal pain not related to eating or bowel movements, alongside occasional bloating and generalised fatigue, with normal clinical and laboratory findings.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): Similar pain presentation, but IBS is often accompanied by diarrhoea or constipation, absent in FAPS.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Distinguished by inflammation markers, weight loss, and abnormal faecal calprotectin levels.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Pain often localised to the epigastrium, associated with eating or relieved by antacids.
  • Chronic Pancreatitis: More likely with history of alcohol use, upper abdominal pain radiating to the back, and steatorrhoea.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Reassurance and patient education: Clarify the functional nature of the condition and address patient concerns.
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): Proven to reduce pain perception and improve coping in FAPS.
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline): Low doses are effective for chronic pain management.
  • Psychological support: Treatment for anxiety or depression if present, often with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Psychosocial impairment: Long-term FAPS can significantly impact quality of life, employment, and social relationships.
  • Increased healthcare utilisation: High rates of healthcare visits and unnecessary testing due to chronic pain.
  • Prognosis: Symptoms are chronic but can be managed with psychological and pharmacological treatment, though complete resolution is rare.
