Bronchiolitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Bronchiolitis is a lower respiratory tract infection primarily affecting infants and young children, characterised by inflammation and obstruction of the small airways (bronchioles). It is commonly caused by viral infections, most notably respiratory syncytial virus (RSV).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Bronchiolitis accounts for up to 20% of consultations for respiratory illnesses in infants.
  • UK hospital prevalence: The most common cause of hospitalisation in infants under 1 year of age, with approximately 3% of all infants hospitalised annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects an estimated 33 million children annually, with 3.2 million requiring hospitalisation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Initial viral infection (commonly RSV) infects the epithelial cells of the bronchioles.
  • The infected cells die, causing sloughing and release of inflammatory mediators.
  • Inflammation leads to oedema, mucous production, and smooth muscle constriction, narrowing the airway.
  • Air trapping and atelectasis occur due to partial obstruction, causing ventilation-perfusion mismatch and hypoxaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Incubation: Typically 4–6 days following viral exposure.
  • Initial phase: Presents with mild upper respiratory tract symptoms such as rhinorrhoea and cough over 1–2 days.
  • Peak phase: Progression to lower respiratory symptoms (wheezing, respiratory distress) by days 3–5.
  • Recovery phase: Gradual resolution over 7–14 days, though coughing may persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Rhinorrhoea - Initial symptom due to viral invasion of nasal epithelial cells.
  • Cough - Caused by airway inflammation and mucus production.
  • Wheezing - Result of turbulent airflow through narrowed bronchioles.
  • Feeding difficulties - Secondary to nasal congestion and respiratory distress.
  • Lethargy - Indicates increased work of breathing and hypoxaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea - Reflects increased respiratory effort to compensate for hypoxaemia.
  • Intercostal and subcostal recessions - Result of increased negative intrathoracic pressure during inspiration.
  • Wheezing - Audible due to airflow through constricted airways.
  • Crackles - Reflects mucous plugging and airway collapse.
  • Hypoxia (low oxygen saturation) - Caused by ventilation-perfusion mismatch.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Central cyanosis - Suggests severe hypoxaemia and impending respiratory failure.
  • Apnoea - Common in young infants; indicates a critical drop in respiratory drive.
  • Severe respiratory distress - Marked by grunting, nasal flaring, and head bobbing; indicates impending respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Isolated apnoea without other signs of respiratory distress in very young infants.
  • Absence of wheezing, particularly in infants with underlying conditions (e.g., neuromuscular disorders).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Nasal swab for RSV - Confirms viral aetiology; helps with cohorting in hospitals.
  • Pulse oximetry - Detects hypoxaemia; oxygen saturation <92% is significant.
  • Chest X-ray - Indicated for atypical or severe cases; may show hyperinflation or patchy consolidation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • An 8-month-old male with a 2-day history of rhinorrhoea, worsening cough, and feeding difficulties, presenting with tachypnoea, subcostal recessions, and oxygen saturations of 90%.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Asthma exacerbation - Less common in infants; typically involves a history of atopy or recurrent wheeze.
  • Pneumonia - May present with fever and focal findings on chest auscultation.
  • Foreign body aspiration - Sudden onset with unilateral wheeze or decreased breath sounds.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care is the mainstay: ensure hydration, oxygen supplementation if saturations <92%, and feeding support.
  • High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) oxygen therapy or CPAP for severe cases.
  • No routine use of bronchodilators, corticosteroids, or antibiotics unless there is a clear indication.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Due to progressive hypoxaemia and work of breathing.
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Rare but may cause pneumonia.
  • Prolonged hospitalisation - Particularly in infants with comorbidities or severe disease.
