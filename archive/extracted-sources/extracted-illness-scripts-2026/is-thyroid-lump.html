Thyroid Lump
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • A thyroid lump (nodule) refers to any discrete swelling or mass within the thyroid gland. It may be benign or malignant, solitary or multiple, and can vary in size and presentation. Most thyroid lumps are benign, but they may cause local or systemic symptoms based on size, location, and whether they are functional (producing thyroid hormones) or non-functional. Malignant thyroid nodules represent a small percentage of cases but are important to identify early due to potential for metastasis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Thyroid nodules are common, with a prevalence of around 5-10% in the UK general population detected via palpation, and up to 50% detectable with high-resolution ultrasound.
  • The prevalence in UK hospital settings is lower, as many nodules are asymptomatic and do not present to secondary care unless suspicious for malignancy.
  • Globally, the prevalence of thyroid lumps varies by geographic region, with iodine deficiency being a common contributing factor in areas with low dietary iodine intake.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Thyroid nodules form due to abnormal growth of thyroid tissue. They may arise from follicular hyperplasia, cyst formation, or neoplasia. The majority of nodules are benign, such as colloid nodules or thyroid adenomas. Malignant nodules, including thyroid carcinoma, can develop from genetic mutations leading to unregulated cellular growth. The functional status of the nodule determines whether it produces excess thyroid hormones, causing symptoms of hyperthyroidism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Thyroid nodules can develop gradually over months to years and are often asymptomatic initially. Functional (toxic) nodules may lead to hyperthyroid symptoms over weeks to months. Malignant nodules may progress over several months with potential for rapid enlargement and symptoms related to local invasion or metastasis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Many thyroid nodules do not cause any symptoms and are often found incidentally during imaging for other conditions.
  • Neck swelling - A palpable or visible lump in the neck is the most common symptom, usually due to the enlargement of the thyroid or a solitary nodule.
  • Difficulty swallowing - Larger nodules may compress the oesophagus, leading to dysphagia.
  • Hoarseness - Compression or invasion of the recurrent laryngeal nerve by a large or malignant nodule may result in vocal cord paralysis and hoarseness.
  • Thyroid hormone imbalance symptoms - Hyperthyroid nodules can cause symptoms such as weight loss, heat intolerance, palpitations, and tremor.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Palpable neck mass - A single or multiple nodules may be felt in the thyroid region, often mobile and firm to touch.
  • Thyroid bruit - In the case of a hyperactive thyroid nodule (e.g., toxic adenoma), a bruit may be heard over the gland due to increased vascularity.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlargement of cervical lymph nodes can suggest malignancy, particularly with firm or fixed nodes.
  • Tachycardia - In cases of hyperthyroidism, increased heart rate may be present.
  • Vocal cord palsy - If the recurrent laryngeal nerve is affected, there may be signs of vocal cord weakness or paralysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Rapid nodule growth - Suggestive of malignancy, particularly anaplastic carcinoma, which is aggressive and fast-growing.
  • Hoarseness - May indicate recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement from a malignant nodule or metastatic spread.
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing - Can indicate compression of the trachea or oesophagus by a large mass, possibly malignant.
  • Lymphadenopathy - The presence of firm, non-tender, and fixed cervical lymph nodes raises concern for metastatic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Small nodules - May be completely asymptomatic and only detected incidentally during imaging for unrelated issues.
  • Elderly patients - Malignant nodules may present with vague symptoms, such as fatigue or weight loss, rather than overt thyroid-related signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - First-line imaging to assess size, echogenicity, and features suggestive of malignancy (e.g., microcalcifications, irregular margins).
  • Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC) - Used for cytological evaluation of suspicious nodules. Malignant features include the presence of malignant cells, nuclear atypia.
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Includes TSH and free T4 levels. A suppressed TSH with a nodule may suggest a hyperfunctioning (toxic) adenoma.
  • Radionuclide scan - Helps distinguish between 'hot' (hyperfunctioning, typically benign) and 'cold' (non-functioning, with higher malignancy risk) nodules.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a painless, gradually enlarging lump in her neck noticed over the past 3 months. She reports recent hoarseness and difficulty swallowing, but no systemic symptoms of hyperthyroidism. Examination reveals a firm, non-tender, palpable mass in the thyroid region.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Multinodular goitre - Typically presents with multiple nodules and is associated with a longstanding goitre, whereas a solitary thyroid lump may indicate malignancy.
  • Thyroid cyst - Often benign and fluid-filled, differentiating from a solid or malignant nodule on ultrasound or FNAC.
  • Thyroiditis - Inflammatory thyroid disease, often associated with tenderness and systemic symptoms such as fever.
  • Thyroid carcinoma - Malignant nodule with associated red flags (e.g., lymphadenopathy, hoarseness).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Benign nodules - Observation with regular ultrasound monitoring. Surgery considered if symptomatic or cosmetically concerning.
  • Thyroid malignancy - Surgical excision (thyroidectomy), potentially followed by radioactive iodine therapy or external beam radiation, depending on the type and stage of cancer.
  • Hyperfunctioning nodules - May require radioactive iodine ablation or antithyroid drugs (e.g., carbimazole) to control hormone overproduction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thyroid Lump"][/sc]
  • Malignant transformation - Rare in benign nodules but important to monitor.
  • Compression symptoms - Large nodules can compress surrounding structures, leading to dyspnoea, dysphagia, and hoarseness.
  • Metastasis - In thyroid cancer, especially anaplastic carcinoma, which can spread to lungs, bones, and lymph nodes.
