Hypoglycaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia is defined as a blood glucose level below 4.0 mmol/L, particularly in individuals with diabetes. Clinically significant hypoglycaemia, often referred to as severe hypoglycaemia, occurs when the blood glucose level is low enough to cause symptoms or require external assistance for correction. It can be caused by an imbalance between insulin/glucose-lowering medications and food intake or increased physical activity without adequate glucose compensation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, hypoglycaemia is more common among patients with diabetes, particularly those using insulin or sulfonylureas. Roughly 25% of Type 1 diabetes patients experience severe hypoglycaemia annually. Hypoglycaemia is less frequent in the general population but may occur in cases of malnutrition, alcohol abuse, or endocrine disorders. Worldwide, hypoglycaemia prevalence varies, but similar trends are seen in diabetic populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • The primary mechanism in hypoglycaemia is an excessive reduction in blood glucose levels. Glucose is essential for brain function, and the brain depends on a continuous glucose supply. When blood glucose drops, counter-regulatory hormones (glucagon, adrenaline, cortisol, and growth hormone) are released to raise glucose levels. In patients with diabetes, these regulatory responses may be blunted, particularly with long-term insulin therapy or sulfonylureas, making hypoglycaemia more likely. Hypoglycaemia can also result from other conditions like insulinoma, adrenal insufficiency, or severe liver disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia can occur acutely within minutes to hours depending on its cause. It may present during periods of fasting, physical exertion, or following insulin/medication administration. Mild hypoglycaemia may resolve with oral glucose intake within 15 minutes, but severe cases can lead to prolonged confusion, coma, or seizures if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Shakiness - Caused by adrenergic stimulation as the body attempts to counteract the low blood glucose.
  • Sweating - Excessive sweating is triggered by sympathetic nervous system activation in response to low glucose levels.
  • Hunger - The body increases hunger signals due to glucose depletion in an attempt to replenish energy stores.
  • Fatigue/Weakness - Occurs as the body’s cells, especially in muscles and the brain, experience energy deprivation due to low glucose.
  • Confusion - Reflects neuroglycopenia, where insufficient glucose supply to the brain impairs cognitive function.
  • Palpitations - Due to the release of adrenaline as part of the body’s response to hypoglycaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Due to the adrenergic response aimed at mobilising glucose stores and maintaining perfusion.
  • Diaphoresis - Profuse sweating is a sign of the sympathetic nervous system’s attempt to raise blood glucose levels.
  • Altered mental status - Ranging from confusion to coma, this reflects neuroglycopenia, where the brain lacks sufficient glucose to function normally.
  • Seizures - In severe hypoglycaemia, the brain's energy deprivation can trigger seizures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Loss of consciousness - Indicates severe neuroglycopenia and the risk of permanent brain damage if not corrected urgently.
  • Seizures - Sign of severe hypoglycaemia that requires immediate medical intervention to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Prolonged confusion or coma - Suggests severe and prolonged hypoglycaemia with a potential risk of permanent neurological damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • In elderly patients, hypoglycaemia may present primarily with confusion, without the typical adrenergic symptoms such as sweating or shaking. This can lead to missed diagnosis, as cognitive decline may be misattributed to dementia.
  • Patients on beta-blockers may not exhibit typical signs like tachycardia or palpitations due to inhibition of the adrenergic response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Capillary blood glucose - Typically <4.0 mmol/L. Confirms hypoglycaemia and helps in acute management.
  • Serum insulin and C-peptide levels - Elevated in insulinoma but suppressed in exogenous insulin use.
  • Fasting blood glucose - Assesses for hypoglycaemia in cases where spontaneous hypoglycaemia is suspected (e.g., insulinoma or adrenal insufficiency).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with type 1 diabetes presents with sweating, shakiness, and confusion. Her blood glucose on arrival is 2.8 mmol/L. Symptoms resolved with oral glucose.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Insulinoma - Presents with recurrent fasting hypoglycaemia, elevated serum insulin, and C-peptide levels.
  • Adrenal insufficiency - Associated with fatigue, hypotension, and weight loss, along with low cortisol levels.
  • Alcohol-induced hypoglycaemia - Occurs after alcohol consumption, particularly in fasting or malnourished patients, with no hyperinsulinaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate glucose replacement - Oral glucose (15-20g) for mild cases, IV glucose (10% or 50%) or IM glucagon for severe cases.
  • Preventive strategies - Adjusting insulin doses, frequent blood glucose monitoring, and patient education to avoid hypoglycaemic episodes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological damage - Due to prolonged neuroglycopenia, can result in cognitive impairment or coma.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Severe hypoglycaemia can cause arrhythmias due to autonomic dysfunction.
  • Recurrent hypoglycaemia - Frequent episodes can lead to hypoglycaemia unawareness, increasing the risk of severe episodes.
