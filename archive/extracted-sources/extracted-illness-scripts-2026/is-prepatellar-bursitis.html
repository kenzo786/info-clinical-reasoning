Prepatellar Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Prepatellar bursitis, also known as "housemaid's knee," is inflammation of the prepatellar bursa, a small fluid-filled sac located anterior to the patella. It serves as a cushion to reduce friction during movement. This condition commonly results from repetitive trauma, infection, or inflammatory conditions such as gout.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence among UK GP patients: Common in individuals engaged in occupations involving prolonged kneeling, such as gardeners and carpet layers.
  • Hospital prevalence: Frequently seen in emergency or orthopaedic outpatient settings, particularly with traumatic or infectious causes.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Common globally, with higher rates in populations with manual labour occupations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic mechanical irritation from repetitive kneeling leads to bursal thickening and synovial fluid overproduction.
  • Trauma can cause haemorrhagic fluid accumulation, promoting an inflammatory response.
  • Infectious prepatellar bursitis often results from bacterial inoculation, typically Staphylococcus aureus, through minor skin breaches.
  • Crystals (e.g., urate in gout) can precipitate inflammatory bursitis by direct irritation and immune activation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of pain and swelling, often associated with infection or trauma.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual development of swelling and discomfort, typically with mechanical irritation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Swelling - Prominent anterior knee swelling caused by fluid accumulation in the prepatellar bursa.
  • Pain - Localised pain, exacerbated by kneeling or pressure on the knee due to inflammation and increased tissue tension.
  • Reduced mobility - Difficulty bending the knee due to pain and swelling restricting movement.
  • Warmth/redness (in infectious bursitis) - Localised inflammatory response or systemic infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Palpable swelling - Soft, fluctuant swelling over the patella, indicative of fluid accumulation.
  • Erythema and warmth - Suggestive of inflammation or infection.
  • Tenderness - Pain upon palpation due to inflammation.
  • Limited flexion - Pain-induced movement restriction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Severe redness and warmth with fever - Suggestive of septic bursitis, requiring urgent treatment to prevent systemic spread.
  • Purulent aspirate - Indicates infection; a potentially serious condition if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic swelling noted incidentally during routine physical examination, particularly in chronic cases.
  • Non-localised pain misinterpreted as other knee pathologies such as osteoarthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Aspiration and analysis of bursal fluid - Purulent fluid with high WBC count indicates infection; crystals suggest gout.
  • Ultrasound - Identifies fluid collection and helps differentiate bursitis from other soft tissue pathologies.
  • Blood tests - Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) in infectious or inflammatory bursitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male manual labourer presents with a 3-day history of anterior knee pain and swelling following minor trauma. Examination reveals erythema, warmth, and tender swelling over the patella.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Typically involves the joint itself; often with systemic symptoms such as fever and severe pain.
  • Gout - May present similarly but often involves other joints, with monosodium urate crystals in synovial fluid.
  • Cellulitis - Diffuse soft tissue infection, not limited to the bursa, with widespread erythema.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Conservative: Rest, ice application, and NSAIDs for non-infectious bursitis.
  • Aspiration: For symptomatic relief and diagnostic purposes.
  • Antibiotics: For infectious bursitis, based on culture results (e.g., flucloxacillin or clindamycin in the UK).
  • Surgical drainage: Reserved for refractory or severe cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and swelling - Persistent symptoms from unresolved inflammation or repeated trauma.
  • Sepsis - In untreated septic bursitis, bacteria may spread systemically, leading to life-threatening conditions.
