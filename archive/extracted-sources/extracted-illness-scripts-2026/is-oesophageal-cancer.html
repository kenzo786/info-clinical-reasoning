Oesophageal Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal cancer is a malignancy that arises from the cells lining the oesophagus. It is predominantly of two histological types: squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), arising from the squamous cells of the upper/mid oesophagus, and adenocarcinoma, which typically develops in the lower oesophagus from glandular cells, often due to chronic gastroesophageal reflux disease (GORD) and Barrett’s oesophagus.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Smoking, alcohol, obesity, chronic GORD, Barrett’s oesophagus, dietary factors (processed meats, hot beverages), achalasia, prior thoracic radiotherapy, and genetic predisposition increase the risk.
  • Prevention: Smoking cessation, reducing alcohol intake, maintaining a healthy weight, managing GORD, dietary modifications, and surveillance of Barrett’s oesophagus reduce risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) arises from the squamous epithelium, typically due to chronic irritants like alcohol and smoking, leading to dysplastic changes and carcinoma.
  • Adenocarcinoma arises from glandular cells in the lower oesophagus, often linked to Barrett's oesophagus (a metaplastic change due to chronic acid exposure in GORD). Over time, dysplastic cells progress to cancer.
  • Both forms of cancer invade local structures, including the trachea, bronchi, and aorta, causing symptoms based on the extent of tumour spread.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal cancer is typically a slowly progressive disease. Dysplasia in Barrett’s oesophagus or squamous dysplasia evolves over years to decades before symptomatic cancer develops. Early stages may be asymptomatic, while advanced cancer causes rapid symptom progression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing, particularly for solids initially, progressing to liquids. This results from tumour obstruction of the oesophageal lumen.
  • Odynophagia - Painful swallowing due to tumour ulceration or invasion into surrounding tissues.
  • Weight loss - Unintended weight loss is common due to reduced caloric intake and cancer-related cachexia.
  • Retrosternal discomfort - Tumour growth may cause chest pain, especially with swallowing.
  • Regurgitation - Food or liquid may regurgitate, due to partial oesophageal blockage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cachexia - Severe weight loss, muscle wasting, and malnutrition are common in advanced cancer.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged cervical or supraclavicular nodes, suggesting metastasis.
  • Hoarseness - Involvement of the recurrent laryngeal nerve can cause voice changes.
  • Signs of anaemia - Pallor or tachycardia, as chronic blood loss may lead to iron deficiency anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Progressive dysphagia - Increasing difficulty in swallowing is highly suggestive of malignancy obstructing the oesophagus.
  • Weight loss - Significant unintentional weight loss (>5% of body weight) can indicate cancer-related cachexia.
  • Haematemesis or melaena - Vomiting blood or black tarry stools suggest tumour ulceration leading to bleeding.
  • Persistent cough - Indicates possible aspiration or tracheal involvement by the tumour.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Silent, asymptomatic cases may be detected incidentally in patients undergoing endoscopy for other reasons, particularly in early adenocarcinoma. In some cases, mild GORD symptoms or vague epigastric discomfort may be the only clue.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Endoscopy with biopsy - Gold standard for diagnosis. Tumour biopsy reveals histological subtype (adenocarcinoma or SCC).
  • Barium swallow - May show narrowing or irregularity of the oesophageal lumen.
  • CT scan - Used for staging to detect local invasion and distant metastases.
  • PET-CT - Evaluates metabolic activity and detects distant metastasis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • 67-year-old male with a 3-month history of progressive dysphagia for solids, significant weight loss, and occasional retrosternal pain. Non-smoker, long-standing GORD. Oesophageal endoscopy reveals an ulcerative mass in the lower oesophagus, confirmed as adenocarcinoma on biopsy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Achalasia - Differs by the presence of dysphagia for both solids and liquids from the onset, and a "bird-beak" appearance on barium swallow.
  • GORD with stricture - May cause dysphagia but without the significant weight loss or systemic features of cancer.
  • Benign oesophageal tumour - Less likely to cause significant weight loss and typically presents with slower progression of dysphagia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early-stage - Surgical resection (oesophagectomy) with curative intent, possibly combined with neoadjuvant chemotherapy or radiotherapy.
  • Advanced-stage - Palliative chemotherapy and/or radiotherapy. Endoscopic stenting may be used to relieve dysphagia.
  • Targeted therapy - For HER2-positive tumours, trastuzumab may be used in combination with chemotherapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oesophageal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Spread to lymph nodes, liver, lungs, and bones is common and worsens prognosis.
  • Oesophageal perforation - Rare, but can occur with tumour invasion, leading to mediastinitis.
  • Aspiration pneumonia - Due to tumour-induced dysphagia and aspiration of food/liquid into the lungs.
