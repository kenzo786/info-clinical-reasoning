Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) is a condition where the veins in the lower extremities fail to efficiently return blood to the heart due to valvular dysfunction. This leads to venous stasis, increased venous pressure, and resultant symptoms such as leg swelling, skin changes, and venous ulcers.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: 10–15% of adults, more common in women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 1-2% of hospitalised patients show complications like venous ulcers.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 25-40% of the adult population, with higher rates in older adults and women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • In CVI, dysfunction of the venous valves causes blood to pool in the lower extremities. This leads to venous hypertension, capillary leakage, and inflammation. Chronic venous pressure damages the vein walls and surrounding tissues, resulting in oedema, skin changes, and eventually ulcers if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Symptoms may be mild, with occasional swelling and heaviness in the legs, usually worse after prolonged standing.
  • Intermediate Phase: Symptoms worsen with more consistent leg swelling, skin discolouration, and varicose veins.
  • Late Phase: Severe complications like venous ulcers, which may become chronic if not treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Leg swelling (oedema) - Caused by increased venous pressure leading to fluid leakage into the interstitial spaces.
  • Leg heaviness or aching - Due to venous congestion and poor blood return.
  • Itching or tingling - Skin changes and inflammation stimulate local nerves.
  • Skin discolouration (haemosiderin deposits) - Chronic venous pressure causes red blood cell breakdown, leading to iron deposits in the skin.
  • Venous ulcers - Severe form of skin damage due to persistent inflammation and tissue breakdown from prolonged venous stasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Pitting oedema - Venous hypertension forces fluid into the interstitial tissue, which leaves an indentation when pressed.
  • Varicose veins - Dilated, tortuous veins due to prolonged venous stasis.
  • Lipodermatosclerosis - Skin hardening and fibrosis caused by chronic inflammation and tissue remodelling in response to persistent venous hypertension.
  • Venous ulcers - Often located near the medial malleolus, they result from chronic inflammation and skin breakdown.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Sudden increase in pain or swelling - May indicate deep vein thrombosis (DVT), which could complicate into pulmonary embolism.
  • Non-healing venous ulcers - Suggestive of poor management and increased risk of infection or secondary complications like cellulitis.
  • Severe skin changes with necrosis - Possible venous gangrene, a serious complication requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic varicose veins - Some patients may present with significant venous reflux without noticeable symptoms.
  • Venous claudication - Pain upon exertion, mimicking arterial disease, due to venous outflow obstruction.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Doppler ultrasound - Shows reflux and venous insufficiency by visualising blood flow in the veins.
  • Ankle-brachial index (ABI) - Used to rule out arterial disease if there are ulcers, as ABI should be >0.9 in CVI.
  • Venography - Rarely used but can demonstrate venous obstructions or valve incompetence in complex cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old woman presents with progressive leg swelling, skin discolouration, and occasional venous ulcers on her lower legs over the past year. She reports significant aching, worse with prolonged standing.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Acute onset of swelling, pain, and redness, often unilateral, with a risk of pulmonary embolism. Doppler ultrasound confirms absence of thrombus in CVI.
  • Lymphoedema - Often painless swelling, typically non-pitting in later stages. Doppler ultrasound and lack of venous reflux help differentiate from CVI.
  • Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) - Claudication and ulceration but associated with reduced ankle-brachial index (ABI), distinguishing it from CVI where ABI is normal.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Compression therapy - First-line management to reduce venous pressure and oedema. Class II compression stockings are commonly recommended.
  • Elevation of legs - Encourages venous return and reduces stasis.
  • Topical dressings for ulcers - Applied to promote healing of venous ulcers.
  • Ablative procedures - Includes endovenous laser therapy or sclerotherapy for persistent symptoms or large varicose veins.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Venous ulcers - Non-healing ulcers due to persistent venous stasis and inflammation.
  • Cellulitis - Secondary infection due to skin breakdown.
  • Venous gangrene - Rare, severe complication where tissue necrosis occurs due to chronic venous insufficiency and thrombosis.
