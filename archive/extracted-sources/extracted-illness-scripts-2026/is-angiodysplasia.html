Angiodysplasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Angiodysplasia is a vascular malformation of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, characterised by dilated and fragile blood vessels that can lead to chronic or recurrent gastrointestinal bleeding. It is most commonly located in the colon and typically affects older adults.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Angiodysplasia is an uncommon cause of GI bleeding in primary care, with a prevalence of around 1–2% among patients presenting with GI bleeding.
  • Hospital prevalence: It accounts for 3–6% of cases of lower gastrointestinal bleeding in hospital settings.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Angiodysplasia is more common in older adults and is responsible for approximately 4–7% of GI bleeds worldwide, with higher prevalence in individuals aged over 60 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Angiodysplasia arises due to intermittent low-grade obstruction of submucosal veins in the gastrointestinal tract. This leads to dilatation and eventual formation of abnormal, fragile blood vessels.
  • The chronic low oxygen state, often exacerbated by ageing or comorbid conditions (e.g., heart failure, aortic stenosis), promotes neovascularisation (formation of new, fragile vessels), which can rupture, causing GI bleeding.
  • The condition is particularly common in the caecum and ascending colon, where the largest concentration of these malformations is often found.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Chronic course: Angiodysplasia often presents with recurrent, chronic GI bleeding over months to years, resulting in iron-deficiency anaemia.
  • Acute exacerbations: Some patients may present with episodes of significant GI bleeding, though life-threatening haemorrhage is rare.
  • Asymptomatic cases: Many patients may have subclinical disease, which is incidentally discovered during endoscopy or imaging for other reasons.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Occult gastrointestinal bleeding - Often presents with iron-deficiency anaemia without overt signs of blood loss. This occurs due to slow, intermittent bleeding from fragile blood vessels.
  • Haematochezia - Fresh blood in the stool, typically a sign of lower GI bleeding, which may occur with more substantial bleeding from angiodysplasia lesions.
  • Fatigue and pallor - Symptoms related to chronic anaemia caused by long-standing blood loss.
  • Melena - Black, tarry stools due to digested blood, often seen in angiodysplasia of the upper GI tract or more proximal lesions.
  • Asymptomatic - In some cases, angiodysplasia is asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during colonoscopy or imaging for other conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - A common sign of chronic anaemia caused by ongoing blood loss from angiodysplasia.
  • Tachycardia - Reflects compensatory mechanisms in response to blood loss and anaemia.
  • Hypotension - A sign of more significant acute blood loss, though rare, and typically seen in more severe cases.
  • Positive faecal occult blood test (FOBT) - Can indicate the presence of occult GI bleeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Severe haematochezia or melena with hypotension - May signify large-volume bleeding and risk of haemodynamic instability.
  • Refractory anaemia despite iron therapy - Suggests ongoing occult blood loss that is not being managed effectively and may necessitate endoscopic evaluation.
  • Syncope or near-syncope - Indicates significant blood loss with hypovolaemia, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation is common, particularly in elderly patients undergoing colonoscopy for other reasons.
  • Patients with aortic stenosis may have a syndrome called Heyde’s syndrome, where angiodysplasia and aortic stenosis coexist, increasing the risk of bleeding.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Colonoscopy - Direct visualisation of dilated, fragile blood vessels, typically located in the caecum or ascending colon.
  • Angiography - Useful in cases of active bleeding, where contrast extravasation may identify the site of bleeding.
  • Capsule endoscopy - Used when colonoscopy is non-diagnostic, particularly to evaluate the small bowel.
  • Faecal occult blood test (FOBT) - May be positive in cases of chronic occult bleeding.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman presents with fatigue and iron-deficiency anaemia. She reports occasional episodes of haematochezia and is found to have positive faecal occult blood test results. Colonoscopy reveals angiodysplasia in the caecum.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Diverticular disease - Unlike angiodysplasia, diverticular bleeding is often more acute and massive, usually associated with known diverticula.
  • Colorectal cancer - Colorectal malignancy presents with GI bleeding but often with associated weight loss and change in bowel habits, absent in angiodysplasia.
  • Haemorrhoids - Typically present with painless bright red blood per rectum, particularly after defecation, unlike the occult bleeding seen in angiodysplasia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Endoscopic therapy - Endoscopic argon plasma coagulation (APC) or laser therapy can be used to coagulate the bleeding vessels.
  • Iron supplementation - Chronic iron-deficiency anaemia is treated with oral or intravenous iron replacement.
  • Blood transfusion - Indicated in cases of significant anaemia or acute blood loss.
  • Surgical resection - Reserved for patients with refractory or life-threatening bleeding where endoscopic therapy has failed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Recurrent GI bleeding - This is the most common complication, leading to chronic anaemia and the need for repeated interventions.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Chronic blood loss results in anaemia, necessitating long-term iron supplementation and monitoring.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Rare but can occur in the setting of massive bleeding, particularly in older patients with comorbid conditions.
