Valvular Heart Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Valvular heart disease (VHD) refers to any dysfunction or abnormality involving one or more of the heart valves, which can affect blood flow within the heart. The two main types are stenosis (narrowing of the valve opening) and regurgitation (leakage of blood backward through the valve). The most commonly affected valves are the aortic and mitral valves.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: ~2% of adults over 65 years have clinically significant VHD.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hospital admissions related to VHD have increased with the ageing population, with aortic stenosis being the most common condition requiring surgical intervention.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 13% of people over the age of 75 have moderate to severe VHD, with aortic stenosis and mitral regurgitation being the most prevalent types.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • VHD occurs when one or more of the heart valves fail to open or close properly. This dysfunction can be due to congenital defects, degenerative changes, infection (endocarditis), or rheumatic heart disease.
  • Stenosis: The valve leaflets thicken or fuse, narrowing the valve opening. This restricts forward blood flow and increases the workload on the heart, potentially leading to hypertrophy.
  • Regurgitation: Valve leaflets do not close completely, allowing blood to flow backward, causing volume overload and eventually dilatation of the heart chambers.
  • Both conditions can lead to heart failure, arrhythmias, and increased risk of thromboembolism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Many cases are asymptomatic in the early stages and detected incidentally during routine exams or investigations.
  • Progressive phase: Over time, symptoms develop gradually as the valve dysfunction worsens, leading to compensatory changes in the heart.
  • Late phase: As the heart decompensates, symptoms become more severe, leading to heart failure, arrhythmias, and other complications. Intervention is often required in this stage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Caused by increased left atrial and pulmonary pressures due to left-sided valve dysfunction (e.g., mitral regurgitation or aortic stenosis).
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced cardiac output, especially in stenotic conditions where forward blood flow is restricted.
  • Palpitations - Secondary to arrhythmias, such as atrial fibrillation, which may arise due to atrial enlargement in response to volume overload or increased pressure.
  • Angina - Often occurs in aortic stenosis due to increased myocardial oxygen demand with left ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Syncope - Particularly in aortic stenosis, caused by reduced cerebral perfusion during exertion due to fixed cardiac output.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Heart murmur - A common finding, the type and location depend on the affected valve. For example, a systolic murmur at the right upper sternal border may indicate aortic stenosis.
  • Peripheral oedema - Develops in advanced stages due to right-sided heart failure and venous congestion.
  • Jugular venous distension (JVD) - Sign of right-sided heart failure or significant tricuspid regurgitation.
  • Orthopnoea/PND - Reflects left-sided heart failure with pulmonary congestion, often due to mitral valve disease.
  • Reduced exercise tolerance - Often a manifestation of decreased cardiac output and heart failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Indicates severe aortic stenosis or other critical valve disease leading to insufficient cerebral perfusion.
  • Acute pulmonary oedema - Suggests acute valve failure (e.g., ruptured mitral valve chordae tendineae or aortic regurgitation), which is a medical emergency.
  • Severe chest pain - May indicate aortic stenosis with concurrent coronary artery disease or critical aortic regurgitation.
  • Sudden onset of arrhythmia - Atrial fibrillation can lead to rapid decompensation in patients with mitral stenosis or severe mitral regurgitation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic until late stage - Many patients with valvular heart disease may not exhibit symptoms until the disease has progressed significantly.
  • Subtle fatigue or decreased exercise tolerance - Especially in older adults, where these symptoms might be attributed to normal ageing rather than VHD.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Echocardiogram - The mainstay for diagnosis, providing visualisation of valve structure, stenosis severity, and regurgitation volumes. It can also assess chamber size and function.
  • ECG - May show signs of chamber hypertrophy or arrhythmias, such as atrial fibrillation (common in mitral valve disease).
  • Chest X-ray - May show cardiomegaly or pulmonary congestion in cases of advanced disease.
  • Cardiac MRI - Provides more detailed anatomical information, especially in assessing valve morphology and ventricular function.
  • BNP/NT-proBNP - Elevated in cases of heart failure secondary to severe valve dysfunction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male presents with progressive exertional dyspnoea and fatigue over 6 months, with occasional chest pain and a history of hypertension. A systolic murmur is noted on examination, and echocardiography confirms severe aortic stenosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Coronary artery disease (CAD) - Similar presentation with chest pain and dyspnoea, but CAD would show ischaemic changes on ECG or stress testing without the characteristic murmur of VHD.
  • Heart failure (non-valvular) - Could present with similar symptoms, but there would be no murmur or valvular abnormalities on echocardiography.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) - Can mimic aortic stenosis with left ventricular outflow tract obstruction, but echocardiography would show characteristic septal hypertrophy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Medical management - Focuses on symptom relief and managing heart failure (diuretics, ACE inhibitors), and rate control for arrhythmias.
  • Valve replacement or repair - Surgical aortic or mitral valve replacement is the definitive treatment for severe symptomatic stenosis or regurgitation. Transcatheter aortic valve implantation (TAVI) is an option for those at high surgical risk.
  • Anticoagulation - Indicated in patients with mechanical valves or those with atrial fibrillation to prevent thromboembolism.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Valvular Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Common in advanced disease due to volume or pressure overload.
  • Atrial fibrillation - Especially in mitral valve disease due to atrial dilatation.
  • Thromboembolism - Increased risk due to blood stasis in conditions like atrial fibrillation or prosthetic valves.
  • Endocarditis - Valvular abnormalities increase the risk of infective endocarditis.
