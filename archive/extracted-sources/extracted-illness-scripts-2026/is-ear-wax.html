Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Ear wax (cerumen) is a natural secretion of the external ear canal that helps protect the ear from infection, debris, and water. However, excessive accumulation, or cerumen impaction, occurs when ear wax builds up and causes symptoms such as hearing loss, discomfort, or ear fullness.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, cerumen impaction is a common reason for GP consultations, affecting approximately 2-6% of the population.
  • Globally, ear wax impaction prevalence is 10% in children, 5% in adults, and over 30% in older adults, especially those in care homes.
  • It accounts for a significant proportion of ENT outpatient visits in both primary and secondary care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Cerumen is produced by sebaceous and ceruminous glands in the outer third of the ear canal. Normally, the ear self-cleans by the migration of epithelial cells and cerumen out of the canal. Impaction occurs when this process is disrupted by factors like overproduction of cerumen, narrow or tortuous ear canals, use of hearing aids, or frequent ear cleaning, which can push wax deeper.
  • This results in blockage, which can lead to conductive hearing loss, discomfort, and infection risk due to trapped moisture and debris.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • The development of cerumen impaction is typically gradual, with symptoms worsening over weeks to months. Acute symptoms may arise after water exposure or attempts to clean the ear.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Caused by physical blockage of the external auditory canal, leading to conductive hearing loss. This is the most common presenting symptom.
  • Ear discomfort or fullness - Due to the accumulation of cerumen pressing against the ear canal walls.
  • Tinnitus - The blockage may cause ringing or buzzing sounds, as cerumen can interfere with normal sound conduction.
  • Itchiness - Due to irritation of the ear canal lining.
  • Dizziness - Occasionally, cerumen impaction can affect the balance due to pressure effects on the ear.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Visible cerumen in the ear canal - On otoscopic examination, cerumen will be visibly blocking part or all of the external auditory canal.
  • Conductive hearing loss - Tuning fork tests, such as Rinne and Weber tests, may show findings consistent with conductive hearing loss (e.g., bone conduction > air conduction).
  • Normal tympanic membrane - Typically, the eardrum is not affected, but it may be obscured by wax.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Severe pain or discharge - Could indicate otitis externa or perforation of the tympanic membrane.
  • Sudden sensorineural hearing loss - While conductive hearing loss is expected with ear wax impaction, any signs of sensorineural hearing loss require urgent investigation for other causes, such as acoustic neuroma.
  • Facial paralysis - Suggests involvement of the facial nerve, which can indicate a more serious condition such as cholesteatoma or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients, particularly older adults, may have significant cerumen impaction without any noticeable symptoms. This is often detected incidentally during routine exams.
  • Persistent cough - Rarely, cerumen impaction can stimulate the auricular branch of the vagus nerve, leading to a chronic cough.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Otoscopy - Direct visualisation of the ear canal shows the extent of wax impaction, and whether the tympanic membrane is affected.
  • Tuning Fork Tests - Rinne and Weber tests confirm conductive hearing loss. Rinne test shows negative (bone conduction > air conduction), while Weber lateralises to the affected ear.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with gradual onset hearing loss in the right ear, fullness, and occasional tinnitus over the past 2 months. Otoscopy reveals impacted cerumen in the external auditory canal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa - Differentiated by pain, erythema, discharge, and often a history of swimming or ear trauma.
  • Otitis media - Features include middle ear fluid, fever, and eardrum bulging rather than external canal obstruction.
  • Cholesteatoma - Can cause chronic ear discharge and hearing loss, but typically associated with more severe symptoms and signs of bone erosion on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Cerumenolytics - Over-the-counter ear drops containing olive oil, sodium bicarbonate, or hydrogen peroxide-based solutions can soften the wax for easier removal.
  • Irrigation - Syringing with warm water or saline is a common and safe method for wax removal, but contraindicated if there is a history of tympanic membrane perforation.
  • Manual removal - Performed by a healthcare professional using a curette or microsuction for severe impaction or when irrigation is contraindicated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Infection - Impacted cerumen can increase the risk of otitis externa by trapping moisture and bacteria.
  • Tympanic membrane perforation - Risk is increased during attempts at ear wax removal, especially with improper syringing or excessive pressure.
  • Hearing loss - Persistent conductive hearing loss may result if cerumen impaction is not addressed.
