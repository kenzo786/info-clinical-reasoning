Renal Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Renal failure, also known as kidney failure, is a condition where the kidneys are unable to filter waste products and maintain fluid and electrolyte balance adequately. It can be acute (acute kidney injury, AKI) or chronic (chronic kidney disease, CKD), with chronic cases typically progressive and leading to end-stage renal disease (ESRD). The condition results in the accumulation of toxins, fluid imbalance, and electrolyte disturbances.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • In the UK, around 6-7% of the adult population has CKD, with approximately 1-2% progressing to ESRD. Hospital admissions for AKI are common, with a prevalence of 20-30% among hospitalised patients. Worldwide, the prevalence of CKD varies widely, from 8-16%, with AKI rates influenced by health system quality and access to care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Renal failure results from impaired nephron function. In AKI, this is often due to acute tubular necrosis (ATN) from ischaemia or toxins, while CKD is driven by progressive nephron loss, often from diabetes or hypertension. In both cases, the decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) leads to uremia, fluid overload, hyperkalaemia, acidosis, and impaired calcium-phosphate homeostasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • AKI: Onset within hours to days, potentially reversible if underlying cause is treated promptly.
  • CKD: Progressive decline over months to years. Patients remain asymptomatic in early stages but develop symptoms as GFR declines.
  • ESRD: Final stage of CKD where dialysis or transplant is required for survival.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Accumulation of uremic toxins disrupts energy metabolism, leading to tiredness and weakness.
  • Oedema - Fluid overload occurs due to impaired renal excretion, causing swelling in the legs and periorbital region.
  • Shortness of breath - Fluid retention can lead to pulmonary oedema, causing dyspnoea.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Uremic toxins irritate the gastrointestinal tract, causing these symptoms.
  • Pruritus - Accumulation of uremic toxins and phosphate crystals in the skin can cause itching.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Due to volume overload and dysregulation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
  • Peripheral oedema - Fluid accumulation in dependent areas due to impaired kidney function.
  • Pallor - Anaemia resulting from decreased erythropoietin production by the failing kidneys.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Uremic encephalopathy due to toxin accumulation in severe renal failure.
  • Pericardial rub - May indicate uraemic pericarditis in severe, untreated renal failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Oliguria or anuria - Significant reduction or absence of urine output indicates severe AKI or advanced CKD and impending dialysis need.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Life-threatening arrhythmias due to potassium accumulation; needs urgent treatment.
  • Severe acidosis - Uncompensated metabolic acidosis can cause Kussmaul breathing and cardiac dysfunction.
  • Uremic pericarditis - Inflammation of the pericardium caused by uremic toxins, requiring immediate dialysis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic CKD - Early CKD may be entirely asymptomatic, with diagnosis made only on routine blood tests showing raised creatinine or reduced eGFR.
  • Atypical oedema distribution - Fluid overload may present with ascites or pleural effusions without peripheral oedema.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Serum creatinine - Elevated in renal failure due to reduced GFR. In the UK, normal levels are 60-120 µmol/L. A rise of ≥26 µmol/L within 48 hours suggests AKI.
  • eGFR - Estimated glomerular filtration rate, used to stage CKD. A value of <60 mL/min/1.73m² indicates significant kidney impairment.
  • Urinalysis - Proteinuria and haematuria suggest glomerular disease. Urine microscopy may reveal casts or crystals.
  • Serum electrolytes - Hyperkalaemia, hyponatraemia, or metabolic acidosis are common in renal failure.
  • Renal ultrasound - May show shrunken kidneys in CKD or obstructive causes of AKI (hydronephrosis).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • 65-year-old male with a history of diabetes presents with fatigue, oedema, and decreased urine output over the past week. Blood tests show elevated creatinine and potassium levels, with an eGFR of 25 mL/min/1.73m², suggesting chronic kidney disease with acute-on-chronic kidney injury.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Prerenal AKI - Differs by history of dehydration, hypotension, or heart failure, with a rapid rise in creatinine that improves with fluid resuscitation.
  • Postrenal AKI - Differs by a history of urinary obstruction, seen on ultrasound as hydronephrosis.
  • Glomerulonephritis - Presents with proteinuria, haematuria, and hypertension, often associated with autoimmune or infectious triggers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury - Fluid resuscitation for hypovolaemia, avoid nephrotoxic medications, treat underlying causes (e.g., infection, obstruction). In severe cases, renal replacement therapy (RRT) may be needed.
  • Chronic Kidney Disease - Control blood pressure (ACE inhibitors or ARBs), manage diabetes, restrict dietary potassium and phosphate, and start dialysis when necessary. Referral for renal transplantation in eligible patients.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Most common cause of death in CKD patients due to hypertension, left ventricular hypertrophy, and hyperlipidaemia.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Can cause fatal arrhythmias if untreated.
  • Bone disease - Renal osteodystrophy due to disrupted calcium and phosphate balance leads to fractures and skeletal deformities.
