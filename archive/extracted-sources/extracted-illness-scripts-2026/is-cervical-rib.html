Cervical Rib
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • A cervical rib is a congenital anomaly in which an extra rib arises from the seventh cervical vertebra. It can vary in size from a small nub of bone to a fully formed rib and may be unilateral or bilateral. While many cases are asymptomatic, it can compress surrounding structures like the brachial plexus or subclavian artery, leading to neurovascular symptoms commonly referred to as thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Cervical ribs are relatively rare, affecting approximately 0.5-1% of the population worldwide. The majority of cases are asymptomatic, with only a small proportion developing symptoms of thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS). Prevalence in the UK is consistent with global statistics. Hospital cases in the UK are more likely to involve symptomatic individuals, such as those presenting with neurogenic or vascular complications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • A cervical rib forms due to abnormal segmentation during embryonic development. It may compress adjacent structures like the brachial plexus, subclavian artery, or subclavian vein, leading to symptoms. Compression of the brachial plexus can result in neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome, while vascular compression may lead to arterial or venous thoracic outlet syndrome. Chronic compression can cause ischaemia, pain, and neurological dysfunction in the upper limb.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • The condition is typically present from birth but may remain asymptomatic for years or even a lifetime. Symptomatic cases tend to develop gradually over months to years, particularly if repetitive upper limb movement, trauma, or postural changes exacerbate the compression of neurovascular structures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Neck and shoulder pain – Often due to brachial plexus compression, leading to pain radiating from the neck into the shoulder or upper limb.
  • Numbness or tingling in the arm – Neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome can lead to sensory deficits, including paraesthesia, particularly in the ulnar nerve distribution.
  • Weakness of the hand – Compression of motor nerves within the brachial plexus can result in weakness, most commonly affecting hand grip strength.
  • Ischaemic pain in the arm – Subclavian artery compression may reduce blood flow to the arm, leading to ischaemic symptoms such as pain or coldness in the extremity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Positive Adson’s test – Loss of the radial pulse when the patient turns their head toward the symptomatic side and inhales deeply, indicating vascular compression of the subclavian artery.
  • Weak or absent radial pulse – This may be present in severe cases of arterial thoracic outlet syndrome due to subclavian artery compression.
  • Muscle wasting of the hand – Chronic neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome may result in atrophy of intrinsic hand muscles due to long-term nerve compression.
  • Swelling or discolouration of the arm – Venous thoracic outlet syndrome may result in venous congestion, leading to swelling and cyanosis of the affected arm.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Severe arm ischaemia – Sudden or severe arm pain with pallor and coldness suggests significant subclavian artery compression or embolism, a potential surgical emergency.
  • Venous thrombosis – Swelling, discolouration, and pain in the arm may indicate subclavian vein thrombosis (Paget-Schroetter syndrome), requiring urgent treatment to prevent further complications.
  • Progressive neurological deficits – Worsening sensory or motor loss may indicate irreversible nerve damage due to chronic compression, requiring prompt intervention to prevent permanent dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Isolated hand symptoms – In some cases, patients may present with isolated hand weakness or numbness without neck or shoulder pain, potentially leading to misdiagnosis as carpal tunnel syndrome or ulnar neuropathy.
  • Unilateral symptoms only – Even if a cervical rib is present bilaterally, patients may only develop symptoms on one side, complicating diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • X-ray of the neck – Reveals the presence of a cervical rib, confirming the anatomical anomaly. Often the first-line investigation in suspected cases.
  • Ultrasound Doppler – Assesses blood flow through the subclavian vessels and may reveal arterial compression or venous thrombosis.
  • Nerve conduction studies – Useful in neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome to evaluate brachial plexus function and locate sites of nerve compression.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) – Provides detailed imaging of soft tissues and may help visualise nerve or vascular compression not seen on X-ray.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a six-month history of neck pain radiating into her right shoulder and arm, accompanied by intermittent paraesthesia in her right hand. Examination reveals a positive Adson’s test, and X-ray confirms the presence of a cervical rib.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome – Differs in that symptoms are usually confined to the hand and wrist without neck or shoulder involvement.
  • Ulnar neuropathy – Causes sensory and motor deficits in the ulnar distribution but typically without vascular involvement or whole-arm ischaemia.
  • Pancoast tumour – May mimic neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome with shoulder pain and hand weakness but is often accompanied by systemic features like weight loss and is visible on imaging as a mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Conservative management – Involves physiotherapy, posture correction, and pain management for mild symptoms.
  • Surgical resection – Indicated in cases of severe neurogenic or vascular thoracic outlet syndrome or where conservative measures fail. Cervical rib resection relieves compression of neurovascular structures.
  • Anticoagulation – Required in cases of venous thrombosis to prevent further clot formation and embolisation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Chronic nerve damage – Long-term compression can result in irreversible brachial plexus damage, leading to permanent sensory or motor deficits.
  • Arterial embolism – In severe cases of arterial compression, emboli can form and migrate distally, leading to acute limb ischaemia.
  • Venous thrombosis – Can result in pulmonary embolism if untreated, significantly increasing morbidity and mortality.
