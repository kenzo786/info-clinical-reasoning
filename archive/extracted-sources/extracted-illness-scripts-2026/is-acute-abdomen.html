Acute Abdomen
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Acute abdomen is a term for sudden, severe abdominal pain requiring urgent medical attention. It encompasses various causes, often associated with inflammation, obstruction, infection, or vascular impairment of abdominal organs. Key features include pain onset within hours to days and often significant tenderness upon examination.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Acute abdomen is a common presentation in primary care, with around 5% of emergency visits relating to acute abdominal pain.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: 10-15% of emergency department cases relate to acute abdomen, with cases varying significantly by aetiology.
  • Global prevalence: Rates vary globally, with acute abdomen commonly encountered in emergency and surgical settings, affecting 6-12% of hospitalised patients in high-resource settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Acute abdomen results from various pathophysiological processes, depending on the underlying cause:
    • Inflammation (e.g., appendicitis, cholecystitis): Inflammatory mediators cause localised pain, peritoneal irritation, and systemic symptoms.
    • Obstruction (e.g., bowel obstruction): Blockage increases luminal pressure, leading to pain, bowel distention, and possible ischemia.
    • Ischaemia (e.g., mesenteric ischaemia): Reduced blood flow causes tissue hypoxia and necrosis, often with severe, sudden pain.
    • Perforation (e.g., perforated ulcer): Leakage of gastrointestinal contents leads to peritonitis, triggering intense pain and systemic inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms develop within hours to days, often abruptly. Initial pain and symptoms may localise depending on the underlying cause.
  • Progressive phase: Without treatment, pain may intensify, spread, or change location (e.g., migratory pain in appendicitis), with associated systemic signs (e.g., fever, tachycardia).
  • Complications phase: If untreated, risks of systemic infection, sepsis, and multiorgan failure increase, particularly in cases with perforation or vascular compromise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain - Typically severe and acute, often localised initially (e.g., right lower quadrant in appendicitis) due to localised inflammatory or ischaemic processes.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Common with many causes of acute abdomen; results from gastrointestinal irritation or obstruction.
  • Fever - Suggests an infectious or inflammatory aetiology; caused by the body’s systemic response to infection or tissue injury.
  • Loss of appetite - Often present in inflammatory conditions like appendicitis or cholecystitis due to visceral inflammation affecting the vagal response.
  • Constipation or absence of flatus - Common in bowel obstruction as peristalsis is impaired.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Rebound tenderness - Suggests peritonitis; pain upon release of pressure reflects peritoneal irritation.
  • Guarding - Reflexive muscle contraction over painful area, often seen in peritoneal irritation.
  • Tachycardia - Indicates systemic response to pain, fever, or infection.
  • Hypotension (late stage) - May suggest sepsis or significant fluid loss from third-spacing in peritonitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe pain - Indicates possible vascular compromise (e.g., ruptured AAA, mesenteric ischaemia).
  • Hypotension or shock - Suggests potential for systemic infection (sepsis) or major bleeding (e.g., ruptured ectopic pregnancy).
  • Rigid abdomen - Sign of peritonitis; suggests perforation or intra-abdominal infection.
  • Haematemesis or rectal bleeding - Can indicate upper or lower GI bleeding, often linked to severe underlying pathology (e.g., perforated ulcer, ischaemic bowel).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Subacute or gradual onset - May occur in elderly or immunocompromised patients; pain may be mild despite serious pathology.
  • Minimal pain but systemic signs - Seen in diabetic patients with neuropathy; systemic symptoms (fever, tachycardia) may be more prominent than pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - FBC (elevated WBC in infection), CRP (raised in inflammation), U&E (electrolyte imbalance in dehydration or renal impairment).
  • Imaging - Abdominal X-ray (air under diaphragm in perforation), CT abdomen (to assess causes like appendicitis, ischaemia), Ultrasound (gallstones or AAA).
  • Urine analysis - Rules out UTI, assesses for ketones in diabetic ketoacidosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with sudden onset of severe right lower quadrant pain, nausea, and low-grade fever for the last 12 hours. Physical examination reveals rebound tenderness and guarding.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis - Localised right lower quadrant pain, migratory pattern; distinct from more diffuse pain in peritonitis.
  • Cholecystitis - Right upper quadrant pain, often radiating to shoulder; Murphy's sign positive, differentiating it from other causes.
  • Peptic ulcer disease with perforation - Epigastric pain radiating across abdomen; free air under diaphragm on imaging.
  • Pancreatitis - Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back; elevated serum amylase/lipase.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Initial resuscitation - IV fluids, analgesia, antibiotics if infection suspected; follows UK Resuscitation Council guidelines.
  • Surgical intervention - Indicated for conditions like appendicitis, perforation, or bowel obstruction based on imaging and clinical assessment.
  • Monitoring - Regular vital signs, fluid balance assessment; intensive monitoring if sepsis is suspected.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Secondary to untreated infection or perforation; can progress rapidly without intervention.
  • Peritonitis - Inflammatory response to GI leakage into the peritoneum, causing severe pain, systemic symptoms, and risk of multiorgan failure.
  • Adhesions - Scar tissue from surgical intervention; may lead to future bowel obstruction.
