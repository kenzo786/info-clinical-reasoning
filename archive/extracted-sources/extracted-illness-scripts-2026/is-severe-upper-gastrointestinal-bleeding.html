Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Severe upper gastrointestinal bleeding (UGIB) refers to significant bleeding originating from a source proximal to the ligament of Treitz, including the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum. It manifests as haematemesis (vomiting blood) or melaena (dark, tarry stools). Severe UGIB is defined by haemodynamic instability or a substantial drop in haemoglobin levels requiring urgent intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Incidence of UGIB in primary care is about 50-150 per 100,000 adults annually.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: UGIB accounts for 50-100 hospital admissions per 100,000 people each year, with about 10-20% requiring endoscopic or surgical intervention.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, UGIB incidence ranges from 40 to 150 per 100,000 individuals, with higher rates in developing nations due to untreated Helicobacter pylori infections.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • UGIB can arise from multiple sources, including peptic ulcers, oesophageal varices, gastritis, and malignancies. Peptic ulcers result from an imbalance between gastric acid secretion and mucosal protection, often exacerbated by NSAID use or H. pylori infection. Varices are engorged veins due to portal hypertension, commonly seen in cirrhotic patients, which can rupture under pressure.
  • The bleeding leads to haemodynamic compromise, with hypovolaemia triggering compensatory mechanisms such as tachycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction, and a drop in urine output. If untreated, severe blood loss can cause shock and multi-organ failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Rapid onset of haematemesis or melaena, often within hours to days. If untreated, rapid deterioration with hypotension, tachycardia, and shock may follow.
  • Stabilisation Phase: After initial resuscitation and management, bleeding may cease, but close monitoring is required to detect re-bleeding.
  • Recovery Phase: In stable patients, underlying causes (e.g., ulcers, varices) are treated. Full recovery may take days to weeks, with ongoing risk of recurrence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis: Vomiting of bright red blood or "coffee-ground" material due to blood exposure to gastric acid. This suggests bleeding from the stomach or oesophagus.
  • Melaena: Black, tarry stools caused by the digestion of blood, indicating bleeding has occurred proximal to the colon.
  • Syncope or Dizziness: Caused by hypovolaemia, resulting from significant blood loss and reduced perfusion to the brain.
  • Epigastric Pain: Common in peptic ulcer disease, due to irritation of the stomach lining or duodenum.
  • Fatigue and Weakness: Resulting from anaemia due to chronic or severe blood loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Hypotension: Low blood pressure due to hypovolaemia from blood loss.
  • Tachycardia: A compensatory increase in heart rate in response to reduced circulating volume.
  • Pallor: Pale skin due to reduced haemoglobin and perfusion, often a sign of significant blood loss.
  • Cool, Clammy Skin: Due to vasoconstriction as part of the body’s compensatory response to shock.
  • Reduced Urine Output: A sign of hypovolaemia and impaired renal perfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic Instability: Signifies ongoing bleeding or shock, and may require urgent transfusion or surgery.
  • Altered Mental Status: Suggests severe hypovolaemia leading to poor cerebral perfusion or multi-organ failure.
  • Recurrent Bleeding After Stabilisation: May indicate a high-risk lesion such as a large peptic ulcer or oesophageal varices.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Occult GI Bleeding: Some patients, especially the elderly, may present with iron-deficiency anaemia or positive faecal occult blood testing without overt haematemesis or melaena.
  • Chronic Fatigue or Dyspnoea: Chronic UGIB may present subtly as fatigue or shortness of breath due to progressive anaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Reduced haemoglobin and haematocrit levels indicating blood loss.
  • Endoscopy: Gold standard for diagnosing the source of bleeding; allows for visualisation and therapeutic intervention.
  • Urea-to-Creatinine Ratio: Elevated urea with a normal creatinine suggests a significant upper GI bleed, as blood is digested and absorbed in the gut, leading to increased nitrogenous waste.
  • Coagulation Profile: Assess for any underlying coagulopathy, especially in patients on anticoagulants.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old male presents with sudden onset of haematemesis, dizziness, and hypotension. He has a history of peptic ulcer disease and NSAID use. On examination, he is pale and tachycardic, with a systolic blood pressure of 90 mmHg.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal Varices: Common in patients with cirrhosis; presents with massive, painless haematemesis.
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear: Vomiting followed by sudden haematemesis, usually less severe than peptic ulcer bleeding.
  • Gastric Cancer: Chronic blood loss with weight loss and early satiety, usually not associated with haemodynamic instability in early stages.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Initial Resuscitation: ABC approach, with intravenous fluids and blood transfusion as required to stabilise the patient.
  • Endoscopic Therapy: Endoscopic clipping, banding, or injection to control bleeding at the site of ulcers or varices.
  • Pharmacological Management: Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are used to reduce acid secretion and stabilise clots. Terlipressin is used in suspected variceal bleeding to reduce portal pressure.
  • Surgical Intervention: Rarely required but may be necessary if endoscopic measures fail or if there is massive bleeding.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhagic Shock: Severe hypovolaemia leading to multi-organ failure, with a high risk of mortality if untreated.
  • Recurrent Bleeding: Approximately 10-20% of patients experience re-bleeding after initial haemostasis, particularly those with high-risk lesions.
  • Aspiration Pneumonia: Risk increases if a patient vomits large volumes of blood, potentially leading to respiratory compromise.
