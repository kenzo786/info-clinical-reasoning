Chronic Infections
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Chronic infections refer to infections that persist over a prolonged period, typically lasting several months to years. Unlike acute infections, which are short-lived and often self-limiting, chronic infections may evade the immune system and continue to cause systemic or localized symptoms due to ongoing microbial presence and inflammation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Chronic infections make up a small but notable percentage, with chronic viral infections such as hepatitis B, hepatitis C, and HIV affecting around 1-2% of the population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Chronic infections are more commonly seen in hospital settings, especially in immunocompromised patients, where they can account for approximately 10-15% of infectious disease admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies significantly by infection type, with chronic hepatitis B and C affecting over 350 million and 71 million people globally, respectively, and tuberculosis affecting approximately 10 million people worldwide annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Chronic infections often result from pathogens that have developed mechanisms to evade or resist the host immune response. These include encapsulation, mutation, or latency in immune-privileged sites.
  • In chronic infections, ongoing inflammation and immune cell recruitment contribute to tissue damage, leading to fibrosis and dysfunction in affected organs (e.g., liver in hepatitis).
  • Persistent pathogens can alter immune function, promoting an immune-tolerant state or leading to chronic immune activation, which contributes to symptoms and complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Acute infection symptoms may develop if the infection starts suddenly, potentially including fever, malaise, and localised symptoms depending on infection site.
  • Latent phase: Symptoms may decrease as the infection reaches a steady state, but pathogen replication continues at low levels.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent low-level symptoms, with episodic exacerbations due to immune system responses, or even asymptomatic progression in some cases (e.g., chronic hepatitis or HIV).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common in chronic infections due to ongoing immune activation and cytokine release.
  • Fever - May be intermittent, often due to cytokine-mediated responses to persistent infection.
  • Weight loss - Often seen in chronic infections like tuberculosis, associated with high metabolic demands of inflammation and cytokine effects on appetite.
  • Night sweats - Characteristic of chronic infections like tuberculosis, thought to result from cytokine fluctuations.
  • Localised pain - Depending on infection site, such as joint pain in chronic osteomyelitis or abdominal pain in chronic hepatitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Low-grade fever - Persistent due to ongoing inflammation.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Often present due to lymph node immune response to infection.
  • Organ-specific findings - For example, hepatomegaly or splenomegaly in chronic hepatitis.
  • Skin changes - Infections like syphilis or tuberculosis may have characteristic skin manifestations.
  • Cachexia - Weight loss and muscle wasting, common in advanced or systemic infections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Severe, unexplained weight loss - May indicate progression to disseminated infection or malignancy in chronic infections.
  • High fever with rigors - Suggests possible superimposed acute bacterial infection or sepsis.
  • Altered mental status - Indicates possible central nervous system involvement or metabolic encephalopathy in systemic infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic carriers in cases like chronic hepatitis B or HIV, where low viral loads may not produce noticeable symptoms.
  • Subtle mental health changes, as in latent tuberculosis, where chronic immune activation can lead to low-grade depression or anxiety.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - Full blood count may show anaemia of chronic disease or raised white cell count in inflammation.
  • Serology - Detects chronic infections like HIV or hepatitis B/C through antigen or antibody presence.
  • Imaging - X-rays or CT scans may reveal structural changes like lung cavitation in tuberculosis.
  • Cultures - Positive cultures (e.g., mycobacterial cultures) confirm bacterial chronic infections.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a 6-month history of fatigue, low-grade fever, and weight loss. Physical examination reveals lymphadenopathy and hepatomegaly, and serology confirms chronic hepatitis B infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - Differentiated by absence of infectious signs such as fever or lymphadenopathy.
  • Malignancy - Often with more rapid onset, accompanied by severe weight loss and sometimes anaemia.
  • Autoimmune diseases - Conditions like lupus may mimic infection but usually have other specific autoantibodies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Antiviral/antibacterial agents - E.g., antiretroviral therapy for HIV, or antibiotics for tuberculosis.
  • Supportive therapy - Nutrition, hydration, and mental health support.
  • Vaccination (prevention) - Vaccines like hepatitis B to reduce chronic infection risk.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Infections"][/sc]
  • Fibrosis or organ failure - Common in chronic liver infections like hepatitis, due to persistent inflammation and scarring.
  • Immune compromise - Patients with chronic infections like HIV are at high risk for opportunistic infections.
  • Cancer risk - E.g., hepatocellular carcinoma in chronic hepatitis B/C due to cellular mutation risks from chronic inflammation.
