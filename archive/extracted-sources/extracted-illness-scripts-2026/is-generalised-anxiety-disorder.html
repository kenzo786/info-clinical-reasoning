Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is a common psychiatric disorder characterised by excessive, persistent worry about a variety of everyday activities and events, lasting for six months or more. This worry is often difficult to control, is disproportionate to the circumstances, and is accompanied by symptoms such as restlessness, muscle tension, fatigue, and difficulties in concentration.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, GAD affects around 5% of adults, with a higher prevalence in females and individuals over 35.
  • UK hospital prevalence is lower, as GAD is often managed in primary care settings.
  • Worldwide, GAD prevalence is estimated at around 3-6%, with significant variation between countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • GAD is believed to involve dysregulation of neurotransmitters, particularly serotonin, norepinephrine, and gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA).
  • Functional and structural abnormalities in brain regions associated with fear and emotional regulation (e.g., amygdala, prefrontal cortex) play a role in heightened anxiety responses.
  • Chronic activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis leads to stress hormone imbalance, contributing to physiological symptoms like muscle tension and fatigue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • GAD is often a chronic condition, with symptoms developing gradually over months or years.
  • Episodes may fluctuate, with periods of improvement and exacerbation triggered by stressful events.
  • Without treatment, GAD can persist long-term, although symptoms may lessen with age in some individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Excessive worry - Persistent, uncontrollable worry about various aspects of daily life; due to hyperactivity in fear-regulation centres in the brain.
  • Restlessness or feeling “on edge” - Commonly described as feeling tense or unable to relax; related to chronic sympathetic nervous system activation.
  • Muscle tension - Due to chronic stress and heightened HPA axis activity, often presenting as neck, back, or jaw tightness.
  • Fatigue - Often due to sustained mental exertion from chronic worry and poor sleep quality; linked to neurotransmitter dysregulation.
  • Difficulty concentrating - Due to hyperfocus on worries, leading to attention deficits in other areas.
  • Sleep disturbances - Including difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep, caused by excessive worrying and dysregulated circadian rhythms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Restlessness or fidgeting - Visible restlessness, such as pacing or tapping, due to heightened arousal and anxiety.
  • Muscle stiffness - Palpable tightness in muscles, particularly neck and shoulders, due to chronic muscle tension.
  • Hypervigilance - Patients may appear alert and hyperaware of surroundings; related to chronic anxiety and alertness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Suicidal thoughts or behaviours - May indicate severe anxiety or comorbid depression; urgent assessment needed to prevent self-harm.
  • Significant weight loss - Can suggest chronic stress response or indicate other medical causes of anxiety-like symptoms.
  • Substance abuse - May co-occur in attempts to self-medicate, requiring a comprehensive substance use history.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms - Such as nausea or irritable bowel symptoms, possibly due to anxiety-induced dysregulation of the enteric nervous system.
  • Generalised physical symptoms - Including chronic pain, especially in older adults, which may obscure the primary psychological symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • GAD-7 questionnaire - A validated self-report tool; scores help assess severity of anxiety symptoms.
  • Thyroid function tests - Hyperthyroidism can mimic anxiety symptoms; TSH and T4 levels are useful in ruling out thyroid disease.
  • Complete blood count - To exclude anaemia or other medical conditions that may contribute to fatigue and anxiety-like symptoms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old female presents with a six-month history of excessive worry about multiple daily activities, associated with restlessness, muscle tension, and difficulty sleeping.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Major Depressive Disorder - Can present with fatigue and concentration issues, but is more marked by pervasive low mood.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Overlaps with anxiety symptoms like restlessness and palpitations; differentiated by elevated T3/T4, suppressed TSH.
  • Panic Disorder - Episodic panic attacks rather than chronic worry; sudden onset of intense anxiety episodes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) - First-line psychological therapy, focused on managing thought patterns and behaviours.
  • Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) - First-line pharmacotherapy, e.g., sertraline; reduces anxiety by balancing serotonin levels.
  • Pregabalin - Alternative if SSRIs are ineffective or contraindicated; useful for severe anxiety symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Depression - Common comorbidity; anxiety can worsen depressive symptoms and increase risk of suicidal thoughts.
  • Substance use disorders - Patients may self-medicate, leading to dependency on substances such as alcohol.
  • Cardiovascular issues - Chronic anxiety has been associated with hypertension and increased risk of heart disease.
