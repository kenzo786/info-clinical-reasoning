Disuse Atrophy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Disuse atrophy refers to the gradual loss of muscle mass and strength due to prolonged inactivity or reduced use of skeletal muscles. This condition often occurs when there is reduced movement or immobility due to injury, prolonged bed rest, or neurological impairments. It is characterised by a reduction in muscle size, strength, and endurance.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: The condition is relatively common among patients who experience long periods of immobility, including the elderly or those recovering from surgery or trauma. Approximately 1 in 10 elderly patients may experience disuse atrophy due to age-related immobility.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Disuse atrophy is prevalent in hospitalised patients, especially in intensive care units and post-operative settings, affecting 20-40% of patients depending on length of hospitalisation.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Global rates of disuse atrophy are hard to estimate, but the condition is common among patients with chronic illness, neurological disorders, and prolonged immobilisation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Disuse atrophy occurs when skeletal muscles are not subjected to regular contraction or loading. Lack of use leads to a reduction in muscle protein synthesis and an increase in muscle protein degradation. Key mechanisms include:
    • Decreased mechanical load and neural stimulation lead to the downregulation of pathways involved in muscle growth, such as the insulin-like growth factor (IGF-1) signalling pathway.
    • Activation of the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway increases protein degradation.
    • Over time, muscle fibres shrink, particularly type I (slow-twitch) fibres, resulting in reduced muscle mass and strength.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Disuse atrophy can begin within days of immobility. Noticeable muscle loss may occur within two weeks.
  • Subacute Phase: Over 4-6 weeks, there is significant reduction in muscle size and strength, particularly in bedridden patients.
  • Chronic Phase: After 3-6 months of disuse, the muscle atrophy may become severe, and recovery requires prolonged rehabilitation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Muscle Weakness - The most common symptom, due to reduced muscle fibre size and loss of contractile proteins.
  • Reduced Muscle Mass - Patients often notice visible muscle wasting in affected limbs or areas, due to loss of muscle bulk.
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced muscle endurance, even light physical activities may become tiring.
  • Joint Stiffness - Immobility often leads to stiffness in joints due to a lack of movement, further limiting mobility.
  • Immobility - In severe cases, the affected muscles are unable to support movement or weight-bearing activities.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Muscle Wasting - Visible reduction in muscle bulk, especially in unused or immobilised limbs.
  • Decreased Muscle Tone - Muscles may feel flaccid or soft upon palpation due to loss of contractile proteins.
  • Weakness on Examination - Reduced strength on manual muscle testing (graded using MRC scale).
  • Impaired Mobility - Difficulty or inability to perform simple movements or maintain posture due to muscle weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Rapid Muscle Wasting - Can indicate an underlying neuromuscular disorder or malignancy rather than simple disuse atrophy.
  • Loss of Reflexes - This could suggest a neurological cause for atrophy, such as motor neuron disease or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Severe Pain - Unexplained or severe pain alongside atrophy may point to a different cause, such as complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) or osteomyelitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Early Stages - In some cases, muscle atrophy may occur without noticeable symptoms until significant weakness develops.
  • Disuse Atrophy in Bedridden Elderly Patients - Often attributed to "old age" rather than a reversible condition, leading to under-treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Muscle Strength Testing - Manual muscle testing using the Medical Research Council (MRC) scale to assess degree of weakness.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - EMG can help distinguish between disuse atrophy and neurogenic causes by showing normal nerve function in disuse atrophy.
  • Muscle Biopsy - Rarely needed, but can show fibre atrophy with preserved architecture, ruling out primary myopathies.
  • Imaging - MRI or ultrasound may show reduced muscle bulk and fatty infiltration, particularly in chronic stages.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male presents with progressive muscle weakness and visible wasting of the lower limbs following 8 weeks of bed rest after a hip fracture. He reports difficulty walking and fatigue.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Sarcopenia - Similar age-related loss of muscle mass, but with more diffuse and gradual progression compared to disuse atrophy.
  • Motor Neurone Disease - Presents with muscle atrophy and weakness, but typically involves fasciculations, hyperreflexia, and a progressive course.
  • Myopathy - Weakness is more proximal, often associated with inflammatory markers or muscle enzymes like creatine kinase (CK).
  • Peripheral Neuropathy - Accompanied by sensory loss, reflex changes, and distal muscle wasting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Physical Therapy: Regular physiotherapy and muscle strengthening exercises to promote muscle growth and restore function.
  • Nutritional Support: Ensuring adequate protein intake to support muscle protein synthesis.
  • Gradual Mobilisation: A tailored programme to gradually increase activity and reduce immobility.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Contractures: Prolonged disuse can lead to irreversible joint contractures due to shortening of muscles and tendons.
  • Bone Loss: Chronic immobility can result in reduced bone density and increased risk of fractures (disuse osteoporosis).
  • Pressure Ulcers: In bed-bound patients, muscle atrophy can exacerbate the risk of pressure sores due to reduced cushioning over bony prominences.
