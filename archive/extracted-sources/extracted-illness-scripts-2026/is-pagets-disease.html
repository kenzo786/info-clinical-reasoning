Paget's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Paget's disease of bone is a chronic, localised skeletal disorder characterised by excessive and disorganised bone remodelling. It leads to enlarged and weakened bones, which may be prone to fractures, deformities, and joint problems. The disease often affects the pelvis, spine, skull, and long bones, with both lytic (bone resorption) and sclerotic (excessive bone formation) phases. Paget's disease is often asymptomatic, discovered incidentally through imaging or raised alkaline phosphatase levels.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK general population: Approximately 2-4% of adults over 55 years old.
  • Hospital prevalence: Around 1 in 2000 hospital admissions relate to Paget's disease.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher in the UK, New Zealand, and Australia, with lower rates in Asia and Scandinavia. The disease primarily affects people of European descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • The disease begins with overactive osteoclasts, leading to excessive bone resorption (lytic phase). In response, osteoblasts overcompensate by producing large amounts of new bone in a disorganised manner (sclerotic phase). This results in structurally abnormal and weakened bones that are prone to deformities, fractures, and arthritis.
  • Osteoclast dysfunction may be linked to viral infections (e.g., paramyxovirus) or genetic mutations (e.g., SQSTM1 gene).
  • The abnormal bone structure increases vascularity, leading to warmth over affected areas.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Paget's disease is typically chronic, progressing slowly over decades. Many patients remain asymptomatic for years. The disease may be discovered incidentally or due to complications such as fractures, deformities, or secondary arthritis.
  • Phases of the disease include the initial lytic phase, followed by the mixed lytic and sclerotic phase, and finally a sclerotic phase where the bone is dense but abnormal.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Bone pain - Most common symptom, often deep and aching. This is caused by microfractures or abnormal bone remodelling.
  • Joint pain - Results from secondary osteoarthritis due to joint deformities caused by abnormal bone growth.
  • Warmth over affected bones - Due to increased vascularity of the abnormal bone.
  • Hearing loss - Involvement of the skull, particularly the temporal bone, can lead to conductive or sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Headache - Skull involvement may lead to thickening, causing pressure on surrounding structures.
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients have no symptoms and are diagnosed based on elevated alkaline phosphatase or imaging done for another reason.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Bony deformities - Including bowing of long bones or an enlarged skull due to abnormal bone growth.
  • Fractures - Pathological fractures are common due to weakened, disorganised bone structure.
  • Hearing loss - Seen in patients with skull involvement, particularly affecting the temporal bone.
  • Increased warmth over affected areas - A sign of increased bone vascularity in the affected regions.
  • Reduced range of motion in joints - Due to secondary osteoarthritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden increase in bone pain - May signify a pathological fracture or progression to osteosarcoma (rare but serious complication).
  • Neurological symptoms - Such as weakness, paraesthesia, or incontinence, which can occur due to compression of the spinal cord or cranial nerves in advanced disease.
  • Sudden deafness - Indicative of temporal bone involvement with compression of the auditory structures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated skull involvement - Can lead to purely neurological or hearing symptoms without the classical signs of deformity or pain.
  • Silent presentation - Incidentally discovered on imaging or blood tests without any symptoms, especially in older adults.
  • Asymptomatic vertebral involvement - Can cause vertebral compression fractures with minimal trauma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Serum alkaline phosphatase (ALP) - Elevated in most patients due to increased osteoblastic activity. Normal calcium and phosphate levels help differentiate from other bone disorders.
  • X-ray - Shows lytic and sclerotic bone changes, cortical thickening, and bone expansion.
  • Bone scintigraphy - Helps identify areas of high metabolic activity in the bone, particularly useful in asymptomatic patients or unclear cases.
  • Serum calcium and phosphate - Usually normal, which differentiates Paget's disease from primary hyperparathyroidism or osteomalacia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • An elderly male presents with chronic deep bone pain in the pelvis and femur. He reports hearing loss over the past year, with a family history of bone disorders. X-rays show cortical thickening and bowing of the femur. Serum alkaline phosphatase is elevated.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia - Causes bone pain, but X-rays show Looser's zones and lab results reveal low calcium and phosphate, unlike Paget's disease.
  • Osteosarcoma - Rapidly worsening pain and more aggressive bone destruction on imaging; often requires biopsy for definitive diagnosis.
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism - Elevated calcium levels and subperiosteal bone resorption, unlike the normal calcium in Paget's disease.
  • Metastatic bone disease - Typically presents with multiple lytic lesions, while Paget's disease shows both lytic and sclerotic changes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Bisphosphonates - First-line treatment, such as zoledronic acid or risedronate, which help to inhibit bone resorption by osteoclasts.
  • Analgesia - NSAIDs or paracetamol for bone and joint pain.
  • Calcium and vitamin D supplementation - Recommended to support bone health during bisphosphonate treatment.
  • Orthopaedic surgery - For fractures, severe deformities, or joint replacements in advanced disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Fractures - Occur frequently due to weakened bone structure.
  • Osteoarthritis - Common in joints adjacent to affected bones due to deformities and abnormal stress.
  • Osteosarcoma - A rare but serious complication, occurring in less than 1% of patients.
  • Neurological complications - Including spinal stenosis and cranial nerve compression.
