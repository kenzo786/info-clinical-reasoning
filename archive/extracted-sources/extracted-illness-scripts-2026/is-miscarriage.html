Miscarriage
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • A miscarriage, also known as a spontaneous abortion, is the loss of a pregnancy before 24 weeks of gestation. It is characterised by the cessation of embryonic or foetal development, leading to uterine expulsion.
  • In clinical terms, miscarriage is often subdivided into threatened, incomplete, complete, missed, and recurrent miscarriage based on clinical presentation and ultrasound findings.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • In the UK, miscarriage occurs in approximately 15-20% of recognised pregnancies in general practice settings.
  • Hospital data suggest an annual miscarriage rate of around 50,000 cases in England.
  • Globally, the prevalence of miscarriage is estimated to be 10-20% of clinically recognised pregnancies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • The process begins with embryonic or foetal demise, often due to chromosomal abnormalities, accounting for up to 70% of cases.
  • Failure of placental development leads to insufficient nutrient and oxygen transfer, exacerbating foetal compromise.
  • Inflammatory or immune-mediated responses, such as antiphospholipid syndrome, may disrupt implantation or placental function.
  • The uterus responds to foetal demise with contractions to expel the pregnancy tissue, facilitated by hormonal changes, including a drop in progesterone.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic with embryonic demise detected on routine ultrasound.
  • Symptomatic phase: May involve vaginal bleeding, pelvic pain, and the passage of tissue over hours to days.
  • Resolution phase: Uterine involution and cessation of bleeding, typically over 1-2 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Vaginal bleeding - The most common presenting symptom, resulting from disruption of the decidua and chorionic tissue.
  • Lower abdominal pain - Cramping pain caused by uterine contractions attempting to expel pregnancy tissue.
  • Passage of tissue - Expulsion of products of conception, particularly in incomplete or complete miscarriage.
  • Asymptomatic - Common in missed miscarriages, where the foetus has died but there are no clinical signs.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Pelvic tenderness - Due to inflammation or uterine contractions.
  • Cervical dilation - Indicated in inevitable or incomplete miscarriage, allowing passage of tissue.
  • Visible products of conception - May be present at the os or expelled vaginally.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage - Suggests incomplete miscarriage or uterine atony and may lead to hypovolaemic shock.
  • Sepsis - Can result from retained products of conception; presents with fever, tachycardia, and systemic signs of infection.
  • Unilateral severe pelvic pain - Could indicate ectopic pregnancy rather than miscarriage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Chronic bleeding with anaemia - May occur in retained products of conception or incomplete miscarriage.
  • Absence of pain or bleeding - Common in missed miscarriages detected incidentally.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound scan - Confirms embryonic or foetal demise; key findings include absence of foetal heart activity or empty gestational sac.
  • Serum hCG levels - Low or declining levels indicate failed pregnancy.
  • Full blood count - Identifies anaemia or signs of infection in suspected sepsis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 1-week history of vaginal bleeding and cramping abdominal pain at 8 weeks gestation. No passage of tissue reported. Ultrasound confirms an empty gestational sac.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Typically presents with unilateral pain, adnexal mass, and rising hCG despite absent intrauterine pregnancy on ultrasound.
  • Molar pregnancy - Characterised by excessive hCG levels and "snowstorm" appearance on ultrasound.
  • Implantation bleeding - Light bleeding occurring around the time of missed menstruation without cramping or other symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Expectant management - Suitable for stable patients; involves monitoring for spontaneous resolution over 1-2 weeks.
  • Medical management - Misoprostol is used to induce expulsion in incomplete or missed miscarriage.
  • Surgical management - Manual vacuum aspiration or dilation and curettage for heavy bleeding or retained tissue.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhage - Occurs in incomplete miscarriage or uterine atony, potentially leading to shock.
  • Sepsis - Results from retained products of conception; can progress to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS).
  • Psychological impact - Miscarriage is associated with grief, anxiety, and depression in up to 30% of cases.
