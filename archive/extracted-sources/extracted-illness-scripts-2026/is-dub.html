Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB) refers to abnormal uterine bleeding in the absence of identifiable structural or systemic pathology. It is characterized by irregular, prolonged, or heavy menstrual bleeding without an underlying organic cause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK General Practice Population: Approximately 20% of gynaecological consultations in the UK are due to heavy menstrual bleeding, a common presentation of DUB.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Heavy menstrual bleeding accounts for about 20% of all referrals to gynaecological services in the UK.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Around 3% to 30% of women globally are affected by DUB, with a higher incidence observed during menarche and perimenopause.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Anovulation: In many cases, DUB is associated with anovulation, leading to a lack of progesterone and unopposed estrogen stimulation of the endometrium. This hormonal imbalance causes the endometrium to proliferate excessively, resulting in irregular shedding and bleeding.
  • Ovulatory DUB: In ovulatory cycles, DUB may result from a defect in the local endometrial hemostasis mechanisms, leading to prolonged or heavy bleeding despite regular ovulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Sudden onset of irregular heavy bleeding, often following a period of amenorrhoea or anovulation.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent irregular or prolonged bleeding over months to years, leading to anaemia and affecting quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Heavy Menstrual Bleeding: Excessive menstrual blood loss due to prolonged endometrial proliferation and irregular shedding.
  • Irregular Menstrual Cycles: Due to anovulation and inconsistent endometrial shedding.
  • Prolonged Menstrual Bleeding: Caused by defective clotting and hemostatic dysfunction within the endometrium.
  • Intermenstrual Bleeding: Occurs due to unstable endometrial tissue resulting from unopposed estrogen stimulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Secondary to iron deficiency anaemia due to chronic blood loss.
  • Tachycardia: A compensatory response to anaemia.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Seen in cases of significant acute blood loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal Bleeding: Could indicate endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy.
  • Severe Anaemia: May present with syncope, dyspnoea, or heart failure symptoms.
  • Pelvic Mass: Suggests a structural cause such as fibroids or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Minimal or No Menstrual Symptoms: Some patients may present primarily with fatigue due to undiagnosed chronic anaemia.
  • Gradual Onset Heavy Bleeding: Some women may have initially normal cycles before developing progressively heavier periods.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Checks for anaemia and thrombocytopenia.
  • Serum Ferritin: Assesses iron deficiency.
  • Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs): Evaluates for thyroid disorders contributing to abnormal bleeding.
  • Pelvic Ultrasound: Helps rule out structural abnormalities like fibroids.
  • Endometrial Biopsy: Performed if there are concerns about endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]

A 35-year-old woman presents with progressively heavy menstrual bleeding over six months, with irregular cycles and worsening fatigue. Examination reveals conjunctival pallor and a tachycardia of 105 bpm. FBC shows microcytic anaemia, and pelvic ultrasound is unremarkable.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids: Typically present with heavy menstrual bleeding and pelvic mass on examination.
  • Endometrial Hyperplasia: More common in obese or postmenopausal women; endometrial biopsy helps differentiate.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause heavy periods but is associated with fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • First-line: Combined oral contraceptives or levonorgestrel intrauterine system (LNG-IUS) as per NICE guidelines.
  • Second-line: Tranexamic acid or NSAIDs if contraception is not required.
  • Surgical Options: Endometrial ablation or hysterectomy in refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)"][/sc]
  • Iron Deficiency Anaemia: Due to chronic blood loss.
  • Endometrial Hyperplasia: Risk of progression to malignancy if untreated.
  • Impaired Quality of Life: Due to fatigue and unpredictable bleeding.
