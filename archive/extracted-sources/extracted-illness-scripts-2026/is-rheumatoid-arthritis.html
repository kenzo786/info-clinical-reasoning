Rheumatoid Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic, systemic autoimmune condition characterised by persistent, symmetrical inflammation of synovial joints, leading to joint destruction, deformity, and disability if untreated. It often involves the small joints of the hands and feet and can have extra-articular manifestations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, RA affects approximately 1% of the population, with a higher prevalence among women than men (3:1 ratio).
  • It is commonly diagnosed between ages 30 and 50, but can present at any age.
  • Globally, RA prevalence is estimated at 0.5–1% in developed countries, with variations based on genetic and environmental factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • RA is an autoimmune disorder where genetic and environmental factors (e.g., smoking) trigger an abnormal immune response.
  • CD4+ T cells activate B cells to produce rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA), leading to immune complex formation.
  • These immune complexes deposit in the synovium, activating macrophages and synovial fibroblasts, resulting in the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α, IL-1, and IL-6.
  • Chronic inflammation leads to synovial hyperplasia, pannus formation, cartilage damage, and eventual joint destruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Often insidious onset with vague symptoms such as malaise, fatigue, and joint stiffness.
  • Established Disease: Progressive joint pain, swelling, and morning stiffness; joint deformities may begin to appear.
  • Late Stage: Joint destruction, deformity, and disability are prominent; extra-articular manifestations are more common.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Pain: Pain typically affects small joints symmetrically (e.g., MCP, PIP joints) due to synovial inflammation.
  • Morning Stiffness: Lasts >1 hour due to overnight synovial fluid accumulation; improves with movement.
  • Fatigue: Common systemic symptom from chronic inflammation and cytokine release.
  • Swelling: Soft tissue swelling and warmth around joints due to synovitis and effusion.
  • Reduced Range of Motion: Due to pain, swelling, and pannus formation limiting joint mobility.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Swelling: Visible and palpable swelling from synovial proliferation and effusion in affected joints.
  • Tenderness: Especially on palpation of affected joints, indicative of active inflammation.
  • Deformities: In advanced cases, e.g., ulnar deviation, swan neck deformity, due to chronic joint damage.
  • Rheumatoid Nodules: Firm, subcutaneous nodules over extensor surfaces, often elbows, due to granulomatous inflammation.
  • Reduced Grip Strength: Due to pain and joint damage, particularly in hand joints.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly Worsening Pain and Swelling: May indicate septic arthritis, which requires urgent intervention.
  • Neurological Symptoms: Such as tingling or weakness; possible spinal cord compression from cervical spine involvement.
  • Weight Loss and Fever: Could indicate systemic involvement or possible malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Monoarthritis: RA usually affects multiple joints but can initially present in a single joint, mimicking gout or infection.
  • Extra-Articular Disease Only: Rare, but patients may present with systemic symptoms (e.g., vasculitis) without joint symptoms.
  • Older Age at Onset: Patients >60 years may have fewer joints affected or atypical joint involvement.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Factor (RF): Positive in ~70% of patients; antibodies form immune complexes contributing to inflammation.
  • Anti-CCP Antibodies: Highly specific for RA; indicates immune response to citrullinated proteins.
  • CRP and ESR: Raised levels indicate active inflammation.
  • Imaging (X-ray, Ultrasound, MRI): Early findings include soft tissue swelling; later stages show joint space narrowing, erosions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with 6 months of progressive bilateral hand pain, stiffness lasting >1 hour each morning, and fatigue. Examination reveals swelling and tenderness in the MCP and PIP joints.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis: Typically asymmetric, affects DIP joints, and lacks systemic features.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: May involve DIP joints and has dactylitis, nail changes, and a history of psoriasis.
  • Gout: Acute, often monoarticular; tophi and hyperuricaemia present.
  • SLE: Symmetric polyarthritis but with other systemic signs (e.g., skin rash) and is typically non-erosive.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • DMARDs: Methotrexate is first-line; alternatives include sulfasalazine, leflunomide.
  • Biologics: Anti-TNF agents (e.g., etanercept) if inadequate response to DMARDs.
  • NSAIDs and Steroids: For symptomatic relief; steroids used for flares or bridging until DMARDs take effect.
  • Physiotherapy and Occupational Therapy: For joint function preservation and disability prevention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rheumatoid Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Deformity and Disability: Result from progressive cartilage and bone damage.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Increased risk due to chronic inflammation; includes atherosclerosis.
  • Osteoporosis: Due to inflammation, immobility, and steroid use.
  • Infections: Due to immunosuppressive therapy.
