Pre-eclampsia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Pre-eclampsia is a hypertensive disorder of pregnancy, typically occurring after 20 weeks of gestation. It is characterised by new-onset hypertension (blood pressure ≥140/90 mmHg) and proteinuria (≥300 mg in 24 hours) or the presence of systemic involvement such as renal insufficiency, thrombocytopaenia, liver dysfunction, or neurological symptoms. Pre-eclampsia can progress to eclampsia, which involves seizures, posing significant risk to both maternal and foetal health.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, pre-eclampsia affects 2-8% of pregnancies, with 1 in 25 pregnant women being diagnosed in general practice. Hospital prevalence may be higher due to referral of complicated cases. Globally, it accounts for 10-15% of maternal deaths.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Pre-eclampsia results from abnormal placentation, where inadequate remodelling of spiral arteries leads to poor placental perfusion. This causes oxidative stress and the release of anti-angiogenic factors into the maternal circulation. These factors disrupt endothelial function, causing widespread vascular dysfunction, leading to hypertension, proteinuria, and multi-organ damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Pre-eclampsia typically develops after 20 weeks of gestation and can occur up to six weeks postpartum. Early-onset pre-eclampsia (before 34 weeks) is more severe and often requires earlier delivery. The disease may remain mild or progress to severe pre-eclampsia or eclampsia over days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Headache - A common symptom due to cerebral vasospasm and elevated blood pressure.
  • Visual disturbances - Blurred vision or scotomata occur due to retinal artery vasospasm or cerebral oedema.
  • Upper abdominal pain - Typically epigastric or right upper quadrant pain due to hepatic capsule distension from liver oedema.
  • Oedema - Generalised swelling, especially in the hands and face, due to endothelial dysfunction and capillary leakage.
  • Breathlessness - Pulmonary oedema may develop secondary to increased vascular permeability or heart failure.
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients are asymptomatic and diagnosed based on routine blood pressure checks and urinalysis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Blood pressure ≥140/90 mmHg due to systemic vasoconstriction and endothelial dysfunction.
  • Proteinuria - ≥300 mg in 24 hours of urine, indicating glomerular endothelial injury and altered filtration barrier.
  • Oedema - Generalised swelling, particularly in the face and extremities, due to increased capillary permeability.
  • Hyperreflexia - Increased reflexes are a sign of neuromuscular irritability due to cerebral oedema or hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Severe hypertension (≥160/110 mmHg) - Indicates risk of stroke or other cardiovascular events.
  • Severe headache and visual disturbances - Suggest cerebral involvement, potentially signalling imminent eclampsia.
  • Upper abdominal pain - May indicate hepatic involvement (HELLP syndrome) with the risk of liver rupture.
  • Reduced urine output - Suggests renal impairment or failure.
  • Seizures - Progression to eclampsia, which is life-threatening for both mother and foetus.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Postpartum pre-eclampsia - May occur up to six weeks after delivery, often missed as hypertension is not expected postpartum.
  • Normotensive pre-eclampsia - Rare cases where proteinuria and systemic involvement occur without overt hypertension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Blood pressure measurement - ≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions, at least 4 hours apart, confirms hypertension.
  • Urinalysis for proteinuria - A dipstick showing ≥1+ protein or a spot protein:creatinine ratio ≥30 mg/mmol confirms proteinuria.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Low platelet count (<100,000/mm³) may indicate severe disease or HELLP syndrome.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated AST or ALT indicates hepatic involvement.
  • Serum creatinine - Elevated levels (>90 μmol/L) suggest renal impairment.
  • Foetal monitoring - Doppler ultrasound to assess foetal growth restriction and amniotic fluid volume.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old primigravida at 32 weeks gestation presents with headache, visual disturbances, and upper abdominal pain. Blood pressure is 162/112 mmHg, with 2+ proteinuria on dipstick.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Gestational hypertension - Hypertension without proteinuria or systemic involvement, resolves postpartum.
  • Chronic hypertension - Pre-existing hypertension prior to pregnancy, lacks proteinuria and multi-organ involvement.
  • HELLP syndrome - Haemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets, with similar overlap but more severe liver and haematological involvement.
  • Acute fatty liver of pregnancy - Presents with liver failure, hypoglycaemia, and coagulopathy, without significant hypertension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Antihypertensive therapy - Labetalol is first-line treatment in the UK; nifedipine or methyldopa are alternatives.
  • Magnesium sulphate - Used for seizure prophylaxis and treatment of eclampsia.
  • Delivery - Definitive treatment; decision is based on maternal and foetal health, gestational age, and severity of pre-eclampsia.
  • Corticosteroids - Given to promote foetal lung maturity if delivery is anticipated before 34 weeks.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
  • Eclampsia - Seizures resulting from cerebral oedema and endothelial dysfunction; a major cause of maternal mortality.
  • HELLP syndrome - A variant of severe pre-eclampsia with haemolysis, liver dysfunction, and thrombocytopaenia; increases maternal morbidity.
  • Foetal growth restriction - Due to placental insufficiency, leading to low birth weight and preterm birth.
  • Placental abruption - Detachment of the placenta from the uterine wall due to vascular injury, risking foetal death and maternal haemorrhage.
