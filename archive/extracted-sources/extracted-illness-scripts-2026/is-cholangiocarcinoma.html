Cholangiocarcinoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Cholangiocarcinoma is a rare and malignant tumour originating in the epithelial cells of the biliary tree, which can be intrahepatic, perihilar, or distal based on anatomical location. It is characterised by slow growth and high levels of invasiveness, often involving local structures and producing symptoms only in advanced stages.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, cholangiocarcinoma incidence is <1 per 100,000 people annually, representing around 1,500 new cases each year.
  • Hospital prevalence is higher due to more advanced cases requiring specialist care, with cholangiocarcinoma comprising up to 10% of primary liver cancers in some UK studies.
  • Worldwide prevalence varies significantly, with higher rates in regions with endemic liver fluke infections, such as Thailand and other parts of Southeast Asia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Cholangiocarcinoma arises from genetic and environmental factors leading to dysplasia and malignant transformation of biliary epithelium.
  • Risk factors include chronic biliary inflammation, primary sclerosing cholangitis, liver fluke infections (Opisthorchis viverrini, Clonorchis sinensis), hepatitis B and C, and exposure to certain toxins.
  • Inflammation and subsequent fibrosis create a pro-carcinogenic environment in the biliary ducts, leading to genetic mutations (e.g., TP53, KRAS) and progressive tumour formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Asymptomatic in early stages, often detected incidentally.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms develop over months to years as the tumour grows, eventually obstructing bile flow and causing jaundice, pruritus, and abdominal pain.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease may present with weight loss, systemic symptoms, and complications such as liver failure or metastasis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Jaundice - Due to biliary obstruction causing bilirubin build-up, presenting with yellowing of skin and eyes.
  • Pruritus - Resulting from bile salt accumulation in the skin due to biliary obstruction.
  • Right upper quadrant abdominal pain - Localised pain related to the site of the biliary tumour and pressure on nearby structures.
  • Weight loss and anorexia - Due to cancer-related metabolic changes and loss of appetite.
  • Fever - May occur due to tumour necrosis or concurrent cholangitis in obstructed ducts.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Jaundice - Observable in skin and sclera, indicative of obstructive pathology in biliary ducts.
  • Hepatomegaly - Often due to liver congestion and sometimes direct tumour involvement.
  • Palpable gallbladder (Courvoisier’s sign) - May indicate extrahepatic obstruction, typically in perihilar or distal tumours.
  • Cachexia - Advanced stage sign related to significant weight loss and metabolic effects of cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Painless jaundice - A classic presentation for malignancy-related biliary obstruction and should raise concern for cholangiocarcinoma.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Suggests systemic effects of malignancy, necessitating further imaging and investigations.
  • Palpable gallbladder with jaundice - Indicates obstructive biliary pathology, commonly in malignancies rather than benign causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Cholangitis-like symptoms - Some patients may present with fever, chills, and jaundice, mimicking ascending cholangitis but without infection, due to tumour-induced biliary obstruction.
  • Non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms - Nausea, vomiting, and early satiety can be misleading, especially if jaundice is absent in early intrahepatic disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated bilirubin, ALP, and GGT levels indicate cholestasis. ALT and AST may be mildly elevated.
  • CA 19-9 - Tumour marker often elevated in cholangiocarcinoma, though not specific; can aid in diagnosis and monitoring.
  • Imaging (MRCP, CT) - MRCP provides detailed imaging of biliary ducts, showing ductal strictures or mass lesions; CT can help detect metastases.
  • Biopsy/Fine needle aspiration (FNA) - Necessary for definitive histological diagnosis, often performed via ERCP-guided biopsy or percutaneous FNA.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of painless jaundice, weight loss, and pruritus. Liver function tests reveal cholestatic pattern, and imaging shows a mass in the perihilar region.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) - Progressive bile duct scarring; tends to show “beaded” appearance on MRCP but lacks mass lesion.
  • Gallstone disease with choledocholithiasis - Often presents with pain and fever; stones visible on ultrasound, lacking tumour markers.
  • Pancreatic head cancer - Presents similarly with jaundice and weight loss; however, mass usually visible in pancreatic head on imaging.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) - Typically seen in chronic liver disease; usually presents with liver mass rather than biliary obstruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection - Preferred curative approach if the tumour is resectable.
  • Palliative stenting - Endoscopic or percutaneous stenting for biliary obstruction in inoperable cases.
  • Adjuvant chemotherapy - Cisplatin and gemcitabine are commonly used post-surgery or in unresectable cases.
  • Radiotherapy - Limited to palliative cases due to high toxicity in hepatic tissues.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholangiocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Liver failure - Due to progressive biliary obstruction leading to cholestasis and cirrhosis.
  • Ascites - Secondary to liver dysfunction, particularly in cases of portal hypertension.
  • Metastasis - Common sites include the liver, lungs, and peritoneum, leading to further systemic symptoms.
  • Poor prognosis - Overall 5-year survival rate is low; early detection and surgical intervention improve outcomes.
