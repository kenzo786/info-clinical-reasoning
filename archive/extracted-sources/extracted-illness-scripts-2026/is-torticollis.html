Torticollis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Torticollis, also known as wry neck, is a condition characterised by involuntary contraction or shortening of the cervical muscles, particularly the sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles, leading to an abnormal head posture. It may present as a congenital condition or develop later due to neurological, musculoskeletal, or acquired causes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, congenital muscular torticollis affects approximately 0.3–1.9% of live births. Acquired torticollis is less common but can present in adults, with varying prevalence depending on the underlying cause.
  • Worldwide prevalence mirrors UK statistics for congenital cases. The acquired form, particularly spasmodic torticollis (cervical dystonia), has an estimated prevalence of 8–15 per 100,000 individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Congenital muscular torticollis: Caused by fibrosis or injury to the sternocleidomastoid muscle during delivery, leading to limited range of motion.
  • Spasmodic torticollis: Result of basal ganglia dysfunction, often linked to abnormal neural signalling, leading to sustained muscle contractions.
  • Acquired torticollis: Secondary to trauma, infections, or tumours causing muscle spasm, inflammation, or nerve compression in the cervical region.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Congenital: Symptoms present at or shortly after birth. Without intervention, progressive muscle fibrosis may worsen the deformity.
  • Acquired: Develops over days to weeks (e.g., post-trauma or infection) or insidiously in chronic conditions like cervical dystonia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Neck pain or discomfort: Results from muscle spasm or strain, commonly seen in acquired forms.
  • Abnormal head posture: Tilted or rotated head due to sternocleidomastoid contraction, observed in all types.
  • Reduced range of motion: Stiffness in neck movements, particularly in congenital or fibrotic cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Head tilt: Typically towards the affected side with chin rotated to the opposite side due to sternocleidomastoid involvement.
  • Palpable mass: Fibrotic nodule in the sternocleidomastoid muscle in congenital cases.
  • Asymmetry: Facial or cranial asymmetry in untreated congenital torticollis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Neurological symptoms: Weakness, sensory loss, or reflex changes may indicate spinal cord or nerve root compression.
  • Fever or systemic signs: Suggests infectious causes such as retropharyngeal abscess or meningitis.
  • Rapid onset with severe pain: May indicate trauma, tumours, or vascular emergencies like carotid artery dissection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Intermittent spasms: Seen in cervical dystonia, which may be mistaken for anxiety or posture-related pain.
  • Painless torticollis: Common in congenital cases or slowly progressive acquired cases without acute inflammatory features.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: To assess sternocleidomastoid fibrosis in congenital cases.
  • MRI: Evaluates soft tissue, cervical spine, or neural abnormalities in acquired cases.
  • Blood tests: Full blood count and inflammatory markers to rule out infections.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • An 8-week-old infant presents with a right-sided head tilt and palpable mass in the sternocleidomastoid. Alternatively, a 35-year-old with a gradual onset of neck stiffness and head deviation reports mild discomfort, without fever.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Klippel-Feil Syndrome: Associated with vertebral fusion and limited neck movement.
  • Retropharyngeal abscess: Acute onset with fever, pain, and systemic symptoms.
  • Parkinson’s disease: Gradual onset of dystonia in older adults.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Physiotherapy: First-line for congenital torticollis, focusing on stretching exercises.
  • Botulinum toxin: For spasmodic torticollis to relieve muscle spasm.
  • Surgery: Rarely indicated but considered for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Torticollis"][/sc]
  • Facial asymmetry: From untreated congenital torticollis.
  • Chronic pain: Due to prolonged muscle imbalance in acquired cases.
  • Functional impairment: Difficulty with head movements affecting daily activities.
