Ocular Sarcoidosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ocular sarcoidosis is a chronic inflammatory condition characterised by the formation of non-caseating granulomas in ocular tissues, often associated with systemic sarcoidosis. It can affect various parts of the eye, including the uvea, conjunctiva, and lacrimal glands, leading to a spectrum of clinical manifestations from mild discomfort to severe vision loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • UK General Population: Ocular sarcoidosis is uncommon, with an estimated prevalence of 10-20 cases per 100,000 individuals.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Among patients with systemic sarcoidosis, 25-50% may exhibit ocular involvement.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Varies geographically, with higher prevalence in populations of African or Scandinavian descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ocular sarcoidosis results from an exaggerated immune response leading to the formation of granulomas. Antigen-presenting cells present unknown antigens to T-helper cells, activating them and causing cytokine release (e.g., TNF-α, IL-2). This promotes macrophage aggregation and granuloma formation, disrupting normal tissue function in the eye.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Sudden onset of symptoms such as red eye, pain, and photophobia, often in anterior uveitis.
  • Chronic Phase: Insidious development of complications like glaucoma, cataracts, or retinal damage, leading to progressive visual impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Blurred Vision: Due to inflammation affecting the cornea, lens, or retina.
  • Eye Pain: Associated with anterior uveitis or scleritis from granulomatous inflammation.
  • Photophobia: Caused by irritation of the iris and ciliary body.
  • Dry Eyes: Resulting from lacrimal gland involvement and reduced tear production.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Conjunctival Nodules: Reflect granuloma formation in the conjunctiva.
  • Mutton-Fat Keratic Precipitates: Large inflammatory deposits on the corneal endothelium, indicating granulomatous uveitis.
  • Posterior Synechiae: Adhesions between the iris and lens due to prolonged inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Severe Vision Loss: May signify retinal detachment or optic neuropathy.
  • Intense Eye Pain with Nausea: Could indicate secondary glaucoma.
  • Systemic Symptoms with Ocular Signs: Suggestive of multi-organ involvement requiring urgent systemic evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Presentation: Detected incidentally during examination for systemic sarcoidosis.
  • Isolated Posterior Uveitis: Without systemic symptoms, which may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Slit-Lamp Examination: Identifies keratic precipitates, iris nodules, or posterior synechiae.
  • Chest X-ray: May show hilar lymphadenopathy, supporting systemic sarcoidosis.
  • Serum ACE Levels: Elevated in sarcoidosis but nonspecific.
  • Ocular Imaging: OCT to assess retinal or choroidal involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a two-week history of blurred vision, eye pain, and photophobia. Examination reveals anterior uveitis with granulomatous keratic precipitates and mild conjunctival nodules.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Infectious Uveitis: Differentiated by positive infectious serologies or PCR testing.
  • Ankylosing Spondylitis-associated Uveitis: Associated with HLA-B27 positivity and back pain.
  • Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada Syndrome: Accompanied by auditory and neurological symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Topical Corticosteroids: Mainstay for anterior uveitis.
  • Systemic Corticosteroids: Indicated for posterior segment involvement or systemic disease.
  • Immunosuppressive Agents: Methotrexate or TNF inhibitors for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Glaucoma: Due to chronic inflammation or steroid use.
  • Cataracts: Often secondary to prolonged corticosteroid therapy.
  • Retinal Detachment: From severe granulomatous involvement.
