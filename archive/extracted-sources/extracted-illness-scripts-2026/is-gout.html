Gout
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Gout is a metabolic disorder characterised by the deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in joints and tissues, resulting from prolonged hyperuricaemia. It typically presents as acute, recurrent episodes of inflammatory arthritis, often affecting the first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 2.5% of adults.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Gout accounts for about 1.5% of rheumatology consultations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 0.08%-10%, depending on the region, with higher rates in developed nations.
  • Predominantly affects middle-aged to older men and postmenopausal women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Hyperuricaemia arises from either overproduction or underexcretion of uric acid.
  • MSU crystals precipitate in joint spaces when uric acid levels exceed saturation thresholds.
  • Crystals trigger activation of the inflammasome in resident macrophages, leading to the release of interleukin-1β (IL-1β).
  • Neutrophil recruitment causes intense inflammation, manifesting as acute arthritis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Initial asymptomatic hyperuricaemia: Elevated uric acid levels without symptoms.
  • Acute gout flares: Sudden onset of severe joint pain, swelling, and redness lasting days to weeks.
  • Intercritical gout: Asymptomatic periods between attacks.
  • Chronic tophaceous gout: Persistent joint inflammation with visible tophi and joint damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Severe joint pain - Typically monoarticular, often affecting the big toe (podagra); caused by MSU crystal deposition and acute inflammatory response.
  • Swelling and warmth - Localised inflammation due to neutrophilic infiltrate.
  • Erythema - Caused by capillary dilation and increased blood flow during inflammation.
  • Reduced joint mobility - Stiffness secondary to swelling and pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Swollen, tender joint - Indicates acute synovitis due to crystal-induced inflammation.
  • Tophi - Subcutaneous nodules representing urate crystal deposits in chronic disease.
  • Limited range of motion - Associated with joint effusion and pain during movement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Fever and systemic symptoms - May indicate septic arthritis, a medical emergency.
  • Rapid joint destruction - Suggests advanced gout or concurrent infection.
  • Unrelenting pain - May be a sign of joint complications such as osteomyelitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Polyarticular gout: Multiple joints involved, mimicking rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Spinal gout: Presents as back pain, often misdiagnosed as degenerative disease.
  • Chronic indolent disease: Subtle symptoms resembling osteoarthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Serum urate levels - Elevated in most cases (>360 µmol/L), though may normalise during acute flares.
  • Joint aspiration - Identification of negatively birefringent MSU crystals under polarised light microscopy confirms diagnosis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound may show double contour sign; X-rays reveal tophi and erosions in chronic gout.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with sudden onset severe pain, redness, and swelling in the left big toe for 24 hours. He has a history of hypertension and is on diuretics.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Distinguished by fever, systemic symptoms, and purulent joint fluid.
  • Pseudogout - Calcium pyrophosphate deposition; rhomboid crystals seen on microscopy.
  • Reactive arthritis - Preceding infection, often polyarticular involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Acute management: NSAIDs (e.g., naproxen), colchicine, or corticosteroids to reduce inflammation.
  • Chronic management: Urate-lowering therapy (e.g., allopurinol or febuxostat) to maintain serum urate <360 µmol/L.
  • Lifestyle modification: Reduce purine-rich foods, alcohol, and weight management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gout"][/sc]
  • Chronic joint damage - Resulting from recurrent flares and tophi deposition.
  • Renal complications - Uric acid nephropathy, nephrolithiasis.
  • Cardiovascular risks - Associated with increased risk of hypertension, MI, and stroke.
