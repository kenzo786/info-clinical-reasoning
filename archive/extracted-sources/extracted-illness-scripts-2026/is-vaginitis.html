Vaginitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis is an inflammation of the vaginal mucosa commonly caused by infections such as bacterial vaginosis (BV), vulvovaginal candidiasis (VVC), and trichomoniasis. Non-infectious causes, such as allergic reactions, irritants, and hormonal changes, can also lead to vaginitis. The condition is typically characterised by abnormal vaginal discharge, pruritus, irritation, and sometimes dyspareunia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis is a common condition among women of reproductive age. In the UK, bacterial vaginosis affects 12-30% of women visiting GPs. Globally, prevalence estimates vary, but BV is the most common vaginal infection, affecting about 30% of women in some populations. Vulvovaginal candidiasis is experienced by about 70-75% of women at least once in their lifetime.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis occurs when the normal vaginal environment is disrupted, often by a change in pH or microbial flora. In bacterial vaginosis, overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria like *Gardnerella vaginalis* replaces the normal lactobacilli. In vulvovaginal candidiasis, overgrowth of *Candida* species, typically *Candida albicans*, results in infection. In trichomoniasis, the protozoan *Trichomonas vaginalis* infects the vagina. Non-infectious vaginitis can result from allergic reactions, hormonal changes, or irritants.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Acute vaginitis symptoms usually appear within a few days to weeks of exposure to the causative agent. Symptoms of bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis can develop within a few days, whereas vulvovaginal candidiasis may manifest more gradually. Chronic or recurrent vaginitis, especially with fungal infections or irritant-induced vaginitis, may persist or recur over months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Abnormal vaginal discharge - In bacterial vaginosis, discharge is typically thin, grey, and malodorous ("fishy" smell), while in vulvovaginal candidiasis, discharge is thick and white ("cottage cheese-like"). These symptoms result from microbial overgrowth altering the vaginal flora.
  • Vaginal pruritus - Common in vulvovaginal candidiasis due to inflammatory responses to the overgrowth of *Candida*. Itching can also occur in non-infectious vaginitis due to irritants or allergic reactions.
  • Dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse, seen in all forms of vaginitis, is due to inflammation and irritation of the vaginal mucosa.
  • Burning sensation - Vaginal burning can result from inflammation of the vaginal tissue, commonly seen in candidiasis and irritant-induced vaginitis.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Up to 50% of cases of bacterial vaginosis and trichomoniasis may present without symptoms, particularly in the early stages, due to the body's ability to initially tolerate changes in vaginal flora.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginal discharge - On examination, BV may show thin, grey, homogeneous discharge. Candidiasis is characterised by thick, white, clumpy discharge. Trichomoniasis may present with frothy, yellow-green discharge. These variations in discharge reflect the differing pathogens and their effect on vaginal secretions.
  • Vaginal erythema - Inflammation of the vaginal mucosa is common in candidiasis and irritant-induced vaginitis due to immune system activation.
  • Vulvar excoriation - Scratching due to pruritus can lead to skin excoriation, particularly in vulvovaginal candidiasis or irritant vaginitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic pain - May suggest pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), a complication of untreated bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis. This can lead to infertility if not treated.
  • Fever - Not typical in uncomplicated vaginitis but may indicate upper genital tract infection or systemic involvement.
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - Can suggest atrophic vaginitis or, in rare cases, malignancy. Should always prompt further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Women with vaginitis caused by *Candida glabrata* may present with minimal discharge or pruritus, as this strain is less inflammatory compared to *Candida albicans*.
  • Chronic or recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis can present with mild, persistent symptoms without the typical thick discharge, making it harder to recognise.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginal pH testing - A pH > 4.5 is indicative of bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis, as these organisms thrive in less acidic environments. In vulvovaginal candidiasis, pH remains normal (≤ 4.5).
  • Microscopy (wet mount) - A saline wet mount may reveal clue cells (BV), motile trichomonads (trichomoniasis), or hyphae and spores (candidiasis).
  • Gram stain - Used to identify bacterial vaginosis by assessing the proportion of lactobacilli versus other bacteria. Nugent scoring system is often employed.
  • NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test) - Used to detect *Trichomonas vaginalis* and other pathogens with high sensitivity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents with 3 days of thick, white vaginal discharge, associated with pruritus and discomfort during intercourse. pH testing reveals normal vaginal acidity, and microscopy confirms the presence of *Candida* species.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Typically presents with dysuria and frequency, but without abnormal vaginal discharge.
  • Cervicitis - Inflammation of the cervix due to sexually transmitted infections (e.g., chlamydia or gonorrhoea) may cause purulent discharge, but often lacks the pruritus of vaginitis.
  • Atrophic vaginitis - Occurs in postmenopausal women due to oestrogen deficiency, leading to dryness, itching, and dyspareunia but without infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial vaginosis - Treatment involves oral or vaginal metronidazole or clindamycin.
  • Vulvovaginal candidiasis - Topical antifungal agents (clotrimazole) or oral fluconazole are commonly prescribed.
  • Trichomoniasis - Single-dose oral metronidazole is the recommended treatment. Sexual partners should also be treated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Untreated bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis can ascend the genital tract, leading to PID, which can cause chronic pelvic pain and infertility.
  • Recurrent vaginitis - Repeated infections, particularly with candidiasis, can occur and may require longer courses of treatment.
  • Adverse pregnancy outcomes - BV and trichomoniasis during pregnancy are associated with preterm birth and low birth weight.
