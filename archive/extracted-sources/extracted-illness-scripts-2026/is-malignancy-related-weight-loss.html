Malignancy-related Weight Loss
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Malignancy-related weight loss refers to unintentional weight loss associated with underlying cancer. This weight loss is typically multifactorial, caused by the tumour’s metabolic demands, systemic inflammation, and catabolic state induced by the cancer. It may precede other symptoms and can be an early indicator of malignancy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, unintentional weight loss affects approximately 0.2-1% of patients annually, with malignancy being a common underlying cause in older patients. In UK hospitals, cancer is responsible for up to 20-30% of cases of unexplained weight loss. Globally, malignancy is a leading cause of weight loss, particularly in cancers such as lung, gastrointestinal, and pancreatic cancer.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of malignancy-related weight loss involves several mechanisms:
    • **Increased Metabolic Demand**: Cancer cells exhibit rapid and unregulated growth, leading to high energy consumption.
    • **Systemic Inflammation**: Tumours induce the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6, TNF-alpha), which contribute to muscle and fat catabolism.
    • **Tumour Necrosis**: Some tumours cause necrosis, leading to a systemic catabolic state and anorexia (loss of appetite).
    • **Reduced Nutritional Intake**: Patients may experience early satiety, dysphagia, or nausea due to tumour location or treatment-related effects, contributing to poor caloric intake.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Malignancy-related weight loss typically occurs gradually over months, but can be rapid depending on the tumour type and aggressiveness. Initial phases may be subtle, with progressive cachexia (muscle wasting) occurring later. Weight loss may continue despite adequate nutritional intake as the disease progresses.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Unintentional Weight Loss - Progressive loss of body mass, typically more than 5% of body weight over 6-12 months, is the hallmark symptom, driven by cancer’s increased metabolic demand and systemic inflammation.
  • Anorexia - Loss of appetite due to the tumour or its metabolic by-products, contributing to reduced caloric intake.
  • Fatigue - A common symptom related to the body’s inability to meet energy demands due to both poor nutrition and the cancer’s metabolic activity.
  • Night Sweats - Often associated with lymphomas or cancers causing systemic inflammation and fever.
  • Early Satiety - Frequently seen in gastrointestinal cancers, where the tumour compresses surrounding organs or causes obstruction, limiting food intake.
  • Fever - Occurs due to tumour necrosis or cytokine release, especially in lymphomas and leukaemias.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Due to anaemia, often seen in advanced cancer or as a consequence of bone marrow infiltration.
  • Cachexia - Visible muscle wasting and loss of fat stores, driven by catabolic processes initiated by the tumour.
  • Palpable Mass - A mass may be felt on examination, indicating tumour growth, particularly in abdominal or breast cancers.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes, often a sign of metastatic or haematological cancers.
  • Jaundice - Indicates liver involvement, either due to metastatic disease or primary liver/pancreatic cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Weight loss of >5% in 6 months - Significant unintentional weight loss is concerning for malignancy.
  • Night Sweats and Fever - Associated with haematological malignancies such as lymphoma or leukaemia.
  • Dysphagia or Early Satiety - Suggests oesophageal or gastric cancer.
  • Painless Jaundice - Indicates possible pancreatic cancer with liver metastasis or obstruction.
  • Palpable Abdominal Mass - May indicate advanced cancer, such as colon or ovarian malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Minimal Weight Loss - In early-stage cancers, patients may experience anorexia or fatigue before significant weight loss occurs.
  • Chronic Fatigue without Weight Loss - Some patients may primarily complain of fatigue, particularly in haematological malignancies, before weight loss is noticeable.
  • Non-specific Gastrointestinal Symptoms - Mild gastrointestinal symptoms like bloating or indigestion can precede weight loss in gastrointestinal cancers.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia, leukocytosis, or thrombocytosis, suggesting bone marrow infiltration or chronic disease.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) - Abnormal LFTs, particularly raised bilirubin, may indicate liver metastasis or primary liver cancer.
  • CT Scan - Cross-sectional imaging to identify primary tumour, metastases, or lymphadenopathy. CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is often done in cases of weight loss of unknown origin.
  • Tumour Markers - CEA, CA 19-9, PSA (depending on suspected malignancy) can provide supporting evidence for diagnosis but are not definitive.
  • Endoscopy (Upper and Lower) - For gastrointestinal weight loss, to visualise and biopsy potential tumours in the oesophagus, stomach, or colon.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • A 67-year-old male presents with a 10% unintentional weight loss over 6 months, fatigue, early satiety, and a palpable mass in the left upper quadrant. He also reports night sweats and intermittent low-grade fever. No significant pain noted.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Chronic Infection (e.g., TB) - Also causes weight loss, fever, and night sweats, but would likely show pulmonary symptoms or positive Mantoux test.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Presents with weight loss and fatigue, but typically accompanied by heat intolerance, tremors, and tachycardia.
  • Gastrointestinal Conditions (e.g., Coeliac Disease) - Causes weight loss and malabsorption, but without systemic signs like fever or night sweats.
  • Heart Failure - Can cause fatigue and weight loss (cardiac cachexia), but usually presents with signs of fluid overload such as oedema and dyspnoea.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Treat the Underlying Cancer - The primary treatment is directed at the cancer itself, through surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or a combination.
  • Nutritional Support - High-calorie, high-protein diets, enteral or parenteral nutrition may be necessary for cachectic patients.
  • Appetite Stimulants - Medications such as megestrol acetate or corticosteroids may be used to improve appetite.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Cachexia - Progressive muscle wasting that is difficult to reverse, leading to poor prognosis.
  • Malnutrition - Further weakens immune response and contributes to overall deterioration.
  • Organ Dysfunction - Advanced cancer can lead to liver, kidney, or heart failure depending on metastasis location.
