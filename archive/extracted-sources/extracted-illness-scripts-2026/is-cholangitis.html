Cholangitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Cholangitis is an infection and inflammation of the biliary tree, typically due to bacterial pathogens entering the biliary system secondary to obstruction (e.g., gallstones, strictures, or malignancy). It presents most often as a combination of fever, jaundice, and right upper quadrant abdominal pain, known as Charcot’s triad.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Cholangitis is relatively uncommon in primary care due to its acute nature; however, recurrent cases may present in chronic biliary disease.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Cholangitis accounts for approximately 20-30% of biliary emergencies, with incidence around 0.2 per 1,000 hospital admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated incidence of acute cholangitis varies but has been reported around 0.2-1.5 per 1,000 people, with increased risk in East Asian populations due to high incidence of hepatobiliary disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Biliary obstruction leads to increased biliary pressure, stasis, and impaired bile flow.
  • Stasis and impaired flow allow bacterial colonisation, most commonly from the duodenum. Gram-negative bacteria (e.g., E. coli, Klebsiella) are typical causative agents.
  • Bacterial colonisation triggers localised inflammation and systemic infection.
  • This can lead to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and, if untreated, severe sepsis, organ dysfunction, and shock.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of fever, jaundice, and abdominal pain over hours to a few days.
  • Subacute phase: If untreated, progression to sepsis and multi-organ failure within days to a week.
  • Recovery phase: Following treatment, gradual resolution of symptoms within days to weeks, depending on severity and response to intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - Common due to systemic response to infection and inflammation; often high-grade.
  • Jaundice - Due to bile duct obstruction and hepatocyte dysfunction leading to elevated bilirubin.
  • Right upper quadrant abdominal pain - Due to distension of biliary ducts and local inflammation.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Secondary to pain and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Malaise - A result of systemic infection and cytokine release.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - High temperature often over 38°C, a systemic sign of infection.
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of skin and sclerae, visible at bilirubin levels over 2.5 mg/dL.
  • Tenderness in right upper quadrant - Localised due to inflammation of the biliary tract.
  • Tachycardia - Often seen due to systemic infection and fever.
  • Hypotension (in severe cases) - Due to sepsis, indicating potential progression to septic shock.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status - Sign of advanced sepsis with possible encephalopathy due to systemic infection and bilirubin elevation.
  • Hypotension - Suggests sepsis with potential progression to septic shock.
  • Refractory fever - May indicate an abscess or persistent biliary obstruction needing urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Atypical presentation in elderly patients - May present with subtle symptoms, such as confusion or general malaise, without the classic triad.
  • Immunocompromised patients - Less likely to mount a fever but may have disproportionate hypotension and jaundice.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Blood cultures - Often show growth of gram-negative organisms, aiding in pathogen identification.
  • Complete blood count (CBC) - Leukocytosis common due to infection and inflammation.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated bilirubin, ALP, and GGT are typical, indicating bile obstruction and hepatic inflammation.
  • Imaging (Ultrasound or MRCP) - Visualises biliary dilation and possible obstruction due to stones or strictures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with acute fever, jaundice, and right upper quadrant pain, worsening over 48 hours. Blood cultures reveal gram-negative bacilli, and ultrasound confirms bile duct dilation suggestive of acute cholangitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis - Similar pain but often without jaundice or systemic features unless complicated.
  • Hepatitis - Elevated liver enzymes with jaundice, but usually lacks biliary dilation and right upper quadrant tenderness.
  • Biliary pancreatitis - Jaundice and right upper quadrant pain are possible, but elevated lipase and amylase suggest pancreatic involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Initial antibiotics - Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam) covering gram-negative and anaerobic organisms as per UK guidelines.
  • Biliary drainage - Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) to relieve obstruction if no rapid improvement is seen.
  • Supportive care - IV fluids, pain control, and monitoring for signs of sepsis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - A common complication due to systemic infection, often requires intensive care if severe.
  • Septic shock - High mortality risk if unresponsive to initial therapy; requires vasopressors and ICU admission.
  • Liver abscess - May occur if infection persists or is inadequately treated; needs further drainage and antibiotics.
