Lichen Planus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Lichen planus is a chronic inflammatory skin and mucosal condition characterised by the presence of purple, polygonal, pruritic, and flat-topped papules and plaques. It is an immune-mediated disorder primarily affecting the skin, oral mucosa, and, occasionally, other areas such as the scalp, nails, and genitals.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Prevalence: Lichen planus affects approximately 0.1-4% of the general population worldwide. In the UK, it is less commonly seen in GP practices, with a hospital prevalence of about 0.1-0.3%. It is most common in middle-aged adults, with a slight female predominance.
  • Age: Most cases occur between the ages of 30 and 60.
  • Geography: Global prevalence is relatively consistent, though some regional variations may occur.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Lichen planus is thought to be an autoimmune disorder, where T cells target basal keratinocytes, leading to their destruction. The cause is often idiopathic, but it may be triggered by drugs (lichenoid drug reaction), infections (e.g., hepatitis C), or as a result of stress. A dense band-like lymphocytic infiltrate forms at the dermal-epidermal junction, causing apoptosis of basal keratinocytes and leading to the characteristic skin lesions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Lichen planus typically develops over weeks to months. Acute flare-ups may last several weeks, with lesions persisting for months or years. Spontaneous resolution is common within 6 months to 2 years, but some cases may become chronic, especially mucosal forms such as oral lichen planus.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Pruritus (itching) - One of the most common symptoms. Itching results from the inflammation and damage to the skin's keratinocytes.
  • Skin lesions - Papules and plaques that are purplish, polygonal, and flat-topped, often forming on the flexor surfaces of the wrists, forearms, and lower legs.
  • Oral discomfort - In cases of oral lichen planus, patients may experience a burning sensation or pain, especially when consuming spicy or acidic foods. The oral mucosa becomes eroded or ulcerated due to persistent inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Wickham’s striae - Fine white lines over the papules, thought to be caused by changes in the epidermal structure due to the inflammation.
  • Violaceous papules - The characteristic purple lesions on the skin.
  • Koebner phenomenon - Lesions appearing at the sites of trauma, which is believed to be due to the immune response being activated by injury to the skin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Erosive oral lichen planus - Risk of malignant transformation into squamous cell carcinoma. Chronic inflammation increases the likelihood of dysplasia in oral mucosal cells.
  • Genital involvement - Lichen planus can cause scarring and adhesions, particularly in the vulva, which may lead to sexual dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophic lichen planus - Presents with thick, verrucous plaques, typically on the shins, which may be confused with psoriasis.
  • Atrophic lichen planus - Causes thinning of the skin, leading to a misdiagnosis of lichen sclerosus.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Skin biopsy - Histopathology shows hyperkeratosis, basal cell degeneration, a dense band of lymphocytes at the dermoepidermal junction, and Civatte bodies (apoptotic keratinocytes).
  • Direct immunofluorescence - May show fibrin and IgM deposits in the basement membrane zone.
  • Hepatitis C serology - Recommended due to the association of lichen planus with hepatitis C infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of itchy, purple, flat-topped papules on her wrists and forearms, accompanied by a burning sensation in her mouth. Examination reveals violaceous papules with Wickham’s striae.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Psoriasis - Differs in that psoriasis plaques are typically silvery and scaly, often on extensor surfaces.
  • Lichen sclerosus - Lichen sclerosus presents with white, atrophic plaques, often in the genital region.
  • Contact dermatitis - Can mimic lichen planus but typically has a clear allergen or irritant trigger and lacks the characteristic violaceous hue.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Topical corticosteroids - First-line treatment to reduce inflammation. Potent corticosteroids such as clobetasol propionate are commonly used.
  • Systemic corticosteroids - Used in severe or refractory cases to control widespread disease.
  • Immunosuppressive agents - Agents like cyclosporine may be used in cases unresponsive to steroids.
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors - Tacrolimus can be used, particularly for oral or genital lichen planus.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lichen Planus"][/sc]
  • Oral squamous cell carcinoma - A rare but serious complication, particularly with erosive oral lichen planus.
  • Scarring alopecia - Can occur in cases where the scalp is involved, leading to permanent hair loss.
  • Nail dystrophy - Can lead to permanent damage and loss of nails in severe cases.
