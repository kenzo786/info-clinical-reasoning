Chronic Pancreatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pancreatitis (CP) is a progressive inflammatory disease of the pancreas that leads to irreversible damage, resulting in fibrosis and loss of both exocrine and endocrine function. It is characterised by recurrent episodes of abdominal pain, malabsorption, and diabetes. The condition often follows a prolonged course and is associated with a high risk of complications such as pancreatic insufficiency and pseudocyst formation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: ~10 per 100,000 adults
  • UK hospital prevalence: ~20 per 100,000 admissions
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 50-100 per 100,000 adults
  • CP is more common in men than women and typically presents between the ages of 30-50 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pancreatitis develops due to recurrent inflammation that leads to pancreatic fibrosis. This is driven by mechanisms such as oxidative stress, ductal obstruction, and necrosis-fibrosis sequences. Over time, the destruction of acinar cells reduces enzyme production, resulting in exocrine insufficiency and malabsorption. Damage to the islet cells may also occur, leading to diabetes mellitus.
  • Key factors include alcohol consumption (a major cause), genetic predispositions (e.g., mutations in CFTR or PRSS1), and autoimmune conditions. The progressive fibrosis and atrophy of the pancreas result in irreversible damage and loss of function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Recurrent episodes of abdominal pain with normal pancreatic function.
  • Mid phase: Persistent pain with gradual onset of exocrine insufficiency, manifesting as steatorrhoea and weight loss.
  • Late phase: Chronic pain or pain-free stage with severe exocrine and endocrine insufficiency (diabetes).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain – Recurrent or chronic epigastric pain radiating to the back, often worsened by eating. This is due to inflammation, fibrosis, and pancreatic duct obstruction.
  • Steatorrhoea – Fatty, foul-smelling stools due to pancreatic exocrine insufficiency and malabsorption of fat.
  • Weight loss – Caused by malabsorption and dietary restriction to avoid pain episodes.
  • Diabetes mellitus – Loss of endocrine function as the islets of Langerhans are progressively destroyed.
  • Nausea and vomiting – May occur due to severe pain or mechanical obstruction of the duodenum by pseudocysts.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Epigastric tenderness – Common during painful flare-ups due to local inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Malnutrition – Wasting and signs of fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (e.g., dry skin, night blindness) from malabsorption.
  • Pseudocyst formation – Palpable abdominal mass or fullness due to fluid-filled collections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss – May indicate malignancy or severe malabsorption.
  • New-onset diabetes – A sudden diagnosis of diabetes in someone with chronic pancreatitis raises suspicion of pancreatic cancer.
  • Jaundice – Suggests biliary obstruction, possibly due to a pseudocyst or pancreatic cancer.
  • Persistent vomiting – May indicate gastric outlet obstruction from a large pseudocyst or mass effect.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Painless chronic pancreatitis – Some patients, particularly those with advanced disease, may present without significant pain but with exocrine insufficiency (steatorrhoea, weight loss).
  • Isolated diabetes – In rare cases, diabetes may be the presenting feature, especially in older adults.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Faecal elastase – Low levels (<200 µg/g) indicate exocrine pancreatic insufficiency.
  • CT scan or MRI – May reveal pancreatic calcifications, ductal dilatation, atrophy, or pseudocysts, which are signs of chronic inflammation and fibrosis.
  • MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography) – Provides detailed images of pancreatic and bile ducts to assess for strictures, stones, or ductal changes.
  • Serum glucose/HbA1c – To assess for endocrine dysfunction (diabetes).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with recurrent epigastric pain radiating to the back, significant weight loss, and frequent oily stools over the past year. He has a 15-year history of heavy alcohol use and now reports worsening symptoms suggestive of diabetes (polyuria, polydipsia).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer – Rapid weight loss, jaundice, and new-onset diabetes are more suggestive of malignancy rather than CP.
  • Gallstone disease – Often causes episodic pain, usually colicky and associated with meals, but lacks the malabsorption or diabetes seen in CP.
  • Peptic ulcer disease – Pain may be similar but typically improves with food or antacids, without signs of pancreatic insufficiency.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications – Complete alcohol cessation and smoking cessation are crucial for halting disease progression.
  • Pain management – Initially, paracetamol or NSAIDs. In refractory cases, opioids or coeliac plexus blocks may be needed.
  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) – To manage steatorrhoea and malabsorption (e.g., Creon®).
  • Insulin therapy – For managing diabetes secondary to endocrine insufficiency.
  • Surgical or endoscopic interventions – For complications such as pseudocysts or bile duct obstruction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pseudocyst formation – Occurs in up to 25% of cases due to fluid accumulation in areas of necrotic pancreatic tissue.
  • Pancreatic cancer – CP is a risk factor for pancreatic adenocarcinoma, particularly in those with hereditary pancreatitis.
  • Diabetes mellitus – Occurs in approximately 50% of patients due to destruction of islet cells.
  • Malnutrition – Due to prolonged malabsorption and decreased caloric intake.
