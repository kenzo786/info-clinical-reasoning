Baker's Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • A Baker's cyst, also known as a popliteal cyst, is a fluid-filled swelling in the popliteal fossa (the space behind the knee) caused by the distension of the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa. It often results from underlying knee joint pathology such as osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or meniscal tears, leading to excess synovial fluid production and its leakage into the bursa.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population Prevalence: Approximately 10% of adults with knee complaints.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Common in patients with knee osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis, often identified during imaging.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Similar prevalence globally, especially in populations with a high burden of joint disorders.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Underlying knee joint pathology increases synovial fluid production.
  • Excess synovial fluid causes increased intra-articular pressure.
  • Fluid dissects into the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa through a communication between the bursa and knee joint capsule.
  • The bursa distends, forming a palpable cyst in the popliteal region.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Rapid onset in association with trauma or knee effusion.
  • Chronic Phase: Gradual development over weeks to months, often asymptomatic unless complicated by rupture or compression of surrounding structures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Posterior knee swelling - Most common, caused by distension of the bursa.
  • Knee pain - Often associated with underlying pathology such as osteoarthritis.
  • Stiffness or restricted range of motion - Due to mechanical obstruction or joint inflammation.
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases are incidental findings during imaging for other reasons.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass in the popliteal fossa - Soft, fluctuant swelling on physical examination.
  • Tenderness - Over the cyst, indicating inflammation or rupture.
  • Reduced knee flexion - Mechanical limitation due to the size of the cyst.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe calf pain - May indicate cyst rupture and fluid tracking down the leg, mimicking deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Redness or warmth - Could suggest infection or inflammatory arthritis.
  • Severe leg swelling - Compression of venous structures, potentially leading to venous insufficiency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Isolated calf swelling without popliteal mass - Due to fluid tracking after rupture.
  • Pain radiating down the leg - Mimics nerve impingement syndromes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - Identifies a cystic lesion in the popliteal fossa and distinguishes it from solid masses or DVT.
  • MRI - Provides detailed imaging of the knee joint and identifies associated intra-articular pathology.
  • Doppler ultrasound - Excludes DVT in cases of suspected rupture.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman with a history of knee osteoarthritis presents with gradual onset of posterior knee swelling and mild discomfort, worsened by prolonged standing.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis - Unilateral calf swelling, erythema, and warmth.
  • Popliteal artery aneurysm - Pulsatile mass, confirmed by Doppler ultrasound.
  • Soft tissue tumour - Solid mass with no fluctuation or transillumination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Conservative - Observation, physiotherapy, and management of underlying joint pathology.
  • Aspiration - Symptomatic relief, though recurrence is common.
  • Surgical intervention - For persistent or complicated cysts, such as rupture or neurovascular compression.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Cyst rupture - Leads to severe calf pain and swelling.
  • Neurovascular compression - Results in leg paraesthesia or venous insufficiency.
