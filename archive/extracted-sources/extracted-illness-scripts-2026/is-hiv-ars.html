HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome) Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Acute retroviral syndrome (ARS) refers to the early symptomatic phase of HIV infection, typically occurring 2-4 weeks after initial exposure. This phase resembles a flu-like or mononucleosis-like illness and represents the body's initial response to the rapid viral replication of HIV. It is often characterised by a wide range of non-specific symptoms and can be mistaken for common viral infections.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, HIV prevalence among adults is estimated to be around 2.5 per 1,000, with higher rates in certain populations such as men who have sex with men (MSM) and Black African communities.
  • Worldwide, approximately 39 million people live with HIV, with the highest prevalence in sub-Saharan Africa.
  • Acute HIV infections are often underreported due to the non-specific presentation and may go unrecognised unless there is a high index of suspicion.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Following exposure, HIV infects CD4+ T cells, dendritic cells, and macrophages. The virus rapidly replicates, leading to a significant drop in CD4+ T cells during the acute phase.
  • The immune response becomes activated, producing antibodies and HIV-specific cytotoxic T cells, which helps reduce viral load, but does not eliminate the infection.
  • This leads to a period of clinical latency, but during ARS, the rapid viral replication and immune activation are responsible for the flu-like symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Incubation: After exposure to HIV, symptoms of ARS typically develop within 2-4 weeks.
  • Acute Phase: This phase lasts from 1 to 3 weeks, where symptoms resolve as the immune system mounts an initial response.
  • Latency: Following the acute phase, the infection enters a clinical latency period, lasting years to decades, during which the virus continues to replicate at low levels.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Fever - Due to systemic immune activation and cytokine release in response to high viral replication.
  • Fatigue - Caused by immune system activation and the body’s response to high viral loads.
  • Sore throat - Represents mucosal immune response and inflammation of lymphoid tissues in the oropharynx.
  • Rash - A non-pruritic maculopapular rash, typically on the trunk, due to immune complex deposition in the skin.
  • Myalgia/Arthralgia - Related to inflammatory mediators and cytokine release.
  • Headache - Due to systemic inflammation and possible meningeal irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy - Generalised lymphadenopathy reflects the immune system's response to widespread viral dissemination.
  • Oropharyngeal erythema - Inflammation of the throat and tonsils due to immune cell activation.
  • Maculopapular rash - An immune-mediated rash typically seen on the trunk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent headache - May indicate early neurological involvement or HIV-associated meningitis.
  • Severe weight loss or chronic diarrhoea - Could signify progression to advanced HIV or opportunistic infections.
  • Altered mental status or focal neurological deficits - Suggests possible HIV encephalitis or opportunistic CNS infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Some patients present with mild or no symptoms, leading to missed diagnoses. This is more likely in individuals with partial immune compromise or those on pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • HIV antibody and p24 antigen test - This combination test detects HIV earlier than antibody-only tests. Positive within 2-4 weeks of infection.
  • HIV RNA PCR - Detects viral RNA, typically positive within 10 days post-exposure and useful during the acute phase.
  • Full blood count - May show lymphopenia during ARS.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old man presents with a 10-day history of fever, sore throat, fatigue, and a maculopapular rash after unprotected sexual contact. He reports generalised lymphadenopathy and muscle aches.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis (EBV) - Similar presentation but often includes more prominent pharyngitis and splenomegaly, and negative HIV testing.
  • Influenza - Acute onset of fever and myalgia, but typically lacks lymphadenopathy and rash.
  • Secondary syphilis - Presents with rash and systemic symptoms, but syphilis serology will be positive, and rash typically involves palms and soles.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • UK guidelines recommend starting antiretroviral therapy (ART) as soon as HIV is diagnosed, even in the acute phase, with a combination of three drugs (e.g., tenofovir, emtricitabine, and dolutegravir).
  • Symptomatic treatment for fever, sore throat, and other symptoms may be provided as supportive care.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="HIV (Acute Retroviral Syndrome)"][/sc]
  • Early progression to AIDS if untreated, with the development of opportunistic infections such as pneumocystis pneumonia or tuberculosis.
  • HIV encephalopathy, presenting with cognitive decline and neurological deficits.
  • Persistent generalised lymphadenopathy, which can be a marker of chronic immune activation.
