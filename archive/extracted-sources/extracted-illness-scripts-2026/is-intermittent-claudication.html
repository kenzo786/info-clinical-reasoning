Intermittent Claudication
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Intermittent claudication is a symptom characterised by cramping pain in the muscles, typically in the legs, induced by exercise and relieved by rest. It is caused by insufficient blood flow to the affected muscle due to peripheral arterial disease (PAD).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: ~1-4% in individuals over 40 years of age.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: ~15-20% of patients with PAD.
  • Worldwide prevalence: ~3-10% in individuals aged 50 years or older, increasing with age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerosis leads to arterial lumen narrowing in the lower limbs.
  • During physical activity, the demand for oxygenated blood exceeds the supply due to reduced perfusion, causing ischaemia and pain.
  • Rest alleviates symptoms as the metabolic demand decreases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset with progressive worsening over months or years.
  • May stabilise in some cases but can progress to critical limb ischaemia if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Exertional leg pain - Cramping pain, often in the calf, occurring after a consistent walking distance due to muscle ischaemia.
  • Rest relief - Pain resolves within minutes of rest as oxygen demand normalises.
  • Progressive pain threshold - Distance walked before pain onset decreases over time as the disease progresses.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Reduced peripheral pulses - Weak or absent pulses in the affected limb due to arterial narrowing or occlusion.
  • Pallor on elevation - Limb becomes pale when raised due to compromised blood flow.
  • Delayed capillary refill - Indicates poor arterial perfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Rest pain - Suggests critical limb ischaemia and impending tissue damage.
  • Ulceration or gangrene - Indicates severe ischaemia and risk of limb loss.
  • Rapid worsening of symptoms - May signal acute limb ischaemia due to arterial thrombosis or embolism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Atypical pain location - Pain in thighs, buttocks, or feet due to proximal or distal arterial involvement.
  • Minimal symptoms despite advanced disease - Seen in individuals with limited mobility or extensive collateral circulation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Ankle-brachial index (ABI) - ABI ≤ 0.9 confirms PAD; ≤ 0.4 suggests severe ischaemia.
  • Duplex ultrasound - Visualises arterial stenosis and flow characteristics.
  • CT/MR angiography - Detailed imaging to locate stenosis or occlusion.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with progressive calf pain after walking 200 metres, relieved by rest, over the past year.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis - Associated with neurogenic claudication, relieved by sitting rather than standing.
  • Chronic venous insufficiency - Symptoms include leg heaviness and swelling rather than pain on exertion.
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Presents with burning or tingling rather than exertional pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification - Smoking cessation and supervised exercise programmes to improve symptoms.
  • Pharmacological therapy - Antiplatelet agents (e.g., aspirin), statins, and cilostazol (if appropriate).
  • Revascularisation - Angioplasty or bypass surgery for severe, refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intermittent Claudication"][/sc]
  • Critical limb ischaemia - Persistent rest pain, ulcers, or gangrene; occurs in ~1-2% of cases annually.
  • Cardiovascular events - High risk of myocardial infarction and stroke due to systemic atherosclerosis.
