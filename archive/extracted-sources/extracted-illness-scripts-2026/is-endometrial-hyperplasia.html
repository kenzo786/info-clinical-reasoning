Endometrial Hyperplasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia is a condition characterised by the thickening of the endometrium (the lining of the uterus) due to excessive proliferation of endometrial cells. It results from prolonged exposure to unopposed oestrogen without progesterone. It can be classified into simple or complex types, with or without atypia, where atypia indicates abnormal cellular changes that may increase the risk of progression to endometrial cancer.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • The exact prevalence of endometrial hyperplasia in the general population is not well established. In the UK, it is more commonly diagnosed in women over 35 years of age. Endometrial hyperplasia is a precursor to endometrial carcinoma, with complex atypical hyperplasia having a 29% risk of progression to cancer. Worldwide, endometrial hyperplasia occurs more frequently in women with obesity, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), or those on long-term oestrogen therapy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia occurs due to chronic stimulation of the endometrium by oestrogen without the balancing effect of progesterone, which normally induces shedding of the endometrial lining. This prolonged oestrogen exposure can lead to uncontrolled proliferation of endometrial cells, resulting in glandular crowding and thickening of the endometrium. Simple hyperplasia features increased gland-to-stroma ratio, whereas complex hyperplasia shows more glandular complexity. Atypical hyperplasia involves cellular atypia, increasing the risk of malignant transformation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia typically develops gradually over months to years, particularly in the presence of risk factors such as unopposed oestrogen exposure. Symptoms like abnormal uterine bleeding may present early, but progression to atypia or cancer can take several years, particularly in untreated cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB) - Most common symptom, presents as heavy menstrual bleeding, irregular cycles, or postmenopausal bleeding. This occurs due to the irregular shedding of an excessively thickened endometrial lining.
  • Menorrhagia - Prolonged and excessive menstrual bleeding due to increased endometrial surface area.
  • Intermenstrual Bleeding - Bleeding between periods, related to unstable endometrial lining and irregular shedding.
  • Postmenopausal Bleeding - Often a key symptom in older women, potentially signifying malignancy in cases of atypia or complex hyperplasia.
  • Asymptomatic - Some patients may remain asymptomatic, with the condition identified during evaluation for other issues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Examination - Usually normal but may reveal an enlarged uterus in cases of concurrent pathology such as uterine fibroids.
  • Abnormal Uterine Size - In rare cases, the uterus may appear bulky due to concurrent uterine pathology.
  • Endometrial Thickening on Ultrasound - Transvaginal ultrasound often shows increased endometrial thickness, particularly >4 mm in postmenopausal women, indicating hyperplasia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal Bleeding - May suggest malignancy, as endometrial hyperplasia can progress to cancer, especially in women with atypia.
  • Rapidly Progressive Symptoms - Worsening bleeding or pelvic pain may indicate progression to endometrial carcinoma.
  • Severe Anaemia - Due to heavy blood loss from prolonged or excessive bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Minimal Symptoms - In some cases, particularly in perimenopausal women, mild or infrequent bleeding may mask the underlying severity of the hyperplasia.
  • Silent Hyperplasia - Some women, particularly those on hormone therapy, may have hyperplasia without significant symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Transvaginal Ultrasound (TVUS) - Shows thickened endometrium (>4 mm in postmenopausal women). This is used to assess the degree of endometrial thickening.
  • Endometrial Biopsy - Gold standard for diagnosis, identifying cellular atypia and differentiating between simple and complex hyperplasia. Atypical changes indicate a higher risk for cancer.
  • Hysteroscopy with Biopsy - Provides direct visualisation of the endometrium, useful for diagnosing focal lesions and confirming hyperplasia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with intermittent postmenopausal bleeding for 3 months. Transvaginal ultrasound shows an endometrial thickness of 6 mm. Endometrial biopsy reveals complex atypical hyperplasia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial Carcinoma - Unlike hyperplasia, carcinoma often presents with more aggressive and rapid symptoms, with histopathology showing malignant cells.
  • Uterine Fibroids - Present with heavy menstrual bleeding but typically do not cause postmenopausal bleeding. Ultrasound may show fibroid masses.
  • Endometrial Polyps - Cause intermenstrual bleeding, but endometrial thickness is often normal or only slightly increased on TVUS. Diagnosis confirmed with hysteroscopy.
  • Adenomyosis - Characterised by heavy, painful periods and a uniformly enlarged uterus. TVUS shows a heterogeneous myometrial pattern.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Progestin Therapy - First-line treatment for simple hyperplasia without atypia, using oral or intrauterine progestins to counteract oestrogen effects.
  • Hysterectomy - Recommended for women with complex atypical hyperplasia, particularly if fertility is not a concern, due to the high risk of progression to endometrial carcinoma.
  • Endometrial Ablation - Considered in some cases of non-atypical hyperplasia for women who have completed childbearing, although recurrence risk exists.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial Carcinoma - A major complication, especially in cases with atypia, with a risk of progression in up to 30% of cases of complex atypical hyperplasia.
  • Severe Anaemia - Resulting from prolonged heavy bleeding, requiring iron supplementation or blood transfusions.
  • Infertility - Long-standing untreated hyperplasia can affect fertility, particularly in cases with hormonal imbalances like PCOS.
