Medication-Induced Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced hypertension refers to elevated blood pressure (BP) caused by the use of specific medications. These medications may directly or indirectly alter cardiovascular, renal, or hormonal function, resulting in sustained or transient increases in BP. Common offenders include nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), corticosteroids, oral contraceptives, certain antidepressants, and immunosuppressants like cyclosporine.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Approximately 1-2% of hypertensive cases are thought to be due to medications.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Likely higher in hospital settings where certain drugs (e.g., immunosuppressants, corticosteroids) are frequently used.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Varies globally depending on the common use of hypertensive-inducing drugs in different healthcare systems.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Medications can cause hypertension through various mechanisms:
    • Increased sodium and water retention (e.g., NSAIDs, corticosteroids).
    • Sympathetic nervous system activation (e.g., sympathomimetic drugs, certain antidepressants).
    • Vasoconstriction or vascular remodelling (e.g., calcineurin inhibitors like cyclosporine).
    • Hormonal effects, such as increased aldosterone secretion or estrogen/progesterone-related BP elevation (e.g., oral contraceptives).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: BP elevation can occur within hours to days after initiating the offending medication.
  • Chronic Phase: Prolonged use of the medication leads to persistent hypertension over weeks to months.
  • Resolution: BP usually returns to baseline within days to weeks after discontinuing the offending drug, although chronic hypertension may persist in some cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Medication-induced hypertension is often discovered incidentally during routine BP checks, as elevated BP often causes no symptoms in the early stages.
  • Headaches - This symptom arises from increased intracranial pressure due to elevated systemic BP.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness - Caused by changes in vascular tone or cerebral perfusion in response to high BP.
  • Palpitations - May result from sympathetic nervous system stimulation caused by certain medications (e.g., sympathomimetics or antidepressants).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Elevated BP - Defined as systolic BP >140 mmHg and/or diastolic BP >90 mmHg, directly measurable using a sphygmomanometer.
  • Peripheral oedema - May occur in response to fluid retention caused by drugs such as NSAIDs or corticosteroids.
  • Weight gain - Linked to fluid retention seen with corticosteroids or certain hormonal therapies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Severe headache or visual disturbances - Suggestive of hypertensive urgency or emergency with end-organ damage (e.g., hypertensive encephalopathy).
  • Chest pain or dyspnoea - Could indicate acute coronary syndrome or heart failure as a result of untreated severe hypertension.
  • Acute confusion or altered mental status - May signify hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke, both of which are emergencies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Fatigue or general malaise - These nonspecific symptoms may be the only indication of medication-induced hypertension, particularly in older adults.
  • Worsening of pre-existing hypertension - Patients with controlled hypertension may experience a gradual increase in BP that may be misattributed to poor compliance or disease progression.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • 24-hour ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM) - Helps confirm sustained hypertension, ruling out "white coat" hypertension. Medication-induced hypertension typically shows sustained BP elevation throughout the day.
  • Serum electrolytes - Look for hypokalaemia in corticosteroid or diuretic-induced hypertension. Hyperkalaemia may occur with NSAID or calcineurin inhibitors due to reduced renal function.
  • Renal function tests - Assess serum creatinine and eGFR, as certain medications (e.g., NSAIDs) may impair renal function, contributing to BP elevation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of gradually increasing blood pressure after being prescribed ibuprofen for chronic arthritis. She has been asymptomatic but has recently noticed intermittent headaches and mild ankle swelling.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - Unlike medication-induced hypertension, this develops more gradually and is not linked to recent medication changes.
  • Renal artery stenosis - This may also cause secondary hypertension, but usually presents with renal bruit and a significant rise in creatinine after ACE inhibitors or ARBs.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - Episodic hypertension, palpitations, and sweating are more characteristic of this catecholamine-secreting tumour.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation or replacement of the offending drug - First-line approach. If discontinuation is not possible (e.g., immunosuppressants), adjust the dose or consider BP-lowering agents.
  • Antihypertensive medications - Options include ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics, depending on the patient's comorbidities and drug profile.
  • Monitoring - Regular BP monitoring to assess for improvement after stopping or adjusting the medication.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) - Persistent hypertension may lead to CKD, particularly if medications impair renal perfusion (e.g., NSAIDs).
  • Hypertensive emergency - Severe cases can lead to organ damage, including acute coronary syndromes, stroke, or heart failure.
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) - Prolonged elevated BP increases myocardial workload, causing LVH, which can progress to heart failure.
