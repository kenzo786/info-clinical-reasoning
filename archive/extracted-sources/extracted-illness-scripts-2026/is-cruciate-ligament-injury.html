Cruciate Ligament Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Cruciate ligament injuries involve damage to one of the two major stabilising ligaments within the knee joint: the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) or posterior cruciate ligament (PCL). These ligaments cross each other within the knee joint, providing stability during movement and preventing excessive anterior or posterior tibial translation relative to the femur.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • In the UK, ACL injuries have an incidence of approximately 30 per 100,000 person-years.
  • PCL injuries are less common, accounting for less than 10% of knee ligament injuries.
  • Worldwide, the incidence of cruciate ligament injuries is higher in populations involved in sports, particularly those requiring pivoting and rapid changes in direction (e.g., football, skiing).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Excessive forces (e.g., sudden deceleration, pivoting, or impact trauma) result in mechanical failure of the ligament fibres.
  • The ACL is commonly injured through a non-contact mechanism involving hyperextension or valgus stress with rotation.
  • PCL injuries typically occur from direct impact, such as a dashboard injury during a car accident, causing posterior tibial translation.
  • Ligament tears lead to joint instability, impaired proprioception, and subsequent risk of secondary joint damage, including meniscal tears and early osteoarthritis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Sudden onset of pain, swelling, and instability immediately following injury.
  • Subacute Phase: Symptoms improve over days to weeks but instability may persist.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent instability, difficulty with high-demand activities, and potential secondary complications such as meniscal tears or osteoarthritis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Instability or "giving way" sensation - Reflects impaired ligament function leading to joint instability.
  • Acute pain - Due to ligament rupture and associated tissue injury.
  • Swelling - Rapid-onset effusion from haemarthrosis in ACL injuries; slower in PCL injuries.
  • Restricted range of motion - Secondary to swelling and pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Lachman test positive - Increased anterior tibial translation indicative of ACL injury.
  • Posterior drawer test positive - Excessive posterior tibial translation indicating PCL injury.
  • Effusion - Visible or palpable swelling of the knee joint due to haemarthrosis or synovial fluid accumulation.
  • Quadriceps avoidance gait - Patients may exhibit an altered gait to compensate for instability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Locking of the knee - May indicate associated meniscal tear or loose bodies.
  • Gross instability - Suggests severe ligamentous injury or multiple ligament involvement.
  • Neurovascular compromise - Rare but requires urgent evaluation to rule out vascular injury or compartment syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic instability without acute trauma - May occur in low-grade or missed cruciate injuries.
  • Isolated pain without instability - Could indicate partial tears or compensatory stress injuries.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • MRI - Gold standard imaging to confirm ligament tears and associated injuries (e.g., bone bruising, meniscal tears).
  • Knee X-ray - Used to exclude fractures; may show Segond fracture (pathognomonic for ACL tear).
  • Clinical tests - Lachman test, pivot shift test (ACL); posterior drawer test (PCL).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male football player presents with acute knee pain, swelling, and a sensation of instability after twisting his knee during a match.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Meniscal tear - Often presents with locking and joint line tenderness.
  • Collateral ligament injury - Localised pain and instability without effusion.
  • Patellar dislocation - Visible deformity and lateral knee pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Conservative: Physiotherapy for muscle strengthening and proprioception, particularly in non-athletic individuals or partial tears.
  • Surgical: Arthroscopic reconstruction (commonly for ACL injuries in active individuals).
  • Post-operative rehabilitation: Gradual physiotherapy for functional recovery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cruciate Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic instability - Persistent functional limitations.
  • Osteoarthritis - Increased risk due to altered biomechanics and joint stress.
  • Meniscal injuries - Commonly occur secondary to instability.
