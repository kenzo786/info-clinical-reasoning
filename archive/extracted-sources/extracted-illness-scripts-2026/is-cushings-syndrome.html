Cushing's Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cushing's syndrome is a disorder caused by chronic exposure to excessive glucocorticoids, primarily cortisol. This can be endogenous (due to conditions such as an adrenocorticotropic hormone [ACTH]-secreting pituitary adenoma) or exogenous (from prolonged glucocorticoid therapy). It leads to a characteristic constellation of symptoms and signs, including central obesity, muscle weakness, hypertension, and glucose intolerance.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Rare, with a prevalence of approximately 1-2 per 1,000,000 people.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Slightly higher, as more severe cases are identified and referred; 2-3 per 1,000,000 people per year.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, Cushing's syndrome affects around 10-15 people per million each year, with the majority of cases being caused by exogenous corticosteroid use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cushing's syndrome arises from excessive cortisol, which is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) from the hypothalamus stimulates the pituitary to release ACTH, which then promotes cortisol production in the adrenal cortex.
  • In ACTH-dependent forms (Cushing's disease or ectopic ACTH production), excessive ACTH drives adrenal hyperplasia and cortisol overproduction.
  • In ACTH-independent forms, primary adrenal overproduction (e.g., adrenal adenoma) leads to excessive cortisol without elevated ACTH.
  • Cortisol affects glucose metabolism, immune function, fat distribution, and protein breakdown, causing many of the clinical features of Cushing's syndrome.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset over months to years, with worsening symptoms over time.
  • Acute presentation is rare and typically associated with rapid-onset cases of ACTH-producing tumours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Weight gain (especially central obesity) - Excess cortisol causes fat redistribution to the trunk, neck, and abdomen due to altered fat metabolism.
  • Muscle weakness - Cortisol induces protein breakdown, leading to muscle atrophy, particularly in the proximal limbs.
  • Hypertension - Cortisol enhances the vasoconstrictive effects of catecholamines and increases sodium retention, raising blood pressure.
  • Glucose intolerance or diabetes - Cortisol increases gluconeogenesis and inhibits insulin sensitivity, leading to hyperglycaemia.
  • Easy bruising - Cortisol impairs collagen synthesis, weakening blood vessels and making the skin more fragile.
  • Fatigue - This results from the combination of muscle atrophy, glucose dysregulation, and disrupted circadian rhythms due to cortisol excess.
  • Depression or anxiety - Cortisol affects neurotransmitter systems, contributing to mood disorders in patients.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Moon face - Round facial appearance due to fat deposition.
  • Dorsocervical fat pad ("buffalo hump") - Accumulation of fat at the back of the neck, a characteristic feature of cortisol excess.
  • Abdominal striae - Purple, wide stretch marks caused by skin thinning and collagen breakdown due to excess cortisol.
  • Hirsutism - Excessive hair growth in women due to increased androgens, often accompanying cortisol dysregulation.
  • Acne - Elevated cortisol increases sebaceous gland activity, leading to acne, particularly in women.
  • Osteoporosis - Cortisol inhibits osteoblast function, causing decreased bone density and increased fracture risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Severe hypertension - Suggests adrenal crisis or rapid disease progression, risking cardiovascular events.
  • Uncontrolled hyperglycaemia - Can lead to diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state if untreated.
  • Pathological fractures - Indicate severe osteoporosis and increased risk of morbidity.
  • Psychiatric instability (psychosis or severe depression) - Can signal severe cortisol-induced psychiatric disturbances.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with isolated symptoms such as hypertension or diabetes without the classic appearance, delaying diagnosis.
  • Subclinical Cushing's syndrome: Mild cortisol excess, often found incidentally in imaging studies, without overt clinical features.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • 24-hour urinary free cortisol - Elevated cortisol levels (>50 nmol/day) confirm cortisol excess.
  • Dexamethasone suppression test - Failure to suppress cortisol (<50 nmol/L) after 1 mg dexamethasone indicates Cushing's syndrome.
  • Late-night salivary cortisol - Elevated levels (>5.7 nmol/L) reflect the loss of the normal diurnal variation of cortisol secretion.
  • ACTH levels - Differentiates ACTH-dependent (high ACTH) from ACTH-independent (low ACTH) causes.
  • Imaging (MRI or CT) - MRI of the pituitary for ACTH-dependent cases or CT of the adrenal glands for ACTH-independent causes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old woman presents with progressive weight gain, easy bruising, and new-onset hypertension over 18 months. Physical examination reveals central obesity, a moon face, and abdominal striae.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) - Both conditions present with hirsutism and obesity, but PCOS lacks the typical signs of Cushing's (e.g., purple striae) and cortisol excess on testing.
  • Metabolic syndrome - Overlaps with features of Cushing's (obesity, hypertension, glucose intolerance), but cortisol levels are normal.
  • Pseudo-Cushing's (e.g., chronic alcoholism, depression) - Elevated cortisol due to stress-related causes, but biochemical testing shows normal circadian rhythm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Surgery - First-line for ACTH-dependent Cushing's disease (pituitary adenoma resection) and adrenal causes (adrenalectomy).
  • Medical management - Ketoconazole or metyrapone can suppress cortisol production in patients unsuitable for surgery or as a bridge to surgery.
  • Radiotherapy - Used in cases where pituitary surgery is unsuccessful or contraindicated.
  • Glucocorticoid replacement - Required post-adrenalectomy or in secondary adrenal insufficiency post-pituitary surgery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cushing's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Hypertension, hyperglycaemia, and dyslipidaemia increase the risk of myocardial infarction and stroke.
  • Osteoporosis and fractures - Due to impaired bone metabolism, patients have a higher risk of pathological fractures.
  • Infections - Immunosuppression from chronic cortisol excess predisposes to recurrent infections.
  • Psychiatric disorders - Depression, anxiety, and cognitive dysfunction can persist even after biochemical remission.
