Drug Abuse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Drug abuse is defined as the recurrent, maladaptive use of psychoactive substances despite adverse consequences on physical, mental, or social health. This includes use of both legal (prescription or over-the-counter) and illegal drugs. Core characteristics include cravings, compulsive drug-seeking behaviour, tolerance, and withdrawal symptoms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 5-10% of UK adults report drug misuse each year, with younger adults (16-24 years) having higher rates of drug use.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Around 1-3% of hospital admissions are related to drug misuse, though this varies by hospital and region.
  • Worldwide prevalence: The global prevalence of drug abuse varies but is estimated at 5.5% among adults aged 15-64, with significant regional variations depending on cultural, legal, and social factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Reward pathway activation: Drug abuse often activates the brain's mesolimbic dopamine system, leading to dopamine release, which reinforces the behaviour and causes euphoria.
  • Neuroadaptation and tolerance: Repeated exposure causes adaptations in neural pathways, decreasing drug sensitivity and leading to tolerance.
  • Withdrawal syndrome: When drug use is reduced, neuroadaptations create a dysphoric state, often with physiological symptoms, reinforcing the cycle of use to avoid withdrawal.
  • Chronic neural impact: Long-term use causes structural changes in the brain, altering cognition, emotion, and decision-making.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Initiation phase: Typically begins with experimental or recreational use, often during adolescence or early adulthood.
  • Dependence phase: Regular use escalates to dependence, with tolerance and withdrawal symptoms, typically within months to years.
  • Chronic phase: Prolonged use leads to long-term health issues and higher doses to avoid withdrawal, occurring over years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Cravings - Intense desire to use the drug, driven by changes in the brain's reward system reinforcing drug-seeking behaviour.
  • Withdrawal symptoms - Physical and psychological symptoms (e.g., anxiety, sweating, tremors) arise from neuroadaptations after discontinuation.
  • Compulsive use - Continued use despite harm due to decreased impulse control linked to prefrontal cortex changes.
  • Mood swings - Alternating euphoria and depression are common due to neurotransmitter fluctuations.
  • Social isolation - Users often withdraw from non-drug-related activities due to prioritisation of drug use.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Pupillary changes - Constriction (opiates) or dilation (stimulants) based on drug type, reflecting autonomic nervous system effects.
  • Track marks - Visible marks on skin from intravenous use, indicating injection sites.
  • Weight changes - Often weight loss (stimulants) or gain (sedatives) due to altered appetite and metabolic effects.
  • Dental decay - Known as "meth mouth" for methamphetamine users due to dry mouth and poor oral hygiene.
  • Skin infections - Recurrent skin abscesses, often related to unclean injection practices in intravenous users.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Severe withdrawal - Risk of life-threatening complications like seizures (especially with alcohol or benzodiazepine dependence).
  • Overdose - Signs include respiratory depression (opiates) or seizures (stimulants), indicating potential fatality.
  • Psychosis - Hallucinations or paranoia, particularly with stimulants or hallucinogens, can lead to harm to self or others.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Subclinical symptoms - Some individuals present with mild symptoms, only recognisable with a detailed history.
  • Functional decline without overt substance history - Presentations such as unexplained cognitive decline, weight loss, or chronic infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Urine toxicology screen - Identifies commonly abused drugs within hours to days of use.
  • Blood tests - Liver function tests may show abnormalities in long-term alcohol or drug users.
  • Imaging (e.g., CT/MRI) - Used to identify complications such as brain damage in cases of overdose or trauma from use.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male with a 5-year history of heroin use presents with track marks, weight loss, and recent social withdrawal, reporting intense cravings and unsuccessful cessation attempts.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Schizophrenia - Psychotic features may mimic stimulant-induced psychosis but lack substance use history.
  • Depression with substance misuse - Both share mood instability, but drug misuse patterns differentiate them.
  • Bipolar disorder - Manic episodes may present with similar euphoria but without substance dependence signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Detoxification and withdrawal management: Medically supervised detox, often with benzodiazepines for alcohol, and methadone or buprenorphine for opioids.
  • Psychosocial support: Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), group therapy, and peer support programs (e.g., Narcotics Anonymous).
  • Harm reduction strategies: Needle exchange programmes and safe injection advice to prevent infections.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Drug Abuse"][/sc]
  • Infective complications - Risk of HIV, hepatitis B and C, and bacterial infections from injection practices.
  • Cardiovascular issues - Chronic stimulant use is associated with myocardial infarction, stroke, and hypertension.
  • Neurological complications - Cognitive impairment and risk of seizure disorders from stimulant or alcohol misuse.
