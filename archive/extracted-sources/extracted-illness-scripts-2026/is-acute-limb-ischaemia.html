Acute Limb Ischaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute limb ischaemia (ALI) is a sudden decrease in arterial blood flow to a limb, leading to a potential threat to limb viability if not promptly managed. It is characterised by the rapid onset of the "six P's": pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, and poikilothermia. ALI is a vascular emergency requiring urgent diagnosis and treatment to prevent irreversible damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe limb pain - Sudden onset pain due to ischaemia of nerve and muscle tissues, causing stimulation of pain receptors.
  • Paraesthesia - Tingling or numbness results from early nerve ischaemia.
  • Weakness - Motor dysfunction occurs as ischaemia progresses, leading to muscle compromise.
  • Cold sensation - Affected limb feels cold due to loss of arterial supply and heat exchange with the environment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Pale appearance of the limb caused by lack of arterial blood flow.
  • Pulselessness - Absent pulses distal to the occlusion point, indicating vascular obstruction.
  • Poikilothermia - Limb temperature matches the environment due to cessation of blood flow.
  • Paralysis - Severe ischaemia affects motor nerves, resulting in loss of movement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Paralysis and paraesthesia - Indicate advanced ischaemia threatening limb viability and may signal irreversible tissue damage.
  • Absent capillary refill - Suggests profound ischaemia with no microcirculation.
  • Skin necrosis - Late-stage indicator of tissue death and potential for systemic complications (e.g., sepsis).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Incidence in the UK GP population is approximately 1 in 10,000 annually.
  • Hospital prevalence is higher due to associated comorbidities such as atrial fibrillation and atherosclerosis.
  • Worldwide prevalence correlates with ageing populations and increasing prevalence of cardiovascular risk factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • ALI results from an acute obstruction of arterial blood flow, often due to embolism (e.g., thrombus from the heart in atrial fibrillation) or thrombosis in situ (e.g., atherosclerotic plaque rupture).
  • Ischaemia leads to a lack of oxygen and nutrient delivery, causing cellular dysfunction and tissue necrosis if prolonged.
  • Peripheral nerve and muscle tissues are particularly sensitive, with irreversible damage occurring after 6 hours in most cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Hyperacute phase: Onset within minutes to hours, dominated by pain and sensory disturbances.
  • Acute phase: Progression of symptoms with worsening ischaemia over hours to a day.
  • Late phase: Tissue necrosis, systemic complications such as rhabdomyolysis or sepsis after 6–12 hours.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain: Seen in neuropathic patients or delayed presentations; high risk of missed diagnosis.
  • Intermittent symptoms: Caused by collateral blood flow that temporarily alleviates ischaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Doppler ultrasound: Absence of flow in affected vessels confirms obstruction.
  • CT angiography: Reveals the site and cause of obstruction, such as emboli or thrombosis.
  • ABPI (ankle-brachial pressure index): Severely reduced (<0.3), indicating critical ischaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation presents with sudden onset of severe pain in the left leg, associated with pallor, coldness, and absent distal pulses, lasting 3 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Chronic limb ischaemia: Gradual onset, intermittent claudication, presence of collaterals.
  • Deep vein thrombosis: Swelling, warmth, and tenderness without arterial findings.
  • Compartment syndrome: Severe pain with swelling and tense compartments, but pulses usually present initially.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Anticoagulation with intravenous heparin to prevent thrombus propagation.
  • Revascularisation: Surgical embolectomy or thrombolysis depending on the cause.
  • Supportive care: Pain control, hydration, and monitoring for complications such as reperfusion injury.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Irreversible tissue damage: Limb loss due to necrosis.
  • Systemic complications: Rhabdomyolysis, hyperkalaemia, and renal failure from reperfusion injury.
  • Sepsis: Resulting from necrotic tissue infection.
