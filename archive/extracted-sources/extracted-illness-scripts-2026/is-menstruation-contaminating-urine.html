Menstruation Contaminating Urine
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Menstruation contaminating urine is the presence of menstrual blood in a urine sample, leading to false positives for blood and possibly affecting other urinary test results, such as red blood cell (RBC) count. It is not a pathological condition but can complicate the interpretation of urinalysis by obscuring true urinary abnormalities.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Prevalence: Common among menstruating women who undergo urine testing, particularly in outpatient and primary care settings.
  • Estimates suggest that a notable proportion of false-positive haematuria results in women of reproductive age are attributable to menstrual contamination.
  • Worldwide, menstruation as a source of urinary contamination is prevalent wherever standardised urinalysis is employed without specific timing instructions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • During menstruation, blood from the uterus may enter the external urethral area, mixing with urine during sample collection. Blood in the urine sample can interfere with dipstick tests and microscopic examination by mimicking haematuria or urinary tract infections (UTIs).
  • The presence of red blood cells in urine due to menstrual contamination does not indicate intrinsic urinary tract pathology. However, this contamination can lead to misinterpretation unless clearly accounted for.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Occurs acutely with the onset of menstruation and is present throughout the duration of menses.
  • After menstruation ends, any contamination from menstrual blood in urine samples typically resolves, making it transient and cyclic in nature.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Typically Asymptomatic - Menstrual contamination itself is usually asymptomatic and only noted upon lab testing when false haematuria is detected.
  • False Haematuria - A positive dipstick test for blood in a patient without urinary symptoms; caused by the presence of menstrual blood rather than actual urinary pathology.
  • Possible Anxiety or Concern - The detection of blood can cause patient concern, especially if they are unaware of the impact of menstruation on test results.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Positive Urine Dipstick for Blood - The dipstick test may show a false positive for blood due to the presence of menstrual blood, without actual RBCs from the urinary tract.
  • Microscopic RBCs in Urine - Urine microscopy might reveal RBCs; however, these cells originate from menstrual blood rather than from the urinary system.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Persistent Haematuria Outside of Menstrual Periods - Persistent blood in urine outside menses suggests true urinary pathology (e.g., UTI, stones, or malignancy).
  • Flank Pain, Dysuria, or Fever - Presence of these symptoms may indicate an underlying UTI or other urinary pathology requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Menstrual Haematuria in Postmenopausal Women - Rarely, postmenopausal bleeding could contaminate urine; this is atypical and warrants further investigation for gynaecological pathology.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Urine Dipstick - May show a positive result for blood; menstrual blood can cause this even in the absence of urinary tract bleeding.
  • Urine Microscopy - Red blood cells seen, but without signs of infection or tubular cells. RBC casts would be absent as the blood is extrinsic to the urinary system.
  • Repeat Urinalysis Post-Menses - Negative results post-menses typically confirm menstrual contamination as the source of prior positive findings.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman with no urinary symptoms presents for a routine urinalysis which shows blood on dipstick, aligning with her menstrual cycle. Repeat urinalysis post-menses is negative, confirming menstrual contamination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - Typically presents with dysuria, frequency, and may have positive nitrites or leukocyte esterase on dipstick, which menstrual contamination lacks.
  • Urinary Calculi - May present with haematuria and severe flank pain, which is absent in cases of menstrual contamination.
  • Glomerulonephritis - Often accompanied by RBC casts on microscopy and proteinuria, not seen in menstrual contamination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • No treatment is required for menstrual contamination in urinalysis. Patients should be educated on timing urine sample collection post-menses if possible to avoid false positives.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Menstruation Contaminating Urine"][/sc]
  • False Diagnosis of Haematuria - May lead to unnecessary investigations if not recognised as menstrual contamination.
  • Patient Anxiety - Can lead to patient concern over erroneous haematuria findings; mitigated through proper education.
