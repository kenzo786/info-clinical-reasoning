GI Tract Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • GI tract cancer encompasses malignancies that arise in the various organs of the gastrointestinal system, including the oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, colon, rectum, and anus. These cancers are characterised by uncontrolled cell growth, with potential to invade nearby tissues and metastasise to distant organs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 0.3% for colorectal cancer, lower for other GI cancers.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Colorectal cancer is one of the most common GI malignancies, with high admission rates. Stomach, oesophageal, and pancreatic cancers also contribute significantly to hospital cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Colorectal cancer has the third highest cancer incidence globally, with stomach and oesophageal cancers also ranking within the top 10 cancers worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • 1. Genetic mutations (e.g., APC, KRAS, TP53 in colorectal cancer) lead to abnormal cell growth.
  • 2. Disruption in normal cellular apoptosis causes accumulation of atypical cells in GI mucosa.
  • 3. Formation of polyps or dysplastic lesions that progress to invasive cancer.
  • 4. Tumour cells invade the mucosa, submucosa, and muscularis layers, eventually reaching lymphatic and blood vessels.
  • 5. Metastasis to liver, lungs, and distant lymph nodes may occur in advanced stages.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Often asymptomatic, particularly for cancers like colorectal and stomach.
  • Intermediate Stage: Development of local symptoms (e.g., obstruction in bowel cancer, dysphagia in oesophageal cancer).
  • Advanced Stage: Symptoms related to metastasis (e.g., jaundice if liver is affected in GI cancers, or respiratory symptoms if lungs are involved).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain - Often due to tumour obstruction or invasion; common in colon cancer.
  • Weight loss - Caused by anorexia, catabolic tumour effects, and nutrient malabsorption.
  • Change in bowel habits - Persistent diarrhoea or constipation, commonly seen in colorectal cancer.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing, especially with oesophageal cancer.
  • Fatigue - Resulting from anaemia or systemic cancer effects.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass - Abdominal or rectal mass may indicate advanced tumour.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver suggests metastasis, common in GI tract cancers.
  • Jaundice - Due to bile duct obstruction, often associated with metastatic spread to the liver.
  • Anaemia - Common in GI cancers due to chronic blood loss or nutrient malabsorption.
  • Ascites - Peritoneal carcinomatosis may cause fluid accumulation in the abdomen.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Significant due to underlying cancer cachexia and metabolic demands of tumour growth.
  • Progressive dysphagia - Indicates possible oesophageal or gastric tumour invasion.
  • Persistent rectal bleeding - Raises suspicion of colorectal malignancy, especially with anaemia.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Chronic blood loss in the GI tract suggests malignancy until proven otherwise.
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate obstruction or tumour perforation, both requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases - Early-stage GI cancers may have no symptoms, detected incidentally or through screening.
  • Mild anaemia without GI symptoms - Particularly in older adults, this could be due to occult GI bleeding.
  • Nonspecific symptoms (fatigue, nausea) - May delay diagnosis as they are attributed to benign causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Colonoscopy - Detects polyps or tumours in the colon; biopsy can confirm cancer.
  • Upper endoscopy - Identifies oesophageal or gastric lesions and allows biopsy.
  • CT scan - Provides staging information; can detect metastases in liver, lungs, and lymph nodes.
  • Blood tests (e.g., CEA for colorectal cancer) - Tumour markers may aid diagnosis and monitor treatment response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • 65-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of weight loss, fatigue, and intermittent rectal bleeding. Physical examination reveals pallor and a palpable mass in the left lower quadrant.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Diverticular disease - Similar symptoms but typically lacks weight loss and presents with localised pain.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - May mimic symptoms, but chronic course, younger age, and extra-intestinal symptoms are distinguishing features.
  • Benign polyps - May cause bleeding but are less likely to present with systemic signs (e.g., weight loss).
  • IBS - Typically lacks red-flag features such as rectal bleeding and weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection - Primary treatment for localised cancers in the colon, stomach, and oesophagus.
  • Chemotherapy - Used in advanced stages or as an adjunct post-surgery (e.g., FOLFOX regimen for colorectal cancer).
  • Radiotherapy - Often used in oesophageal and rectal cancers for local control.
  • Palliative care - Important for symptom management in metastatic disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="GI Tract Cancer"][/sc]
  • Obstruction - Common in colorectal cancer, leading to bowel obstruction.
  • Perforation - Possible if tumour causes weakened bowel wall; requires urgent surgery.
  • Metastasis - Often spreads to liver, lungs, or peritoneum, worsening prognosis.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes - Rare, but may include hypercoagulability or cachexia.
