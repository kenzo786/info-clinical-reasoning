Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus (HZO) is a reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV) within the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V1). It manifests as a painful, vesicular rash over the forehead, upper eyelid, and nose and can involve ocular structures, potentially leading to vision loss if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approx. 3 per 1,000 annually for shingles; HZO accounts for ~10-20% of cases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common cause of ophthalmic A&E visits, particularly among immunocompromised and elderly patients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher incidence in populations with lower vaccination rates or ageing demographics; similar proportion of HZO among shingles cases globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • After primary infection with VZV (chickenpox), the virus becomes latent in sensory ganglia.
  • Reactivation occurs under conditions of immune suppression, leading to viral replication and migration along sensory nerves.
  • In HZO, reactivation affects the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve, causing inflammation, neuronal damage, and vesicular eruptions in the dermatomal distribution.
  • Ocular involvement arises from direct viral spread or inflammation-induced damage to ocular structures.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Prodromal phase: 1-3 days of fever, malaise, and localised pain or paraesthesia in the V1 dermatome.
  • Acute phase: Vesicular rash appears, progressing to pustules and crusting over 7-10 days.
  • Chronic phase: Postherpetic neuralgia or chronic ocular complications can persist for months or years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Pain in the forehead or eye region: Due to inflammation and viral replication in the trigeminal nerve.
  • Vesicular rash: Typically unilateral and confined to the V1 dermatome.
  • Photophobia: From anterior uveitis or keratitis.
  • Blurred vision: Caused by corneal involvement, uveitis, or secondary glaucoma.
  • Systemic symptoms: Low-grade fever, malaise, and fatigue during the prodromal phase.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Vesicular rash on the forehead or nose: Sign of trigeminal nerve V1 involvement (Hutchinson's sign if on the nose).
  • Conjunctival injection: Indicates conjunctivitis or episcleritis.
  • Corneal opacities: Due to keratitis or stromal involvement.
  • Anterior uveitis: Presents as perilimbal redness, photophobia, and keratic precipitates.
  • Reduced visual acuity: Secondary to corneal scarring, uveitis, or glaucoma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Hutchinson’s sign: Vesicles on the tip of the nose; indicates high risk of ocular involvement due to nasociliary nerve involvement.
  • Severe eye pain or vision loss: May indicate acute glaucoma or severe keratitis.
  • Prolonged fever or neurological symptoms: Suggests encephalitis or meningoencephalitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Absence of rash (zoster sine herpete): May still have pain and ocular involvement; diagnosis requires PCR confirmation.
  • Bilateral involvement: Rare, usually in immunocompromised patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis: Based on history and dermatological findings.
  • PCR for VZV: Confirms diagnosis in atypical cases.
  • Ophthalmologic examination: Detects keratitis, uveitis, or raised intraocular pressure.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old immunocompetent male presents with a 3-day history of unilateral forehead pain, vesicular rash, and photophobia, with reduced vision in the right eye.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Herpes simplex keratitis: Similar corneal findings but lacks dermatomal rash.
  • Cellulitis: Diffuse erythema and swelling without vesicles.
  • Orbital pseudotumour: Painful eye movement and proptosis without vesicular rash.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Antivirals: Oral aciclovir or valaciclovir within 72 hours of rash onset.
  • Corticosteroids: May be used to reduce inflammation, particularly in severe ocular involvement.
  • Topical therapy: Artificial tears or topical antivirals for keratitis.
  • Pain management: NSAIDs or neuropathic pain agents for postherpetic neuralgia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)"][/sc]
  • Postherpetic neuralgia: Chronic neuropathic pain due to nerve damage.
  • Corneal scarring: Can lead to vision loss.
  • Secondary glaucoma: From increased intraocular pressure due to uveitis.
  • Encephalitis: Rare but severe complication from CNS involvement.
