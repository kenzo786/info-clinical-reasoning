Gastroenteritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis is an acute inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract, usually caused by infection with viruses, bacteria, or parasites. It primarily affects the stomach and intestines, leading to symptoms like diarrhoea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and dehydration. The condition is typically self-limiting but can lead to complications, particularly in vulnerable populations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Gastroenteritis accounts for 1-2% of consultations annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Gastroenteritis is a leading cause of emergency hospital admissions, especially in children and the elderly.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 2 billion cases occur globally each year, with the highest incidence in low-income countries, where sanitation is poor. Diarrhoeal diseases, including gastroenteritis, are a major cause of mortality in children under 5 years of age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Pathogens like viruses (e.g., rotavirus, norovirus), bacteria (e.g., E. coli, Salmonella), or parasites (e.g., Giardia) enter the gastrointestinal tract, often through contaminated food or water. The infection disrupts normal fluid absorption and digestion in the intestines, leading to increased secretion of water and electrolytes into the bowel lumen, resulting in diarrhoea and vomiting. This process also triggers the immune system’s inflammatory response, contributing to symptoms like fever and abdominal cramping.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Typically, gastroenteritis has an acute onset, with symptoms appearing 12 to 48 hours after exposure to the pathogen. Symptoms usually peak within 24-72 hours and resolve within a week. In viral cases, symptoms are often self-limiting. Bacterial gastroenteritis may last longer and have a more severe course, requiring intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea - Increased frequency and liquidity of stool due to inflammation and impaired absorption in the intestines.
  • Vomiting - Reflex action triggered by irritation of the stomach lining or toxins produced by the infecting pathogen.
  • Abdominal pain/cramping - Caused by irritation of the intestinal walls and increased peristalsis.
  • Fever - Inflammatory response to infection, with pyrogens increasing body temperature.
  • Fatigue - A result of dehydration and the body’s inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, and delayed capillary refill due to fluid loss from diarrhoea and vomiting.
  • Fever - Raised body temperature secondary to the inflammatory response.
  • Tachycardia - Elevated heart rate due to dehydration and compensatory response to fluid loss.
  • Hypotension - Low blood pressure in severe dehydration due to reduced circulating blood volume.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Bloody diarrhoea - Can indicate invasive bacterial infection or haemorrhagic colitis, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Severe dehydration - Associated with lethargy, sunken eyes, or absence of tears, requiring hospitalisation for intravenous fluids.
  • High fever (>39°C) - May indicate a more severe infection, such as bacterial gastroenteritis or sepsis.
  • Prolonged symptoms (>1 week) - May suggest complications like post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome or chronic infections (e.g., parasitic gastroenteritis).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - May present with confusion, lethargy, or falls rather than typical symptoms due to dehydration or electrolyte imbalances.
  • Immunocompromised individuals - May have prolonged or more severe symptoms, and a higher risk of invasive infection or secondary bacterial complications.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Stool microscopy and culture - Identifies bacterial, parasitic, or viral pathogens. Positive cultures for Salmonella, Shigella, or E. coli are common in bacterial cases.
  • Stool PCR - Can rapidly detect viral pathogens like norovirus and rotavirus.
  • FBC - Leucocytosis suggests a bacterial infection, while a normal WBC count may be seen in viral cases.
  • U&Es - May show electrolyte imbalances such as hypokalaemia or hyponatraemia, indicating dehydration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old boy presents with a 2-day history of diarrhoea and vomiting. He has had reduced fluid intake, appears lethargic, and has dry mucous membranes on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Food poisoning - More rapid onset (within hours of ingestion), often associated with a specific meal, typically resolves within 24 hours.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - Chronic diarrhoea, often with bloody stools, and associated with weight loss or extra-intestinal manifestations.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - Chronic, recurrent symptoms of diarrhoea, constipation, or abdominal discomfort, often triggered by stress, without an infectious cause.
  • Coeliac disease - Chronic diarrhoea and malabsorption, often associated with fatigue, weight loss, and nutrient deficiencies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) - First-line treatment for mild to moderate dehydration.
  • Intravenous fluids - Indicated in severe dehydration or if oral rehydration fails.
  • Antibiotics - Generally not recommended except for specific bacterial infections such as Campylobacter, Shigella, or in immunocompromised patients.
  • Antiemetics - Considered for persistent vomiting, such as ondansetron in children.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - Can lead to hypovolaemic shock and renal failure, especially in young children and the elderly.
  • Electrolyte imbalance - Hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia can occur, particularly in patients with severe diarrhoea and vomiting.
  • Post-infectious IBS - Chronic diarrhoea and abdominal discomfort can persist after the acute infection resolves.
