Pancoast's Tumour
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Pancoast's tumour, also known as a superior sulcus tumour, is a form of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) that arises at the lung apex. It often invades nearby structures such as the brachial plexus, subclavian vessels, or sympathetic chain, leading to a characteristic set of symptoms and signs including shoulder pain, Horner's syndrome, and upper limb weakness or paresthesia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Rare, accounting for <1% of all lung cancers.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Represents approximately 3-5% of all NSCLCs diagnosed in tertiary centres.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Occurs globally with similar rates in populations with high smoking prevalence, given its strong association with smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Pancoast's tumours originate in the superior sulcus of the lung apex.
  • Local invasion into surrounding structures such as the brachial plexus leads to neuropathic pain and motor/sensory deficits in the upper limb.
  • Invasion of the sympathetic chain causes Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis).
  • Compression of subclavian vessels can lead to venous congestion or arterial compromise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with vague shoulder pain.
  • Progressive phase: Localised symptoms develop as the tumour invades adjacent structures (e.g., worsening pain, Horner's syndrome, neurological deficits).
  • Advanced phase: Systemic symptoms such as weight loss, fatigue, and dyspnoea may occur due to metastatic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Shoulder or upper chest pain - Resulting from invasion of the brachial plexus, causing neuropathic pain.
  • Arm weakness or numbness - Caused by brachial plexus involvement leading to motor and sensory deficits.
  • Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) - Due to invasion of the sympathetic chain.
  • Dyspnoea - May occur with vascular or tracheal compression.
  • Weight loss and fatigue - Non-specific systemic symptoms of advanced malignancy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Horner's syndrome - Ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis due to sympathetic chain involvement.
  • Weakness or atrophy of hand muscles - Indicating lower brachial plexus involvement (C8-T1).
  • Supraclavicular mass - Palpable tumour in advanced cases.
  • Venous engorgement - From subclavian vein compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Severe shoulder pain radiating to the arm - Suggestive of brachial plexus invasion.
  • Horner's syndrome - Indicates sympathetic chain involvement and tumour progression.
  • Rapid weight loss - May signify advanced disease or metastasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic apical tumour detected incidentally on imaging.
  • Chronic shoulder pain misdiagnosed as musculoskeletal pathology.
  • Isolated Horner's syndrome without other neurological deficits.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - May show an apical mass.
  • CT/MRI of the thorax - Identifies tumour size, extent, and local invasion.
  • Biopsy - Confirms histological diagnosis.
  • PET-CT - Evaluates metastatic spread.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old male smoker presents with 3 months of severe shoulder pain radiating to the arm, associated with ptosis and weight loss.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy - No Horner's syndrome; imaging shows cervical spine pathology.
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome - Neurovascular symptoms without mass on imaging.
  • Mesothelioma - Typically involves pleura rather than apical structures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Multimodal therapy: Combination of radiotherapy and chemotherapy (e.g., cisplatin-based regimens).
  • Surgical resection: Considered if the tumour is resectable and there is no metastatic disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pancoast's Tumour"][/sc]
  • Brachial plexopathy - Permanent motor/sensory deficits.
  • Superior vena cava syndrome - Due to venous compression.
  • Metastasis - Common sites include brain, liver, and bones.
