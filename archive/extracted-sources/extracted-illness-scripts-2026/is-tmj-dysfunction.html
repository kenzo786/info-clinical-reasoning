TMJ Dysfunction
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) dysfunction refers to a group of disorders affecting the masticatory muscles, the temporomandibular joint, and associated structures. It is characterised by pain, joint sounds (clicking or crepitus), and restricted jaw movement.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • UK prevalence: Approximately 10% of the general population reports TMJ-related symptoms, with 5% seeking treatment.
  • Hospital prevalence: TMJ dysfunction accounts for around 1-2% of GP consultations in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies from 5% to 12%, with a higher incidence in women between ages 20-40.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • TMJ dysfunction typically involves muscle hyperactivity or joint derangement. Overloading the joint due to parafunctional habits (e.g., bruxism) leads to inflammation and degeneration of the joint or its disc. The condition may result from stress, jaw misalignment, trauma, or arthritis, altering the joint's mechanics and causing pain or restricted movement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • TMJ dysfunction is typically chronic, with periods of exacerbation and remission. Symptoms can persist for months to years, with many patients experiencing episodic flare-ups, often triggered by stress or jaw overuse.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Jaw pain - Localised pain in the jaw, temples, or around the ear due to inflammation of the TMJ or surrounding muscles.
  • Clicking or popping sounds - Result of disc displacement or joint misalignment, leading to mechanical interference in the joint.
  • Limited jaw movement - Due to joint derangement or muscle spasm, restricting the normal range of motion.
  • Headaches - Muscle tension or strain in the temporalis and masseter muscles can lead to referred pain in the form of tension headaches.
  • Ear discomfort - Inflammation in the TMJ may cause earache, fullness, or tinnitus due to the anatomical proximity of the joint to the ear structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over TMJ - Palpation of the joint often reveals tenderness, indicating local inflammation.
  • Crepitus or clicking - Audible joint sounds during jaw movements, which suggest disc displacement or degenerative changes.
  • Deviation of the jaw - During mouth opening, the jaw may deviate to one side due to joint asymmetry or muscle dysfunction.
  • Bruxism (teeth grinding) - Evidence of worn teeth or complaints from the patient can be indicative of night-time teeth grinding, contributing to TMJ stress.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent pain - May indicate underlying serious joint pathology such as an infection, tumour, or significant inflammatory condition like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Trismus (inability to open mouth) - Suggests severe joint inflammation or potential infection, requiring urgent evaluation to rule out life-threatening causes like a peritonsillar abscess.
  • Unilateral swelling - May signify a joint tumour or severe inflammation, particularly in the absence of trauma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Some patients may present with no pain but noticeable joint sounds or jaw locking. These cases may be missed, especially if the patient does not perceive the sounds as problematic.
  • Aural symptoms - Isolated ear symptoms such as tinnitus or ear fullness without jaw pain can lead to misdiagnosis of an ear disorder, though TMJ dysfunction should be considered.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Palpation of the TMJ, assessing for tenderness, clicking, and jaw deviation.
  • Imaging - MRI is the gold standard for evaluating soft tissue changes, including disc displacement, while CT may reveal bony changes in advanced disease. X-ray can exclude fractures or degenerative joint disease.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Sometimes used to evaluate muscle activity in bruxism but not routinely performed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with intermittent jaw pain, clicking during mouth opening, and occasional headaches. She reports jaw stiffness in the mornings, and her symptoms worsen with stress.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Dental issues (e.g., tooth abscess) - Localised pain may be similar but is often more focal to a specific tooth, with no clicking or jaw deviation.
  • Ear infections - Can cause aural symptoms but do not typically present with jaw pain or clicking.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - Produces sharp, electric-like facial pain, but lacks the joint-specific symptoms such as clicking or limited movement.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) - Can affect the TMJ, but typically presents with systemic joint involvement and inflammatory markers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Includes rest, soft diet, and warm compresses.
  • Physiotherapy - Targeted exercises to improve jaw mobility and relieve muscle tension.
  • Medications - NSAIDs are commonly used to manage pain and inflammation. Muscle relaxants or low-dose tricyclic antidepressants may help with bruxism.
  • Dental splints - Can reduce the strain on the TMJ, particularly in patients with bruxism.
  • Surgical options - Reserved for refractory cases, including arthrocentesis or open-joint surgery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="TMJ Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Prolonged TMJ dysfunction can lead to persistent pain that may become difficult to manage.
  • Degenerative joint changes - Chronic inflammation or misuse may cause arthritis within the joint, leading to permanent restriction of movement.
  • Psychological impact - Persistent pain and dysfunction can result in anxiety or depression, particularly in those with stress-related exacerbations.
