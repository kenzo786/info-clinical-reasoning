Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is a functional gastrointestinal disorder characterised by chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits in the absence of an identifiable organic cause. The condition often fluctuates between episodes of diarrhoea, constipation, or a combination of both, and symptoms are frequently exacerbated by stress or dietary factors.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, IBS is estimated to affect about 10-20% of the general population, with women being more commonly affected than men (2:1 ratio).
  • Approximately 1 in 5 GP consultations in the UK involve gastrointestinal symptoms, with IBS being a frequent diagnosis.
  • Worldwide prevalence ranges from 5% to 15%, depending on diagnostic criteria and population studies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Although the exact pathophysiology of IBS is not fully understood, it is thought to result from an interaction of several factors:
    • Gut-brain axis dysregulation – Abnormal communication between the central nervous system and the gastrointestinal tract, leading to heightened sensitivity (visceral hypersensitivity).
    • Altered motility – Dysfunctional bowel motility patterns can cause constipation or diarrhoea.
    • Intestinal inflammation – Low-grade inflammation and immune activation may play a role in symptom generation.
    • Microbiota imbalance – Dysbiosis or altered gut microbiota composition is implicated in IBS pathogenesis.
    • Food hypersensitivity – Some patients have symptoms triggered by certain foods, possibly due to fermentable carbohydrates (FODMAPs).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic relapsing course – Symptoms of IBS tend to persist for months to years, fluctuating in severity over time.
  • Trigger-related exacerbations – Episodes of symptom worsening may be triggered by stress, infections, dietary changes, or hormonal fluctuations.
  • Stable in some patients – For some individuals, symptoms remain constant without significant variation over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain – Recurrent or continuous pain, often crampy in nature, typically worsened by eating and relieved by defecation. Pathophysiologically, this is due to abnormal motility and visceral hypersensitivity.
  • Bloating – A sensation of abdominal distension, possibly related to altered gas handling or gut fermentation by bacteria (dysbiosis).
  • Diarrhoea – Loose, frequent stools associated with urgency, often related to accelerated intestinal transit and abnormal motility.
  • Constipation – Infrequent, hard stools, usually reflecting slowed gut motility or impaired colonic propulsion.
  • Mucous stools – The passage of mucous in the stool without visible blood, which may be due to increased mucus production in response to gut irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal tenderness – Mild, diffuse tenderness on palpation, often without guarding or rebound tenderness, suggesting visceral pain without peritoneal irritation.
  • Abdominal distension – Visible bloating may be noted, corresponding to the patient’s subjective sensation of bloating.
  • Normal physical examination – In many cases, the physical examination is normal, reflecting the functional rather than structural nature of IBS.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss – May indicate a more serious underlying pathology such as malignancy or inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Rectal bleeding – Suggestive of organic disease such as colorectal cancer, polyps, or IBD rather than IBS.
  • Family history of colorectal cancer – Increases the likelihood of hereditary gastrointestinal conditions.
  • Nocturnal symptoms – Symptoms that wake the patient from sleep may indicate IBD, coeliac disease, or other organic pathologies rather than IBS.
  • Onset after age 50 – New-onset symptoms in older adults should prompt investigation for other serious conditions such as colorectal cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Non-GI symptoms – Patients may report fatigue, fibromyalgia, or psychological symptoms such as anxiety and depression, likely due to the overlapping functional nature of these disorders and central nervous system involvement.
  • Predominant bloating without pain – Some patients may present with significant bloating as their main symptom, with less emphasis on pain or bowel habit changes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • FBC (Full Blood Count) – To exclude anaemia (suggesting coeliac disease or IBD) or infection.
  • CRP (C-Reactive Protein) – Normal in IBS, used to rule out inflammation suggestive of IBD.
  • Coeliac serology – Anti-TTG antibodies to rule out coeliac disease.
  • Faecal calprotectin – Low in IBS but elevated in IBD, helping differentiate between the two conditions.
  • Stool microscopy and culture – To exclude infection, particularly in cases of diarrhoea-predominant IBS (IBS-D).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of crampy abdominal pain, alternating diarrhoea and constipation, and bloating. Symptoms are worsened by stress and relieved by defecation. She denies weight loss or rectal bleeding.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) – Unlike IBS, IBD presents with raised inflammatory markers, visible intestinal inflammation on endoscopy, and may involve bloody diarrhoea and weight loss.
  • Coeliac Disease – Characterised by malabsorption and positive coeliac serology (anti-TTG), coeliac disease often presents with weight loss, diarrhoea, and anaemia.
  • Colorectal Cancer – IBS does not typically present with rectal bleeding, weight loss, or significant change in bowel habits in those over 50, all of which raise suspicion for malignancy.
  • Chronic infection (e.g., giardiasis) – A history of recent travel or persistent diarrhoea may point to an infectious cause, which can be identified with stool tests.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification – Regular exercise, stress reduction, and dietary changes, including a low FODMAP diet, have shown efficacy in symptom control.
  • Pharmacological treatments – Depending on predominant symptoms:
    • Antispasmodics (e.g., mebeverine) for crampy pain.
    • Laxatives (e.g., polyethylene glycol) for constipation.
    • Anti-diarrhoeals (e.g., loperamide) for diarrhoea.
  • Probiotics – May improve symptoms in some patients by modulating gut flora.
  • Psychological therapies – Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) or gut-directed hypnotherapy may be beneficial for patients with severe or refractory symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)"][/sc]
  • Impact on quality of life – Chronic pain, frequent bowel disturbances, and psychological distress can significantly impair daily functioning.
  • Psychiatric comorbidities – Increased prevalence of anxiety and depression, possibly related to the gut-brain axis.
