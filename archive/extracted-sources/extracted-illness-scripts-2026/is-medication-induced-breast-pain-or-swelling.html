Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced breast pain or swelling is discomfort or enlargement of breast tissue resulting from the side effects of certain medications. Common culprits include hormonal treatments (e.g., oestrogen or testosterone therapies), antipsychotics, antidepressants, and certain cardiovascular drugs like calcium channel blockers. Symptoms can vary based on drug type, dosage, and individual patient response.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Varies by drug, with up to 5-10% of patients on antipsychotics and oestrogen therapies reporting breast symptoms.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less commonly reported, but higher in endocrine and oncology wards due to use of hormone-related drugs.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Comparable to the UK, with variation based on prescription patterns and drug accessibility.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Certain medications can increase levels of prolactin or mimic oestrogenic effects, leading to increased fluid retention and cellular proliferation in breast tissue.
  • Antipsychotics and antidepressants may increase prolactin secretion by blocking dopamine, resulting in breast enlargement and tenderness.
  • Oestrogenic medications may increase ductal and stromal tissue proliferation, causing discomfort or visible swelling.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms typically begin within weeks to months of starting the medication.
  • Progression: Symptoms may progress gradually and stabilise with continued use; alternatively, they may worsen if dosage is increased.
  • Resolution: Symptoms often improve within weeks after discontinuing the offending medication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Breast tenderness or pain - Due to increased fluid retention and proliferation in breast tissue, leading to pain or a dull ache.
  • Swelling or enlargement - Caused by ductal and stromal growth, often noted in those on hormonal therapies.
  • Nipple discharge - Often related to medications that increase prolactin (e.g., antipsychotics), leading to galactorrhoea in some cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Palpable breast enlargement - Tender or firm breast tissue may be observed during examination.
  • Discharge upon nipple expression - Milky discharge may be noted, particularly in those with elevated prolactin levels.
  • Symmetric or asymmetric swelling - Enlargement may be unilateral or bilateral, depending on individual response and medication effect.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • New-onset, unilateral breast mass - May indicate malignancy rather than medication-induced changes and warrants further evaluation.
  • Bloody nipple discharge - Suggestive of intraductal papilloma or malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Skin changes (dimpling, erythema) - Potential signs of inflammatory breast cancer or infection, not typical of medication-induced effects.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with asymmetry or minimal pain, which may be mistaken for normal variation rather than a medication side effect.
  • Patients with minimal prolactin elevation may still experience swelling or tenderness without galactorrhoea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Prolactin levels: Elevated in cases where prolactin-elevating medications are involved; aids in distinguishing from other causes.
  • Mammography/Ultrasound: Performed to rule out structural abnormalities in cases with unilateral symptoms or mass.
  • Medication review: Comprehensive assessment of prescribed and over-the-counter drugs to identify possible causative agents.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with bilateral breast tenderness and mild swelling, starting approximately two months after initiating an antipsychotic medication.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Mastitis: Typically presents with erythema and warmth, often accompanied by systemic symptoms such as fever.
  • Fibrocystic breast changes: Cyclic breast pain that is typically associated with the menstrual cycle, and not linked to medications.
  • Breast cancer: Usually unilateral, presenting as a firm, fixed mass with possible skin changes or nipple retraction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Medication adjustment: Consider lowering dose or discontinuing the offending agent where feasible.
  • Supportive treatment: NSAIDs or cold compresses for pain relief.
  • Alternative medications: If antipsychotics are necessary, consider options with lower prolactin-elevating effects.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain and swelling: Can impact quality of life, especially if symptoms persist despite medication changes.
  • Emotional and psychological impact: Breast changes can be distressing, potentially exacerbating underlying mental health conditions.
