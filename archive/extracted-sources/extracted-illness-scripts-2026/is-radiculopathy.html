Radiculopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathy is a condition characterised by the compression or irritation of a nerve root as it exits the spinal column, causing pain, numbness, weakness, or altered sensation along the distribution of the affected nerve. The condition typically affects the cervical or lumbar spine, and it may be due to degenerative changes, disc herniation, or less commonly, tumours or infections.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, lumbar radiculopathy is estimated to affect approximately 3-5% of the population, while cervical radiculopathy is less common, affecting about 0.1-0.3%. In primary care settings, radiculopathy accounts for a significant portion of back and neck pain consultations.
  • Hospital admissions for radiculopathy in the UK are less common unless associated with severe symptoms or complications. Worldwide, the prevalence ranges from 2-8%, depending on age and occupational risks.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathy occurs when a nerve root becomes compressed or irritated, typically by degenerative disc disease, herniated discs, osteophytes, or spinal stenosis. Mechanical compression leads to local inflammation and disruption of nerve conduction, causing sensory or motor deficits along the distribution of the affected nerve. The lumbar and cervical regions are most commonly involved due to their increased mobility and susceptibility to degenerative changes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of symptoms due to trauma, disc herniation, or acute injury. Symptoms can persist for days to weeks.
  • Subacute: Gradual worsening over weeks to months, often seen with degenerative causes like spondylosis.
  • Chronic: Persistent symptoms lasting longer than 12 weeks, often associated with long-term degenerative changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Pain: Radiating pain in the arm or leg, following the dermatome of the affected nerve root. This is caused by nerve irritation or compression and is the most common presenting symptom.
  • Numbness and paraesthesia: Patients often report tingling or "pins and needles" in the distribution of the affected nerve. This occurs due to altered sensory nerve conduction.
  • Weakness: Depending on the affected nerve root, patients may experience muscle weakness in the corresponding myotome due to impaired motor function.
  • Loss of reflexes: Affected reflexes are diminished due to disruption of the nerve pathway responsible for deep tendon reflexes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Positive straight leg raise (SLR) test: For lumbar radiculopathy, raising the leg passively causes sciatic pain, indicating nerve root irritation.
  • Weakness in specific muscle groups: Depending on the nerve root involved (e.g., wrist extension weakness in C6 radiculopathy or ankle dorsiflexion weakness in L5 radiculopathy).
  • Sensory loss: Loss of sensation in the dermatome of the affected nerve root, such as in the thumb and index finger in C6 radiculopathy or the lateral foot in S1 radiculopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Saddle anaesthesia: Indicates potential cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency requiring urgent intervention to prevent permanent damage.
  • Progressive neurological deficit: Suggests worsening compression or damage to the nerve root, possibly due to a large herniated disc or tumour.
  • Unexplained weight loss, fever, or night sweats: Could indicate malignancy or spinal infection, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • In some cases, patients may present with non-specific back or neck pain without clear radicular symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis. In others, they may only experience isolated weakness or sensory changes without significant pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • MRI of the spine: This is the gold standard for visualising nerve root compression. Findings may include disc herniation, spinal stenosis, or osteophytes compressing the nerve root.
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG): Used to assess the electrical activity of muscles and nerves, which helps confirm the presence of radiculopathy and distinguish it from peripheral neuropathy.
  • X-ray of the spine: May show degenerative changes, such as disc space narrowing or osteophytes, but is less sensitive for soft tissue or nerve compression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with a 6-week history of worsening lower back pain radiating down the right leg, associated with numbness in the lateral foot and weakness in dorsiflexion of the right ankle. He reports difficulty walking and worsening symptoms after prolonged sitting.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Typically presents with bilateral symptoms and is often associated with diabetes or other systemic causes, unlike the unilateral, dermatomal pattern seen in radiculopathy.
  • Spinal stenosis: Similar to radiculopathy but often involves multiple nerve roots and presents with symptoms worsening with standing or walking (neurogenic claudication).
  • Piriformis syndrome: Affects the sciatic nerve but typically presents with buttock pain radiating down the leg, without the clear dermatomal pattern seen in radiculopathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Conservative management: Initial treatment includes analgesia (e.g., NSAIDs or paracetamol), physiotherapy, and patient education on posture and activity modification. In the UK, the NICE guidelines recommend conservative treatment for most cases.
  • Interventional options: Epidural steroid injections may be considered for severe pain or where conservative management has failed.
  • Surgical management: For cases with significant neurological deficit or intractable pain, surgery such as discectomy or laminectomy may be required.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent nerve root compression can lead to ongoing pain and disability.
  • Permanent neurological deficits: If untreated, severe cases may result in muscle weakness, loss of sensation, or loss of reflexes.
  • Cauda equina syndrome: A rare but serious complication involving compression of the lower spinal nerve roots, leading to bowel or bladder dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia, and potentially irreversible neurological damage.
