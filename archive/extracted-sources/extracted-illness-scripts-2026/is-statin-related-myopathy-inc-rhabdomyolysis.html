Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Statin-related myopathy encompasses a spectrum of muscle-related symptoms ranging from mild myalgias to severe rhabdomyolysis, a potentially life-threatening condition characterised by the breakdown of muscle tissue and release of intracellular contents into the bloodstream.
  • It is associated with the use of statins, a class of lipid-lowering drugs that inhibit HMG-CoA reductase.
  • Rhabdomyolysis represents the severe end of the spectrum, often associated with marked elevations in creatine kinase (CK) levels and acute kidney injury (AKI).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of statin-associated muscle symptoms (SAMS) in UK GP populations is 1-5%, though self-reported rates may be higher (~10-15%).
  • Hospital-based prevalence of severe rhabdomyolysis related to statins is rare, approximately 0.1-0.2% in statin users.
  • Worldwide prevalence of rhabdomyolysis due to statins is estimated at less than 0.01%.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, reducing cholesterol synthesis and potentially impacting muscle cell membrane stability.
  • Disruption in mitochondrial function leads to decreased energy production and increased oxidative stress in muscle fibres.
  • Muscle fibre damage releases intracellular enzymes (e.g., CK) and myoglobin into the bloodstream, causing systemic effects and potentially AKI due to myoglobin-induced renal tubular injury.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Onset typically occurs within weeks to months of starting statins but can occur at any time, including after dose increases or drug interactions.
  • Rhabdomyolysis develops acutely over hours to days following substantial muscle injury.
  • Resolution occurs within days to weeks after cessation of statin use and supportive care, although severe cases may have prolonged recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Myalgia - Generalised muscle pain, often symmetrical, due to muscle fibre irritation and inflammation.
  • Muscle weakness - Proximal muscle groups are often involved due to impaired muscle function and damage.
  • Dark-coloured urine - Indicates myoglobinuria from muscle breakdown.
  • Fatigue - Due to systemic inflammation and reduced muscle function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Tender muscles - Palpable tenderness from muscle inflammation or injury.
  • Reduced muscle strength - Assessed during neurological examination, particularly in proximal groups.
  • Oliguria - Suggests AKI secondary to rhabdomyolysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Severe muscle pain and weakness - May indicate rhabdomyolysis and systemic muscle breakdown.
  • Dark urine - Suggests myoglobinuria and potential AKI.
  • Signs of AKI - Oliguria or anuria, elevated creatinine.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue without significant muscle symptoms.
  • Late-onset myopathy years after initiating statins.
  • Persistent CK elevation without symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Serum CK - Elevated, often >10 times the upper limit of normal in rhabdomyolysis.
  • Renal function tests - Elevated creatinine indicating AKI.
  • Urinalysis - Presence of myoglobinuria (positive dipstick for blood, negative for RBCs).
  • Liver function tests - May show transaminitis due to muscle damage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]

A 65-year-old man presents with diffuse muscle pain, weakness, and dark urine 3 weeks after increasing his atorvastatin dose. CK levels are markedly elevated, and renal function shows AKI.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Polymyositis - Distinguished by autoimmune markers and absence of statin use.
  • Hypothyroid myopathy - Associated with thyroid dysfunction, slower onset.
  • Drug-induced myopathy (other drugs) - Different drug history, often milder CK elevation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Statin cessation - Immediate discontinuation of the offending statin.
  • Hydration - Intravenous fluids to prevent or treat AKI.
  • CK monitoring - Regular testing to assess resolution of muscle injury.
  • Alternative lipid-lowering strategies - Consider non-statin options or lower-dose statins with monitoring.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Statin-Related Myopathy (Including Rhabdomyolysis)"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury - Due to myoglobinuria; occurs in severe cases.
  • Electrolyte abnormalities - Hyperkalaemia, hypocalcaemia due to muscle breakdown.
  • Long-term muscle weakness - Rare but possible in severe or untreated cases.
