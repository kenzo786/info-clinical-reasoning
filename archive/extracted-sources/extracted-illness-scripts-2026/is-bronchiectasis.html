Bronchiectasis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Bronchiectasis is a chronic pulmonary condition characterised by permanent dilatation and distortion of the bronchial airways, typically resulting from chronic inflammation and recurrent infections. It leads to impaired mucociliary clearance, chronic cough, sputum production, and recurrent respiratory infections.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 0.56% (rising with age).
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Often diagnosed in secondary care, especially in patients with recurrent pneumonia or chronic respiratory symptoms.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Highly variable; higher in lower-income countries due to untreated infections and lower vaccination rates.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Initial insult (infection, aspiration, or obstruction) damages the bronchial epithelium.
  • Chronic inflammation leads to neutrophilic infiltration and release of proteases, damaging elastin and cartilage.
  • Permanent bronchial dilatation ensues, impairing mucociliary clearance.
  • Stagnant mucus promotes recurrent infections, perpetuating inflammation and structural damage (vicious cycle hypothesis).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Often triggered by an infection or exacerbation, marked by increased symptoms.
  • Chronic phase: Ongoing symptoms of cough and sputum production with intermittent exacerbations.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening with increasing frequency of exacerbations and reduced lung function over years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Chronic productive cough - Caused by impaired mucociliary clearance leading to mucus accumulation.
  • Daily sputum production - Reflects chronic inflammation and recurrent infections.
  • Recurrent chest infections - Due to colonisation of pathogens in damaged airways.
  • Dyspnoea - Resulting from airway obstruction and decreased lung function.
  • Haemoptysis - Occurs due to erosion of bronchial vessels by chronic inflammation or infection.
  • Pleuritic chest pain - Indicates an exacerbation or associated infection such as pneumonia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Crackles - Reflect turbulent air passing through mucus-filled and dilated airways.
  • Wheezing - Due to airway narrowing or obstruction.
  • Clubbing - Indicates chronic hypoxaemia and systemic inflammation.
  • Hypoxaemia - May be evident in severe or exacerbated disease due to impaired gas exchange.
  • Cyanosis - A late sign indicating significant hypoxaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Massive haemoptysis - May indicate erosion of major blood vessels, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Rapidly progressive dyspnoea - Suggests acute respiratory failure or superimposed infection.
  • Weight loss - May indicate malignancy or severe systemic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Minimal symptoms - Some patients may present with limited cough or sputum production despite significant radiological findings.
  • Unexplained fatigue or malaise - Could indicate systemic inflammation in the absence of significant respiratory symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • High-resolution CT (HRCT) scan - Shows airway dilatation (signet ring sign) and mucus plugging.
  • Spirometry - Often reveals obstructive or mixed obstructive-restrictive pattern.
  • Sputum culture - Identifies pathogens like Pseudomonas aeruginosa or Haemophilus influenzae.
  • Blood tests - Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female with a history of recurrent pneumonia presents with a 6-month history of chronic cough, daily purulent sputum, and worsening breathlessness.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Less likely if productive cough persists without smoking history or significant dyspnoea.
  • Cystic fibrosis - Consider if early onset or systemic symptoms like malabsorption.
  • Tuberculosis - Associated with systemic symptoms and apical lung involvement.
  • Asthma - Usually lacks significant sputum production or structural changes on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Physiotherapy - Airway clearance techniques.
  • Antibiotics - Targeted based on sputum culture during exacerbations.
  • Bronchodilators - For symptomatic relief in obstructive disease.
  • Vaccination - Influenza and pneumococcal vaccines to prevent exacerbations.
  • Surgery - Reserved for severe, localised disease unresponsive to medical therapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Due to progressive lung damage and infections.
  • Cor pulmonale - Secondary to chronic hypoxaemia and pulmonary hypertension.
  • Recurrent infections - Can lead to abscess formation or sepsis.
