Cervical Ectropion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion, also known as cervical eversion, occurs when the columnar epithelium that lines the endocervical canal extends outward to the ectocervix. This condition is benign and is primarily associated with hormonal changes, such as those occurring during pregnancy or in women taking oestrogen-containing contraceptives.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Common, especially in women of reproductive age. No specific prevalence data, but frequently encountered in primary care settings.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Often seen incidentally during cervical smears or gynaecological examinations, especially in younger women.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Cervical ectropion is common globally, particularly in populations with high use of hormonal contraceptives.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • The endocervix is lined by columnar epithelium, while the ectocervix is covered by stratified squamous epithelium. In cervical ectropion, the columnar epithelium extends outward onto the ectocervix, which is often caused by increased levels of oestrogen. This can lead to increased cervical mucus production and a more fragile surface that is prone to irritation or minor trauma, leading to symptoms such as post-coital bleeding or increased vaginal discharge.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion typically develops gradually with hormonal changes and may persist for months or years, particularly in women who are pregnant or taking oral contraceptives. It may resolve spontaneously or after hormonal changes, such as cessation of contraceptive use or post-menopause.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic (commonest) - Most women with cervical ectropion do not experience any symptoms, and it is usually found incidentally during a routine cervical smear or gynaecological exam.
  • Increased vaginal discharge - Excessive mucus production due to exposure of the glandular cells, which produce more mucus than squamous epithelium.
  • Post-coital bleeding - The exposed columnar epithelium is more fragile and prone to trauma, leading to bleeding after intercourse.
  • Intermenstrual spotting - Similar to post-coital bleeding, the fragile columnar cells may bleed spontaneously between menstrual periods.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Visible ectropion on speculum examination - The cervix appears redder around the os due to the presence of glandular columnar epithelium, which is thinner and redder than the surrounding squamous epithelium.
  • Cervical tenderness - Occasionally, there may be mild tenderness on palpation if the ectropion is inflamed or irritated.
  • Increased cervical mucus - A speculum examination may reveal excess mucus production from the ectropion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Persistent post-coital bleeding - While this is often benign, it could signify more serious pathology, such as cervical cancer, and requires further investigation with colposcopy.
  • Abnormal cervical appearance - Any irregular, raised, or ulcerated areas should raise suspicion for malignancy.
  • Unexplained pelvic pain - If present with bleeding, this may suggest a more sinister condition, such as cervical infection or malignancy, requiring urgent attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Severe pelvic pain - Cervical ectropion usually doesn’t cause significant pain. If severe pain is present, consider an alternate diagnosis like pelvic inflammatory disease or endometriosis.
  • Recurrent infections - Women with cervical ectropion may report recurrent infections, though this is not a typical feature. Increased exposure of glandular cells could make the cervix more susceptible to infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Speculum examination - Allows direct visualisation of the cervix, revealing the characteristic redder appearance of cervical ectropion.
  • Cervical smear test - Ensures that there are no abnormal cells indicative of pre-malignant or malignant conditions. Ectropion does not affect the results but helps rule out other causes of symptoms like post-coital bleeding.
  • Colposcopy (if red flags present) - If there is persistent bleeding or an abnormal cervical appearance, colposcopy with biopsy may be performed to exclude dysplasia or malignancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents with post-coital bleeding and increased vaginal discharge for 6 months. She is taking combined oral contraceptives and is otherwise asymptomatic. Speculum examination reveals a red area around the external os, consistent with cervical ectropion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer - Unlike ectropion, cervical cancer may present with an irregular or ulcerated cervix, weight loss, and persistent pelvic pain.
  • Cervicitis - Inflammation of the cervix, often due to infection (e.g., chlamydia, gonorrhoea), which can cause discharge and bleeding but may also be accompanied by pain or systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Endometriosis - May cause post-coital bleeding but is usually associated with severe dysmenorrhea and chronic pelvic pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Observation - In asymptomatic women or those with mild symptoms, no treatment is necessary. Ectropion often resolves after pregnancy or cessation of hormonal contraceptives.
  • Ablation (if symptomatic) - Cryotherapy, cauterisation, or laser ablation may be offered if symptoms like persistent bleeding or discharge are bothersome.
  • Hormonal contraceptive change - Switching to a non-oestrogen contraceptive may be beneficial in resolving symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Recurrent bleeding - Persistent post-coital bleeding may affect quality of life and sexual health, necessitating further evaluation.
  • Infection risk - The exposed glandular epithelium may have a higher susceptibility to infection, though this is not well-established.
  • Anxiety or concern - Due to the bleeding or discharge, many women may become anxious about more serious conditions like cancer, requiring reassurance and appropriate investigation.
