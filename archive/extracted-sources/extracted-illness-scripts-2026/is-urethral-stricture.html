Urethral Stricture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Urethral stricture is a condition characterised by the narrowing of the urethral lumen due to scarring or fibrosis. This narrowing impedes the normal flow of urine, leading to varying degrees of obstruction. The condition can arise from trauma, infections, iatrogenic causes (e.g., surgical interventions), or inflammatory processes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Approximately 0.9–1% in adult males.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for 2–5% of urological presentations in secondary care.
  • Worldwide prevalence: More common in regions with a high incidence of urogenital infections, especially due to untreated gonococcal urethritis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Initial insult (e.g., trauma, infection) leads to inflammation and disruption of the urothelium and underlying tissue.
  • Healing response involves fibroblast proliferation and excessive collagen deposition, resulting in scar formation.
  • Fibrosis causes concentric narrowing of the urethral lumen, obstructing urinary flow.
  • Chronic obstruction can lead to secondary complications such as bladder wall thickening, diverticula, and upper urinary tract damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset of symptoms after trauma or infection, marked by inflammation and initial narrowing.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual worsening of obstruction over weeks to months due to progressive fibrosis.
  • Stable phase: Long-term complications such as recurrent urinary tract infections or bladder dysfunction may develop if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Decreased urinary flow - Results from mechanical obstruction of the urethral lumen by fibrotic tissue.
  • Straining during urination - Occurs due to increased intravesical pressure required to overcome the obstruction.
  • Incomplete bladder emptying - Due to the obstruction, residual urine remains in the bladder.
  • Dysuria - Inflammatory changes or infection associated with the stricture can irritate the urethra.
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs) - Stasis of urine promotes bacterial growth.
  • Haematuria - May result from irritation of the urethra or associated trauma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Palpable bladder - Indicative of urinary retention due to obstructed outflow.
  • Dribbling of urine - Reflects incomplete bladder emptying and overflow incontinence.
  • Urethral discharge - Seen in cases where infection coexists, particularly with a history of urethritis.
  • Decreased urinary stream calibre - Often observed directly during urination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Acute urinary retention - Complete inability to void, necessitating emergency intervention to prevent renal damage.
  • Haematuria with clots - May signify severe irritation or associated malignancy.
  • Recurrent UTIs with fever - Could indicate progression to upper tract infection or urosepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Detected incidentally during investigations for recurrent UTIs or infertility.
  • Chronic pelvic pain: May occur due to prolonged urinary stasis and bladder overactivity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Uroflowmetry - Reduced peak flow rate (<10 mL/sec), consistent with obstruction.
  • Retrograde urethrography - Demonstrates narrowing of the urethral lumen and location of the stricture.
  • Cystoscopy - Direct visualisation of the stricture.
  • Urine culture - To identify secondary infections.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]

A 45-year-old male presents with progressive difficulty in urination, straining, and a weak urinary stream over the past six months. He reports recurrent urinary infections but denies fever or haematuria.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia - Typically presents with similar obstructive symptoms but is associated with an enlarged prostate on examination.
  • Bladder neck contracture - Post-surgical scarring at the bladder outlet causing similar obstructive symptoms.
  • Urethral carcinoma - May present with haematuria and a palpable mass.
  • Neurogenic bladder - Differentiated by history of neurological disease and lack of mechanical obstruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Urethral dilation - Temporary relief of obstruction but with high recurrence rates.
  • Urethroplasty - Definitive surgical repair, particularly for long or recurrent strictures.
  • Suprapubic catheterisation - For acute retention or severe obstruction as a temporary measure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urethral Stricture"][/sc]
  • Recurrent UTIs - Due to urine stasis.
  • Bladder stones - Resulting from chronic urinary stasis and infection.
  • Hydronephrosis - Due to prolonged obstruction, leading to renal impairment if untreated.
