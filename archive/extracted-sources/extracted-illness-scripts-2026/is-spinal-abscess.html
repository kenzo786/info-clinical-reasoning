Spinal Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A spinal abscess, also known as an epidural abscess, is a collection of pus within the spinal epidural space, often caused by infection. It is a rare but serious condition that can lead to severe neurological deficits or paralysis if untreated. The abscess typically arises due to bacterial infection, most commonly from Staphylococcus aureus, though it can also involve other organisms. It requires urgent diagnosis and treatment to prevent permanent spinal cord damage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice prevalence: Very rare, estimated at 0.2 cases per 100,000 individuals annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 2-3 per 10,000 hospital admissions, with higher incidence in populations with risk factors (e.g., diabetes, intravenous drug use).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK, rare but with higher rates reported in developing countries due to healthcare access limitations and higher rates of uncontrolled infections.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Initial Infection: The causative bacteria often enter the bloodstream, commonly via skin infections, intravenous drug use, or during spinal instrumentation (e.g., lumbar punctures or spinal surgeries).
  • Spread to the Epidural Space: The pathogens travel through the bloodstream to the vertebral blood vessels and localise in the epidural space around the spinal cord.
  • Inflammation and Pus Formation: The infection induces an inflammatory response, leading to pus formation. As pus accumulates, it compresses the spinal cord, causing ischemia and nerve damage.
  • Progression to Neurological Damage: Persistent compression and lack of blood flow lead to permanent damage to spinal cord tissues if untreated, potentially resulting in paralysis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (days to weeks): Rapid onset of pain and neurological symptoms, particularly if abscess is large or rapidly expanding.
  • Subacute Phase (weeks): Neurological deficits develop more gradually if the abscess grows slowly.
  • Chronic Phase: Uncommon; some abscesses develop over months, especially in immunocompromised individuals, with slow progression of symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Back Pain - Persistent, severe pain at the level of the abscess, often the first symptom, caused by inflammation and pressure on local tissues.
  • Fever - Common due to systemic infection response, often seen with bacterial infections.
  • Radicular Pain - Pain radiating along nerve roots, indicating nerve compression from the abscess.
  • Neurological Deficits - Weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction develop due to spinal cord compression and nerve ischemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Localised Tenderness - Pain on palpation of the vertebrae or muscles near the abscess site, caused by inflammation.
  • Motor Weakness - Due to nerve root or spinal cord compression, often affecting the lower limbs.
  • Sensory Deficits - Reduced sensation below the level of the lesion due to nerve involvement.
  • Fever - Persistent or intermittent fever due to the underlying infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Rapidly Progressive Weakness - Signifies severe spinal cord compression; potential for irreversible damage if untreated.
  • Loss of Bladder or Bowel Control - Indicates advanced compression affecting autonomic nerves, a marker for urgent intervention.
  • Severe, Unrelenting Pain - Especially when combined with fever, this may indicate expanding infection and abscess growth.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Slow progression without fever, more common in immunocompromised patients (e.g., diabetes or HIV), can lead to missed diagnosis until neurological symptoms appear.
  • Isolated back pain without neurological symptoms; delays diagnosis as it may mimic musculoskeletal issues.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • MRI with Contrast - Gold standard, shows an epidural fluid collection and compression; contrast highlights abscess walls and inflammation.
  • Blood Cultures - Positive for organisms like Staphylococcus aureus, helps guide antibiotic therapy.
  • ESR and CRP - Typically elevated, indicating inflammation, although nonspecific.
  • FBC - May show leukocytosis as part of infection response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with severe lumbar back pain, fever, and progressive lower limb weakness over 2 weeks, with recent loss of bladder control.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Discitis - Often more localised pain, typically lacks fever or neurological deficits until late.
  • Vertebral Osteomyelitis - Similar infection but within bone; MRI shows bone involvement without epidural fluid collection.
  • Spinal Tumour - Presents with back pain and neurological deficits but lacks fever; MRI shows a mass rather than abscess.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Empiric Antibiotic Therapy - IV broad-spectrum antibiotics targeting Staphylococcus aureus and gram-negative organisms, later tailored based on culture results.
  • Surgical Drainage - Required for larger abscesses, especially with neurological deficits, to decompress the spinal cord.
  • Pain Management - Typically with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and opioids as needed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Paralysis - Due to irreversible spinal cord damage from delayed treatment.
  • Sepsis - Systemic spread of the infection, especially if untreated or in immunocompromised patients.
  • Chronic Pain - Persistent pain in cases with delayed treatment or residual nerve damage.
