Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (WKS) is a neurological disorder caused by thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, commonly due to chronic alcohol misuse. It consists of two stages: Wernicke encephalopathy, an acute and reversible condition characterised by confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia; and Korsakoff syndrome, a chronic stage marked by significant memory impairment and confabulation. Untreated Wernicke encephalopathy can lead to Korsakoff syndrome, resulting in lasting cognitive deficits.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: WKS is rare in the general population; however, among those with chronic alcohol use disorder, the prevalence is estimated to be around 12-14%.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Among hospitalised individuals with alcohol dependence, Wernicke encephalopathy is present in 10-20% of cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Global prevalence is difficult to determine due to underdiagnosis; however, it is estimated to affect 1-2% of the general population and up to 12% of individuals with chronic alcoholism.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Thiamine deficiency disrupts cerebral energy metabolism, leading to a decrease in ATP production, particularly affecting regions with high metabolic demands (such as the mammillary bodies, thalamus, and hypothalamus).
  • This deficiency causes neuronal cell death and damage, specifically in brain regions controlling memory, coordination, and ocular movements, leading to the characteristic symptoms of Wernicke encephalopathy.
  • If untreated, chronic thiamine deficiency can result in irreversible neuronal damage, leading to Korsakoff syndrome, characterised by persistent cognitive impairment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • **Acute phase (Wernicke encephalopathy):** Symptoms can develop rapidly within days to weeks, especially following a period of malnutrition or sudden reduction in dietary thiamine intake.
  • **Chronic phase (Korsakoff syndrome):** Develops gradually over months to years, especially if Wernicke encephalopathy is untreated. Symptoms persist and may worsen, with long-term memory and cognitive impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Confusion - Common and early symptom in Wernicke encephalopathy, reflecting thiamine-dependent metabolic dysfunction in the cerebral cortex.
  • Ataxia - Disruption of cerebellar and vestibular function leads to uncoordinated movements and gait disturbances.
  • Ophthalmoplegia - Weakness or paralysis of eye muscles due to thiamine deficiency affecting the oculomotor and abducens nerves, resulting in abnormal eye movements.
  • Memory impairment - In the chronic phase (Korsakoff syndrome), significant short-term memory loss occurs due to damage to the mammillary bodies and thalamus.
  • Confabulation - Patients may create stories to fill memory gaps, indicative of advanced memory dysfunction associated with Korsakoff syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Nystagmus - Involuntary eye movements due to damage to vestibular and oculomotor pathways affected by thiamine deficiency.
  • Ataxic gait - Wide-based, unsteady gait caused by cerebellar dysfunction, a common feature in acute Wernicke encephalopathy.
  • Disorientation - Patients may have difficulty with orientation to time, place, and person due to cortical involvement.
  • Hypotension - Low blood pressure can occur due to autonomic dysfunction associated with chronic alcohol misuse and thiamine deficiency.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Profound confusion or stupor - Suggests severe Wernicke encephalopathy and indicates risk of permanent cognitive impairment if not treated.
  • Coma - Occurs in advanced untreated cases and signals critical thiamine depletion; can lead to death.
  • Persistent amnesia - Indicates progression to Korsakoff syndrome, signalling irreversible damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • **Atypical mood changes** - Some patients may present primarily with agitation or depression rather than confusion, due to cortical dysfunction.
  • **Peripheral neuropathy** - Unexplained numbness or tingling may be seen in patients with chronic alcohol misuse, as thiamine deficiency also affects peripheral nerves.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Serum thiamine levels - May be low; however, not always reliable due to variability.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) - Characteristic findings include symmetric changes in the thalamus, mammillary bodies, and periventricular regions, indicating Wernicke encephalopathy.
  • Blood tests - Low blood glucose and other vitamin deficiencies (e.g., B12, folate) are common in chronic alcoholism and may be contributory.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • A 50-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol misuse presents with confusion, unsteady gait, and ophthalmoplegia, suggesting acute thiamine deficiency due to Wernicke encephalopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Hepatic encephalopathy - Similar presentation with confusion and ataxia, but typically seen in patients with liver disease and elevated ammonia levels.
  • Delirium tremens - Alcohol withdrawal syndrome causing tremor, confusion, and agitation, but lacks characteristic ophthalmoplegia of WKS.
  • Hypoglycaemia - Causes confusion and ataxia, but usually rapid improvement with glucose administration.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Administer high-dose intravenous thiamine (500 mg three times daily) over several days, followed by oral supplementation to replenish thiamine stores.
  • Supportive care, including rehydration, correction of electrolyte imbalances, and management of coexisting alcohol-related complications.
  • Close monitoring of neurological status to assess for improvement in confusion and coordination.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome (Alcohol)"][/sc]
  • Korsakoff syndrome - Chronic memory impairment due to irreversible brain damage if Wernicke encephalopathy is untreated.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Common in chronic alcohol users with thiamine deficiency, leading to sensory deficits and motor weakness.
  • Coma and death - Rare but possible in untreated Wernicke encephalopathy with severe thiamine deficiency.
