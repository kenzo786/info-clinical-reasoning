Nasal Polyps
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Nasal polyps are benign, soft, painless, and non-cancerous growths that develop on the lining of the nasal passages or sinuses due to chronic inflammation. They are often associated with conditions like allergic rhinitis, asthma, and chronic sinusitis. These growths can vary in size and can cause nasal obstruction, leading to breathing difficulties or loss of smell.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Estimated at 2-4% of the adult population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Nasal polyps account for around 20-40% of chronic rhinosinusitis cases requiring specialist care.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Roughly 1-4% of the global population, more common in adults over 40 years of age, and more frequently seen in men than women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Chronic inflammation, often due to conditions like allergies, infections, or asthma, leads to an overproduction of cytokines and other inflammatory mediators. This causes oedema and hyperplasia of the nasal mucosa. Over time, this results in the formation of soft, gelatinous polyps, most commonly in the middle meatus of the nasal cavity.
  • In some patients, particularly those with aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD), nasal polyps are associated with systemic eosinophilic inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset over months to years. Chronic sinus inflammation can lead to the slow formation of polyps, with symptoms progressively worsening if left untreated. They may recur despite treatment, particularly in patients with chronic inflammatory conditions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Nasal obstruction - Caused by physical blockage from the polyp tissue obstructing airflow through the nasal passages.
  • Rhinorrhoea (runny nose) - Occurs due to chronic inflammation and excessive mucus production in response to the polyp-induced irritation.
  • Hyposmia (reduced sense of smell) - Polyps physically block the olfactory cleft, preventing airflow to the olfactory nerve endings.
  • Facial pain/pressure - Secondary to sinus congestion and inflammation, especially if the sinuses become infected.
  • Snoring or sleep apnoea - Due to obstruction of the upper airway, leading to increased airway resistance during sleep.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Visible nasal polyps on anterior rhinoscopy - Soft, smooth, pale grey or yellowish masses seen in the nasal cavity.
  • Nasal congestion or reduced nasal airflow - Evident during examination as the nasal passages are physically narrowed.
  • Postnasal drip - Mucus accumulation in the throat, caused by increased mucus production due to inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Unilateral nasal obstruction or polyps - May indicate a neoplasm, such as inverted papilloma or nasopharyngeal carcinoma.
  • Recurrent epistaxis - Could suggest malignancy or underlying vascular abnormality.
  • Severe headache or visual changes - Potential signs of sinus involvement extending into the orbit or brain, indicating invasive fungal disease or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Absence of typical symptoms - Some patients, especially in early stages, may have minimal symptoms despite large polyps.
  • Children - Nasal polyps in children are rare and could be a sign of cystic fibrosis; a sweat test should be considered in these cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Nasal endoscopy - Direct visualisation of polyps in the nasal passages, providing a clear assessment of size, location, and extent.
  • CT scan of the sinuses - Used to assess the extent of sinus involvement, rule out complications, and evaluate for bone erosion or extension into the orbit or brain.
  • Allergy testing - Skin prick or serum IgE testing to identify underlying allergic triggers.
  • Blood eosinophil count - Elevated levels may indicate an underlying eosinophilic disorder, especially in patients with severe or recurrent polyps.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of progressive nasal congestion, loss of smell, and frequent sinus infections. Examination reveals bilateral nasal polyps on rhinoscopy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis without polyps - Presents with nasal congestion and rhinorrhoea but lacks visible polyps.
  • Inverted papilloma - Typically unilateral, often associated with epistaxis, and can be more locally destructive than benign nasal polyps.
  • Nasopharyngeal carcinoma - Unilateral obstruction, recurrent epistaxis, and potential facial or cranial nerve involvement.
  • Fungal sinusitis - Presents with facial pain, nasal discharge, and often more severe systemic symptoms, sometimes with visible fungal debris.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Intranasal corticosteroids - First-line treatment to reduce inflammation and polyp size (e.g., fluticasone nasal spray).
  • Oral corticosteroids - Short course may be used for more severe cases or exacerbations.
  • Surgical intervention - Functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS) is considered if medical therapy fails or in cases of large, obstructive polyps.
  • Biologic therapy - Dupilumab, a monoclonal antibody, is used for patients with recurrent or severe nasal polyps associated with asthma or eosinophilic disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Recurrent infections - Chronic nasal obstruction and inflammation predispose to bacterial sinus infections.
  • Sleep apnoea - Due to upper airway obstruction, polyps can contribute to obstructive sleep apnoea in some patients.
  • Asthma exacerbations - Particularly in patients with AERD, nasal polyps can be part of a triad that includes asthma and sensitivity to aspirin.
