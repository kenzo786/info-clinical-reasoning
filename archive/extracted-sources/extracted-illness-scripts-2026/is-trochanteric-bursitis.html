Trochanteric Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Trochanteric bursitis is the inflammation of the trochanteric bursa, a fluid-filled sac located near the greater trochanter of the femur. It is characterised by lateral hip pain, often exacerbated by movement or pressure on the affected side.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1.8 per 1,000 adults per year.
  • Hospital prevalence: Less common, typically seen in orthopaedic or rheumatology referrals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK rates, with higher incidences in older adults and females.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Microtrauma or overuse leads to inflammation of the bursa.
  • Compression from surrounding muscles, particularly the iliotibial band, exacerbates the inflammatory response.
  • Degenerative changes in surrounding tendons (e.g., gluteus medius and minimus tendons) may contribute to symptom development.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Pain develops over days to weeks, often after increased activity or trauma.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms persist for weeks to months if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Long-standing pain with associated muscle weakness or gait changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Lateral hip pain - Commonest symptom, due to inflammation of the bursa compressing adjacent nerve endings.
  • Pain worsened by lying on the affected side - Pressure on the inflamed bursa exacerbates discomfort.
  • Pain exacerbated by prolonged walking, climbing stairs, or rising from a seated position - Movements that involve the iliotibial band or gluteal muscles increase friction over the bursa.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over the greater trochanter - Localised inflammation causes sensitivity to palpation.
  • Reduced range of motion in the hip - Pain limits active movement, particularly abduction and extension.
  • <b+Gait abnormalities - Patients may limp or avoid putting weight on the affected side to reduce pain.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Fever or systemic symptoms - May indicate septic bursitis, a serious infection requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe, unrelenting pain - Could suggest malignancy or a fracture.
  • Neurological symptoms - Numbness or weakness might indicate nerve impingement or other neurological pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Referred pain to the knee or buttock, mimicking sciatica or lumbar radiculopathy.
  • Minimal or no tenderness at the greater trochanter in chronic cases with predominant gait issues.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Tenderness over the greater trochanter and reproduction of pain with hip abduction against resistance.
  • Ultrasound - Shows bursal fluid and may reveal tendon pathology.
  • MRI - Used in refractory cases to exclude other causes like tendon tears or neoplasms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old female presents with 3 months of worsening lateral hip pain, worsened by lying on her side and climbing stairs, with tenderness on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Hip osteoarthritis - Pain more generalised to the groin and exacerbated by weight-bearing.
  • Lumbar radiculopathy - Pain radiates below the knee with neurological findings.
  • Gluteus medius tendinopathy - Localised tenderness but less swelling compared to bursitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • First-line: NSAIDs and activity modification.
  • Physiotherapy: Focused on strengthening the hip abductors and stretching the iliotibial band.
  • Corticosteroid injections: For refractory cases or significant inflammation.
  • Surgical intervention: Rare, reserved for chronic, refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Can lead to functional limitations and reduced quality of life.
  • Muscle atrophy: Secondary to disuse or prolonged pain-related immobility.
