Sebaceous Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • A sebaceous cyst is a non-cancerous, slow-growing cyst filled with sebum, a thick, greasy substance. It forms within sebaceous glands, typically beneath the skin. These cysts are benign and often asymptomatic but may become painful or infected.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Sebaceous cysts are relatively common in primary care, presenting in approximately 0.5-1% of the population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Rarely seen in secondary care unless complicated by infection or located in sensitive areas (head/neck).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Prevalence is similar worldwide and affects individuals of all ages, more commonly seen in adults than children.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Sebaceous cysts arise from the blockage of a sebaceous gland duct, often due to trauma, damage, or underlying skin conditions. This leads to an accumulation of sebum within the gland. As the sebum collects, a sac forms beneath the skin, surrounded by a fibrous capsule. Over time, the cyst may grow, and in some cases, bacteria can enter the cyst, leading to infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Slow-growing: Sebaceous cysts typically develop over months or even years.
  • Asymptomatic phase: Most sebaceous cysts are asymptomatic initially and may remain unnoticed unless they become infected or inflamed.
  • Complicated phase: Infected cysts can become acutely painful, swollen, and red, with rapid changes occurring over days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic lump - Most cysts are painless and discovered incidentally. The accumulation of sebum causes the swelling without causing discomfort initially.
  • Discomfort or pain - Pain may arise if the cyst is in a pressure-prone area or becomes inflamed, typically due to local irritation.
  • Redness and swelling - These occur when the cyst becomes infected, leading to inflammatory changes in the surrounding tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Visible subcutaneous lump - Typically smooth, round, and movable under the skin; the cyst is often visible to the naked eye and palpable as a well-defined mass.
  • Central punctum - A small dark point on the surface of the cyst, representing the blocked duct opening.
  • Fluctuance and tenderness - Indicative of infection; the cyst becomes soft, filled with pus, and painful on palpation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth or large size - May suggest malignancy (rare), such as a sebaceous carcinoma.
  • Recurrent infections - Suggests a complicated or improperly drained cyst, increasing the risk of abscess formation.
  • Ulceration or discharge - May indicate chronic infection, rupture, or fistula formation, which can complicate the course.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Deep-seated cysts - May be mistaken for a lipoma or tumour due to their firm and less visible nature.
  • Multiple cysts - May occur in association with conditions such as Gardner’s syndrome, which can predispose to numerous skin cysts.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on history and physical examination. No imaging is typically required unless malignancy or another diagnosis is suspected.
  • Ultrasound - Used if the diagnosis is uncertain. It will show a well-defined, hypoechoic lesion with posterior enhancement, consistent with a cystic structure.
  • Histopathology - Performed after excision, confirming the presence of a cyst lined with squamous epithelium and filled with keratinous material.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old male presents with a painless, slow-growing lump on the upper back. The lump has been present for 6 months and is now slightly tender, with some surrounding redness.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Lipoma - A soft, mobile, non-tender fatty lump; lacks central punctum and does not become inflamed.
  • Epidermoid cyst - Similar in appearance but forms from hair follicle components, not sebaceous glands.
  • Abscess - Typically more painful with signs of infection (fluctuance, heat, redness) and may have systemic symptoms (fever).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Watchful waiting - Asymptomatic cysts can be left alone unless they cause cosmetic concerns or become painful.
  • Surgical excision - Definitive treatment, recommended for symptomatic, infected, or cosmetically concerning cysts.
  • Incision and drainage - Performed if the cyst is infected. However, recurrence is common if the cyst wall is not removed.
  • Antibiotics - If secondary infection is present, use antibiotics like flucloxacillin in line with UK guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Infection - The most common complication, leading to abscess formation, pain, and swelling.
  • Recurrence - Can occur if the cyst wall is not fully excised.
  • Rupture - An inflamed cyst can rupture, releasing its contents and causing localised inflammation or infection.
