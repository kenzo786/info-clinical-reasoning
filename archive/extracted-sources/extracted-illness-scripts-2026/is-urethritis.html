Urethritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Urethritis is the inflammation of the urethra, the tube that carries urine from the bladder out of the body. It commonly results from bacterial infections, particularly sexually transmitted infections (STIs) such as gonorrhoea and chlamydia, but can also arise from non-infectious causes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: 2-4% of patients presenting with urethral symptoms.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Predominantly treated in outpatient settings, with rare cases needing hospitalisation for complications.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Urethritis is prevalent globally, particularly among sexually active adults. Incidence rates vary, with higher rates reported in regions with high STI prevalence.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Infectious urethritis is most often caused by bacterial pathogens entering the urethra, such as Neisseria gonorrhoeae or Chlamydia trachomatis. The bacteria infect the mucosal lining of the urethra, causing an inflammatory immune response.
  • This inflammation leads to increased blood flow, redness, and swelling, which contribute to symptoms like pain and dysuria (painful urination).
  • In non-infectious urethritis, irritants such as chemical agents or mechanical trauma can trigger inflammation, similarly leading to urethral discomfort.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically develop within days to weeks after infection. Bacterial urethritis symptoms often appear within 2-7 days post-exposure.
  • Persistent phase: If untreated, symptoms may continue and lead to chronic urethritis or complications like epididymitis in men or pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women.
  • Resolution: With appropriate antibiotic treatment, symptoms usually resolve within days, though some cases may take up to two weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Dysuria (painful urination) - Caused by inflammation of the urethral lining due to bacterial infection, resulting in discomfort during urination.
  • Urethral discharge - Often purulent in gonococcal urethritis and clear in chlamydial urethritis, discharge results from the body’s response to infection and cellular debris from the urethral lining.
  • Itching or irritation in the urethra - Due to local inflammation and immune response.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Particularly common in chlamydial urethritis, which may present without noticeable symptoms in up to 50% of cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Urethral discharge on examination - Visible discharge at the urethral meatus, especially if gonococcal.
  • Redness and swelling at urethral meatus - Due to inflammatory response, commonly seen in severe or acute infections.
  • Tenderness of the urethra - Indicates underlying inflammation and may be present on palpation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Fever or systemic symptoms - May indicate ascending infection, such as pyelonephritis or pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Severe pelvic pain - Could signify complications like PID or prostatitis.
  • Testicular pain - Could indicate epididymitis or orchitis, requiring urgent attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Minimal or absent symptoms in chlamydial infection - Up to 50% of individuals may lack symptoms, posing a challenge for diagnosis and increasing the risk of transmission.
  • Non-STI causes such as reactive arthritis - Urethritis as part of Reiter’s syndrome, often with joint pain and conjunctivitis, may be overlooked if only urethritis is assessed.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • First-catch urine test - Commonly used to detect chlamydia and gonorrhoea using nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs). High sensitivity and specificity due to detection of bacterial DNA.
  • Urethral swab - Can be used in cases where urine samples are not possible, and to identify causative organisms via culture or PCR testing.
  • Full STI panel - Includes tests for other STIs like HIV and syphilis, as co-infections are common.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • A 26-year-old male presents with two days of dysuria and purulent urethral discharge, following recent unprotected sexual intercourse. No fever or systemic symptoms reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - More common in females; symptoms include dysuria without urethral discharge. Confirmed by urine culture showing pathogens like E. coli.
  • Prostatitis - Presents with dysuria, pelvic pain, and possibly fever; confirmed by prostate tenderness on examination.
  • Reactive arthritis - May include urethritis, conjunctivitis, and arthritis, typically without discharge; often following a GI or urogenital infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • First-line antibiotics - For gonorrhoeal infection: Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose. For chlamydial infection: Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice daily for 7 days, per UK guidelines.
  • Partner notification and testing - Crucial for preventing re-infection and further transmission.
  • Patient education - Emphasise safe sexual practices and importance of completing treatment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urethritis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - Ascending infection in females, leading to chronic pelvic pain and infertility.
  • Epididymitis - Painful inflammation of the epididymis in men, potentially leading to infertility if untreated.
  • Chronic urethritis - Persistent symptoms can occur due to inadequate treatment or resistance, leading to ongoing discomfort and risk of complications.
