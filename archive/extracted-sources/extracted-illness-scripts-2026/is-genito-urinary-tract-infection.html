Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Genito-urinary tract infection (UTI) is a bacterial infection affecting any part of the urinary system, including the urethra, bladder, ureters, and kidneys. It is characterised by symptoms such as dysuria, frequency, and urgency, and can range from uncomplicated cystitis to complicated pyelonephritis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: ~5-10% of consultations annually are for suspected UTIs.
  • Hospital prevalence: UTIs account for approximately 20-30% of healthcare-associated infections in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence: UTIs are among the most common infections, affecting 150 million people globally each year.
  • Women are disproportionately affected, with nearly 50% experiencing a UTI during their lifetime.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Pathogens, most commonly Escherichia coli, ascend from the urethra to the bladder, adhering to the urothelium via fimbriae.
  • Infection triggers an inflammatory response, leading to symptoms such as dysuria and urgency.
  • In more severe cases, pathogens ascend to the kidneys, causing pyelonephritis and potential systemic infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Acute UTI: Symptoms develop over hours to days.
  • Chronic/recurrent UTI: Persistent or recurrent infections, often linked to underlying structural or functional abnormalities.
  • Complicated UTI (e.g., pyelonephritis): Symptoms may evolve over days and include systemic involvement.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Dysuria - Painful urination due to inflammation of the urothelium.
  • Frequency - Increased urination driven by irritation and inflammation of the bladder.
  • Urgency - Strong need to urinate, caused by inflammatory changes in the bladder.
  • Suprapubic pain - Localised pain from bladder wall inflammation.
  • Haematuria - Blood in the urine due to urothelial irritation or damage.
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria - Presence of bacteria in urine without symptoms, often in elderly or pregnant individuals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Fever - Suggestive of systemic involvement, particularly in pyelonephritis.
  • Costovertebral angle tenderness - Pain over the flank area, indicative of kidney involvement.
  • Suprapubic tenderness - Common in cystitis due to bladder inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • High fever, rigors - May indicate pyelonephritis or sepsis.
  • Flank pain - Suggests upper urinary tract involvement.
  • Haematuria with clots - Could indicate malignancy or severe infection.
  • Persistent symptoms despite treatment - Raises concern for resistant pathogens or complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Elderly - Confusion, general malaise, or falls instead of classic symptoms.
  • Children - Non-specific symptoms like fever, irritability, or vomiting.
  • Pregnant women - Asymptomatic bacteriuria more common, increasing risk of pyelonephritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Positive leukocytes, nitrites, and red blood cells indicate infection.
  • Urine culture - Confirms causative organism; significant bacteriuria is >105 CFU/mL.
  • Full blood count - Elevated white blood cells suggest systemic infection.
  • Imaging (ultrasound or CT) - Indicated for recurrent or complicated UTIs to detect structural abnormalities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old female presents with dysuria, urinary frequency, and suprapubic pain for two days, with no fever or flank pain.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Urethritis - Often sexually transmitted; discharge and gradual onset distinguish it.
  • Interstitial cystitis - Chronic pelvic pain and urinary symptoms without infection.
  • Pyelonephritis - Flank pain, fever, and systemic symptoms differ from simple UTI.
  • Vaginitis - Vaginal discharge and irritation are more pronounced.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Uncomplicated UTI - Nitrofurantoin (first-line, 3-day course) or trimethoprim.
  • Complicated UTI - Longer courses (7-10 days) and broader-spectrum antibiotics such as ciprofloxacin.
  • Recurrent UTIs - Prophylactic antibiotics or behavioural measures (hydration, post-coital voiding).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Genito-Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis - Ascension of infection; may lead to renal scarring.
  • Sepsis - Systemic dissemination of bacteria; potentially life-threatening.
  • Recurrent infections - Can lead to chronic kidney disease over time.
