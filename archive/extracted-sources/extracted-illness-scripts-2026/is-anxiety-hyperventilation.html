Anxiety (Hyperventilation)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Hyperventilation is a common manifestation of anxiety, characterised by rapid or deep breathing that leads to a decrease in arterial carbon dioxide levels (hypocapnia), resulting in physiological and psychological symptoms. It occurs in response to stress or fear, often as part of panic or anxiety disorders.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of anxiety disorders in the UK GP population is around 13%, with hyperventilation syndrome affecting about 6-10% of the general population.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK is less clearly documented but is often seen in emergency departments, where anxiety-related conditions account for up to 20% of psychiatric emergency visits.
  • Worldwide, anxiety disorders have a prevalence of approximately 4% in the general population, with hyperventilation being a frequent feature of acute anxiety episodes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Anxiety leads to a heightened autonomic response, activating the sympathetic nervous system.
  • This results in rapid breathing (hyperventilation), where the rate of CO2 elimination exceeds production, causing a drop in CO2 levels (hypocapnia).
  • Hypocapnia triggers vasoconstriction in cerebral blood vessels, leading to symptoms such as dizziness and light-headedness.
  • Low CO2 levels also shift the haemoglobin-oxygen dissociation curve, reducing oxygen release to tissues and causing sensations of breathlessness, chest tightness, and paraesthesia.
  • This feedback loop exacerbates the anxiety, perpetuating the hyperventilation and associated symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset, often triggered by stress, fear, or panic, lasting from minutes to hours.
  • Chronic phase: In some individuals, recurrent hyperventilation episodes may occur as part of an ongoing anxiety disorder, persisting for months or longer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Occurs due to hyperventilation-induced hypocapnia, which can cause a sensation of air hunger.
  • Chest pain or tightness - Caused by increased respiratory effort and tension in the chest muscles.
  • Dizziness or light-headedness - Result of cerebral vasoconstriction secondary to reduced CO2 levels.
  • Paraesthesia (tingling, especially in the hands or lips) - Linked to shifts in blood pH (respiratory alkalosis) affecting nerve conduction.
  • Palpitations - Due to sympathetic nervous system activation during anxiety, causing increased heart rate.
  • Feeling of impending doom - A common psychological feature of anxiety and panic attacks.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - Direct consequence of hyperventilation.
  • Tachycardia - Reflects sympathetic nervous system activation due to anxiety.
  • Carpopedal spasm - Prolonged hypocapnia may cause neuromuscular irritability, leading to spasms in the hands and feet.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Severe chest pain - Could indicate a more serious condition such as myocardial infarction, especially if accompanied by radiation to the arm or jaw.
  • Syncope - Loss of consciousness may signify a more severe underlying cause of hyperventilation, such as pulmonary embolism or cardiac arrhythmia.
  • Hypoxia or cyanosis - These are not typical of anxiety-related hyperventilation and may indicate a serious underlying pulmonary or cardiac disorder.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Silent anxiety - Some patients may not report overt psychological distress but present with somatic symptoms like chest pain or dizziness.
  • Chronic hyperventilation - Some individuals may develop long-term symptoms like chronic fatigue, unexplained dyspnoea, and non-cardiac chest pain, which can be mistaken for other disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Expected to show respiratory alkalosis (pH >7.45) with low partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2 <4.5 kPa). This reflects the hyperventilation-induced hypocapnia.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) - Typically normal, but important to rule out cardiac causes of symptoms (e.g., arrhythmias, ischaemia).
  • Pulmonary function tests - Often normal, ruling out underlying lung disease in chronic cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with sudden onset of chest tightness, shortness of breath, and tingling in her hands during a stressful event. Symptoms are episodic and improve with controlled breathing. No significant medical history.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Panic disorder - Similar episodes of hyperventilation but often associated with recurrent panic attacks and intense fear.
  • Asthma - Typically associated with wheezing and reversible airway obstruction, confirmed by spirometry.
  • Myocardial ischaemia - Presents with chest pain, potentially radiating, often with ECG changes or elevated cardiac biomarkers.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute dyspnoea with pleuritic chest pain, hypoxia, and potentially elevated D-dimer.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Psychological therapies - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is the first-line treatment for anxiety-related hyperventilation.
  • Breathing techniques - Techniques such as diaphragmatic breathing or breathing into a paper bag can help restore CO2 levels.
  • Pharmacological treatment - Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) may be used for long-term anxiety management. Short-term use of benzodiazepines may be considered in acute settings.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anxiety (Hyperventilation)"][/sc]
  • Chronic anxiety - Recurrent episodes of hyperventilation can lead to a prolonged anxiety disorder, affecting quality of life.
  • Respiratory alkalosis - Persistent hyperventilation may result in metabolic disturbances, leading to further symptoms such as muscle cramps and spasms.
