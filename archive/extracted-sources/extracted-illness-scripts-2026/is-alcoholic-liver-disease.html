Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD) refers to liver damage caused by chronic alcohol consumption. It progresses through stages of fatty liver (steatosis), alcoholic hepatitis, and cirrhosis, with potential complications including liver failure, portal hypertension, and hepatocellular carcinoma. ALD is marked by inflammation, fibrosis, and cell necrosis due to direct and indirect toxic effects of alcohol.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, ALD accounts for approximately 60% of liver disease cases and is the most common cause of liver-related deaths. Hospitalisation rates for ALD in the UK are estimated to be around 100 per 100,000 population annually.
  • Globally, alcohol contributes to 50% of cirrhosis cases, with a higher prevalence in regions with high alcohol consumption.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic alcohol consumption leads to hepatocyte injury through oxidative stress, direct toxicity of acetaldehyde (alcohol metabolite), and disruption of lipid metabolism, resulting in fatty liver (steatosis).
  • In alcoholic hepatitis, continued injury triggers an inflammatory response with immune cell infiltration and cytokine release, causing necrosis and fibrosis.
  • Over time, fibrosis advances to cirrhosis, characterised by nodular regeneration and severe scarring, impairing liver function, blood flow, and ultimately causing portal hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Steatosis: May develop within weeks of heavy drinking but is usually reversible if alcohol intake is reduced.
  • Alcoholic Hepatitis: Typically manifests after years of heavy alcohol use and can be life-threatening if severe.
  • Cirrhosis: Develops over years to decades of alcohol abuse, representing end-stage liver disease with poor prognosis if drinking continues.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common due to reduced liver function, metabolic disturbances, and systemic inflammation.
  • Abdominal Pain - Typically in the right upper quadrant; inflammation and hepatomegaly exert pressure on the liver capsule.
  • Nausea and Anorexia - Results from impaired metabolism and liver dysfunction affecting appetite.
  • Jaundice - Caused by hyperbilirubinaemia due to impaired bile excretion and liver cell injury.
  • Ascites - Fluid accumulation in the peritoneal cavity from portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia in cirrhosis.
  • Weight Loss - Results from metabolic changes, malabsorption, and loss of appetite.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Hepatomegaly - Common early finding due to fatty infiltration or inflammation.
  • Spider Naevi - Hyper-oestrogenism secondary to liver dysfunction causing vascular changes.
  • Palmar Erythema - Increased oestrogen leads to capillary dilation.
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of skin/sclera due to elevated bilirubin levels from impaired liver function.
  • Ascites - Visible abdominal distension from fluid accumulation due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Asterixis - Due to hepatic encephalopathy, causing tremor with outstretched hands.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy - Confusion, tremors, and coma from ammonia toxicity; indicates severe liver failure.
  • Gastrointestinal Bleeding - Haematemesis or melaena due to variceal rupture in portal hypertension; life-threatening complication.
  • Severe Jaundice - Indicates significant liver dysfunction and potential acute decompensation.
  • Rapid Ascites Accumulation - Could indicate portal hypertension or spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, a severe infection risk.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Silent or Mild Presentation - Especially in early stages, patients may lack obvious symptoms until advanced liver damage has occurred.
  • Neuropsychiatric Symptoms - Depression, memory impairment due to subclinical hepatic encephalopathy without marked liver failure signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Raised AST, ALT (usually AST>ALT in ALD), elevated bilirubin, low albumin, and prolonged PT in severe cases. Reflects hepatocellular damage and impaired liver function.
  • Ultrasound: Detects hepatomegaly, steatosis, and ascites. Useful in staging and assessing liver morphology.
  • FibroScan: Assesses liver stiffness, indicating fibrosis or cirrhosis in advanced cases.
  • Complete Blood Count (CBC): May show anaemia (due to GI bleeding or malnutrition) and thrombocytopaenia (from splenic sequestration).
  • Biopsy (if needed): Shows steatosis, inflammation, and fibrosis but is often reserved for unclear cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old man with a 20-year history of heavy alcohol consumption presents with fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal distension over the past 3 months.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD): Similar presentation but occurs in non-drinkers or minimal alcohol intake; associated with metabolic syndrome.
  • Viral Hepatitis (B and C): Presents with similar symptoms but detectable viral markers; may progress without alcohol use.
  • Haemochromatosis: Iron overload leading to liver damage; presents with bronze skin pigmentation, diabetes, and family history.
  • Autoimmune Hepatitis: Associated with other autoimmune conditions, positive autoimmune markers, and younger female predominance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Abstinence: Essential for all patients to halt disease progression. Medical and psychological support for withdrawal recommended.
  • Nutrition Support: Corrects malnutrition and deficiencies, common in advanced ALD.
  • Corticosteroids: For severe alcoholic hepatitis, particularly with high Maddrey’s Discriminant Function score.
  • Management of Complications: Diuretics for ascites, lactulose for hepatic encephalopathy, and beta-blockers for variceal bleeding prophylaxis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Portal Hypertension: Common in cirrhosis, leading to ascites, splenomegaly, and varices due to increased resistance in the portal venous system.
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy: Cognitive decline and coma in severe liver failure from ammonia and other toxin accumulation.
  • Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC): Risk increases in cirrhotic patients; regular surveillance recommended in advanced cases.
  • Renal Failure: Known as hepatorenal syndrome; poor prognosis as liver function declines further.
