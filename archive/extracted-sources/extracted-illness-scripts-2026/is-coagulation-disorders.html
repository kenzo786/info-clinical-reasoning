Coagulation Disorders
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Coagulation disorders are conditions characterised by an inability of the blood to clot normally due to defects in the coagulation cascade, platelet function, or blood vessel integrity. These disorders lead to excessive bleeding or thrombosis, depending on whether the clotting mechanism is impaired or excessively activated. Common types include haemophilia, von Willebrand disease, and acquired coagulopathies from liver disease or vitamin K deficiency.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Estimated at 1 in 1,000 for inherited bleeding disorders (like von Willebrand disease), with haemophilia A affecting about 1 in 10,000 males.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Higher prevalence due to cases associated with trauma, liver disease, or anticoagulation therapy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies with haemophilia A and B collectively affecting approximately 400,000 people globally; acquired disorders are common worldwide in conditions like liver disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Coagulation involves a complex cascade of plasma proteins that act sequentially to form a blood clot at injury sites. Disorders arise from deficiencies or dysfunctions in coagulation factors (e.g., Factor VIII in haemophilia A), von Willebrand factor, or platelets, leading to either excessive bleeding or clot formation. Liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, or autoimmune responses can further disrupt this cascade, contributing to acquired coagulopathies.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Congenital coagulation disorders (e.g., haemophilia) are lifelong and present from childhood, with bleeding severity often correlated to coagulation factor deficiency levels.
  • Acquired coagulopathies (e.g., liver disease) have a progressive course linked to the underlying condition, where bleeding tendencies worsen as liver function deteriorates.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Easy bruising - Due to inadequate clot formation, minor trauma causes bruises that are larger than expected.
  • Frequent nosebleeds - Common in disorders like von Willebrand disease where mucosal surfaces are prone to bleeding due to deficient platelet adhesion.
  • Excessive bleeding from cuts - Prolonged bleeding occurs due to factor deficiencies, common in haemophilia and severe liver disease.
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding - Often seen in von Willebrand disease and platelet function disorders due to defective platelet or factor interaction with the endothelium.
  • Joint or muscle bleeding - Common in haemophilia, where factor VIII or IX deficiency leads to spontaneous bleeding into joints and muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Ecchymoses - Large, bruise-like areas indicate abnormal bleeding, especially in coagulation factor deficiencies.
  • Petechiae - Small, pinpoint haemorrhages are common in platelet disorders, where minor vessel rupture cannot be sealed.
  • Haemarthrosis - Painful, swollen joints seen in haemophilia due to recurrent bleeding episodes into the joints.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Intracranial haemorrhage - Severe, life-threatening complication of haemophilia, indicated by severe headache, seizures, or altered consciousness.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding - May signify severe coagulation disorder or liver disease, leading to melena or haematemesis.
  • Severe anaemia - Resulting from chronic bleeding, indicating persistent coagulation dysfunction and requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Isolated deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - May occur in cases of protein C or S deficiency, manifesting as clot formation rather than bleeding.
  • Haematuria without visible trauma - Can occur in mild cases of haemophilia or von Willebrand disease due to microvascular injury.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Prothrombin Time (PT) - Prolonged in vitamin K deficiency or liver disease, reflecting extrinsic pathway dysfunction.
  • Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (aPTT) - Prolonged in haemophilia, indicating intrinsic pathway impairment.
  • Platelet count - Normal in coagulation factor disorders but low in platelet-related coagulopathies.
  • Factor assays - Identifies specific factor deficiencies (e.g., Factor VIII or IX in haemophilia).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • A 12-year-old male with a history of easy bruising, prolonged bleeding after minor cuts, and joint swelling following minor trauma. PT and aPTT are prolonged, and Factor VIII assay shows deficiency, suggesting haemophilia A.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Vitamin K deficiency - Prolonged PT with normal aPTT, related to dietary or absorption issues.
  • Liver disease - Both PT and aPTT may be prolonged; signs include jaundice, hepatomegaly.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) - Prolonged PT and aPTT with low platelet count, indicating systemic clotting and bleeding.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Replacement therapy - Regular infusions of deficient clotting factor (e.g., Factor VIII or IX) in haemophilia.
  • Desmopressin (DDAVP) - Stimulates release of von Willebrand factor; used in mild von Willebrand disease.
  • Vitamin K supplementation - For deficiency-related coagulopathies; given orally or via injection.
  • Antifibrinolytic agents - Tranexamic acid or aminocaproic acid for mucosal bleeding.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Coagulation Disorders"][/sc]
  • Chronic joint disease - Due to recurrent haemarthrosis in haemophilia, leading to joint damage.
  • Neurological deficits - From intracranial bleeding, a serious complication in severe haemophilia.
  • Severe anaemia - Results from chronic bleeding, particularly in von Willebrand disease or DIC.
