Angina
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Angina is a clinical syndrome characterised by chest pain or discomfort due to [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the metabolic consequences of myocardial ischaemia?" instructions="A state of insufficient oxygen supply to the heart muscle, usually due to reduced blood flow. This supply-demand mismatch is the core cause of angina symptoms." text="myocardial ischaemia"][/sc]. It is typically triggered by exertion or emotional stress, and relieved by rest or sublingual [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is the mechanism of action of GTN in angina?" instructions="A potent vasodilator, primarily working on veins to reduce preload, and also dilating coronary arteries. Used sublingually for rapid relief of angina episodes. Key patient advice: sit down before use, usual dose is 1-2 puffs/tablets, repeat after 5 mins if needed, call 999 if pain persists 5 mins after second dose or is unusually severe. Common side effects: headache, dizziness." text="Glyceryl Trinitrate (GTN)"][/sc]. The most common underlying cause is [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How does atherosclerosis develop in coronary arteries?" instructions="Progressive build-up of fatty plaques (atheroma) within the coronary artery walls, leading to reduced lumen diameter (stenosis) and restricted blood flow. This is the primary pathological process in most cases of stable angina." text="atherosclerotic narrowing"][/sc] of the coronary arteries, but remember some patients have [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is INOCA (Ischaemia with Non-Obstructive Coronary Arteries)?" instructions="Angina symptoms can occur without obstructive epicardial disease (often termed INOCA - Ischaemia with Non-Obstructive Coronary Arteries). Key subtypes include:
    • Microvascular Angina: Dysfunction of small intramyocardial vessels impairing flow reserve.
    • Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) Angina: Transient coronary artery spasm causing ischaemia (often at rest, may cause ST elevation).
    Clinical Pearl: Management differs; CCBs/nitrates often preferred for vasospasm (avoid beta-blockers if solely vasospastic). Microvascular angina treatment is complex and evolving." text="non-obstructive (microvascular / vasospastic) angina"][/sc].

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the stages of atherosclerosis development?" instructions="The fundamental pathological process: lipid-laden plaques (atheroma) develop within coronary artery walls, leading to progressive luminal narrowing (stenosis) and restricted blood flow." text="Atherosclerosis"][/sc]: The fundamental process in obstructive CAD.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How does myocardial oxygen demand change during exercise?" instructions="Restricted blood flow through stenosed arteries causes an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand, particularly during periods of increased cardiac workload (e.g., physical exertion, emotional stress). This oxygen deficit is myocardial ischaemia." text="Myocardial Ischaemia"][/sc]: Results from the oxygen supply-demand mismatch.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What substances mediate anginal pain?" instructions="Insufficient oxygen forces anaerobic metabolism. This produces metabolic byproducts, such as lactic acid and adenosine, which are thought to stimulate myocardial pain receptors (nerve endings), causing the characteristic sensation of angina." text="Pain Generation"][/sc]: Caused by metabolic byproducts stimulating pain receptors.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How does endothelial dysfunction contribute to cardiovascular events?" instructions="Atherosclerosis is often associated with dysfunction of the endothelium (the inner artery lining), impairing its ability to dilate (vasodilate) appropriately in response to increased demand. This further worsens the supply-demand mismatch." text="Endothelial Dysfunction"][/sc]: Impaired vasodilation contributes, particularly in atherosclerosis.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How do coronary spasm and microvascular dysfunction cause angina without fixed stenosis?" instructions="Underlying mechanisms for non-obstructive angina (INOCA):
    • Coronary Artery Spasm (Vasospastic): Transient, focal constriction of an epicardial artery causing severe ischaemia.
    • Microvascular Dysfunction: Impaired dilation or excessive constriction of small intramyocardial arterioles, limiting blood flow increase during demand." text="Coronary spasm & microvascular dysfunction"][/sc]:     Mechanisms causing ischaemia even without significant fixed stenosis (Ischaemia with Non-Obstructive Coronary Arteries - INOCA).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Stable Angina: Characterised by a [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How is the severity of stable angina classified (e.g., Canadian Cardiovascular Society - CCS class)?" instructions="Symptoms reliably triggered by a similar level of exertion/stress, consistently relieved promptly by rest/GTN. Frequency, severity, and triggers remain relatively constant over weeks or months." text="predictable pattern"][/sc] of symptoms over time.
  • Unstable Angina: Defined by a [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the key criteria differentiating unstable angina from NSTEMI?" instructions="Any change from the stable pattern suggesting higher risk. Includes: angina occurring at rest or minimal exertion, new onset severe angina, or crescendo angina (increasing frequency, duration, or severity; lower trigger threshold). Indicates plaque instability and high risk of MI. Requires urgent hospital assessment." text="change from the stable pattern"][/sc]. This constitutes an Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) and requires urgent hospital assessment.
  • Progression: Without effective secondary prevention, stable angina can worsen, potentially becoming unstable, or leading to complications like myocardial infarction or heart failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Chest Pain or Discomfort: The cardinal symptom, typically a constricting, heavy, tight, or pressing sensation, usually felt retrosternally. The discomfort [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are less common radiation sites for anginal pain?" instructions="Common radiation sites include the neck, jaw, shoulders (epaulette region), or down one/both arms (often the inner aspect of the left arm)." text="may radiate"][/sc].
  • Triggers & Relief: Classically provoked by physical exertion (walking uphill/fast, carrying loads) or emotional stress, and characteristically [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Why does GTN provide rapid relief for angina?" instructions="Typically relieved within <5-10 minutes by stopping the activity (rest) or by using sublingual GTN. Pain lasting longer suggests possible ACS - a red flag." text="relieved rapidly"][/sc] by rest or GTN.
  • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="When are associated symptoms like dyspnoea more prominent in angina?" instructions="Episodes may sometimes include: dyspnoea (often signifying more severe ischaemia or LV dysfunction), nausea, sweating (diaphoresis), or fatigue." text="Associated Symptoms"][/sc]: May sometimes accompany the chest discomfort.

  • Signs of Risk Factors and Comorbidities: Examination should actively seek [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are xanthelasma and corneal arcus indicators of?" instructions="Look for evidence of cardiovascular risk factors: hypertension (measure BP accurately), signs of hyperlipidaemia (e.g., xanthelasma, corneal arcus), signs of peripheral artery disease (check peripheral pulses, listen for bruits), assess for obesity (BMI, waist circumference)." text="related signs"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Pain Suggestive of Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS): Be highly alert for [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is the immediate management for suspected ACS in primary care / pre-hospital setting?" instructions="Includes pain that is severe, prolonged (>15-20 mins), occurring at rest, poorly responsive to GTN, OR associated with significant nausea, vomiting, marked breathlessness, pallor, sweating, or haemodynamic instability (hypotension/tachycardia).   • Change in Angina Pattern: Any recent, definite [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How should a patient with suspected unstable angina be assessed urgently?" instructions="Includes angina occurring more frequently, lasting longer, triggered by less exertion ('crescendo angina'), or new onset rest angina. This indicates plaque instability and requires urgent assessment." text="deterioration in pattern"][/sc] indicates instability and warrants urgent assessment.
  • Associated Syncope or Near-Syncope: Fainting or near-fainting with chest pain is a critical red flag; understand the [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What investigations are needed if a patient presents with syncope and chest pain?" instructions="Suggests possible severe widespread myocardial ischaemia, critical coronary stenosis (e.g., Left Main Stem disease), or significant cardiac arrhythmias. Carries a high risk of sudden cardiac death." text="implications of syncope with pain"][/sc].

Consider [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Why might diabetes lead to atypical or silent myocardial ischaemia?" instructions="Symptoms substituting for typical chest pain can include: exertional dyspnoea (resolving with rest), unexplained fatigue, epigastric discomfort or indigestion-like symptoms related to exertion, or exertional nausea." text="Examples of anginal equivalents"][/sc].

Stable Angina Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Angina"][/sc]
  • Clinical Assessment: Diagnosis starts with a thorough history, often structured using the [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How do you differentiate cardiac from non-cardiac chest pain using history?" instructions="A useful mnemonic: Provocation/Palliation, Quality, Radiation, Severity/Site, Timing/Triggers/Relief.   • Resting 12-Lead ECG: An essential baseline test. Note [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What specific ECG changes indicate myocardial ischaemia during stable angina?" instructions="Often normal between episodes. May show: prior MI (pathological Q waves), Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH), Bundle Branch Block (BBB), or non-specific ST-T wave changes. During pain: transient horizontal or downsloping ST depression or T wave inversion may occur." text="potential ECG findings"][/sc], but a normal ECG does not exclude angina.
  • CT Coronary Angiography (CTCA): Generally the [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the main contraindications and limitations of CTCA?" instructions="Recommended by NICE (CG95) for most patients with new onset stable chest pain where CAD is not already known. Non-invasively visualises coronary artery anatomy to assess for stenosis." text="first-line diagnostic investigation"][/sc] in the UK for suspected stable angina.
  • Non-invasive Functional Imaging: Consider [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="How does stress echocardiography detect myocardial ischaemia?" instructions="Tests like stress echocardiography, myocardial perfusion scintigraphy (MPS), or stress cardiac MRI assess the functional significance (haemodynamic impact/ischaemia) of coronary stenosis.   • Invasive Coronary Angiography: Understand the [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the main risks associated with invasive coronary angiography?" instructions="The 'reference standard' for coronary anatomy visualisation. Typically reserved for: suspected severe/high-risk disease based on non-invasive tests, unstable or medically refractory symptoms, or as part of planning for revascularisation (PCI/CABG)." text="indications for invasive angiography"][/sc].
  • Exercise Tolerance Test (ETT): Understand the current [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What findings on an ETT indicate high cardiovascular risk?" instructions="Also known as exercise ECG. Less commonly used now for initial diagnosis in UK (lower diagnostic accuracy than imaging per NICE CG95). May still have a role in risk stratification or assessing functional capacity in patients with known CAD." text="role of ETT"][/sc] in UK practice (primarily not for diagnosis).
  • Blood Tests: Perform [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Why is it important to check renal function before starting certain cardiac medications like ACE inhibitors or considering contrast imaging?" instructions="Check: Full Blood Count (FBC - exclude anaemia as contributor), Renal function (U&Es - baseline for meds/contrast), Lipid profile (risk factor), HbA1c (diabetes screen/control), consider Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs). Treatment (for Stable Angina) [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Angina"][/sc]

Clinical goals: relieve symptoms (improve quality of life) and improve long-term prognosis (prevent MI, death) by aggressive risk-factor modification and appropriate therapy.

  • Immediate Symptom Relief:
    • Prescribe [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What advice should be given to patients about using their GTN spray/tablets?" instructions="Used 'prn' (as needed) for acute episodes (spray or sublingual tablet). Key patient education: Sit down before use, 1-2 doses 5 mins apart, call 999 if pain persists 5 mins after 2nd dose or is severe/different. Warn about headache/dizziness." text="Sublingual Glyceryl Trinitrate (GTN)"][/sc].
  • Anti-anginal Prevention Therapy (Regular treatment):
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Compare the common side effect profiles of beta-blockers and calcium channel blockers." instructions="Offer either a beta-blocker (e.g., bisoprolol - cardioselective preferred) or a calcium channel blocker (CCB) (e.g., amlodipine [dihydropyridine] or diltiazem/verapamil [rate-limiting]). Choice depends on patient factors (e.g., heart rate, comorbidities like asthma/heart failure, potential side effects)." text="First-line therapy"][/sc] aims to prevent episodes.
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Why is the combination of a beta-blocker and verapamil generally avoided?" instructions="If monotherapy insufficient, typically combine a beta-blocker + dihydropyridine CCB (e.g., bisoprolol + amlodipine). Avoid combining beta-blocker + rate-limiting CCB (verapamil/diltiazem) due to risk of excessive bradycardia/heart block." text="Second-line therapy"][/sc] often involves combining classes.
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What is the mechanism of action of nicorandil in angina?" instructions="If symptoms persist despite optimal dual therapy, consider adding a third agent. Options (often specialist advice): long-acting nitrate (e.g., isosorbide mononitrate), ivabradine (if sinus rhythm & HR >70bpm on beta-blocker), nicorandil, or ranolazine." text="Third-line therapy"][/sc] may involve additional agents.
  • Secondary Prevention (Essential for Prognosis):
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the main contraindications to aspirin therapy?" instructions="Offer low-dose aspirin (75mg OD) lifelong unless contraindicated/not tolerated. Use Clopidogrel 75mg OD as alternative if aspirin unsuitable." text="Antiplatelet Therapy"][/sc] is vital.
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What monitoring is required for patients starting high-intensity statin therapy (e.g., LFTs, CK)?" instructions="Offer high-intensity statin (e.g., Atorvastatin 80mg OD) to all patients, regardless of baseline cholesterol, as stable angina is CVD. Aim for >40% reduction in non-HDL cholesterol. Crucial for plaque stabilisation and reducing future events." text="Lipid Modification"][/sc] with statins is standard.
    • [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the guideline blood pressure targets for patients with CVD like stable angina?" instructions="Ensure optimal management of comorbidities: Treat hypertension to target, manage diabetes aiming for target HbA1c, advise on weight management. Consider adding an ACE inhibitor (or ARB), especially if patient has hypertension, diabetes, or LV dysfunction." text="Address Other Risk Factors"][/sc] comprehensively.
  • Lifestyle Modification: Provide comprehensive [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What are the core components of a cardiac rehabilitation programme in the UK?" instructions="Includes strong advice & support for: smoking cessation, adopting a healthy balanced diet (e.g., Mediterranean style), undertaking regular physical activity (tailored to functional capacity), achieving/maintaining a healthy weight, and moderating alcohol intake. Offer referral to cardiac rehabilitation." text="key lifestyle advice"][/sc].
  • Revascularisation:
    • Consider referral based on [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="What defines 'optimal medical therapy' for stable angina before considering revascularisation?" instructions="Consider for patients whose symptoms are not adequately controlled despite optimal medical therapy, OR those identified as having prognostically significant coronary artery disease on investigation (e.g., significant Left Main Stem stenosis, severe triple vessel disease)." text="indications for revascularisation"][/sc].
    • Understand the [sc name="lmm-clickable-term" search="Compare the long-term outcomes of PCI versus CABG for complex multi-vessel coronary artery disease." instructions="Main options are Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI) with stenting, or Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG) surgery.
