Motor Neurone Disease (MND)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Motor Neurone Disease (MND) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterised by the degeneration of motor neurons in the brain and spinal cord. This leads to muscle weakness, atrophy, and eventual loss of voluntary movement. Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is the most common form of MND, encompassing approximately 85-90% of cases.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK: Around 1 in 50,000 annually; lifetime risk of 1 in 400.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 5-7 per 100,000.
  • Common age of onset: Usually between ages 50-70; slightly more common in men.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • MND involves the progressive degeneration of both upper motor neurons (UMN) and lower motor neurons (LMN).
  • UMN damage: Loss of cortical neurons disrupts voluntary movement control, causing spasticity and hyperreflexia.
  • LMN damage: Loss of spinal cord and brainstem neurons leads to muscle atrophy and weakness due to denervation.
  • Mechanisms: Complex pathogenesis involves glutamate excitotoxicity, oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and neuroinflammation, resulting in cellular death of motor neurons.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle muscle weakness, often asymmetric, with muscle twitching or cramps.
  • Progressive phase: Increased weakness, difficulty with fine motor skills, spreading to other regions over months to years.
  • Advanced phase: Loss of mobility, speech, and swallowing abilities, progressing to respiratory failure. Median survival is 3-5 years post-diagnosis, though some individuals may survive longer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Muscle Weakness - Progressive weakness, often starting in limbs, linked to motor neuron loss causing muscle denervation.
  • Muscle Cramps - Involuntary muscle spasms due to LMN hyperexcitability and denervation hypersensitivity.
  • Muscle Wasting - Atrophy occurs as muscles lose neuronal input and are not stimulated to maintain mass.
  • Fasciculations - Visible muscle twitches due to spontaneous discharges from dying motor neurons.
  • Dysarthria - Slurred or slow speech caused by weakness in oropharyngeal muscles.
  • Dyspnoea - Difficulty breathing due to progressive respiratory muscle weakness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Muscle Atrophy - Seen in affected areas, typically beginning asymmetrically in limbs.
  • Hyperreflexia - Exaggerated reflexes from UMN loss and reduced inhibition.
  • Spasticity - Increased muscle tone due to UMN dysfunction, leading to resistance against passive movement.
  • Positive Babinski sign - UMN dysfunction causes an abnormal plantar reflex, with toe extension rather than flexion.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing, resulting from progressive muscle weakness in bulbar muscles.
  • Respiratory compromise - Evident in later stages, with decreased breath sounds and reduced oxygenation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive weakness - Indicates aggressive MND progression; increased risk for early respiratory failure.
  • Dysphagia and aspiration - Raises risk for aspiration pneumonia, a frequent cause of mortality in MND.
  • Respiratory insufficiency - Progressive dyspnoea and hypoventilation necessitate monitoring; risk of respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Predominantly UMN or LMN symptoms - Some patients present with primary lateral sclerosis (UMN-predominant) or progressive muscular atrophy (LMN-predominant).
  • Cognitive impairment - Up to 15% develop frontotemporal dementia, presenting with behavioural or language changes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Electromyography (EMG): Detects denervation and reinnervation patterns, confirming LMN degeneration.
  • Nerve conduction studies: Rule out peripheral neuropathy; typically normal in MND.
  • MRI brain and spine: Excludes structural lesions or other neurological conditions.
  • Serum creatine kinase (CK): Mildly elevated in MND, indicating muscle breakdown.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • 58-year-old male with progressive left-sided limb weakness, muscle wasting, and fasciculations over 1 year, now with mild dysarthria.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Myasthenia Gravis: Fluctuating weakness, typically improves with rest; no UMN signs.
  • Multifocal Motor Neuropathy: Similar weakness, often asymmetrical, but with preserved reflexes and response to immunotherapy.
  • Spinal Muscular Atrophy: Primarily LMN signs, no UMN involvement.
  • Multiple Sclerosis: Often relapsing-remitting; involves sensory and motor pathways with CNS lesions on MRI.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Riluzole: Only approved medication, prolongs survival by ~3 months by reducing glutamate toxicity.
  • Supportive therapy: Includes non-invasive ventilation, gastrostomy for nutrition, and physical therapy for mobility and spasticity management.
  • Palliative care: Focus on quality of life, particularly in advanced stages.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Motor Neurone Disease (MND)"][/sc]
  • Aspiration Pneumonia: Frequent due to dysphagia, and a leading cause of death.
  • Respiratory failure: Progression leads to hypoventilation and eventual respiratory arrest.
  • Malnutrition: Progressive muscle wasting and dysphagia complicate nutritional status.
  • Prognosis: Median survival 3-5 years; some cases may survive 10+ years.
