HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • HIV is a retrovirus that attacks the immune system, specifically the CD4+ T-cells, leading to progressive immune suppression. If untreated, it can progress to Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS), characterised by opportunistic infections and certain malignancies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 100,000 people live with HIV in the UK, with an incidence of about 4,500 new cases annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Most patients with HIV in hospital settings are seen due to complications like opportunistic infections. Hospital admissions remain significant, particularly for late presenters.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 38 million people globally are living with HIV, with the highest burden in Sub-Saharan Africa.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Step 1: HIV enters the body, primarily via sexual contact, blood transfusions, or from mother to child (vertical transmission).
  • Step 2: The virus binds to the CD4 receptor on T-cells and enters the cell by fusion with its membrane.
  • Step 3: HIV uses reverse transcriptase to convert its RNA into DNA, integrating into the host’s genome.
  • Step 4: Viral replication ensues, destroying CD4+ T-cells and weakening the immune system, leading to opportunistic infections and malignancies.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (2-4 weeks): Flu-like symptoms occur as the virus rapidly replicates.
  • Chronic phase (years-decades): Asymptomatic phase with slow viral replication; patients may feel generally well.
  • AIDS (terminal stage): Marked immunosuppression with opportunistic infections and malignancies like Kaposi’s sarcoma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Fever - Caused by acute viral infection during the initial seroconversion phase as the immune system reacts to HIV.
  • Fatigue - Persistent fatigue arises due to immune system activation and later immunosuppression.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Generalised lymphadenopathy occurs as the lymphatic system reacts to viral dissemination.
  • Weight loss - Often seen in late-stage HIV/AIDS, due to chronic infection and wasting (HIV-associated wasting syndrome).
  • Opportunistic infections - Symptoms depend on the infection (e.g., cough from Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia, oral thrush from Candida).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Oral thrush - Indicative of immune suppression, caused by fungal overgrowth (Candida species).
  • Kaposi’s sarcoma - Purple, vascular skin lesions often seen in AIDS, due to HHV-8 infection.
  • Chronic diarrhoea - Results from opportunistic infections such as Cryptosporidium or CMV colitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Severe weight loss - May indicate advanced AIDS or HIV-associated wasting syndrome, which signifies poor prognosis.
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) - Atypical pneumonia with progressive dyspnoea and hypoxia; suggests profound immunosuppression.
  • CNS involvement - Neurological symptoms (e.g., confusion, seizures) may indicate opportunistic infections like toxoplasmosis or progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Isolated thrombocytopenia - May present without typical HIV symptoms, as HIV can affect platelet production in early disease.
  • Severe seborrheic dermatitis - Often out of proportion to what is expected, reflecting immune dysfunction early in the disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • HIV serology (HIV antibody/antigen test) - Positive in most patients within 2-6 weeks of infection; detects both HIV antibodies and p24 antigen.
  • CD4+ count - Low in patients with advanced disease; normal range is 500-1,200 cells/µL. Counts below 200 cells/µL indicate high risk for opportunistic infections.
  • Viral load (HIV RNA PCR) - Quantifies the amount of HIV RNA in the blood; higher levels correlate with rapid disease progression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old man presents with a 3-month history of weight loss, fatigue, and persistent lymphadenopathy. HIV serology is positive, and his CD4 count is 180 cells/µL, indicating advanced disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Mononucleosis (EBV or CMV infection) - Can cause fever, lymphadenopathy, and fatigue, but usually resolves spontaneously without progression to immunosuppression.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - May cause similar constitutional symptoms but typically includes a positive ANA and autoimmune manifestations.
  • Lymphoma - May present with lymphadenopathy and weight loss, but diagnosis requires biopsy, and no viral association like HIV.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Antiretroviral therapy (ART): Initiated immediately after diagnosis, with a combination of agents (e.g., Tenofovir, Emtricitabine, Dolutegravir) to suppress viral replication and prevent immune decline.
  • Opportunistic infection prophylaxis: For CD4 counts <200 cells/µL, co-trimoxazole (Bactrim) is given for Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) prophylaxis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)"][/sc]
  • Opportunistic infections - As CD4 counts decline, infections such as PCP, CMV retinitis, and Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) become more frequent.
  • HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND) - Cognitive decline ranging from mild impairment to dementia, often seen in advanced HIV/AIDS.
