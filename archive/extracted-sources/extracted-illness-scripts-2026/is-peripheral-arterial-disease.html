Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD) is a chronic condition characterised by atherosclerosis of the peripheral arteries, leading to reduced blood flow to the limbs, most commonly the legs. It typically presents with intermittent claudication (pain on exertion) and, in severe cases, critical limb ischaemia (rest pain or tissue loss).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, PAD affects approximately 3-10% of the general population, rising to 15-20% in those over 70. Around 5% of UK hospital admissions involve PAD, particularly in vascular surgery or cardiovascular departments.
  • Globally, it is estimated that over 200 million people suffer from PAD, with higher prevalence in low- and middle-income countries due to risk factors like smoking, hypertension, and diabetes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerosis, the underlying cause of PAD, begins with endothelial injury due to factors like smoking, hypertension, or hyperlipidaemia. This leads to lipid deposition, inflammation, and plaque formation in the arterial walls.
  • As the plaques grow, they narrow the lumen, reducing blood flow. In the limbs, this leads to ischaemia, particularly during exertion when oxygen demand increases.
  • Over time, complete occlusion or plaque rupture can cause acute limb ischaemia, while chronic progressive narrowing leads to intermittent claudication or critical limb ischaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Asymptomatic or mild claudication; progression may be slow.
  • Moderate stage: Claudication becomes more frequent with increased exertion.
  • Late stage: Critical limb ischaemia, characterised by rest pain, ulcers, and risk of gangrene.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Intermittent Claudication - Cramping pain in the calves, thighs, or buttocks during exertion due to reduced blood flow. As exertion increases, oxygen demand exceeds supply, leading to ischaemic pain.
  • Rest Pain - In severe PAD, pain at rest due to critically reduced blood flow, especially at night when legs are elevated, reducing arterial perfusion.
  • Ulceration and Non-healing Wounds - Poor perfusion results in tissue hypoxia, impairing healing and increasing the risk of skin breakdown and ulcers.
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients, especially in early stages, have no symptoms due to adequate compensatory blood flow at rest.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Diminished Peripheral Pulses - Reduced or absent pulses (e.g., dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) due to obstructed arterial flow.
  • Cool, Pale Skin - Ischaemia causes reduced oxygen and nutrient delivery, leading to pallor and cold extremities.
  • Dependent Rubor - When the leg is lowered, it becomes red due to dilation of superficial vessels in response to chronic ischaemia.
  • Muscle Atrophy - Chronic lack of blood supply leads to muscle wasting over time.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Rest Pain - Indicates critical limb ischaemia, a serious form of PAD requiring urgent intervention to prevent limb loss.
  • Ulceration or Gangrene - Sign of tissue necrosis and very poor blood supply. May lead to amputation if not treated rapidly.
  • Sudden Onset Pain - Suggests acute limb ischaemia due to embolic or thrombotic occlusion. Requires emergency treatment to prevent permanent damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Non-specific Leg Fatigue - Some patients may report vague fatigue without the classic claudication pain, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Atypical Location of Pain - Pain may occur in the thighs or buttocks rather than calves, especially with aortoiliac disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) - ABPI < 0.9 confirms PAD. It indicates the ratio of blood pressure in the ankle to the arm, with reduced ratios suggesting arterial stenosis.
  • Duplex Ultrasound - Identifies the location and severity of arterial stenosis or occlusion using Doppler to assess blood flow.
  • CT or MR Angiography - Detailed imaging to evaluate arterial anatomy, especially before surgical interventions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male smoker with a history of hypertension presents with 6-month history of worsening calf pain on exertion, relieved by rest. Physical exam shows diminished dorsalis pedis pulse and a cool, pale left foot.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Venous Insufficiency - Presents with leg swelling and varicosities, typically no pain on exertion. Venous ulcers are usually around the medial malleolus, unlike arterial ulcers on pressure points.
  • Spinal Stenosis - Causes neurogenic claudication, with pain that improves when bending forward. No change in peripheral pulses.
  • Diabetic Neuropathy - Causes burning pain or numbness, not worsened by exertion, and often accompanied by sensory deficits.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle Modifications - Smoking cessation and exercise to improve collateral circulation.
  • Antiplatelet Therapy - Aspirin or clopidogrel to reduce cardiovascular risk.
  • Statins - Reduce cholesterol levels to slow atherosclerosis progression.
  • Revascularisation - Angioplasty or bypass surgery for severe, symptomatic cases or critical limb ischaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Critical Limb Ischaemia - Chronic ischaemia leads to tissue necrosis, ulcers, and gangrene, necessitating amputation in up to 20% of cases.
  • Acute Limb Ischaemia - Sudden blockage can cause irreversible muscle and nerve damage within hours, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Cardiovascular Events - PAD is a strong predictor of myocardial infarction and stroke, with a 3-5 times increased risk of cardiovascular mortality.
