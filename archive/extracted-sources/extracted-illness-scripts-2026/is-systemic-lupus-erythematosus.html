Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) is a chronic, autoimmune, multisystemic inflammatory disease characterised by the production of pathogenic autoantibodies that target nuclear components. This results in widespread immune complex deposition, inflammation, and tissue damage, affecting multiple organs, including skin, joints, kidneys, and the cardiovascular system.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence: Approximately 0.03% of the general population in the UK, with higher prevalence in hospital settings due to severity and complications.
  • Global prevalence: Varies but estimated at 0.02-0.1% worldwide, with a higher incidence among women of childbearing age (9:1 female to male ratio).
  • Higher prevalence in populations of African, Asian, and Hispanic descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Autoantibody production - Genetic, environmental, and hormonal factors trigger the formation of autoantibodies targeting nuclear antigens (e.g., anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm).
  • Immune complex deposition - Autoantibodies form complexes with antigens and deposit in tissues, causing local inflammation and damage.
  • Inflammation and tissue injury - Complement activation and recruitment of inflammatory cells lead to chronic inflammation and progressive organ damage, notably in the skin, joints, kidneys, and CNS.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual onset with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue, malaise, and low-grade fever.
  • Chronic phase: Episodic relapses and remissions with variable intensity and periods of symptom resolution. Disease progression and complications may arise over years.
  • Advanced phase: Involvement of major organs such as kidneys and heart, leading to chronic disability and increased morbidity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Often profound and multifactorial, due to inflammation, anaemia, and chronic illness effects.
  • Arthralgia - Joint pain without deformity, caused by immune-mediated inflammation in joint structures.
  • Photosensitivity - Rash upon sun exposure due to skin cell damage from immune complexes and antibodies.
  • Malar Rash - Characteristic “butterfly” rash over cheeks and nose, linked to immune-mediated dermal inflammation.
  • Alopecia - Hair loss, typically diffuse, due to autoantibody targeting of hair follicles.
  • Raynaud's phenomenon - Vasospasm in response to cold or stress, leading to changes in skin colour in extremities.
  • Chest pain (pleuritic or pericardial) - Due to inflammation of pleura or pericardium from immune complex deposition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Malar rash - Erythematous, butterfly-shaped rash over cheeks and nose due to immune-mediated skin inflammation.
  • Oral/nasal ulcers - Painless, recurrent ulcers from immune complex deposition in mucosal tissues.
  • Synovitis - Tender, swollen joints without erosion, indicating non-deforming inflammatory arthritis.
  • Alopecia - Diffuse hair thinning, linked to antibody-mediated follicular damage.
  • Pericardial rub - Audible rub over the chest, associated with pericardial inflammation.
  • Proteinuria - Due to kidney involvement (lupus nephritis) from glomerular immune complex deposition.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Nephritis (proteinuria, haematuria) - Suggests lupus nephritis, a severe complication that may progress to renal failure.
  • Cerebritis (seizures, psychosis) - Indicates CNS involvement, a serious manifestation linked to high morbidity.
  • Pulmonary haemorrhage - Rare but life-threatening complication due to lung tissue inflammation.
  • Thrombocytopenia and haemolytic anaemia - Haematologic abnormalities suggesting increased disease activity and risk of bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Isolated nephritis or CNS involvement - May present without typical SLE signs, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Late-onset SLE - Symptoms may be milder or present primarily with joint pain and limited systemic features.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • ANA (Antinuclear Antibody) Test - Positive in >95% of SLE cases; suggests autoimmunity.
  • Anti-dsDNA antibodies - Specific for SLE; correlates with disease activity and kidney involvement.
  • Complement levels (C3, C4) - Typically low due to complement consumption from immune complex deposition.
  • Full blood count - May show anaemia, leucopenia, or thrombocytopenia due to immune-mediated destruction.
  • Urinalysis - Presence of protein and red cell casts indicative of lupus nephritis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • A 27-year-old woman presents with chronic fatigue, malar rash, intermittent joint pain, and mild proteinuria. Symptoms have progressed gradually over months with episodic flares.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Lacks typical rash and systemic manifestations; often with joint erosions on imaging.
  • Vasculitis - May have systemic symptoms but often presents with more severe vasculitic signs (e.g., palpable purpura).
  • Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD) - Features overlap but often has antibodies to U1 RNP rather than anti-dsDNA.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • First-line treatment: Hydroxychloroquine for all patients unless contraindicated, as per UK guidelines.
  • Corticosteroids - For active flares or organ involvement, with dose tapering once control is achieved.
  • Immunosuppressive therapy (e.g., azathioprine, mycophenolate) - For moderate to severe disease, especially with organ involvement.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)"][/sc]
  • Renal failure - Due to chronic lupus nephritis; accounts for increased morbidity.
  • Atherosclerosis - Accelerated by chronic inflammation, leading to increased cardiovascular risk.
  • Infections - Immunosuppressive treatment and disease itself increase susceptibility to infections.
