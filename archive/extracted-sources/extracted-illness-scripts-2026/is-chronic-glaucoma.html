Chronic Glaucoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic glaucoma, also known as primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG), is a progressive optic neuropathy characterised by damage to the optic nerve, often associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP). It leads to irreversible loss of peripheral vision and can eventually cause complete blindness if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 2% in those aged over 40.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for a significant proportion of ophthalmology outpatient visits.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 3.5% of individuals aged 40–80 years, with higher prevalence in African and Caribbean populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic glaucoma results from impaired aqueous humour drainage through the trabecular meshwork.
  • Elevated IOP leads to mechanical and ischemic damage to retinal ganglion cells and their axons.
  • This progressive damage leads to characteristic optic disc cupping and peripheral vision loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Often asymptomatic, with subtle peripheral vision loss.
  • Progressive Phase: Gradual, irreversible narrowing of the visual field.
  • Late Phase: Severe vision loss, potentially leading to complete blindness if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Peripheral vision loss - Often unnoticed in early stages due to slow progression and central vision preservation.
  • Blurred vision - May occur in advanced stages due to optic nerve damage.
  • Difficulty seeing in dim light - Related to retinal ganglion cell damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Increased cup-to-disc ratio - Due to optic nerve head damage, observed on fundoscopy.
  • Elevated intraocular pressure - Measured using tonometry, though not always present.
  • Visual field defects - Arcuate scotomas or nasal steps on perimetry testing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - May indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma or retinal detachment.
  • Severe eye pain and redness - Suggests secondary glaucoma or acute angle closure.
  • Halos around lights - May occur with elevated IOP and corneal oedema.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Normal-tension glaucoma: Presents with optic nerve damage and visual field loss despite normal IOP levels.
  • Rapid progression in younger patients: Unusual but can occur, requiring consideration of secondary causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Tonometry - Measures IOP; normal range is 10–21 mmHg, elevated in many cases of chronic glaucoma.
  • Fundoscopy - Reveals optic disc cupping and thinning of the neuroretinal rim.
  • Visual field testing - Identifies characteristic field defects like arcuate scotomas.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman with a history of mild hypertension presents with gradually worsening peripheral vision over several years, confirmed on routine visual field testing. Examination reveals an increased cup-to-disc ratio bilaterally.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - Presents with sudden pain and vision loss, unlike the insidious progression of chronic glaucoma.
  • Optic neuritis - Typically associated with pain and central vision loss, rather than peripheral vision loss.
  • Retinal detachment - Often accompanied by flashes, floaters, and shadowing of vision.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • First-line: Prostaglandin analogues - E.g., latanoprost to reduce IOP by enhancing aqueous outflow.
  • Second-line: Beta-blockers - E.g., timolol to reduce aqueous humour production.
  • Other options: Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, laser trabeculoplasty, or trabeculectomy for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Blindness - Resulting from untreated progressive optic nerve damage.
  • Quality of life impairment - Due to loss of independence from visual impairment.
