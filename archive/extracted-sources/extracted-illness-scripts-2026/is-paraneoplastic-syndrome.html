Paraneoplastic Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes are a group of rare disorders caused by an immune or hormonal response to a neoplasm (cancer) but are not due to the direct invasion of the tumour. These syndromes can affect various systems, including the neurological, endocrine, and dermatological systems, and may precede, coincide with, or follow the diagnosis of cancer.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is low, as paraneoplastic syndromes are uncommon compared to the general cancer burden.
  • UK hospital prevalence is roughly 2-7% of cancer patients, but this can vary depending on the cancer type.
  • Worldwide prevalence is estimated to be 5-15% among patients with malignancies, particularly lung, ovarian, breast, and haematological cancers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes arise due to two main mechanisms:
    1. **Autoimmune response**: Tumour antigens mimic normal cellular proteins, triggering an autoimmune reaction that targets normal tissues, particularly in the nervous system (e.g., Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome).
    2. **Hormonal secretion**: Some tumours secrete bioactive substances such as hormones or cytokines, leading to syndromes like hypercalcaemia of malignancy or Cushing's syndrome (e.g., small cell lung cancer producing ACTH).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes can emerge:
    • **Before** a cancer diagnosis (e.g., dermatomyositis)
    • **During** active cancer treatment
    • **After** cancer remission or progression
  • The course of symptoms can be gradual or acute, depending on the type of cancer and the syndrome.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Weakness and fatigue - Common in many paraneoplastic syndromes due to autoimmune attack on muscles (e.g., myasthenia gravis) or metabolic disturbances (e.g., hypercalcaemia).
  • Neurological symptoms - Seizures, ataxia, or sensory loss are seen in paraneoplastic encephalomyelitis due to autoimmunity targeting the central nervous system.
  • Endocrine symptoms - Weight gain, hyperpigmentation, and hypertension occur in Cushing’s syndrome due to ectopic ACTH production.
  • Dermatological symptoms - Erythematous rash in dermatomyositis, an autoimmune response affecting the skin and muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Proximal muscle weakness - Seen in myasthenia gravis or Lambert-Eaton syndrome due to autoantibodies against neuromuscular junctions.
  • Ataxia and dysarthria - Suggestive of paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration, caused by immune attack on cerebellar neurons.
  • Skin changes - Heliotrope rash or Gottron’s papules in dermatomyositis, indicating an autoimmune process.
  • Hyperreflexia - May be noted in some neurological paraneoplastic syndromes due to altered spinal cord function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute neurological deterioration - Rapid onset of neurological symptoms, such as ataxia or seizures, can indicate a severe paraneoplastic syndrome affecting the brain or spinal cord.
  • Severe hypercalcaemia - Can signify malignancy-related paraneoplastic hypercalcaemia, leading to confusion, coma, or cardiac arrhythmias.
  • Unexplained severe fatigue - Can indicate muscle or neurological involvement, especially when rapidly progressive.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Subtle endocrine disturbances - Mild Cushing’s syndrome with only subtle changes in weight or skin can delay diagnosis.
  • Isolated sensory neuropathy - Atypical presentation of paraneoplastic neurological syndrome with isolated symptoms may lead to misdiagnosis as peripheral neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Antibody testing - Autoantibodies against neuronal or muscular receptors (e.g., anti-Hu, anti-Yo) help confirm paraneoplastic neurological syndromes.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Shows reduced muscle response in conditions like Lambert-Eaton syndrome or myasthenia gravis.
  • Hormonal assays - ACTH or cortisol levels can confirm ectopic ACTH production in paraneoplastic Cushing’s syndrome.
  • Imaging (CT/MRI) - Essential to identify the underlying malignancy, especially in cases with no previously known cancer.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old male with a history of smoking presents with progressive weakness, ataxia, and confusion. He is found to have a lung mass on chest imaging, and serology confirms paraneoplastic antibodies.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune encephalitis - Similar neurological features but without an underlying malignancy. Antibody profiles help distinguish.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Chronic course with demyelinating lesions, lacks association with cancer.
  • Endocrine disorders - Primary endocrine causes (e.g., Cushing’s disease) will not have an associated malignancy or paraneoplastic antibodies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Treat underlying cancer - Removal or treatment of the malignancy often improves paraneoplastic symptoms.
  • Immunotherapy - IV immunoglobulins or plasmapheresis may reduce autoimmune symptoms.
  • Symptomatic treatment - Corticosteroids or hormone antagonists are used to manage symptoms like hypercalcaemia or Cushing’s syndrome.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Paraneoplastic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological damage - Severe autoimmune attack can lead to irreversible neurological deficits despite cancer treatment.
  • Metabolic derangements - Prolonged hypercalcaemia or hormonal disturbances can lead to organ dysfunction (e.g., renal failure).
  • Prognosis - Varies widely depending on the underlying cancer, but early treatment improves the likelihood of symptom resolution.
