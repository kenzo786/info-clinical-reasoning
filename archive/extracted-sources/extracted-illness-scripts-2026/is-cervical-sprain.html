Cervical Sprain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • A cervical sprain refers to an overstretching or tearing of the ligaments and soft tissues in the cervical spine (neck region). It typically results from sudden or excessive force applied to the neck, often causing pain, stiffness, and limited range of motion.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Around 2% of GP visits for musculoskeletal complaints involve neck sprains.
  • Hospital prevalence: Cervical sprains contribute to 5-10% of presentations in accident and emergency departments following minor trauma.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies widely; common in motor vehicle accidents and sports-related injuries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Sudden forceful motion (e.g., whiplash) causes hyperextension or hyperflexion of the cervical spine.
  • Ligaments (such as the anterior longitudinal ligament) and muscles become overstretched or torn, triggering inflammation.
  • Inflammation leads to local pain, swelling, and reduced mobility due to muscle guarding and spasms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: 0-72 hours post-injury - Pain, swelling, and stiffness are prominent.
  • Subacute phase: 3 days to 6 weeks - Inflammatory symptoms gradually subside; stiffness may persist.
  • Chronic phase: >6 weeks - Chronic pain or stiffness can occur in unresolved cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Neck pain - Caused by ligament strain and inflammation.
  • Stiffness - Due to muscle guarding to protect the injured area.
  • Reduced range of motion - A result of pain, swelling, and muscle spasm.
  • Referred shoulder/upper back pain - Involvement of shared nerve pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over the cervical spine - Indicative of local inflammation.
  • Decreased neck mobility - Associated with pain and protective muscle spasm.
  • Paraspinal muscle spasm - Felt as tight, tender areas on palpation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Severe pain or deformity - May indicate cervical fracture or dislocation.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., numbness, weakness) - Suggest potential spinal cord or nerve root involvement.
  • Persistent or worsening symptoms despite treatment - Raises concern for ligamentous instability or more severe injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Minimal or delayed pain - Seen in some cases of whiplash; may delay diagnosis.
  • Headache - Due to referred pain from cervical structures.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Plain X-rays - To exclude fractures or dislocations; typically normal in sprain.
  • MRI - For severe or persistent symptoms to assess ligament or soft tissue injury.
  • Clinical examination - Mainstay of diagnosis, focusing on pain, range of motion, and tenderness.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with acute neck pain and stiffness following a rear-end motor vehicle collision 12 hours ago. Examination reveals tenderness over the cervical spine and reduced range of motion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Cervical fracture - Severe pain, deformity, and abnormal X-ray findings.
  • Cervical disc herniation - Radicular pain, neurological deficits, confirmed on MRI.
  • Muscle strain - Localised pain without significant range of motion limitation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Initial management - Analgesia with paracetamol and NSAIDs, rest with gradual mobilisation.
  • Physical therapy - To restore function and reduce stiffness.
  • Reassurance - Most cases resolve without long-term consequences.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Sprain"][/sc]
  • Chronic neck pain - Due to unresolved inflammation or poor rehabilitation.
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) - Following motor vehicle accidents.
