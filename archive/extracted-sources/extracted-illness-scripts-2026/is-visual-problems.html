Visual Problems
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Visual problems encompass a range of abnormalities in vision, including blurring, double vision, peripheral vision loss, and other visual disturbances. These issues can arise from ocular, neurological, or systemic conditions, and may be temporary or progressive.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1 in 30 consultations involve vision-related complaints.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common presentation in ophthalmology and neurology clinics, especially in patients aged >50 years.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Visual impairment affects over 2.2 billion people globally, with refractive errors, cataracts, and diabetic retinopathy being major causes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Visual problems can result from abnormalities in light focusing (e.g., refractive errors), ocular structures (e.g., cataract), retinal or optic nerve dysfunction (e.g., diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma), or cortical processing (e.g., stroke-related visual field deficits).
  • Key pathways include disruption in ocular transparency, blood flow to retinal and neural tissues, and nerve signal transmission.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Often seen in vascular or traumatic causes (e.g., retinal detachment, optic neuritis).
  • Subacute: Common in progressive disorders (e.g., diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration).
  • Chronic and gradual: Frequently due to refractive errors, cataracts, or chronic glaucoma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Blurry vision - Often due to refractive errors or cataracts. Caused by incorrect light focusing on the retina or lens opacity.
  • Loss of visual field - Can be peripheral or central. Common in glaucoma (peripheral loss) or macular degeneration (central loss), due to optic nerve or macular damage respectively.
  • Photopsia (flashes of light) - Frequently occurs with retinal detachment due to traction on the retina.
  • Diplopia (double vision) - Often arises from cranial nerve palsies or ocular misalignment, affecting coordinated eye movement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Reduced visual acuity - Often seen in refractive errors or retinal disease due to impaired light focus or retinal function.
  • Visual field defects - May present as scotomas (blind spots) in specific patterns, indicating damage to retinal or visual pathways (e.g., bitemporal hemianopia in pituitary adenoma).
  • Abnormal pupillary reflexes - Afferent pupillary defect can signify optic neuropathy or severe retinal disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - Indicates possible retinal detachment, optic neuritis, or vascular occlusion, which can lead to permanent damage without prompt intervention.
  • Severe eye pain - May indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma or optic neuritis, both of which require urgent treatment to prevent vision loss.
  • Flashes and floaters with vision loss - Suggests retinal detachment, requiring urgent ophthalmological evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic until advanced (e.g., glaucoma) - Vision changes may go unnoticed due to gradual progression and lack of acute symptoms.
  • Subtle visual field changes - May present with vague symptoms such as “difficulty reading” without overt vision loss, especially in early macular degeneration.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Visual acuity testing - Assesses clarity of vision; reduced acuity suggests refractive errors or retinal conditions.
  • Fundoscopy - Reveals retinal changes like haemorrhages or macular degeneration signs.
  • Visual field testing - Identifies specific field defects, indicating possible glaucoma or neurological lesions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old female with progressive, painless peripheral vision loss in both eyes over 5 years, noted difficulty with night driving and occasional double vision.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Glaucoma - Typically peripheral vision loss, painless and progressive, unlike acute angle closure which is painful.
  • Macular degeneration - Central vision loss, different from peripheral loss seen in glaucoma.
  • Optic neuritis - Often painful, acute vision loss, especially in young adults; associated with MS.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Refractive errors: Corrective lenses or surgery.
  • Glaucoma: Topical agents (e.g., prostaglandin analogues) or surgery for advanced cases.
  • Macular degeneration: Intravitreal injections of anti-VEGF for wet type; lifestyle adjustments and monitoring for dry type.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss - Seen in advanced glaucoma or untreated retinal detachment.
  • Secondary complications - May include reduced quality of life, increased fall risk, and dependency.
