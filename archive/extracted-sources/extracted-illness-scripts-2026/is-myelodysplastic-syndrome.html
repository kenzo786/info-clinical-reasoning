Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) refers to a group of clonal haematopoietic disorders characterised by ineffective haematopoiesis, leading to peripheral blood cytopenias and an increased risk of progression to acute myeloid leukaemia (AML). The bone marrow produces dysplastic and dysfunctional blood cells. The condition is primarily seen in the elderly and varies widely in severity and clinical presentation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, MDS has a prevalence of approximately 5 per 100,000 in the general population, but rises to 20-50 per 100,000 in individuals aged over 70.
  • Worldwide, MDS prevalence is estimated at 3-4 per 100,000, though the incidence rises with age.
  • MDS accounts for around 7% of haematological malignancies diagnosed in UK hospitals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • MDS is caused by acquired mutations in haematopoietic stem cells, leading to ineffective haematopoiesis. These mutations result in abnormal differentiation and maturation of blood cells in the bone marrow, producing dysplastic cells. Dysplastic cells either die prematurely (intramedullary apoptosis) or survive but are dysfunctional, causing cytopenias. The risk of progression to AML arises from the accumulation of additional mutations that drive uncontrolled proliferation.
  • The abnormal clones replace normal haematopoietic cells, which accounts for the cytopenias observed in peripheral blood.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • MDS usually has an indolent, slowly progressive course, though some patients may remain stable for years. Over time, the disease may evolve to acute myeloid leukaemia (AML) in around 20-30% of cases. The disease is typically diagnosed in the later stages due to non-specific symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Resulting from anaemia due to ineffective erythropoiesis and low haemoglobin levels.
  • Recurrent infections - Due to neutropenia, which impairs the immune system's ability to fight infections.
  • Easy bruising or bleeding - Due to thrombocytopenia, leading to impaired clot formation.
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients, particularly in early stages, may present asymptomatically and are diagnosed incidentally on routine blood tests.
  • Shortness of breath - Related to anaemia and reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Due to anaemia and reduced red blood cell count.
  • Petechiae or purpura - These are signs of thrombocytopenia and manifest as small red or purple spots on the skin.
  • Fever or signs of infection - Suggestive of neutropenia, particularly when infections are recurrent or severe.
  • Splenomegaly - In some cases, extramedullary haematopoiesis can cause splenic enlargement, although this is less common in MDS than other myeloproliferative disorders.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Severe neutropenia - Increases the risk of life-threatening infections.
  • Rapidly worsening cytopenias - May indicate transformation to acute myeloid leukaemia (AML).
  • Unexplained weight loss or night sweats - Can signify disease progression or transformation to AML.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic with normal blood counts - Some patients may present with only mild blood abnormalities, detected incidentally on routine screening.
  • Unexplained infections without significant cytopenias - Occasionally, infections may occur even in patients without marked neutropenia due to qualitative defects in neutrophils.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Shows cytopenias, particularly anaemia (normocytic or macrocytic), neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia.
  • Peripheral Blood Smear - Demonstrates dysplastic cells, such as hypogranular neutrophils or macro-ovalocytes.
  • Bone Marrow Biopsy - Reveals hypercellularity with dysplastic changes in myeloid, erythroid, and megakaryocytic lineages.
  • Cytogenetic Analysis - Detects chromosomal abnormalities (e.g. del(5q), monosomy 7) which are critical for diagnosis and prognosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • An 80-year-old male presents with progressive fatigue, easy bruising, and recurrent respiratory infections over 6 months. Blood tests show anaemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia, with bone marrow biopsy revealing dysplastic changes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Aplastic Anaemia - Pancytopenia with hypocellular bone marrow, but without the dysplastic changes seen in MDS.
  • Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML) - Presence of >20% blasts in the bone marrow and rapid progression, distinguishing it from MDS.
  • Megaloblastic Anaemia - Can cause macrocytic anaemia, but is due to vitamin B12/folate deficiency rather than bone marrow dysfunction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Supportive Care - Blood transfusions for anaemia and platelet transfusions for thrombocytopenia.
  • Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs) - Used to reduce transfusion dependence in patients with anaemia.
  • Hypomethylating Agents (e.g. Azacitidine) - Used in higher-risk MDS to delay progression to AML.
  • Allogeneic Stem Cell Transplantation - The only curative option, though typically reserved for younger, high-risk patients due to associated morbidity and mortality.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML) - Progression to AML occurs in 20-30% of cases.
  • Severe Infections - Resulting from profound neutropenia and immune dysfunction.
  • Bleeding - Due to thrombocytopenia, patients may experience life-threatening haemorrhages.
