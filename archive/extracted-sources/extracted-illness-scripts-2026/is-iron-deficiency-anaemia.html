Iron Deficiency Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia (IDA) is a condition characterised by a decrease in haemoglobin concentration due to insufficient iron availability. Iron is a crucial component of haemoglobin, and a lack of iron impairs red blood cell production, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity. IDA is the most common form of anaemia globally.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, IDA affects about 2-5% of men and post-menopausal women, and approximately 8-18% of menstruating women.
  • In UK hospitals, IDA is common among elderly patients, especially those with chronic diseases. Prevalence rates in the general hospital population are around 15-20%.
  • Globally, IDA affects around 1.6 billion people, making it one of the most prevalent nutritional deficiencies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron is essential for the production of haemoglobin, which binds oxygen for transport in the blood. When iron stores are depleted, erythropoiesis (red blood cell production) is impaired.
  • IDA develops in stages:
    • Stage 1: Depletion of iron stores (serum ferritin falls).
    • Stage 2: Decreased serum iron and transferrin saturation (fewer iron-binding proteins available).
    • Stage 3: Reduced haemoglobin production leads to microcytic, hypochromic anaemia.
  • The reduced oxygen-carrying capacity results in tissue hypoxia, manifesting as fatigue and weakness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Asymptomatic, with gradual depletion of iron stores.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms begin to appear as haemoglobin levels drop.
  • Late Phase: Severe symptoms related to anaemia, such as shortness of breath, dizziness, and palpitations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue and weakness - The decreased oxygen-carrying capacity leads to tissue hypoxia, causing generalised weakness.
  • Dyspnoea on exertion - Reduced haemoglobin means less oxygen delivery during physical activity, causing shortness of breath.
  • Palpitations - The heart compensates for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity by increasing its rate and force of contraction.
  • Headache - Reduced cerebral oxygenation may trigger headaches, often worse with activity.
  • Pica - Craving for non-nutritive substances (e.g., ice) may be linked to changes in dopaminergic pathways caused by iron deficiency.
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases are detected incidentally through blood tests before symptoms manifest, especially in the early stages.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor (skin and conjunctivae) - Reduced haemoglobin levels cause decreased perfusion and oxygenation, visible as pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails) - Chronic iron deficiency alters nail growth, leading to characteristic spooning of the nails.
  • Glossitis - Atrophy of the papillae of the tongue due to insufficient oxygen delivery can result in a smooth, sore tongue.
  • Tachycardia - Compensatory increase in heart rate to improve oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Angular cheilitis - Cracks at the corners of the mouth may result from epithelial changes due to iron deficiency.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe pallor - Indicates severe anaemia that may require urgent intervention.
  • Melena (black, tarry stools) - Suggests gastrointestinal bleeding, a common cause of iron deficiency in older adults, particularly due to malignancy or peptic ulcers.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Raises concern for underlying malignancy causing chronic blood loss and iron deficiency.
  • Dysphagia - Could be a sign of Plummer-Vinson syndrome, where oesophageal webs form in severe iron deficiency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure exacerbation - In elderly patients, IDA may worsen heart failure due to reduced oxygenation and increased cardiac workload.
  • Restless leg syndrome - This neurological symptom has been linked to iron deficiency, though the exact pathophysiology remains unclear.
  • Chronic fatigue without anaemia - Iron deficiency without overt anaemia may still present with fatigue, especially in pre-menopausal women.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Low haemoglobin (<130 g/L for men, <120 g/L for women), microcytosis (MCV <80 fL), and hypochromia (MCH <27 pg).
  • Serum Ferritin - Ferritin <30 µg/L indicates depleted iron stores; however, ferritin can be falsely elevated in inflammation.
  • Serum Iron and Transferrin Saturation - Low serum iron and transferrin saturation <20% suggest iron deficiency.
  • Total Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC) - Elevated in iron deficiency, reflecting increased production of transferrin in an attempt to transport more iron.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 42-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, palpitations, and dyspnoea over 3 months. She reports menorrhagia for several years. Blood tests reveal microcytic anaemia with low ferritin.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia - Microcytic anaemia with normal or elevated ferritin, unlike IDA, where ferritin is low.
  • Chronic Disease Anaemia - Normocytic or microcytic anaemia with normal or raised ferritin due to sequestration of iron in macrophages.
  • Sideroblastic Anaemia - Microcytic anaemia with elevated serum iron and normal ferritin, often associated with mitochondrial dysfunction.
  • Lead Poisoning - Microcytic anaemia with basophilic stippling on blood smear and elevated blood lead levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Oral iron supplements (e.g., ferrous sulphate 200 mg two or three times daily) for 3-6 months to replenish iron stores.
  • Intravenous iron (e.g., ferric carboxymaltose) if oral iron is poorly tolerated or ineffective.
  • Treat the underlying cause of iron deficiency (e.g., management of gastrointestinal bleeding, heavy menstrual bleeding).
  • Encourage dietary modifications, including iron-rich foods (red meat, green leafy vegetables).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Iron Deficiency Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Chronic anaemia increases cardiac workload, leading to left ventricular hypertrophy and eventual heart failure.
  • Impaired cognitive development in children - Iron deficiency in young children can lead to long-term developmental delays.
  • Pregnancy complications - Increased risk of preterm birth, low birth weight, and postpartum depression.
