Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT) is a type of arrhythmia characterised by intermittent episodes of rapid heart rate that originate above the ventricles. Episodes start and stop suddenly and are typically regular, with heart rates between 150-250 beats per minute. This is often caused by re-entry circuits within the atria or the atrioventricular node.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, the incidence of PSVT is about 1.5 cases per 1000 people annually.
  • It accounts for 50,000 to 100,000 hospital admissions annually in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence is estimated at 2.25 per 1000 individuals, with higher rates in females and older adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • PSVT is usually caused by re-entry mechanisms within the atrioventricular (AV) node or accessory pathways between the atria and ventricles.
  • In AV nodal re-entry tachycardia (AVNRT), an electrical impulse re-enters the AV node, creating a self-perpetuating loop, leading to rapid atrial and ventricular activation.
  • Accessory pathways (as seen in AVRT) allow impulses to bypass the AV node, leading to faster-than-normal conduction.
  • Triggers such as emotional stress, caffeine, or alcohol can exacerbate PSVT by increasing sympathetic tone, thus favouring re-entry.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • PSVT episodes can last from a few seconds to several hours and can occur sporadically or more frequently. Without treatment, these episodes may recur.
  • Onset is typically sudden, and termination of episodes is also abrupt.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Sudden awareness of rapid, pounding heartbeats. This occurs due to rapid atrial depolarisation causing increased ventricular contraction frequency.
  • Dizziness - Caused by transient reduced cardiac output during tachycardic episodes, reducing cerebral perfusion.
  • Shortness of breath - Related to poor ventricular filling time during rapid heart rates, leading to decreased stroke volume and oxygen delivery.
  • Chest discomfort - Typically due to increased myocardial oxygen demand during tachycardia or anxiety related to the rapid heart rate.
  • Fatigue - Caused by inefficient cardiac output and the physical strain of maintaining a high heart rate for prolonged periods.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Rapid heart rate (150-250 bpm) - Primary sign due to the tachycardia originating from the re-entry circuit in the AV node or atria.
  • Hypotension - Due to reduced cardiac output as a result of poor ventricular filling time.
  • Pallor or diaphoresis - These signs of sympathetic nervous system activation can occur during episodes due to reduced perfusion and increased adrenergic drive.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Suggests severe haemodynamic compromise due to prolonged tachycardia. Can be a sign of dangerous arrhythmias or heart failure.
  • Chest pain suggestive of ischaemia - May indicate myocardial ischaemia secondary to prolonged high heart rates.
  • Severe hypotension - Indicates poor cardiac output and can be a precursor to cardiogenic shock if prolonged.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may experience asymptomatic PSVT, only detected on routine ECG or during unrelated examinations.
  • Older patients may present with general fatigue or mild dizziness without typical palpitations, due to less sensitivity to rapid heart rates.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • ECG - Narrow complex tachycardia (QRS < 120 ms) is the hallmark finding. P waves may be hidden or occur just after the QRS complex, indicating AV nodal re-entry tachycardia (AVNRT).
  • Holter monitoring - Used for patients with intermittent symptoms. Can capture episodes that may not be present during an in-office ECG.
  • Electrophysiological study (EPS) - Can identify the specific re-entry pathway or accessory pathway involved in the arrhythmia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with sudden-onset palpitations lasting 20 minutes, associated with dizziness and mild chest discomfort. Symptoms started while she was at rest and resolved spontaneously.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Atrial Fibrillation - Irregularly irregular heart rate on ECG, compared to the regular rhythm in PSVT.
  • Atrial Flutter - “Sawtooth” pattern of P waves on ECG with a regular ventricular response, unlike the narrow complex tachycardia in PSVT.
  • Ventricular Tachycardia - Wide complex tachycardia (QRS > 120 ms), distinguishing it from the narrow complex of PSVT.
  • Sinus Tachycardia - Gradual onset and offset, typically related to a clear physiological trigger, compared to the abrupt onset/offset of PSVT.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Vagal manoeuvres - Initial treatment, such as the Valsalva manoeuvre, increases parasympathetic tone to slow AV nodal conduction.
  • Adenosine - Rapid IV push to terminate re-entry tachycardia by transiently blocking AV nodal conduction.
  • Beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers - For longer-term rate control and prevention of recurrence.
  • Catheter ablation - Considered in patients with frequent, symptomatic episodes, aiming to disrupt the re-entry circuit.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Persistent PSVT can lead to impaired ventricular function due to chronically increased heart rates.
  • Thromboembolism - Prolonged episodes of PSVT can increase the risk of clot formation, especially in patients with other risk factors for atrial fibrillation.
  • Cardiogenic shock - Severe, prolonged episodes may compromise cardiac output, leading to hypotension and shock.
