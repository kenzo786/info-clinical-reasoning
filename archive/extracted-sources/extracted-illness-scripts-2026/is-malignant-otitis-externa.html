Malignant Otitis Externa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Malignant otitis externa (MOE) is a severe, life-threatening infection of the external auditory canal, which extends to the temporal bone (osteomyelitis) and surrounding tissues. It predominantly affects immunocompromised patients, especially those with diabetes mellitus. The condition is most commonly caused by *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, leading to necrotising infection and, in severe cases, cranial nerve involvement or meningitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • MOE is rare but more common in the elderly, diabetics, and immunocompromised individuals. It occurs more frequently in hot, humid climates, but in the UK, the incidence is around 0.5-1 per 100,000. Hospital admissions related to MOE account for a small proportion of ENT infections, primarily in tertiary care centres.
  • Globally, MOE is more prevalent in countries with high rates of diabetes and in regions with higher humidity, such as the Middle East and Southeast Asia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Malignant otitis externa begins as an infection of the external auditory canal, often secondary to minor trauma, which allows bacteria like *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* to invade. The infection spreads to adjacent soft tissues, cartilage, and eventually the temporal bone (osteomyelitis). As the condition progresses, it can involve the cranial nerves, particularly the facial nerve (VII), leading to palsy. Chronic infection results in necrosis of surrounding tissues and may spread to the meninges, leading to meningitis or brain abscesses if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (weeks 1-4): Initial symptoms of severe otalgia and otorrhoea. The infection begins locally and may progress to involve deeper structures.
  • Subacute phase (weeks 4-12): Persistent infection with cranial nerve involvement (often CN VII). Erosion of the temporal bone may begin.
  • Chronic phase (after 12 weeks): Progression to osteomyelitis, further cranial nerve palsies, and potential intracranial complications such as abscess formation or meningitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Severe, unrelenting otalgia - Often the most prominent symptom, the pain is out of proportion to findings on otoscopy, due to infection extending into deeper tissues and bone.
  • Otorrhoea - Persistent, purulent discharge from the ear due to the infected tissue and drainage from the external auditory canal.
  • Hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss occurs as the infection affects the external auditory canal, and can worsen if bone involvement progresses.
  • Tinnitus - May occur due to inflammation and irritation of the external auditory canal and deeper structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Granulation tissue at the bony-cartilaginous junction of the ear canal - A hallmark of the condition, signifying deeper tissue involvement.
  • Facial nerve palsy (CN VII) - Indicates more advanced disease with cranial nerve involvement. This is due to inflammation and infection spreading to the facial nerve as it passes through the temporal bone.
  • Swollen, erythematous external auditory canal - Reflects the localised infection and inflammation of the external ear canal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Suggests that the infection has spread beyond the external ear and into the skull base, which may lead to severe neurological deficits.
  • Severe headaches or altered mental status - Could indicate intracranial complications such as meningitis or brain abscess.
  • Persistent fever despite antibiotics - Suggests ongoing osteomyelitis or deeper tissue involvement, requiring more aggressive treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Diabetics with minimal symptoms - Immunocompromised individuals, particularly those with poorly controlled diabetes, may have a blunted inflammatory response and present with only mild ear pain or discharge initially.
  • Absence of ear pain in elderly or immunocompromised patients - These populations may have atypical presentations with primarily neurological symptoms like facial weakness or difficulty swallowing due to cranial nerve involvement.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • CT scan of the temporal bone - Shows bony erosion, confirming osteomyelitis. A hallmark of advanced disease due to spread to the temporal bone.
  • MRI scan with contrast - Useful for identifying soft tissue involvement and assessing cranial nerve and intracranial extension.
  • Ear swab culture - Typically reveals *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, the most common pathogen, which helps guide antibiotic therapy.
  • ESR/CRP - These inflammatory markers are usually elevated, indicating ongoing infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with poorly controlled diabetes presents with severe, unremitting right ear pain and persistent otorrhoea for the past 6 weeks. Examination reveals granulation tissue at the bony-cartilaginous junction and facial nerve palsy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media - Typically involves a perforated tympanic membrane and middle ear involvement, whereas MOE does not.
  • Cholesteatoma - Presents with otorrhoea and hearing loss but does not typically cause severe pain or cranial nerve involvement like MOE.
  • Temporal bone tumour - Can mimic MOE with facial nerve involvement, but imaging typically reveals a mass rather than infection-related bony erosion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • IV antibiotics - Anti-pseudomonal agents such as ceftazidime or piperacillin-tazobactam are recommended initially. According to UK guidelines, treatment should be based on culture results and continued for 6-8 weeks.
  • Hyperbaric oxygen therapy - May be considered in severe cases, as it can enhance tissue oxygenation and aid in the eradication of infection.
  • Surgical debridement - For cases unresponsive to antibiotics or with extensive necrosis, debridement of infected tissues may be necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis of the temporal bone - The infection can spread to the bone, requiring long-term antibiotic therapy and leading to significant morbidity.
  • Cranial nerve palsy - Facial nerve palsy is the most common, but other cranial nerves may also be affected, leading to dysphagia, hearing loss, and balance disturbances.
  • Intracranial extension - Meningitis, brain abscess, and venous sinus thrombosis are severe complications that may arise if the infection progresses unchecked.
