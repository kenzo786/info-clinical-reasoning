Macrocytic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Macrocytic anaemia is a type of anaemia characterised by an elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV) of red blood cells, typically greater than 100 fL. It occurs when red blood cells are larger than normal, often due to impaired DNA synthesis in the bone marrow, which leads to defective erythropoiesis. Common causes include vitamin B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, and alcohol abuse.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, macrocytic anaemia is seen in approximately 1-2% of the general population. Among hospitalised patients, prevalence may rise to 4-5%. Globally, rates vary widely, especially in regions with high prevalence of nutritional deficiencies or chronic alcoholism.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Macrocytic anaemia results from defective DNA synthesis, which slows down nuclear maturation in the bone marrow. Cells remain large and fail to divide normally (megaloblastic anaemia). The primary cause is often a deficiency in vitamin B12 or folate, which are essential for DNA production. Non-megaloblastic macrocytic anaemia may result from alcohol abuse, liver disease, or hypothyroidism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Macrocytic anaemia typically develops over weeks to months as stores of vitamin B12 or folate deplete. Symptoms may be subtle at first but can progressively worsen. In chronic alcoholism or liver disease, macrocytosis may be present for years without overt symptoms of anaemia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - This results from reduced oxygen delivery to tissues due to low haemoglobin levels.
  • Shortness of breath - Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity leads to dyspnoea on exertion.
  • Pallor - A reflection of reduced haemoglobin and red blood cell mass, leading to decreased skin and mucosal perfusion.
  • Glossitis - Deficiencies in B12 or folate lead to inflammation of the tongue.
  • Neurological symptoms (in B12 deficiency) - B12 deficiency affects the myelination of nerves, causing peripheral neuropathy, paraesthesia, or ataxia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Decreased red blood cells and haemoglobin manifest as pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Glossitis - Smooth, red, swollen tongue due to mucosal atrophy from vitamin B12 or folate deficiency.
  • Peripheral neuropathy (B12 deficiency) - Loss of sensation, reflexes, or proprioception due to demyelination of peripheral nerves.
  • Ataxia - Loss of coordination, typically seen in advanced B12 deficiency due to spinal cord involvement (subacute combined degeneration).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe neurological deficits (e.g. paralysis) - Indicates potential irreversible nerve damage from prolonged B12 deficiency.
  • Marked jaundice - Suggests haemolysis or severe liver disease, possibly leading to worsening macrocytosis.
  • Heart failure - Severe anaemia can precipitate high-output heart failure due to compensatory mechanisms being overwhelmed.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Some patients, particularly elderly individuals, may present primarily with cognitive decline or psychiatric symptoms rather than overt signs of anaemia or neurological deficits. B12 deficiency can mimic dementia or depression in these cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Macrocytic indices with MCV >100 fL. Low haemoglobin indicates anaemia.
  • Peripheral blood smear - Macrocytosis and hypersegmented neutrophils (in megaloblastic anaemia).
  • Serum B12 and folate levels - Reduced in B12 or folate deficiency, respectively.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Abnormal in alcohol-induced macrocytic anaemia or liver disease.
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - May show hypothyroidism, a cause of non-megaloblastic macrocytosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with fatigue, shortness of breath, and progressive numbness in his feet over 6 months. Blood tests reveal macrocytic anaemia with low vitamin B12 levels.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Usually presents with microcytosis (low MCV), whereas macrocytic anaemia shows elevated MCV.
  • Thalassaemia - Microcytic anaemia with target cells on blood film, unlike the macrocytosis seen in macrocytic anaemia.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - Typically normocytic or microcytic anaemia rather than macrocytic.
  • Liver disease - Can also cause macrocytosis but may present with elevated liver enzymes and a history of alcohol use.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 replacement - Intramuscular hydroxocobalamin (1 mg) is given 3 times per week for 2 weeks, then maintenance every 2-3 months for life.
  • Folate supplementation - 5 mg of oral folic acid daily for 4 months (after ruling out B12 deficiency).
  • Treat underlying causes - For alcohol-related macrocytosis, cessation of alcohol is crucial. In hypothyroidism, thyroid hormone replacement is required.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Neurological damage - Prolonged B12 deficiency can lead to irreversible peripheral neuropathy and subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.
  • Heart failure - Severe anaemia can cause high-output cardiac failure, especially in the elderly.
  • Gastrointestinal malignancy - Pernicious anaemia is associated with an increased risk of gastric cancer due to chronic atrophic gastritis.
