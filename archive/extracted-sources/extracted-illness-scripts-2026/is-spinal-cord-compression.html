Spinal Cord Compression
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Spinal cord compression refers to the mechanical pressure placed on the spinal cord by an external mass, such as a tumour, bone fragment, or herniated disc. This can result in neurological deficits due to impaired blood supply, demyelination, and nerve cell damage. It is a medical emergency due to the risk of permanent paralysis or other neurological impairment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Malignancy, particularly metastatic cancer, is a leading risk factor, along with spinal trauma, vertebral fractures (due to osteoporosis or trauma), spinal infections (such as epidural abscess), and degenerative spinal conditions like herniated discs and spinal stenosis. Congenital spinal abnormalities and inflammatory conditions such as ankylosing spondylitis also increase the risk.
  • Prevention: Early detection and treatment of primary cancers can reduce the risk of metastatic spinal cord compression. Adequate management of osteoporosis with bisphosphonates and vitamin D supplementation may prevent vertebral fractures. Infection control measures, including timely treatment of spinal infections, reduce the likelihood of infective causes. Regular spinal health assessments, proper posture, and avoiding excessive spinal strain can help prevent degenerative spinal conditions leading to compression.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Spinal cord compression occurs when the spinal cord is compressed by external forces such as a tumour, displaced vertebral bone (fracture), or a herniated disc.
  • Compression impairs blood flow, leading to ischaemia and demyelination, which interrupts nerve transmission.
  • The extent of damage depends on the duration and severity of compression, with irreversible nerve damage occurring if compression persists.
  • Inflammation and oedema further exacerbate neural injury by increasing pressure within the spinal canal.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Spinal cord compression may present acutely (e.g., trauma) or develop over weeks to months (e.g., metastatic cancer). Symptoms can evolve rapidly once nerve tissue is damaged.
  • Early stages: Pain is usually the first symptom, often localised to the spine.
  • Later stages: Neurological deficits progress as motor, sensory, and autonomic pathways are affected, leading to weakness, numbness, and potentially bladder/bowel dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Back pain - Often the earliest symptom, typically severe and localised to the site of compression. Caused by irritation of the periosteum and surrounding structures.
  • Radicular pain - Radiating pain following the nerve root affected by compression, occurring due to irritation of spinal nerve roots.
  • Motor weakness - Progressive loss of strength in muscles below the level of compression, due to disrupted motor pathways (corticospinal tracts).
  • Sensory deficits - Numbness or paraesthesia, typically in a dermatomal pattern below the level of compression, caused by damage to the sensory pathways (spinothalamic and dorsal columns).
  • Autonomic dysfunction - Bladder and bowel incontinence or retention, due to involvement of the autonomic pathways controlling sphincter function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Lower limb weakness - Bilateral weakness in the muscles innervated below the level of compression, due to damage to the corticospinal tracts.
  • Hyperreflexia - Exaggerated reflexes below the level of compression as a result of loss of inhibitory signals from the brain.
  • Spasticity - Increased muscle tone and spasticity due to upper motor neuron lesions in the spinal cord.
  • Sensory loss - Decreased or absent sensation below the level of compression, particularly affecting light touch, vibration, and proprioception if dorsal columns are involved.
  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction - Urinary retention or incontinence due to disruption of autonomic control.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of weakness - Indicates acute cord compression, potentially from trauma or rapidly growing tumour, requiring immediate intervention to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Saddle anaesthesia - Numbness in the perineal region suggests cauda equina syndrome, an urgent neurosurgical emergency.
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction - Retention or incontinence suggests severe compression affecting autonomic nerves, indicating advanced neural compromise.
  • Progressive motor weakness - Worsening weakness over days to weeks points to ongoing cord damage and increasing risk of permanent paralysis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset of non-specific back pain - Especially in elderly patients or those with malignancy, it may be mistaken for degenerative disease. This delays diagnosis of spinal cord compression.
  • Asymmetrical symptoms - In some cases, symptoms may begin unilaterally and progress, leading to diagnostic confusion with other conditions like stroke.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • MRI of the spine - The gold standard for diagnosing spinal cord compression, showing the site and cause of compression (e.g., tumour, disc herniation). MRI reveals soft tissue structures, bone lesions, and cord oedema.
  • CT myelogram - Used if MRI is contraindicated, showing compression by visualising contrast flow around the spinal cord.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count and ESR/CRP may be elevated in cases of infectious or inflammatory causes (e.g., epidural abscess or metastatic malignancy).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old man with a known history of prostate cancer presents with worsening mid-back pain, bilateral leg weakness, and urinary retention over the past week. Examination reveals spasticity in both lower limbs, hyperreflexia, and decreased sensation below T10.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome - Primarily involves lumbar nerves, presenting with saddle anaesthesia, asymmetrical weakness, and bladder dysfunction. Differs from cord compression, which affects more proximal regions and typically presents symmetrically.
  • Multiple Sclerosis - Causes demyelination in the CNS, leading to neurological deficits that may mimic spinal cord compression. However, the relapsing-remitting pattern of MS and lack of a compressive lesion differentiate it.
  • Spinal abscess - An infectious cause of compression, often associated with fever, raised inflammatory markers, and systemic signs of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Emergency surgical decompression - The first-line treatment for spinal cord compression, especially if there is progressive neurological deficit or a rapidly worsening condition. Laminectomy or spinal fusion is typically performed.
  • Dexamethasone - High-dose steroids are administered to reduce inflammation and oedema around the spinal cord.
  • Radiotherapy - For malignant spinal cord compression (MSCC), radiotherapy is used to shrink the tumour and relieve compression.
  • Supportive care - Includes physiotherapy, pain management, and bladder/bowel care to manage residual deficits and complications.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Permanent paralysis - A result of irreversible damage to motor pathways if compression is not relieved promptly.
  • Chronic pain - Neuropathic pain may persist even after decompression, due to lasting nerve injury.
  • Autonomic dysfunction - Persistent bladder or bowel dysfunction can result from damage to autonomic nerve pathways.
