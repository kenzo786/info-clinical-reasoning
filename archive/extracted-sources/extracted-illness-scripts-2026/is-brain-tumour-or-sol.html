Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • A brain tumour or space-occupying lesion (SOL) refers to an abnormal growth within the cranial cavity, which may be benign or malignant. These masses can include primary brain tumours, metastatic lesions, or non-neoplastic conditions like abscesses or vascular malformations. SOLs can cause damage by direct compression of brain structures, raised intracranial pressure (ICP), or obstruction of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Brain tumours are relatively rare in general practice, with primary brain tumours accounting for about 11 per 100,000 person-years.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 10,000 new cases of brain tumours are diagnosed annually in the UK, with metastases being more common than primary tumours.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Incidence rates vary, with primary brain tumours affecting approximately 4-5 per 100,000 people worldwide, with higher rates of metastatic brain tumours due to cancer spread.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Brain tumours can originate from various types of cells within the brain (e.g., astrocytes, oligodendrocytes), or from metastatic cancer spread. These abnormal growths disrupt normal brain function through direct invasion, compression of surrounding structures, and increasing intracranial pressure (ICP). Tumours can block CSF pathways, leading to hydrocephalus. Over time, increased ICP and herniation syndromes can develop, which are life-threatening.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Brain tumours may present over weeks to months with slowly progressive symptoms, but malignant tumours may cause more rapid deterioration. The time course is influenced by tumour type, location, and growth rate. Sudden neurological decline can occur with haemorrhage into a tumour or acute hydrocephalus.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Headache - Often a dull, persistent headache, worse in the morning or with changes in posture, due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Commonly related to increased ICP affecting the vomiting centre in the medulla.
  • Seizures - Tumours, particularly in the cortical regions, can trigger abnormal electrical discharges, leading to focal or generalised seizures.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Symptoms such as weakness, sensory loss, or visual disturbances depend on the tumour's location in the brain.
  • Cognitive or personality changes - Frontal lobe tumours may cause changes in behaviour, mood, or cognition due to disruption of higher cognitive functions.
  • Balance and coordination issues - Tumours in the cerebellum or brainstem can affect gait and fine motor control.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Papilloedema - Swelling of the optic disc seen on fundoscopy, indicating raised ICP.
  • Focal neurological signs - Depending on tumour location, there may be hemiparesis, aphasia, visual field defects, or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Altered consciousness - Advanced cases with raised ICP or brainstem involvement can lead to a reduced level of consciousness.
  • Gait ataxia - Tumours in the cerebellum can lead to uncoordinated movements and balance issues.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe headache - May indicate tumour haemorrhage or hydrocephalus.
  • Rapidly worsening neurological deficits - Suggests aggressive tumour growth or mass effect leading to herniation.
  • Seizures in an adult without prior history - Often indicates a new lesion in the brain, such as a tumour.
  • Progressive visual loss - Suggests involvement of the optic pathways or raised ICP.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Subtle cognitive changes - Slow-growing tumours may initially present with vague symptoms like memory loss or personality changes, which may be misdiagnosed as depression or dementia.
  • Chronic headache - Some patients with benign tumours present with a longstanding, non-specific headache without focal neurological signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • MRI of the brain - Preferred imaging modality, showing detailed tumour characteristics such as size, location, and relation to brain structures. T1-weighted images with gadolinium contrast help differentiate tumour types.
  • CT scan - Useful for quick assessment, especially in emergencies. It can reveal large masses or haemorrhage but is less detailed than MRI.
  • CSF analysis (lumbar puncture) - In cases of suspected malignancy or infection. Elevated protein and pleocytosis may suggest neoplastic or infectious causes. Contraindicated in raised ICP due to the risk of herniation.
  • Biopsy - Definitive diagnosis requires histological examination of the tumour to determine the type and grade.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a 3-month history of persistent headaches, progressive right-sided weakness, and recent onset of seizures. Imaging revealed a left parietal mass, suggestive of a glioblastoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Typically has a more sudden onset compared to the gradual progression of brain tumours.
  • Subdural haematoma - May present with similar symptoms, especially in the elderly, but often has a history of head trauma.
  • Brain abscess - More common in patients with a history of infection or immunosuppression, often accompanied by fever and elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Dementia - Gradual cognitive decline may overlap with brain tumours but lacks focal neurological signs or raised ICP.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Surgery - If resectable, surgical removal of the tumour is the primary treatment for many brain tumours.
  • Radiotherapy - Often used for malignant or inoperable tumours, or after surgery to reduce recurrence.
  • Chemotherapy - Temozolomide is commonly used for gliomas in conjunction with radiotherapy.
  • Dexamethasone - To reduce cerebral oedema and control symptoms like headache and nausea caused by raised ICP.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Seizures - Occur in up to 30% of brain tumour patients, particularly those with cortical involvement.
  • Hydrocephalus - Obstruction of CSF flow can lead to ventricular enlargement and increased ICP.
  • Brain herniation - Life-threatening complication caused by raised ICP leading to compression of vital brain structures.
  • Neurological deficits - Permanent focal deficits may remain depending on the tumour location and degree of resection.
