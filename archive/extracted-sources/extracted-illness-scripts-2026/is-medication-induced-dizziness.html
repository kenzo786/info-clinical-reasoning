Medication-Induced Dizziness
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced dizziness refers to the sensation of light-headedness, vertigo, or disequilibrium caused by the side effects of certain medications. This condition can affect the vestibular system, central nervous system (CNS), or cardiovascular regulation and is a reversible consequence of drug administration, dose adjustments, or drug interactions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Approximately 10-15% of dizziness cases are medication-induced.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Dizziness is a common presenting symptom, with 5-10% of hospitalised patients affected by medication-induced causes.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Difficult to quantify, but medications contributing to dizziness are widely used globally, especially among the elderly population, where polypharmacy is prevalent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Several pathways can lead to medication-induced dizziness depending on the class of drug involved:
    • **Antihypertensives**: Can cause hypotension, reducing cerebral perfusion, leading to dizziness or faintness.
    • **CNS depressants (e.g., benzodiazepines, antipsychotics)**: Can impair the brain's balance centres, resulting in vertigo or imbalance.
    • **Ototoxic drugs (e.g., aminoglycosides, loop diuretics)**: Can directly damage the vestibular apparatus, causing vertigo.
    • **Anticholinergic drugs**: Can interfere with normal autonomic regulation, leading to orthostatic hypotension and dizziness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • The onset of medication-induced dizziness typically correlates with:
    • **Immediate onset**: With medications that directly affect blood pressure or CNS function (e.g., nitrates, sedatives).
    • **Gradual onset**: With medications that accumulate in the system (e.g., ototoxic drugs like aminoglycosides).
    • **Resolution**: Symptoms often resolve upon cessation of the offending drug or dose adjustment, although permanent damage (e.g., ototoxicity) may occur in rare cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Dizziness - A non-specific term describing light-headedness, vertigo, or disequilibrium. Pathophysiology depends on the drug’s effect on blood pressure, vestibular function, or CNS balance centres.
  • Vertigo - A sensation of spinning, usually caused by drugs that affect the vestibular apparatus, such as ototoxic medications.
  • Light-headedness - Often due to hypotension caused by antihypertensive or vasodilatory medications, leading to reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Nausea - Common in vertigo-inducing drugs due to vestibular disturbance, which affects the autonomic system.
  • Imbalance or disequilibrium - Common with CNS depressants or vestibulotoxic drugs, leading to impaired coordination and balance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Postural hypotension - A significant drop in blood pressure upon standing, often seen with antihypertensive drugs, leading to dizziness.
  • Nystagmus - Involuntary eye movement indicating vestibular dysfunction, commonly seen with ototoxic or vestibulotoxic medications.
  • Ataxia - Unsteady gait or lack of coordination, often seen with CNS depressants (e.g., sedatives, antiepileptics).
  • Bradycardia - Slower heart rate caused by drugs affecting autonomic regulation, such as beta-blockers, leading to reduced cardiac output and dizziness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Severe hypotension (BP < 90/60 mmHg) - Suggests excessive drug effect, such as from overmedication with antihypertensives, and may indicate impending syncope.
  • Persistent vertigo - May suggest permanent damage to the vestibular system, particularly with ototoxic medications (e.g., aminoglycosides).
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Indicates CNS depression or overdose of sedative medications, which can lead to respiratory depression and death.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Subtle dizziness in the elderly - Older patients may underreport dizziness or attribute it to age, leading to missed diagnosis, especially in the context of polypharmacy.
  • Intermittent vertigo - Drugs with fluctuating plasma concentrations (e.g., diuretics) may cause episodic dizziness rather than continuous symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Blood pressure monitoring - Measurement of postural changes in BP can identify hypotension as a cause of dizziness.
  • ECG - Useful for identifying bradycardia or arrhythmias related to medication use (e.g., beta-blockers).
  • Vestibular function tests - May be indicated if ototoxicity is suspected (e.g., caloric testing, Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre).
  • Serum drug levels - For drugs with narrow therapeutic ranges (e.g., digoxin, anticonvulsants) to check for toxic levels.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old female presents with dizziness and light-headedness after standing, starting 2 weeks after initiating a new antihypertensive medication. Her symptoms are relieved by sitting or lying down.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Distinguished by brief episodes of vertigo triggered by head movement, unlike continuous dizziness from medications.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension (Non-medication related) - May result from dehydration or autonomic failure but shares symptoms with medication-induced hypotension.
  • Meniere’s Disease - Associated with hearing loss and tinnitus, which are absent in medication-induced vertigo unless ototoxicity is involved.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Drug cessation or adjustment - First-line intervention is stopping or lowering the dose of the offending drug.
  • Hydration and lifestyle modifications - If hypotension is the cause, encourage adequate fluid intake and slow positional changes.
  • Vestibular rehabilitation - May be indicated if vestibular function is affected by ototoxic drugs.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures - Especially in elderly patients with dizziness, increasing the risk of injury.
  • Permanent vestibular damage - From ototoxic drugs like aminoglycosides, leading to chronic vertigo.
  • Severe hypotension - Can lead to syncope, and in extreme cases, hypoperfusion-related organ damage.
