Hyperthyroidism
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism is a clinical syndrome caused by an overproduction of thyroid hormones (thyroxine T4 and triiodothyronine T3) by the thyroid gland, resulting in an increased metabolic rate. This condition can be due to several causes, including Graves’ disease, toxic multinodular goitre, and thyroiditis. It presents with a variety of systemic symptoms affecting multiple organ systems.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: approximately 1.5–2% of the population, with a higher incidence in women compared to men (ratio ~5:1).
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: Common endocrine referral, especially in young to middle-aged women.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Variable, but Graves’ disease is the most common cause, accounting for 60-80% of cases globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Thyroid hormones, T3 and T4, regulate the body's metabolism. In hyperthyroidism, excess production of these hormones leads to an increased metabolic rate.
  • In Graves' disease, thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSI) stimulate the thyroid gland by mimicking thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), causing excess hormone production.
  • Toxic multinodular goitre and toxic adenoma cause hyperthyroidism due to autonomous functioning thyroid nodules that produce thyroid hormone independent of TSH regulation.
  • Thyroiditis (inflammatory causes) leads to the release of preformed thyroid hormones into the circulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Acute: Thyroiditis or toxic adenoma can cause a sudden onset of symptoms.
  • Subacute: Symptoms of Graves’ disease or toxic multinodular goitre may develop gradually over weeks to months.
  • Chronic: Long-term cases can persist over years, with fluctuating severity if untreated or inadequately managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - Due to increased basal metabolic rate from excessive thyroid hormones, patients lose weight despite normal or increased appetite.
  • Heat intolerance - Increased thermogenesis and sensitivity to heat due to the hypermetabolic state.
  • Palpitations - Increased sympathetic activity leads to tachycardia and irregular heartbeats, including atrial fibrillation in some cases.
  • Excessive sweating - Overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system stimulates sweat glands.
  • Fatigue and muscle weakness - Catabolism of muscle proteins due to increased metabolism causes muscle fatigue and weakness, especially in proximal muscles.
  • Increased bowel movements or diarrhoea - Hyperthyroidism accelerates gut motility, leading to more frequent stools.
  • Anxiety, irritability - The heightened metabolic state increases sympathetic drive, leading to mood disturbances and heightened anxiety.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Increased sympathetic tone leads to an elevated heart rate, commonly >100 beats per minute.
  • Goitre - Thyroid enlargement may be present in Graves’ disease or multinodular goitre, felt as a smooth or nodular swelling in the neck.
  • Exophthalmos - Specific to Graves’ disease, caused by autoimmune inflammation and swelling of tissues behind the eyes.
  • Pretibial myxoedema - Localised skin thickening over the shins, also specific to Graves’ disease, due to dermal deposition of mucopolysaccharides.
  • Fine tremor - The hyperadrenergic state leads to an increased physiologic tremor.
  • Hyperreflexia - Exaggerated reflexes due to increased neuronal excitability.
  • Warm, moist skin - Due to increased blood flow and sweating caused by the hypermetabolic state.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Thyroid storm - Life-threatening hyperthyroid crisis with severe tachycardia, hyperthermia, agitation, and delirium. It can lead to heart failure, shock, and death without rapid treatment.
  • Atrial fibrillation - Increased risk of thromboembolism and stroke due to irregular heart rhythm in hyperthyroid patients.
  • Severe ophthalmopathy - Progressive eye disease can lead to vision loss if untreated, particularly in Graves' disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - May present with “apathetic hyperthyroidism,” where typical symptoms like hyperactivity or anxiety are absent, and instead present with fatigue, weight loss, or depression.
  • Silent thyroiditis - Often post-partum, may present with mild or absent symptoms despite biochemical hyperthyroidism.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Suppressed TSH (22 pmol/L) and/or free T3 (>6 pmol/L) confirms hyperthyroidism.
  • Thyroid autoantibodies - Positive thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI) in Graves' disease, and anti-thyroid peroxidase (anti-TPO) antibodies may also be elevated.
  • Radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) - High uptake in Graves’ disease and toxic adenoma, low uptake in thyroiditis.
  • Ultrasound of the thyroid - Can detect nodules or inflammation in cases of toxic nodular disease or thyroiditis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of unintentional weight loss, palpitations, and heat intolerance. On examination, she has a tremor, tachycardia, and a diffuse goitre. TFTs reveal a suppressed TSH with elevated free T4, consistent with hyperthyroidism.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Anxiety disorder - While both conditions present with palpitations and restlessness, hyperthyroidism is associated with weight loss and heat intolerance, which is not typical of anxiety disorders.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - Can cause episodic palpitations, sweating, and tremors but usually features episodic hypertension, which is not a primary feature of hyperthyroidism.
  • Subacute thyroiditis - Presents with a tender thyroid and raised inflammatory markers, differing from the painless goitre seen in Graves’ disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Antithyroid medications - First-line treatment in the UK includes carbimazole or propylthiouracil, aiming to reduce thyroid hormone synthesis. Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) are used to control symptoms like tachycardia and tremor.
  • Radioactive iodine therapy - Used in Graves’ disease or toxic nodular disease to ablate the overactive thyroid tissue.
  • Surgery - Thyroidectomy may be indicated in cases of large goitres, severe ophthalmopathy, or when other treatments fail.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperthyroidism"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - Increased bone turnover due to elevated thyroid hormone levels can lead to weakened bones and fractures.
  • Heart failure - Persistent tachycardia and atrial fibrillation may lead to heart failure if untreated.
  • Thyroid storm - A medical emergency with a high mortality rate if untreated. Prompt recognition and aggressive treatment are necessary.
