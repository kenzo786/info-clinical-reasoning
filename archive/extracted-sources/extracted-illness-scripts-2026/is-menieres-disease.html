Meniere's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease is an idiopathic disorder of the inner ear characterised by recurrent episodes of vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus, and a sensation of aural fullness. It is associated with endolymphatic hydrops, where excessive fluid buildup in the inner ear disrupts normal hearing and balance mechanisms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, Meniere's disease affects approximately 1 in 1,000 individuals, with a general practice prevalence of around 0.2%. It is seen more commonly in hospital settings, particularly in otolaryngology and neurology clinics. Worldwide, the prevalence is estimated to be between 12-200 per 100,000 people.
  • It most commonly presents between the ages of 40-60, with a slight female predominance.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease is thought to be caused by an imbalance in the production and absorption of endolymph within the inner ear, leading to endolymphatic hydrops. Excess endolymph distends the membranous labyrinth, causing episodic dysfunction of the cochlear and vestibular systems. Vertigo arises from abnormal vestibular signalling, while hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness are related to cochlear involvement. The exact aetiology is unknown, but viral infections, autoimmune processes, and genetic factors may play a role.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease typically follows a relapsing-remitting course, with episodic attacks lasting 20 minutes to several hours. Early in the disease, episodes may occur sporadically, often months apart, but with disease progression, they may become more frequent. Hearing loss initially fluctuates but can become permanent over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Vertigo - Sudden, recurrent episodes of severe spinning vertigo lasting 20 minutes to several hours, caused by abnormal vestibular signalling due to endolymphatic hydrops.
  • Hearing loss - Initially fluctuating, low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss during or between episodes. Progressive as cochlear damage accumulates due to endolymphatic pressure changes.
  • Tinnitus - A constant or fluctuating ringing, buzzing, or roaring sound in the affected ear, often exacerbated during episodes of vertigo due to increased endolymphatic pressure affecting cochlear hair cells.
  • Aural fullness - A sensation of pressure or fullness in the affected ear, attributed to the buildup of endolymphatic fluid in the inner ear.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Common during vertigo episodes due to vestibular system dysfunction and its connection to the vomiting centre in the brainstem.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous nystagmus - A key feature during acute vertigo episodes, resulting from vestibular imbalance.
  • Positive Romberg test - Indicates vestibular dysfunction, with patients swaying or falling towards the affected side when eyes are closed.
  • Hearing loss on audiometry - Early in the disease, low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss is noted, progressing to a broader frequency range as the disease advances.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden, persistent hearing loss - May indicate a more serious condition like sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe headache, altered consciousness, or neurological deficit - Suggests a central cause of vertigo such as stroke or brainstem pathology, necessitating urgent imaging and specialist referral.
  • Facial numbness or weakness - Could be a sign of a vestibular schwannoma or other intracranial pathology pressing on the cranial nerves.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with isolated symptoms such as tinnitus or aural fullness without vertigo. In early stages, hearing loss may be mistaken for age-related changes, and vertigo attacks can be infrequent, leading to underdiagnosis.
  • Some patients may have bilateral symptoms, although unilateral involvement is more typical.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Audiometry - Typically shows low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss early on, with progression to involve higher frequencies. This is due to damage to cochlear hair cells from endolymphatic hydrops.
  • Vestibular function tests (caloric testing, video head impulse test) - May reveal unilateral hypofunction of the affected ear, indicating vestibular dysfunction.
  • Electrocochleography (ECochG) - May show increased summating potential to action potential ratio (SP/AP), indicating endolymphatic hydrops.
  • MRI with contrast - Performed to exclude vestibular schwannoma or other central causes of vertigo, as MRI will appear normal in Meniere's disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 50-year-old woman presents with recurrent episodes of vertigo lasting 2-3 hours, associated with fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, and a sensation of fullness in the left ear for the past year. Episodes occur weekly and are increasingly disabling.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Vestibular neuritis - Presents with acute, prolonged vertigo without hearing loss or tinnitus. No fluctuating symptoms like Meniere's.
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) - Short episodes of vertigo triggered by head movements, with no hearing loss or aural fullness.
  • Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) - Progressive unilateral hearing loss with or without vertigo, often accompanied by facial nerve symptoms.
  • Labyrinthitis - Acute onset of vertigo with hearing loss, often following an upper respiratory infection.
  • Migraine-associated vertigo - Associated with migraine headaches, photophobia, and phonophobia, with less prominent hearing loss and tinnitus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Dietary modifications - Low-sodium diet to reduce endolymphatic fluid retention.
  • Pharmacotherapy - Betahistine, a histamine analogue, is commonly used to reduce vertigo frequency and severity. Diuretics may also be considered.
  • Vestibular rehabilitation - For patients with persistent balance problems between episodes.
  • Intratympanic steroids or gentamicin injections - In refractory cases, gentamicin can ablate vestibular function on the affected side, though this carries the risk of further hearing loss.
  • Surgical options - Endolymphatic sac decompression or vestibular nerve section in severe, unresponsive cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss - Progressive loss of cochlear function due to repeated endolymphatic hydrops episodes.
  • Falls and accidents - Due to vertigo-induced imbalance, leading to potential injuries.
  • Psychological impact - Depression, anxiety, and social isolation may result from the unpredictability and disabling nature of symptoms.
