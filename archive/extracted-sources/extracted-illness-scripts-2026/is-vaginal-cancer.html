Vaginal Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Vaginal cancer is a rare malignant tumour originating in the tissues of the vagina. The most common type is squamous cell carcinoma, followed by adenocarcinoma, melanoma, and sarcoma. It often occurs secondary to HPV infection and tends to affect postmenopausal women. It is frequently asymptomatic in early stages, leading to late diagnosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prevalence: Vaginal cancer accounts for only about 1-2% of all gynaecological cancers.
  • UK GP population: Approximately 0.2–0.3 cases per 100,000 women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: About 250–300 new cases are diagnosed annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Incidence varies, with higher rates in countries with poor cervical cancer screening and HPV vaccination programmes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • The majority of vaginal cancers are squamous cell carcinomas, arising from vaginal intraepithelial neoplasia (VaIN) caused by persistent high-risk HPV infection (primarily types 16 and 18). HPV disrupts normal cellular DNA, leading to dysplasia and eventual carcinoma. Adenocarcinomas are usually associated with in utero exposure to diethylstilbestrol (DES). Other rarer subtypes include melanoma and sarcoma, with distinct pathophysiological mechanisms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Vaginal cancer is typically slow-growing, often developing over years. Pre-invasive lesions (VaIN) may persist for years before progressing to invasive cancer. Invasive cancer may remain asymptomatic for an extended period but eventually presents with symptoms as it invades surrounding tissues.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Vaginal bleeding - The most common presenting symptom, particularly postmenopausal or postcoital bleeding, occurs due to tumour invasion and erosion of vaginal blood vessels.
  • Vaginal discharge - Often watery or blood-stained, caused by tumour ulceration and necrosis.
  • Pelvic pain - May occur in advanced disease due to invasion of adjacent pelvic structures, nerves, or muscles.
  • Urinary symptoms - Dysuria or haematuria may result from bladder involvement or proximity to the urethra.
  • Constipation or tenesmus - Can occur if the cancer invades the rectum.
  • Asymptomatic - Many early-stage cases may be asymptomatic, often detected incidentally during routine gynaecological exams.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Visible mass in the vagina - May appear as an exophytic, ulcerated, or irregular growth, commonly in the upper third of the vagina.
  • Ulceration - May be present due to tumour necrosis, often with a foul-smelling discharge.
  • Pelvic tenderness - Suggests invasion of deeper structures such as pelvic muscles or nerves.
  • Enlarged inguinal lymph nodes - Indicative of lymphatic spread in more advanced cases.
  • Vaginal stenosis - Due to long-term tissue scarring or tumour growth, causing narrowing of the vaginal canal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - Could indicate invasive disease or a locally aggressive tumour.
  • Persistent vaginal discharge - Suggests tumour necrosis or infection, raising concern for advanced disease.
  • Pain with urination or defecation - Suggests bladder or rectal invasion, indicating more advanced tumour stages.
  • Pelvic or lower back pain - Suggests advanced cancer with possible invasion into surrounding structures or compression of nerves.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Incidental finding - Asymptomatic cases found during routine gynaecological exams or imaging performed for other reasons.
  • Urinary tract symptoms without obvious pelvic pain - Some women may present with dysuria or recurrent urinary tract infections as their primary complaint without classic gynaecological symptoms.
  • Vaginal pruritus - Rarely, pruritus can be an early symptom due to tumour irritation of the vaginal lining.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pelvic examination - Visualisation of any suspicious vaginal lesions and palpation of masses or tenderness.
  • Colposcopy with biopsy - Allows detailed visualisation and biopsy of abnormal areas to confirm histological diagnosis.
  • MRI pelvis - Useful for staging, to assess the extent of the disease and invasion into adjacent structures.
  • CT scan of abdomen and pelvis - Used to assess metastatic disease in the lymph nodes, liver, or lungs.
  • PET scan - To identify distant metastases or assess recurrence in advanced stages.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old woman presents with postmenopausal bleeding and a foul-smelling vaginal discharge. Examination reveals an ulcerated vaginal lesion. Biopsy confirms squamous cell carcinoma of the vagina.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer - May also present with vaginal bleeding and discharge, but is distinguishable by its origin in the cervix, visible on colposcopy.
  • Vaginal atrophy - Common cause of postmenopausal bleeding but without an ulcerated mass. Often associated with thinning and dryness of the vaginal epithelium.
  • Vaginal infection (e.g., vaginitis) - Causes discharge and irritation but typically lacks a mass or lesion, and resolves with treatment of the infection.
  • Endometrial cancer - Causes postmenopausal bleeding but originates in the endometrium, requiring different diagnostic and treatment approaches.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early-stage cancer (Stage I) - Surgical resection (vaginectomy) or radiotherapy.
  • Advanced-stage cancer - Combination of radiotherapy and chemotherapy (typically with cisplatin) is the preferred treatment.
  • Palliative care - For inoperable cases or late-stage presentations, focus on symptom control and quality of life.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vaginal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Local invasion - May invade bladder, rectum, or pelvic organs, leading to urinary or bowel obstruction.
  • Lymphatic spread - May involve inguinal or pelvic lymph nodes, increasing the risk of distant metastasis.
  • Distant metastases - Lung and liver metastases are common in advanced stages, worsening prognosis.
  • Fistula formation - Invasion of the rectum or bladder can result in rectovaginal or vesicovaginal fistulas, causing significant morbidity.
