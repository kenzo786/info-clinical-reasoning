Salivary Gland Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Salivary gland disorders encompass a group of conditions affecting the salivary glands, including inflammation (sialadenitis), infection, ductal obstruction (sialolithiasis), neoplasms, and autoimmune conditions such as Sjögren’s syndrome. These disorders typically impact the major salivary glands (parotid, submandibular, and sublingual), leading to symptoms such as swelling, pain, and xerostomia (dry mouth).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 0.1–0.2% of GP visits involve salivary gland disorders.
  • UK hospital prevalence: In hospitalised patients, the prevalence of salivary gland disorders, particularly obstructive and neoplastic conditions, is around 1–2% of ENT cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Sjögren’s syndrome, a common autoimmune cause of salivary gland dysfunction, affects about 0.1–0.6% of the global population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sialolithiasis: Stones form within the salivary ducts, often due to salivary stasis or increased calcium content, leading to ductal obstruction and gland swelling.
  • Sialadenitis: Bacterial (often Staphylococcus aureus) or viral infection (e.g., mumps) causes inflammation, which may result in glandular swelling, pain, and purulent discharge.
  • Sjögren’s syndrome: Autoimmune destruction of salivary gland tissue results in reduced salivary flow (xerostomia) and eventual glandular atrophy.
  • Neoplasms: Benign (e.g., pleomorphic adenoma) or malignant tumours (e.g., mucoepidermoid carcinoma) cause localised swelling and, in malignancies, rapid growth with possible nerve involvement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sialadenitis: Typically acute in bacterial infection, with a sudden onset of pain and swelling. Viral sialadenitis (mumps) presents subacutely over several days.
  • Sialolithiasis: Intermittent swelling, particularly with meals, which worsens over weeks to months if untreated.
  • Sjögren’s syndrome: Chronic, insidious progression of xerostomia and dry eyes over months to years.
  • Neoplasms: Benign neoplasms grow slowly over months to years; malignant neoplasms may grow more rapidly over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Swelling of the salivary gland - Due to ductal obstruction or inflammation, often intermittent in sialolithiasis (especially after meals) and persistent in neoplasms or infections.
  • Pain - Common in sialadenitis due to inflammation and in sialolithiasis when the duct is blocked. Pain may be absent in chronic conditions like Sjögren’s syndrome.
  • Xerostomia (dry mouth) - A hallmark of Sjögren’s syndrome, caused by reduced salivary secretion. This can also be seen in advanced sialadenitis due to glandular damage.
  • Pus discharge - Seen in bacterial sialadenitis when infection results in suppuration within the salivary ducts.
  • Dysphagia or difficulty opening the mouth - May occur in more severe cases due to significant swelling, particularly in infections and neoplasms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Visible swelling - Most common over the parotid or submandibular glands, often unilateral in sialolithiasis or bacterial infection, bilateral in mumps or Sjögren’s syndrome.
  • Palpable mass - May suggest a neoplasm or sialolithiasis; the mass in neoplasms tends to be firm, while stones may feel hard.
  • Tenderness - Present in acute sialadenitis or obstructive sialolithiasis but often absent in neoplasms and chronic autoimmune conditions.
  • Purulent discharge from the duct - Seen when the salivary duct is compressed, especially in acute bacterial sialadenitis.
  • Dry mucosa - Reflects significant salivary dysfunction, common in Sjögren’s syndrome and advanced sialadenitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve palsy - Involvement of the facial nerve, particularly with malignant parotid tumours, suggests invasion of surrounding structures.
  • Rapidly growing mass - Raises suspicion for malignancy, as benign tumours typically grow slowly.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) - In acute bacterial sialadenitis, these may indicate the spread of infection or abscess formation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Chronic painless swelling - Could suggest a chronic autoimmune process like Sjögren’s syndrome or a slowly growing benign tumour, rather than acute infection.
  • Unilateral dry eye and dry mouth - Involvement of only one side may be seen in Sjögren’s syndrome but could also suggest a localised obstruction or tumour.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - First-line imaging for salivary gland swelling. Stones appear as echogenic foci with acoustic shadowing, while neoplasms and abscesses may appear as hypoechoic areas.
  • Sialendoscopy - Used to visualise the ductal system and remove stones in sialolithiasis.
  • Salivary gland biopsy - Crucial for diagnosing Sjögren’s syndrome, showing lymphocytic infiltration.
  • Blood tests - In Sjögren’s syndrome, ANA, anti-Ro, and anti-La antibodies are often present. Raised CRP or WBC count may indicate bacterial infection.
  • Fine needle aspiration (FNA) - Used for cytological examination of suspected neoplasms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with recurrent, painful swelling of the left submandibular gland, worsened by eating, with occasional purulent discharge. She has no systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy - Lymph node swelling can mimic salivary gland enlargement but is usually associated with infection, lymphoma, or malignancy elsewhere.
  • Temporomandibular joint disorder - Can cause facial pain and swelling but is associated with joint tenderness and limited jaw movement.
  • Dental abscess - Presents with localised pain and swelling but is usually associated with tooth infection and may have visible gingival involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Bacterial sialadenitis - Empirical antibiotics such as co-amoxiclav; hydration and sialogogues to promote salivary flow.
  • Sialolithiasis - Hydration, sialogogues, and manual massage; if stone removal is needed, sialendoscopy is performed.
  • Sjögren’s syndrome - Artificial saliva and eye drops for symptom relief; systemic immunosuppression (e.g., hydroxychloroquine) for more severe cases.
  • Salivary gland tumours - Surgical excision, with adjuvant radiotherapy in malignant cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Abscess formation - Particularly in untreated bacterial sialadenitis, leading to systemic infection and potential spread to adjacent tissues.
  • Chronic gland dysfunction - In Sjögren’s syndrome or recurrent sialolithiasis, chronic xerostomia can cause dental caries and oral infections.
  • Malignancy - Neoplasms, especially in the parotid gland, can progress to invasive cancer with facial nerve involvement if not treated.
