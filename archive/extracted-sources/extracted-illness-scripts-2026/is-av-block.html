Atrioventricular (AV) Block
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Atrioventricular (AV) block refers to a delay or complete interruption of electrical conduction between the atria and ventricles. It is classified into first-degree, second-degree (Mobitz type I and type II), and third-degree (complete) AV block, depending on the severity of the conduction delay.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: 0.05-0.1% of the population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Up to 1% in patients with acute myocardial infarction (MI).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK, higher rates in elderly populations and patients with heart disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • In AV block, electrical impulses from the atria are delayed or blocked as they pass through the AV node or His-Purkinje system. The extent of delay determines the severity of the block:
    • First-degree AV block involves prolonged conduction without actual block, seen as a prolonged PR interval (>200 ms).
    • Second-degree AV block, Mobitz type I (Wenckebach) involves progressive PR interval prolongation until a beat is dropped.
    • Second-degree AV block, Mobitz type II involves intermittent dropped beats without prior PR prolongation, indicating a more severe block.
    • Third-degree AV block (complete) means no conduction between the atria and ventricles, with independent atrial and ventricular activity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Often occurs in the context of myocardial infarction or drug overdose (e.g., beta-blockers).
  • Chronic progressive course: Develops slowly in degenerative conditions, like Lev’s or Lenègre's disease, over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Most patients with first-degree or mild second-degree AV block are asymptomatic as conduction is minimally impaired.
  • Fatigue - Decreased cardiac output from ventricular bradycardia leads to reduced oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Dizziness or presyncope - Reduced cerebral perfusion due to bradycardia, particularly in more severe blocks (Mobitz II or complete block).
  • Syncope - Complete AV block can cause episodes of syncope due to significant pauses in ventricular activity.
  • Exertional dyspnoea - Inadequate heart rate response during exercise limits cardiac output, leading to breathlessness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia - The hallmark of AV block, especially in Mobitz II and complete AV block, results from impaired ventricular conduction.
  • Hypotension - Poor cardiac output due to bradycardia may cause low blood pressure, particularly in severe cases.
  • Cannon 'a' waves - Prominent jugular venous pulsations, especially in complete AV block, due to atrial contraction against a closed tricuspid valve.
  • Irregular pulse - Seen in second-degree AV block, particularly Mobitz type I, due to dropped beats.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Signifies significant bradycardia or transient asystole, increasing the risk of sudden cardiac death.
  • Sudden onset severe bradycardia - May indicate acute MI or drug overdose, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Heart failure symptoms - Progressive block can lead to heart failure due to chronically reduced cardiac output.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - AV block may present with generalised weakness, confusion, or unexplained falls rather than classic cardiac symptoms.
  • Younger patients - In athletes or individuals on medications like beta-blockers, bradycardia may be dismissed as normal without investigating AV block.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • ECG - Reveals prolonged PR interval (first-degree), dropped beats (second-degree), or dissociated atrial and ventricular activity (complete block). Pathophysiology: Impaired conduction through AV node or His-Purkinje system.
  • Holter monitoring - Detects intermittent block over 24-48 hours, particularly helpful for transient AV block.
  • Electrophysiological studies (EPS) - Can assess the exact location of conduction delay, often used in complex cases or before pacemaker placement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with fatigue, dizziness, and occasional syncope over the past few weeks. ECG shows complete heart block, with a heart rate of 40 bpm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Sick sinus syndrome - Bradycardia with periods of tachycardia or long pauses; unlike AV block, the issue is primarily in the sinoatrial node.
  • Vasovagal syncope - Syncope due to vagal stimulation causing bradycardia; lacks the consistent ECG findings of AV block.
  • Beta-blocker toxicity - Drug-induced bradycardia without the characteristic ECG features of AV block; reversible with discontinuation of the drug.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • First-degree or stable second-degree AV block - No treatment is necessary if asymptomatic. Regular monitoring is advised.
  • Symptomatic second-degree AV block or third-degree AV block - Requires pacemaker insertion to maintain heart rate and prevent asystole.
  • Acute AV block (e.g., post-MI) - May require temporary pacing and management of the underlying cause (e.g., MI, drug toxicity).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atrioventricular (AV) Block"][/sc]
  • Sudden cardiac death - Especially in untreated high-grade AV block, due to ventricular asystole.
  • Heart failure - Chronically reduced cardiac output can lead to dilated cardiomyopathy and heart failure.
  • Arrhythmias - Incomplete conduction block can predispose patients to atrial fibrillation or ventricular arrhythmias.
