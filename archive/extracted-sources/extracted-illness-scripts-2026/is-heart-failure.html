Heart Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome characterised by the heart's inability to pump enough blood to meet the metabolic needs of the body. It can result from structural or functional abnormalities in the heart, leading to inadequate perfusion of tissues and fluid accumulation. Heart failure can be classified into heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) or heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) based on left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population Prevalence: Approximately 1-2% of the UK population, with prevalence increasing with age (up to 10% in those aged ≥70 years).
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Heart failure accounts for around 5% of all emergency medical admissions.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, around 64 million people are living with heart failure, and it contributes to significant morbidity and mortality.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Heart failure occurs when cardiac output is insufficient due to either systolic dysfunction (impaired contraction) or diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation).
  • Systolic Heart Failure (HFrEF): Impaired contractility of the left ventricle leads to reduced ejection fraction (LVEF ≤40%). Causes include ischaemic heart disease, dilated cardiomyopathy, and myocardial infarction.
  • Diastolic Heart Failure (HFpEF): The left ventricle becomes stiff and unable to relax during diastole, leading to elevated filling pressures despite a normal ejection fraction (LVEF ≥50%). Hypertension and left ventricular hypertrophy are common causes.
  • Neurohormonal Activation: The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and sympathetic nervous system (SNS) are activated, leading to fluid retention, vasoconstriction, and increased afterload, further worsening cardiac function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of symptoms, often precipitated by a myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, or acute valvular disease.
  • Chronic: Progressively worsening symptoms over months or years, commonly seen in patients with longstanding hypertension or coronary artery disease.
  • Decompensation: Acute exacerbation of chronic heart failure, usually triggered by infections, dietary noncompliance (salt intake), or medication non-adherence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (breathlessness) - The most common symptom, resulting from pulmonary congestion and increased left atrial pressure. It is exacerbated by exertion and relieved by rest.
  • Orthopnoea - Breathlessness when lying flat, caused by redistribution of fluid from the lower extremities to the lungs, increasing pulmonary venous pressure.
  • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnoea (PND) - Sudden episodes of severe breathlessness at night, due to fluid reabsorption into the circulation when lying down, leading to pulmonary oedema.
  • Fatigue - A result of reduced cardiac output, leading to inadequate perfusion of muscles and tissues.
  • Ankle swelling - Peripheral oedema occurs due to increased venous pressure from fluid retention.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Raised jugular venous pressure (JVP) - Indicates elevated right-sided heart pressures and fluid overload.
  • Bilateral pitting oedema - Peripheral oedema results from sodium and water retention due to RAAS activation.
  • Crackles on lung auscultation - Represents pulmonary oedema from fluid transudation into the alveoli.
  • Displaced apex beat - Lateral displacement of the apex beat suggests left ventricular hypertrophy or dilation.
  • Third heart sound (S3) - A low-pitched sound heard early in diastole, indicating increased ventricular filling pressures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Sudden worsening of dyspnoea or orthopnoea - May indicate acute pulmonary oedema, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Syncope or pre-syncope - Suggests severe arrhythmia or advanced heart failure, which could lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • New onset chest pain - May signify a myocardial infarction or acute coronary syndrome, necessitating emergency management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue without dyspnoea - Especially in the elderly, heart failure may present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, without classic signs of fluid overload.
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, anorexia) - May occur due to congestion of the gastrointestinal tract and liver in right-sided heart failure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide) or NT-proBNP: Elevated levels indicate increased ventricular wall stress. A BNP >100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL is suggestive of heart failure.
  • Echocardiography: Key diagnostic tool to assess ejection fraction, chamber size, and valve function. It helps differentiate HFrEF from HFpEF.
  • Chest X-ray: May show pulmonary congestion, cardiomegaly, or pleural effusion, indicating fluid overload.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Often shows signs of left ventricular hypertrophy, atrial fibrillation, or previous myocardial infarction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old man presents with worsening breathlessness on exertion, orthopnoea, and ankle swelling over several months. He has a history of hypertension and is found to have a raised JVP and bilateral lung crackles on examination. Echocardiogram confirms heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD): Typically presents with chronic cough and wheeze rather than fluid overload. Pulmonary function tests are diagnostic.
  • Pulmonary embolism (PE): Acute dyspnoea with pleuritic chest pain and haemoptysis is more common in PE. A CT pulmonary angiogram can confirm the diagnosis.
  • Renal failure: Can cause oedema and breathlessness but without the typical cardiac features like elevated JVP or displaced apex beat. Blood tests reveal elevated creatinine and urea levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • First-line: ACE inhibitors (e.g. ramipril) or ARBs, and beta-blockers (e.g. bisoprolol) for patients with HFrEF to reduce mortality and hospitalisations.
  • Diuretics: Furosemide for symptom relief in fluid-overloaded patients, though not shown to reduce mortality.
  • Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists: E.g. spironolactone, recommended for HFrEF to reduce morbidity and mortality.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: Recent evidence supports the use of dapagliflozin for heart failure management in HFrEF, improving outcomes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias: Particularly atrial fibrillation, due to atrial enlargement and electrical instability. Increases risk of stroke and worsens heart failure prognosis.
  • Renal dysfunction: Common in advanced heart failure, due to reduced renal perfusion and use of diuretics and ACE inhibitors.
  • Sudden cardiac death: Often due to ventricular arrhythmias, which are more common in severe HFrEF.
