Hidradenitis Suppurativa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa (HS) is a chronic, inflammatory skin disorder characterised by recurrent, painful nodules, abscesses, and sinus tracts, primarily in intertriginous areas (e.g., axillae, groin, and under the breasts). It is considered part of the follicular occlusion spectrum and is frequently associated with scarring and fibrosis due to repeated inflammation and infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: ~1% of the adult population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: More common among dermatology and surgery patients, particularly in severe cases needing surgical intervention.
  • Global prevalence: Estimated to affect 1-4% of the worldwide population, with higher rates in females (approximately 3:1 female-to-male ratio) and typically presenting in late adolescence to early adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • 1. Follicular Occlusion: HS begins with obstruction of the pilosebaceous unit due to hyperkeratosis, leading to follicular plugging.
  • 2. Rupture and Inflammation: Blocked follicles can rupture, spilling keratin, bacteria, and sebum into surrounding tissue, triggering an inflammatory response.
  • 3. Immune Dysregulation: HS is associated with increased inflammatory cytokines, including TNF-α and IL-1β, contributing to a chronic, relapsing inflammatory process.
  • 4. Abscess and Sinus Formation: Chronic inflammation causes the formation of abscesses, sinus tracts, and scarring. Repeated cycles of inflammation and healing result in fibrosis and the development of sinus tracts.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • HS follows a chronic, relapsing-remitting course with periods of exacerbation and remission.
  • Early stage: Recurrent, isolated nodules and small abscesses without sinus tract formation.
  • Intermediate stage: Larger abscesses and interconnected sinus tracts develop, with visible scarring.
  • Advanced stage: Extensive scarring, fibrosis, and deep sinus tracts lead to significant disfigurement and functional impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Recurrent Painful Nodules - Painful lumps caused by inflammation around the follicle due to follicular plugging and immune response.
  • Abscess Formation - Fluid-filled lesions develop as blocked follicles rupture and form pockets of infection and inflammation.
  • Drainage of Purulent Material - Due to sinus tract formation, leading to malodorous discharge as a result of bacterial colonisation.
  • Itching and Burning - Sensation related to ongoing skin inflammation and immune activation.
  • Psychosocial Distress - Due to disfigurement, malodorous drainage, and chronicity, leading to depression and anxiety.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Inflamed Nodules - Raised, tender lesions in intertriginous regions, resulting from follicular occlusion and immune response.
  • Sinus Tracts and Fistulae - Tunnels under the skin that form due to chronic inflammation and repeated rupture of follicles.
  • Scarring and Fibrosis - Thickened, fibrotic skin due to repeated inflammation, often appearing as “rope-like” scars.
  • Malodorous Discharge - Purulent material with a foul odour, associated with bacterial colonisation of sinus tracts.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Significant Constitutional Symptoms (fever, weight loss) - May indicate secondary infection or severe inflammatory response.
  • Rapidly Progressive Lesions - Could suggest a superimposed infection or possible malignancy in long-standing cases (rare).
  • Widespread and Severe Pain - May be a sign of advanced disease with extensive sinus tract formation and nerve involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Single Nodule Without Recurrence - Early-stage HS can present with isolated, small lesions, potentially misdiagnosed as folliculitis or cysts.
  • Lesions Outside Common Intertriginous Areas - Rare but can occur in atypical sites such as the face or back.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Clinical Diagnosis - Based on recurrent nodules, abscesses, and sinus tracts in intertriginous areas. Diagnosis does not require laboratory confirmation.
  • Swab Culture - May be taken if there is purulent drainage; cultures often show mixed bacteria but are not the primary cause.
  • Imaging (Ultrasound) - Useful in assessing extent of sinus tracts and differentiating HS from abscesses. MRI may be used in complex or recurrent cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old female presents with recurrent painful nodules and abscesses in the axillae over a 2-year period, with malodorous drainage and extensive scarring.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Folliculitis - Generally presents with smaller, non-recurrent pustules and lacks sinus tracts.
  • Pilonidal Disease - Usually localised to the sacrococcygeal area, with sinus formation but not intertriginous distribution.
  • Abscess - Acute and localised without chronic sinus tract formation or scarring.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Topical Clindamycin - Used in mild cases, applied to affected areas twice daily.
  • Oral Antibiotics - Tetracyclines or clindamycin and rifampicin are commonly used in moderate cases to reduce inflammation.
  • Biologic Therapy - Adalimumab (anti-TNF) is used for severe or refractory HS, targeting the inflammatory response.
  • Surgical Intervention - Options include incision and drainage for acute abscesses and excision of sinus tracts in chronic, severe cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hidradenitis Suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Scarring and Fibrosis - Common and contributes to functional impairment and psychological distress.
  • Secondary Infection - Common due to chronic open lesions and sinus tracts, often requiring antibiotic treatment.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma - Rare but serious complication, particularly in long-standing, untreated cases.
  • Psychosocial Impact - Depression and anxiety are common due to pain, disfigurement, and chronicity of the disease.
