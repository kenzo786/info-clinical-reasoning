Acute Glaucoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Acute glaucoma, also referred to as acute angle-closure glaucoma, is an ophthalmic emergency characterised by a sudden rise in intraocular pressure (IOP) due to the closure of the anterior chamber angle, obstructing aqueous humour drainage. This results in optic nerve damage and rapid vision loss if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Rare, around 2 per 1000 consultations.
  • Hospital prevalence: Less than 1% of hospital ophthalmic presentations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Acute angle-closure glaucoma accounts for 0.1% to 0.3% of glaucoma cases globally, with higher rates in East Asian populations due to anatomical predisposition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • In acute glaucoma, a structural predisposition such as a narrow anterior chamber angle leads to the sudden closure of the drainage angle for the aqueous humour. This blockage causes a rapid increase in intraocular pressure (IOP), damaging the optic nerve fibres and retinal ganglion cells, resulting in visual impairment. Factors such as pupil dilation (e.g., in dim light or due to medication) or stress may precipitate the closure by pushing the iris forward.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Acute glaucoma typically develops rapidly over several hours to days. The acute phase is characterised by sudden ocular pain, blurred vision, and systemic symptoms like nausea and vomiting. Without treatment, vision loss can occur within hours, and the condition may progress to chronic glaucoma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Severe ocular pain - Caused by the rapid increase in intraocular pressure, which stimulates the trigeminal nerve.
  • Blurred vision - Resulting from corneal oedema secondary to elevated IOP, impairing visual acuity.
  • Halos around lights - Due to corneal oedema causing light diffraction.
  • Headache - Referred pain from the eye due to the trigeminal nerve involvement.
  • Nausea and vomiting - A systemic response triggered by the sudden and intense rise in intraocular pressure, stimulating the vagus nerve.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP) - Detected via tonometry; raised IOP due to blocked aqueous outflow.
  • Mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil - Pupil stuck in a semi-dilated state due to the increased pressure compressing the iris sphincter.
  • Corneal oedema - Causes the cornea to appear hazy due to fluid buildup from elevated IOP.
  • Conjunctival injection - Redness of the eye due to dilated blood vessels caused by the ocular inflammation and increased pressure.
  • Shallow anterior chamber - Seen on slit-lamp examination; a predisposing anatomical factor for angle closure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Severe and sudden ocular pain - Indicates rapid pressure rise, which can lead to permanent vision loss within hours.
  • Loss of vision - Suggests optic nerve damage due to prolonged increased IOP.
  • Nausea and vomiting - A systemic response to the acute rise in pressure, may indicate a dangerously high IOP.
  • Fixed, mid-dilated pupil - Indicates a fully closed angle, often an irreversible stage requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Mild ocular pain - Some patients may have less intense pain, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • Intermittent blurred vision - A prodromal phase may include brief episodes of blurry vision, particularly in dim light, which can be mistaken for refractive errors.
  • Subacute glaucoma - A less severe form with intermittent symptoms, potentially delaying the recognition of an acute angle closure attack.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Tonometry - Measures intraocular pressure. A reading >21 mmHg is abnormal; in acute glaucoma, it is often >40 mmHg due to blocked aqueous humour outflow.
  • Gonioscopy - Directly visualises the angle of the anterior chamber, confirming angle closure.
  • Slit-lamp examination - Reveals corneal oedema, shallow anterior chamber, and a mid-dilated pupil.
  • Optic nerve imaging (OCT) - Shows optic nerve head changes due to raised IOP and provides a baseline for monitoring future damage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old East Asian woman presents with sudden onset severe right eye pain, blurred vision, and nausea. Examination reveals elevated IOP, a mid-dilated pupil, and corneal oedema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Typically presents with a red eye but lacks severe pain, IOP elevation, and pupillary abnormalities.
  • Acute uveitis - Presents with ocular pain and redness, but the pupil is often constricted rather than mid-dilated.
  • Corneal abrasion - Causes ocular pain and tearing, but does not result in elevated IOP or mid-dilated pupil.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Medical management - Initial treatment involves lowering IOP with topical agents (e.g., beta-blockers like timolol, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors like acetazolamide) and systemic osmotic agents like mannitol.
  • Surgical management - Definitive treatment is laser peripheral iridotomy, which creates a new pathway for aqueous humour to drain by puncturing the iris.
  • Pain management - Analgesics and antiemetics to manage symptoms such as pain and nausea.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss - Occurs due to prolonged optic nerve damage from elevated IOP.
  • Chronic glaucoma - Recurrent or untreated acute episodes may lead to chronic angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Corneal scarring - From prolonged corneal oedema, which may impair vision permanently.
