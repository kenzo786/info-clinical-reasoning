Splenomegaly
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Splenomegaly refers to the pathological enlargement of the spleen, which can result from various underlying conditions including haematological disorders, infections, liver disease, and malignancies. It is typically diagnosed when the spleen is larger than 12 cm in its greatest dimension or weighs more than 500 grams.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, splenomegaly is uncommon, as it often presents in secondary care settings. It is primarily associated with conditions such as chronic liver disease and haematological malignancies. Hospital prevalence varies, with studies showing it in 2-3% of inpatients. Globally, splenomegaly is more prevalent in areas endemic for malaria, schistosomiasis, and other parasitic infections.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Splenomegaly occurs due to increased splenic workload or congestion. This can be from excessive haemolysis (as in haemolytic anaemia), increased immune activity (such as in infections), portal hypertension (as seen in liver cirrhosis), infiltration by malignant cells (e.g., lymphomas, leukaemias), or deposition of metabolic products (e.g., storage diseases).
  • The spleen enlarges to accommodate an increased filtration load or the infiltration of abnormal cells, leading to hyperplasia of the lymphoid or red pulp.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • The time course of splenomegaly depends on the underlying cause. Infections like mononucleosis cause acute splenomegaly, typically resolving within weeks. In contrast, chronic conditions like cirrhosis or haematological malignancies cause progressive, long-standing enlargement. Some cases remain asymptomatic for years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Abdominal discomfort or fullness - Due to the enlarged spleen pressing on surrounding structures. Common in moderate to severe cases of splenomegaly.
  • Early satiety - Caused by compression of the stomach by the enlarged spleen, leading to reduced gastric capacity.
  • Fatigue - Commonly linked to underlying conditions like haematological disorders or chronic infections that cause splenomegaly.
  • Asymptomatic - Many patients, especially those with mild splenomegaly, may not present with symptoms and are diagnosed incidentally.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Palpable spleen below the costal margin - Normally, the spleen is not palpable. A palpable spleen indicates significant enlargement, which is often confirmed via imaging.
  • Splenic friction rub - Rare, but may occur in cases of perisplenitis. This is a sign of inflammation of the splenic capsule.
  • Hypersplenism - Manifested by pancytopenia (low white blood cell, red blood cell, and platelet counts) due to excessive sequestration and destruction of blood cells in the enlarged spleen.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Suggests splenic infarction or rupture, which can occur in conditions such as trauma, infection, or haematological malignancy.
  • Marked anaemia, thrombocytopenia, or leukopenia - May indicate hypersplenism, signifying the overactivity of the spleen in sequestering blood cells. This can lead to life-threatening pancytopenia.
  • Fever and weight loss - Could indicate an underlying malignancy or chronic infection such as lymphoma or tuberculosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Isolated cytopenias without obvious splenomegaly - Patients may present with low blood counts, particularly anaemia or thrombocytopenia, in the absence of overt splenomegaly on palpation. Imaging may reveal an enlarged spleen.
  • Splenomegaly without systemic symptoms - Some patients may have incidental findings of splenomegaly on imaging without any constitutional symptoms, which may delay diagnosis of underlying causes such as malignancies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show cytopenias (anaemia, thrombocytopenia, or leukopenia), indicating hypersplenism.
  • Ultrasound or CT scan of the abdomen - Confirms splenic enlargement and can help identify structural causes like tumours or infarctions.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Abnormal LFTs can suggest portal hypertension from cirrhosis as a cause of splenomegaly.
  • Bone marrow biopsy - Indicated in cases where haematological malignancy (e.g., leukaemia, lymphoma) is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with fatigue and early satiety over the past 3 months. Examination reveals a palpable spleen extending 4 cm below the left costal margin. Blood tests show anaemia and thrombocytopenia, with an abdominal ultrasound confirming splenomegaly.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Liver cirrhosis with portal hypertension - Differentiated by signs of chronic liver disease such as ascites, jaundice, and abnormal LFTs.
  • Lymphoma - Systemic features like fever, night sweats, and weight loss are more prominent, along with lymphadenopathy.
  • Leukaemia - Typically presents with more profound pancytopenia and systemic symptoms such as fatigue, infections, and bruising.
  • Infectious mononucleosis - Acute presentation with fever, pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy, commonly seen in younger patients.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Treat the underlying cause - For example, manage cirrhosis with portal hypertension by addressing liver disease or treat haematological malignancies with appropriate chemotherapy.
  • Splenectomy - Considered in cases of severe hypersplenism or when the spleen is the primary site of pathology (e.g., in some haematological conditions or trauma).
  • Supportive care - Includes transfusions for severe cytopenias, analgesia for pain, and vaccines (pneumococcal, meningococcal) for patients undergoing splenectomy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Splenic rupture - A potentially life-threatening complication, particularly in trauma or infections like mononucleosis.
  • Hypersplenism - Leads to pancytopenia, with risk of bleeding, infections, and anaemia.
  • Increased risk of infections - Particularly after splenectomy, due to loss of the spleen’s immune function (especially against encapsulated organisms).
