Subdural Haematoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Subdural haematoma (SDH) is a collection of blood between the dura mater and the arachnoid mater, typically caused by trauma that results in tearing of bridging veins. It can be classified into acute, subacute, or chronic based on the time since the bleeding occurred. Chronic SDH is more common in older adults due to brain atrophy and increased venous vulnerability.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Chronic SDH is seen in 8-14 per 100,000 adults annually, rising with age.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: SDH accounts for approximately 10% of all traumatic brain injuries.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar patterns are observed globally, particularly in populations with a higher proportion of elderly individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Subdural haematomas typically result from trauma, leading to tearing of the bridging veins that traverse the subdural space. Bleeding occurs slowly due to the low-pressure venous system, but as blood accumulates, it compresses the brain tissue, leading to neurological symptoms. In chronic SDH, the initial trauma may be mild and go unnoticed, but blood products gradually break down, causing an inflammatory response and osmotic shift, drawing fluid into the space and enlarging the haematoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Acute SDH occurs within 72 hours of the initial injury and typically presents with rapid neurological deterioration.
  • Subacute SDH develops over 3 to 20 days and may present with more gradual worsening of symptoms.
  • Chronic SDH can develop over weeks or months and often presents with vague or fluctuating symptoms, particularly in elderly patients.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Headache - Commonly presents due to increased intracranial pressure as the haematoma enlarges.
  • Confusion - Results from compression of brain tissue, leading to altered cognition and consciousness.
  • Drowsiness - Seen as intracranial pressure rises, impairing normal brain function.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Symptoms like hemiparesis may develop as pressure affects motor pathways.
  • Seizures - Compression of cortical areas can provoke abnormal neuronal activity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Altered consciousness - Ranges from mild confusion to coma, depending on the extent of brain compression.
  • Focal neurological signs - Hemiparesis, dysphasia, or pupillary asymmetry may occur due to mass effect on motor or sensory pathways.
  • Papilloedema - A sign of raised intracranial pressure, seen on fundoscopic examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Rapid deterioration in consciousness - Indicates a potential increase in haematoma size, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe headache and vomiting - Suggestive of raised intracranial pressure or acute expansion of the haematoma.
  • Unilateral pupil dilation - Can signify brain herniation, an emergency complication of significant brain compression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • In elderly patients, subdural haematomas may present with only mild cognitive impairment or gait disturbances, making diagnosis challenging. Brain atrophy allows the haematoma to expand slowly without causing significant early symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • CT head scan - Reveals crescent-shaped, hyperdense or hypodense areas depending on the age of the haematoma. Hyperdense in acute SDH, hypodense in chronic cases.
  • MRI head - Useful in subacute or chronic cases, showing better resolution of fluid collection and any brain displacement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • An 80-year-old male presents with a two-week history of worsening headache, confusion, and a recent fall. Neurological examination reveals mild right-sided weakness. CT scan confirms a chronic subdural haematoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Stroke - More sudden onset, often associated with vascular risk factors. CT or MRI shows ischemic or haemorrhagic lesions rather than a crescent-shaped haematoma.
  • Epidural haematoma - More often associated with skull fractures and presents with a lucid interval followed by rapid deterioration. CT scan shows a biconvex (lens-shaped) collection.
  • Brain tumour - May cause gradual onset symptoms but typically associated with a mass lesion rather than blood collection on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Non-surgical management - Small, asymptomatic haematomas may be monitored with serial imaging. Corticosteroids may be used to reduce inflammation.
  • Surgical management - Symptomatic or large haematomas often require burr hole evacuation or craniotomy to relieve pressure.
  • Supportive care - Including seizure prophylaxis, management of raised intracranial pressure, and addressing coagulopathy if present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subdural Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Brain herniation - Life-threatening complication due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Recurrent SDH - Occurs in up to 20% of patients following surgical intervention.
  • Seizures - Due to cortical irritation, particularly in chronic cases.
  • Prognosis - Mortality rate is approximately 20-30%, but with early treatment, prognosis improves significantly.
