AC Joint Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • AC (acromioclavicular) joint disorder refers to a spectrum of conditions affecting the acromioclavicular joint, including sprains, osteoarthritis, and traumatic injuries such as dislocations. It commonly manifests with shoulder pain and functional limitations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: ~3-5% of patients presenting with shoulder pain.
  • Hospital prevalence: AC joint injuries account for approximately 10% of all shoulder injuries seen in orthopaedic clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: More common in individuals engaged in high-impact sports or manual labour.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Initial insult may involve ligamentous injury (e.g., AC or coracoclavicular ligament sprain) or cartilage degeneration (in osteoarthritis).
  • Inflammation occurs within the joint capsule, leading to pain and swelling.
  • Chronic cases may involve joint instability or bony deformities, exacerbating functional limitations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of pain following trauma or overuse.
  • Subacute phase: Pain reduces but remains with activity, alongside restricted range of motion.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent discomfort and functional impairment due to instability or degenerative changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Localised shoulder pain - Often described at the top of the shoulder due to inflammation and joint strain.
  • Pain with overhead movements - Compression or tension of the joint structures during arm elevation.
  • Weakness - Resulting from discomfort and reduced joint stability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over the AC joint - Indicative of local inflammation or injury.
  • <b.Visible deformity - May occur in dislocations or severe arthritis.
  • Positive cross-body adduction test - Reproduction of pain due to compression of the AC joint.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Severe pain with visible deformity - Suggests acute dislocation requiring urgent reduction.
  • Neurological symptoms - Such as numbness, may indicate concurrent brachial plexus injury.
  • Infection signs - Fever, warmth, and redness over the joint may point to septic arthritis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Referred pain to the neck or scapula, potentially misdiagnosed as cervical radiculopathy.
  • Minimal pain but functional weakness, especially in chronic cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • X-ray - May reveal joint space narrowing, osteophytes, or clavicular displacement.
  • MRI - Useful for assessing ligamentous injuries or intra-articular pathology.
  • Ultrasound - Helpful in evaluating soft tissue swelling or effusions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male with a 1-week history of shoulder pain after a rugby injury, localised to the top of the shoulder, worsened by overhead activity and cross-body movements.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Rotator cuff tear - Pain is diffuse, and weakness is more pronounced.
  • Frozen shoulder - Significant stiffness with minimal pain at rest.
  • Osteoarthritis of the glenohumeral joint - Pain localised deeper within the shoulder.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute management: Rest, ice, and NSAIDs to reduce inflammation.
  • Physiotherapy: Focus on strengthening and stabilisation exercises.
  • Surgical intervention: Considered for severe dislocations or persistent symptoms despite conservative therapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and instability: Due to unresolved ligament damage or arthritis.
  • Post-traumatic arthritis: Can occur following untreated or severe injuries.
