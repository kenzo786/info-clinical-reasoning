Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI) refers to infections involving the airways below the level of the larynx, including bronchitis, bronchiolitis, and pneumonia. It is typically characterised by inflammation of the bronchi, alveoli, and lung parenchyma, causing symptoms like cough, sputum production, and breathlessness. LRTIs can be bacterial or viral and vary from mild to life-threatening depending on the pathogen and host factors.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, LRTIs account for approximately 6% of all GP consultations annually, and around 5% of hospital admissions are related to severe cases such as pneumonia. Worldwide, LRTIs are a major cause of morbidity and mortality, responsible for over 2.5 million deaths annually, particularly in low-income countries and among vulnerable populations like children and the elderly.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • LRTI begins with the invasion of pathogens (viruses, bacteria, or fungi) into the lower respiratory tract, overcoming the host's defence mechanisms like mucociliary clearance and alveolar macrophages. This leads to an inflammatory response, with release of cytokines and recruitment of immune cells like neutrophils. In viral infections, epithelial damage can occur, whereas in bacterial infections, alveoli may become filled with exudates, causing impaired gas exchange. In more severe infections like pneumonia, consolidation of lung tissue occurs, leading to hypoxaemia and respiratory distress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 1-4 days depending on the pathogen.
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically peak around 3-5 days, with productive cough, fever, and breathlessness.
  • Recovery phase: Over 7-14 days, depending on treatment and severity, with residual cough sometimes lasting longer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Cough (often productive) - Due to irritation of the respiratory tract and increased mucus production from inflammation of the bronchi.
  • Fever - Reflects the systemic inflammatory response to infection, triggered by cytokine release.
  • Dyspnoea - Occurs due to impaired gas exchange from alveolar inflammation and exudation in conditions like pneumonia.
  • Pleuritic chest pain - Inflammation of the pleura can cause sharp chest pain, particularly during deep breathing or coughing.
  • Fatigue and malaise - Caused by the overall systemic infection and the body’s immune response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea - Due to increased respiratory effort from hypoxaemia and impaired gas exchange.
  • Crackles on auscultation - Heard due to air moving through fluid-filled alveoli, commonly seen in pneumonia.
  • Dullness to percussion - Indicates lung consolidation in pneumonia where alveoli are filled with exudates.
  • Use of accessory muscles - Indicative of increased respiratory effort and dyspnoea, often in severe cases.
  • Cyanosis - A late sign of hypoxaemia where the patient develops bluish skin and mucous membranes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis - Can signify severe infection or complications such as lung abscess or malignancy.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - A sign of sepsis, particularly in the elderly or immunocompromised.
  • Persistent high fever - May indicate failure of initial treatment or progression to a more severe infection like empyema or sepsis.
  • Severe dyspnoea or tachypnoea - Can be a sign of respiratory failure requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present without fever or productive cough and instead with confusion or general malaise due to a blunted immune response.
  • Immunocompromised patients: Can have less prominent symptoms and may present later in the disease course, with a higher risk of severe complications.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - Infiltrates or consolidation may indicate pneumonia. This occurs due to alveolar exudates.
  • Sputum culture - To identify bacterial pathogens, guiding antibiotic therapy.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Raised white cell count suggests a bacterial infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated in response to inflammation, useful in assessing severity.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Used to assess oxygenation and ventilation in patients with severe dyspnoea or hypoxaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 5-day history of productive cough, fever, and worsening breathlessness. Examination reveals tachypnoea, crackles on auscultation, and dullness to percussion on the right lower lobe. Chest X-ray confirms right lower lobe consolidation, consistent with community-acquired pneumonia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Asthma exacerbation - Presents with wheezing and reversible airway obstruction, often without fever or productive cough.
  • Heart failure - Dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and bilateral crackles, but typically without fever or focal consolidation on imaging.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute onset of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, and haemoptysis, with clear lung fields on chest X-ray.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) exacerbation - History of smoking, wheezing, and chronic cough, often with no fever.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Amoxicillin or doxycycline as first-line treatment in community-acquired pneumonia as per NICE guidelines. For hospitalised patients, ceftriaxone and clarithromycin may be indicated.
  • Oxygen therapy - For patients with hypoxaemia, titrated to maintain oxygen saturation above 92%.
  • Fluids and supportive care - Intravenous fluids for dehydration, antipyretics for fever, and bronchodilators if there is bronchospasm.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lower Respiratory Tract Infection (LRTI)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - A life-threatening response to infection, occurring in around 10% of hospitalised LRTI cases, leading to multi-organ failure.
  • Empyema - A collection of pus in the pleural space, often requiring drainage, seen in complicated pneumonias.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) - Severe inflammatory response in the lungs causing widespread alveolar damage, requiring intensive care management.
  • Respiratory failure - May develop due to severe hypoxaemia or hypercapnia, necessitating ventilatory support.
