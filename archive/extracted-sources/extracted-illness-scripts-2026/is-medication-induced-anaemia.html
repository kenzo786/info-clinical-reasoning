Medication-Induced Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced anaemia is a form of anaemia caused by the administration of certain drugs that either impair red blood cell production, increase red blood cell destruction, or interfere with vital nutrients necessary for erythropoiesis. Common mechanisms include bone marrow suppression, haemolysis, and interference with folate or vitamin B12 metabolism.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare, with estimates suggesting 0.1-0.5% of patients on medication therapy might develop drug-induced anaemia.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Medication-induced anaemia represents a small subset of anaemia cases in hospital settings, with higher prevalence in those undergoing chemotherapy or immunosuppressive therapy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimates vary, but certain drugs (like chemotherapeutics) are well-documented causes globally, particularly in oncology centres.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Bone marrow suppression: Drugs like chemotherapy agents suppress the bone marrow’s ability to produce red blood cells, leading to anaemia.
  • Haemolysis: Certain drugs can cause immune-mediated or direct damage to red blood cells, leading to premature destruction. For example, antibiotics like penicillin can cause immune haemolytic anaemia.
  • Nutrient interference: Drugs like methotrexate interfere with folate metabolism, essential for DNA synthesis in red blood cell precursors, resulting in megaloblastic anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute: Haemolytic reactions can present within hours to days of drug administration.
  • Subacute: Bone marrow suppression due to medications (e.g., chemotherapy) typically presents within weeks of starting the drug.
  • Chronic: Interference with vitamin metabolism (e.g., methotrexate) often results in anaemia over several months of therapy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common in anaemia due to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. Reduced haemoglobin levels mean that tissues receive less oxygen, leading to generalised weakness and tiredness.
  • Pallor - Caused by reduced red blood cell count, particularly noticeable in the conjunctiva, due to decreased blood perfusion.
  • Dyspnoea on exertion - Due to decreased oxygen delivery to tissues, patients often experience shortness of breath during physical activity.
  • Dizziness - Caused by reduced cerebral oxygenation secondary to decreased haemoglobin levels.
  • Jaundice (in haemolytic anaemia) - Occurs when red blood cells are destroyed, releasing haemoglobin which is then converted into bilirubin, leading to yellowing of the skin and eyes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Visible in the skin and mucous membranes due to decreased haemoglobin.
  • Tachycardia - The body compensates for anaemia by increasing heart rate to maintain oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Systolic ejection murmur - Common in severe anaemia due to hyperdynamic circulation, as the heart pumps harder to meet oxygen demand.
  • Jaundice - Indicates haemolysis, as discussed, resulting from excess bilirubin in the blood.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe jaundice - Suggests massive haemolysis and could indicate a life-threatening haemolytic crisis, often requiring immediate cessation of the offending drug and possible blood transfusion.
  • Haemoglobin <7 g/dL - Severe anaemia, which may necessitate urgent transfusion and could lead to cardiac failure or ischaemia.
  • New or worsening chest pain - Suggests reduced oxygen supply to the myocardium, indicating possible acute coronary syndrome in anaemic patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Some patients, particularly those with chronic mild anaemia, may not exhibit noticeable symptoms, especially if they are elderly or have adapted physiologically to the anaemia over time.
  • Mild cognitive impairment: Older adults may present with confusion or lethargy as their primary symptom due to decreased cerebral oxygenation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Low haemoglobin (<120 g/L for women, <130 g/L for men) is diagnostic of anaemia. Reticulocyte count may help determine if there is bone marrow suppression or haemolysis.
  • Peripheral blood smear: Shows signs of haemolysis such as spherocytes, schistocytes, or megaloblastic changes in folate/vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Serum bilirubin: Elevated in haemolytic anaemia due to increased breakdown of haemoglobin.
  • Direct Coombs test: Positive in immune-mediated haemolysis, where antibodies are attached to red blood cells.
  • Bone marrow biopsy (if indicated): Hypocellularity indicates bone marrow suppression, while hyperplasia suggests a compensatory response to haemolysis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with progressive fatigue and pallor over the last 4 weeks after starting methotrexate for rheumatoid arthritis. Full blood count reveals a haemoglobin level of 95 g/L and macrocytosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic anaemia: Differentiated by pancytopenia and hypocellular bone marrow without clear drug association.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Low serum ferritin and microcytosis, as opposed to drug-induced anaemia, which can show macrocytosis in cases of folate deficiency.
  • Pernicious anaemia: Similar macrocytic anaemia, but with anti-parietal cell or intrinsic factor antibodies, and often no medication history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Discontinue offending medication: Immediate cessation of the causative drug is the first step.
  • Folic acid supplementation: For cases involving folate deficiency (e.g., methotrexate-related), folic acid supplementation is recommended.
  • Corticosteroids: Used for immune-mediated haemolytic anaemia to reduce the immune response against red blood cells.
  • Blood transfusion: May be required for severe anaemia with haemoglobin <7 g/dL or symptomatic anaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Severe anaemia can precipitate heart failure, particularly in elderly or vulnerable patients, due to the heart’s increased workload to supply oxygen.
  • Infection risk: Medications like chemotherapy not only cause anaemia but can also lead to neutropenia, increasing infection susceptibility.
  • Death: If left untreated, severe anaemia, particularly haemolytic types, can be fatal due to multi-organ failure or cardiac complications.
