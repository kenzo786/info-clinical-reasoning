Hip or Pelvis Fracture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Hip or pelvis fractures are breaks in the osseous structures of the hip joint (femoral neck, intertrochanteric region) or the pelvic ring, often resulting from significant trauma or, in the case of osteoporotic patients, low-energy falls.
  • They are classified into intracapsular (femoral neck) and extracapsular (intertrochanteric or subtrochanteric) hip fractures, or into stable and unstable pelvic fractures depending on the structural integrity of the pelvis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Hip fractures are common, particularly in the elderly, with an annual incidence of approximately 65,000 in the UK.
  • Pelvic fractures are less common, accounting for around 3-8% of all adult fractures, with higher prevalence in major trauma cases.
  • Globally, hip fractures are projected to exceed 6 million cases per year by 2050, driven by an ageing population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Hip fractures often occur due to mechanical failure of the weakened trabecular bone in the proximal femur under low-impact forces.
  • Pelvic fractures may result from high-energy trauma causing disruption of the pelvic ring or acetabulum, compromising structural integrity and stability.
  • Osteoporosis, reduced muscle mass, and altered balance increase the risk of fractures in the elderly.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Immediate pain, immobility, and possible haemodynamic instability.
  • Subacute phase: Development of complications like deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or infections if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Impaired mobility, persistent pain, or non-union if improperly managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Pain in the hip or pelvis region - Resulting from bone injury and associated muscle spasms.
  • Inability to bear weight - Due to instability and severe pain exacerbated by loading forces.
  • Referred pain to the knee - From irritation of the obturator nerve or referred musculoskeletal pain pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Shortened and externally rotated leg - Commonly seen in displaced femoral neck fractures due to muscle action on the fragments.
  • Localised tenderness - Indicative of bone injury and periosteal irritation.
  • Bruising over the pelvis or groin - Suggests soft tissue injury or haematoma from vascular damage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability - Signifies potential arterial injury or pelvic haematoma.
  • Neurological deficits in the lower limbs - Suggests nerve root or plexus involvement.
  • Open fractures - Increased risk of infection and complications such as sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Occult fractures detectable only on MRI in patients presenting with hip pain without obvious radiological abnormalities.
  • Insidious onset of pain in pathological fractures from malignancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • X-rays (AP and lateral views) - Detect fractures and displacement; reveals loss of trabecular continuity.
  • MRI - Useful for occult fractures or soft tissue involvement.
  • CT scans - Detailed assessment of complex pelvic fractures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old female presenting with acute hip pain and inability to bear weight following a fall, found to have a shortened and externally rotated leg on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Hip dislocation - Often associated with high-energy trauma and lacks the typical shortening/external rotation.
  • Soft tissue injury - Localised pain but no bony abnormalities on imaging.
  • Pathological fractures - May be associated with underlying malignancy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Surgical intervention - Internal fixation or arthroplasty for hip fractures; pelvic stabilisation for unstable fractures.
  • Pain management - Analgesia tailored to the patient’s needs.
  • Physiotherapy - Early mobilisation to reduce the risk of complications like DVT.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hip or Pelvis Fracture"][/sc]
  • Avascular necrosis - Particularly in femoral neck fractures disrupting blood supply.
  • Deep vein thrombosis - Due to immobility and venous stasis.
  • Non-union or malunion - Leading to chronic pain and disability.
