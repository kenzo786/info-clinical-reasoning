Encephalitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis is an acute inflammation of the brain parenchyma, most commonly caused by viral infections, though it can also result from autoimmune reactions, bacterial infections, or post-infectious processes. It is characterised by altered mental status, seizures, and focal neurological deficits, often progressing rapidly without treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the incidence of encephalitis is around 5-7 cases per 100,000 people annually. Worldwide, viral encephalitis remains more common in areas where arboviruses and other viral infections are endemic. Common causative agents include herpes simplex virus (HSV), varicella-zoster virus (VZV), and enteroviruses.
  • It accounts for around 1 in 1,000 hospital admissions in the UK.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • The most common cause of encephalitis, viral infection, leads to direct viral invasion of brain tissue or a post-infectious autoimmune response. In the case of HSV encephalitis, the virus reactivates from latency, typically in the trigeminal ganglia, and spreads to the temporal and frontal lobes, causing necrosis. The inflammatory response results in cerebral oedema, neuronal damage, and disruption of normal brain function, leading to neurological symptoms.
  • In autoimmune or paraneoplastic encephalitis, immune-mediated destruction of neuronal cells or synaptic proteins leads to encephalitis. These processes may involve antibody formation against neuronal tissue, such as anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Prodromal phase: Often starts with flu-like symptoms, including fever, headache, and fatigue. This phase can last from a few days to a week.
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of neurological symptoms, including confusion, seizures, and focal neurological deficits. The acute phase usually occurs within days to a week.
  • Recovery phase: Recovery can take weeks to months, with potential for persistent neurological deficits, depending on the severity and rapidity of treatment initiation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - The inflammatory process in response to infection leads to pyrexia as part of the body’s immune response.
  • Headache - Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) from cerebral inflammation and oedema causes a diffuse headache.
  • Altered mental status - Ranges from mild confusion to coma, caused by diffuse cortical dysfunction or brain oedema.
  • Seizures - Neuronal damage and cortical irritation lead to increased excitability and risk of seizures.
  • Photophobia - Inflammation of the meninges (meningoencephalitis) can cause sensitivity to light due to irritation of cranial nerves and brain tissues.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Localised brain inflammation can result in weakness, sensory changes, or speech disturbances, depending on the area of the brain affected.
  • Fatigue - Generalised illness, increased metabolic demand, and systemic inflammation lead to profound fatigue in affected patients.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - A raised temperature is a common systemic sign of infection or inflammation.
  • Altered consciousness - Changes in alertness or responsiveness reflect brain dysfunction, typically seen in the acute phase.
  • Neck stiffness - Seen in meningoencephalitis, where meningeal irritation causes resistance to neck flexion.
  • Focal neurological signs - Hemiparesis, aphasia, or other deficits indicate specific brain region involvement, commonly in HSV encephalitis (temporal lobe signs).
  • Kernig’s sign/Brudzinski’s sign - These signs reflect meningeal irritation if encephalitis is accompanied by meningitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of symptoms - This may indicate severe cerebral oedema or widespread brain inflammation, increasing the risk of herniation or death.
  • Refractory seizures - Persistent seizures that are resistant to initial treatment may signify ongoing brain damage or necrosis.
  • Coma or deep altered mental state - A profound loss of consciousness is a critical marker of widespread brain dysfunction, often seen in severe encephalitis and may indicate impending cerebral herniation.
  • New focal neurological deficits - Sudden-onset focal deficits may indicate necrotic damage to specific areas of the brain, such as the temporal lobes in HSV encephalitis, and may worsen the prognosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Subacute encephalitis - Some cases, especially in autoimmune encephalitis, may develop more slowly, with vague cognitive or psychiatric symptoms that may be misdiagnosed as psychiatric illness initially.
  • Non-febrile encephalitis - Rare cases of encephalitis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals, may present without fever due to a blunted inflammatory response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • CSF analysis (Lumbar puncture) - CSF typically shows lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, and normal glucose in viral encephalitis. In bacterial encephalitis, neutrophils may predominate with low glucose.
  • CSF PCR for HSV/VZV - HSV DNA detected via PCR is the gold standard for diagnosing HSV encephalitis.
  • Brain MRI - MRI may show hyperintense lesions in the temporal lobes (especially in HSV encephalitis), reflecting inflammation or necrosis.
  • EEG - EEG can reveal generalised slowing or epileptiform discharges, especially in HSV encephalitis, where it is useful for detecting subclinical seizures.
  • Autoimmune panel (e.g., anti-NMDA receptor antibodies) - This may be performed when autoimmune encephalitis is suspected, showing the presence of specific neuronal antibodies.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 5-day history of fever, headache, confusion, and seizures. MRI shows temporal lobe hyperintensities, and CSF PCR is positive for HSV, confirming herpes simplex encephalitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis - Unlike encephalitis, meningitis primarily causes neck stiffness and photophobia, with less frequent altered mental status or seizures.
  • Brain abscess - Presents with similar symptoms but can be distinguished by focal signs, raised ICP, and ring-enhancing lesions on imaging.
  • Autoimmune encephalitis - Often slower in onset and may present with psychiatric symptoms, whereas viral encephalitis typically progresses more rapidly.
  • Sepsis with delirium - Systemic infection causing delirium without focal neurological deficits, fever may still be present but without specific CSF or MRI findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • IV aciclovir - High-dose aciclovir is the treatment of choice for suspected or confirmed HSV encephalitis. The earlier it is started, the better the prognosis.
  • Supportive care - Seizure management, temperature control, and intracranial pressure management are crucial aspects of supportive care in severe cases.
  • Corticosteroids - In cases of autoimmune encephalitis or significant cerebral oedema, steroids may be used to reduce inflammation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological deficits - Depending on the severity of brain damage, survivors of encephalitis may have lasting impairments, including memory deficits, speech difficulties, and personality changes.
  • Seizures - Some patients may develop chronic epilepsy following an acute episode of encephalitis.
  • Death - Mortality rates for untreated HSV encephalitis are as high as 70%. With appropriate treatment, mortality drops significantly, but can still reach up to 20%.
