Iliopsoas Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • An iliopsoas abscess is a collection of pus within the iliopsoas muscle compartment, resulting from bacterial infection. It can occur either as a primary infection, without an identifiable source, or secondary to an adjacent infection, such as from the gastrointestinal or urinary tract.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Rare, exact prevalence unknown, more common in immunocompromised individuals.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Estimated at <0.4% of hospitalised patients with non-specific back or abdominal pain; commonly occurs in those with concurrent infections or immunosuppression.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Generally rare, with higher incidence in regions with prevalent tuberculosis, as TB infection can lead to iliopsoas abscess.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • The iliopsoas muscle, situated near the spine and pelvis, is vulnerable to infection through haematogenous spread, especially in immunocompromised patients.
  • Primary Abscess: Often due to haematogenous spread from a distant source, especially Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Secondary Abscess: Arises from contiguous spread from adjacent structures, often the bowel or genitourinary tract, commonly involving Gram-negative bacilli such as Escherichia coli.
  • The abscess can grow within the confined compartment, causing pressure on nerves and surrounding structures, leading to pain and potential sepsis if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Rapid onset of symptoms, such as pain and fever, typically over days to weeks.
  • Subacute Phase: Gradual worsening of pain and systemic symptoms over weeks, especially in cases secondary to TB or other chronic infections.
  • Chronic Phase: Less common, marked by intermittent symptoms, often in immunocompromised or TB-endemic patients.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Fever: Due to systemic infection and inflammatory response.
  • Flank or Lower Abdominal Pain: Caused by irritation of the iliopsoas muscle and surrounding tissue.
  • Back Pain: Commonly felt due to anatomical proximity to the lumbar spine.
  • Hip Pain on Movement: Results from inflammation of the iliopsoas muscle, causing pain during hip flexion.
  • Malaise and Weight Loss: Chronic inflammation and infection can lead to systemic symptoms and cachexia, especially in subacute cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Fever: Due to systemic infection and inflammatory response.
  • Pain on Hip Flexion (Psoas Sign): Stretching the inflamed iliopsoas muscle causes pain, indicating irritation.
  • Abdominal Tenderness (often in the lower abdomen): Localised tenderness over the iliopsoas muscle is common.
  • Limp or Antalgic Gait: Patients may favour the opposite leg due to pain with weight-bearing on the affected side.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Rapid Deterioration in Vital Signs (Hypotension, Tachycardia): May indicate sepsis, necessitating urgent management to prevent septic shock.
  • Neurological Deficits (e.g., Lower Limb Weakness): Could suggest abscess extension causing compression of nerves in the lumbar plexus.
  • Severe Unrelenting Pain: May indicate expanding abscess with risk of rupture or compression of vital structures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • No Fever in Immunocompromised Patients: Immunosuppressed individuals may not mount a febrile response, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Isolated Hip Pain in Older Adults: Can mimic hip arthritis, leading to a misdiagnosis if not thoroughly assessed.
  • Insidious Back Pain Without Systemic Symptoms: Chronic cases, particularly in tuberculosis-endemic areas, can present with non-specific back pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • FBC (Full Blood Count): Raised white cell count and neutrophilia are common, indicating bacterial infection.
  • Blood Cultures: May identify the causative organism, especially in haematogenous spread.
  • CRP/ESR: Elevated inflammatory markers suggest active infection.
  • CT Abdomen and Pelvis: Imaging of choice; shows an abscess in the iliopsoas compartment.
  • Ultrasound: May detect abscess, though less sensitive than CT, used when CT is contraindicated.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old man presents with fever, right-sided lower back pain, and difficulty walking for one week, with tenderness on abdominal examination and pain on hip flexion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis: Right lower quadrant pain and fever, but lacks pain on hip flexion and usually presents with localised tenderness over McBurney's point.
  • Septic Arthritis of the Hip: Hip pain with fever, but usually lacks abdominal symptoms and psoas sign.
  • Diverticulitis: Common in the left lower quadrant and typically associated with gastrointestinal symptoms rather than hip pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Empirical Antibiotics: IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., co-amoxiclav or ceftriaxone with metronidazole) tailored based on culture results.
  • Abscess Drainage: CT-guided percutaneous drainage is preferred; surgical drainage is considered if percutaneous approach fails.
  • Supportive Care: Fluids, analgesics, and monitoring for sepsis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Iliopsoas Abscess"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and Septic Shock: Due to systemic infection, can be life-threatening if untreated.
  • Extension of Infection: Spread to retroperitoneal structures, resulting in complex infections and longer recovery.
  • Chronic Pain or Weakness: Residual inflammation or scarring of the muscle may cause prolonged symptoms.
