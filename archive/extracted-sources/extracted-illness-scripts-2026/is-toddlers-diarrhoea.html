Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Toddler’s diarrhoea, also known as chronic nonspecific diarrhoea of childhood, is a benign and self-limiting condition characterised by frequent loose stools in otherwise healthy children. It typically occurs in children aged 1-5 years and is unrelated to infection, malabsorption, or any underlying organic disease. Stool consistency can vary from watery to mushy, often with undigested food particles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated at 5-10% in children under 5 years.
  • UK hospital prevalence is low, as this condition rarely requires hospitalisation.
  • Worldwide, it is one of the most common causes of chronic diarrhoea in young children, with similar prevalence patterns in developed countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology is not fully understood. It is believed to result from a combination of rapid gut transit time, immaturity of the digestive system, and high fluid intake, particularly fruit juices. This leads to incomplete absorption of carbohydrates, causing osmotic diarrhoea. The presence of undigested food particles in the stool is due to the fast transit time, which limits proper digestion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Toddler’s diarrhoea typically begins between 6 months and 2 years of age. It may persist for several months or years, with frequent episodes of diarrhoea. The condition usually resolves spontaneously by 4-5 years of age without treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Frequent loose stools (3-6 per day) - The hallmark symptom due to increased gut motility and incomplete absorption of fluid and nutrients.
  • Presence of undigested food in stools - Occurs due to the rapid transit time through the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Normal growth and weight gain - Unlike pathological causes of diarrhoea, children with toddler's diarrhoea continue to grow normally.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Normal physical examination - The child usually appears well, with no signs of dehydration or malnutrition, as growth is unaffected.
  • Soft or mushy stools in the nappy - Often described as having undigested food particles.
  • Absence of abdominal tenderness or distension - There is no inflammation or mechanical cause for the diarrhoea.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Failure to thrive - This may suggest a more serious condition such as coeliac disease, cow’s milk protein allergy, or malabsorption syndromes.
  • Bloody stools - Indicates possible infectious or inflammatory causes like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Vomiting, persistent fever, or dehydration - These signs are suggestive of acute infections or other gastrointestinal disorders.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Prolonged course beyond age 5 - If diarrhoea persists beyond the usual self-limiting time frame, alternative diagnoses such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or IBD should be considered.
  • Intermittent constipation and diarrhoea - May be a sign of functional gastrointestinal disorders rather than simple toddler’s diarrhoea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Stool examination - Microscopy may reveal undigested food particles. Stool cultures are typically negative.
  • Coeliac screen - If there is suspicion of failure to thrive or other red flags, checking for coeliac disease with anti-tTG antibodies is important.
  • Full blood count, electrolytes - Normal in toddler’s diarrhoea but performed to exclude anaemia or dehydration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • A 2-year-old boy presents with a 6-month history of 4-5 loose stools daily, containing undigested food particles. He is otherwise well and thriving, with normal growth and development. No red flag features are present.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease - Typically associated with failure to thrive, irritability, and sometimes steatorrhoea. Diagnosed with positive coeliac serology.
  • Cow’s milk protein allergy - Presents with vomiting, failure to thrive, and often blood in stools. Resolution with elimination of cow’s milk products.
  • Infectious diarrhoea - Acute onset with fever, vomiting, and dehydration. Stool cultures often positive for pathogens.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - May present with alternating diarrhoea and constipation, more common in older children and adults.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Reassurance - The primary treatment is reassurance of the parents, as the condition is benign and self-limiting.
  • Dietary advice - Limiting fruit juices and other high-sugar fluids, increasing fat and fibre intake may help slow gut transit time.
  • Monitoring - Ensure the child is growing and developing normally, and watch for any emergence of red flags.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Toddler’s Diarrhoea (Paediatrics)"][/sc]
  • Toddler’s diarrhoea rarely leads to complications. If not recognised, there may be unnecessary investigations or treatment. The prognosis is excellent, with most cases resolving by school age.
