Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Transient Global Amnesia (TGA) is a sudden, temporary episode of profound memory loss, specifically affecting the ability to form new memories (anterograde amnesia), while sparing other neurological functions. Typically lasting less than 24 hours, TGA is characterised by repetitive questioning, disorientation in time, and preserved identity knowledge.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is low, as TGA is uncommon and generally presents in hospital settings.
  • Hospital-based incidence estimates are approximately 5–10 cases per 100,000 people per year.
  • TGA is most frequently observed in people aged 50–70, and the condition is rare in those under 40 or over 80.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of TGA remains uncertain, but leading theories suggest transient disruption in hippocampal or mesial temporal lobe function. Potential triggers include vascular factors (venous congestion), stress, or physical exertion, which may cause brief ischaemia, neuronal dysfunction, or migrainous activity in these memory-related regions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Acute, with memory impairment typically lasting 1–8 hours, though symptoms may persist up to 24 hours in some cases.
  • Resolution: Symptoms resolve spontaneously without intervention, and the patient’s memory function gradually returns to normal.
  • Post-episode: Amnesia for the duration of the TGA episode often persists, with no recollection of the amnesic period after recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Anterograde amnesia - Inability to retain new information or memories during the episode. This is due to transient hippocampal dysfunction affecting short-term memory encoding.
  • Repetitive questioning - Patients may ask the same questions repeatedly, reflecting the inability to retain new information.
  • Disorientation in time - Often aware of personal identity but not of the current time or recent events, indicating a selective impairment in episodic memory.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Alertness and orientation to self - Patients remain alert and recognise familiar people and their own identity, indicating preserved procedural and semantic memory.
  • Absence of focal neurological deficits - TGA presents without motor, sensory, or language impairments, distinguishing it from stroke or seizure activity.
  • Normal physical examination - Apart from amnesia, the neurological and systemic examination is typically unremarkable.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Prolonged amnesia (>24 hours) - Suggests alternative diagnoses such as stroke, encephalitis, or seizure disorder.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Presence of motor weakness, visual field defects, or speech impairments suggests a structural brain lesion rather than TGA.
  • Recurrent TGA episodes - While rare, repeated episodes may indicate underlying epilepsy or other neurological conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Presentation in younger patients - TGA is rare in individuals under 40, and such cases may require more thorough evaluation to rule out alternative causes.
  • Brief or recurrent episodes - Could indicate underlying epilepsy or other neurological disorders; shorter episodes are atypical for TGA and should prompt consideration of other diagnoses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Brain MRI - Often shows transient abnormalities in the hippocampus, such as punctate hyperintensities on diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) in the acute phase.
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG) - Typically normal in TGA, ruling out seizure activity as a cause for the memory loss.
  • Laboratory tests - Blood tests are generally unremarkable but may be conducted to exclude metabolic causes if symptoms are atypical.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman presents with sudden onset of memory loss, unable to recall events or retain new information, and repeatedly asks questions about the current date and time. Alert, oriented to self, and with no other neurological deficits. Symptoms began 2 hours ago and are improving gradually.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Epileptic amnesia - Characterised by brief, recurrent episodes of amnesia, often with abnormal EEG findings, unlike TGA's single, prolonged episode.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - May mimic TGA, but TIA typically involves focal neurological symptoms, absent in TGA.
  • Psychogenic amnesia - Often has a gradual onset and may occur in younger individuals, with memory impairment focused on personal information.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Reassurance - As TGA resolves spontaneously, reassurance is key to alleviate patient and family concerns.
  • Follow-up - Arrange follow-up to ensure complete resolution of symptoms and rule out recurrent events or alternate diagnoses if the presentation deviates from typical TGA.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)"][/sc]
  • Residual memory gaps - Patients often have retrograde amnesia for the episode, which may persist but is typically well-tolerated.
  • Psychological impact - Anxiety related to recurrence or underlying pathology may occur, especially if the episode was distressing for the patient.
