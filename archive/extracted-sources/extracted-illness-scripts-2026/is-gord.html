Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease
GORD Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) is a chronic condition where stomach acid and contents reflux back into the oesophagus, causing symptoms such as heartburn and regurgitation. It is caused by the dysfunction of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS) and leads to oesophageal irritation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Obesity, smoking, alcohol consumption, pregnancy, hiatal hernia, delayed gastric emptying, and a diet high in fatty or acidic foods increase the risk of developing GORD. Certain medications, such as calcium channel blockers, nitrates, and NSAIDs, can also contribute to reflux.
  • Prevention: Lifestyle modifications, including maintaining a healthy weight, avoiding large or late meals, reducing alcohol and caffeine intake, and elevating the head of the bed, can help prevent GORD symptoms. Smoking cessation and dietary adjustments to avoid trigger foods also play a key role in prevention.
GORD Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • GORD develops due to dysfunction of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS), which normally prevents backflow of gastric contents into the oesophagus. When the LOS becomes weak or relaxes inappropriately, acid and digestive enzymes like pepsin can irritate the oesophageal lining.
  • Factors like hiatal hernia, increased intra-abdominal pressure (due to obesity, pregnancy), or delayed gastric emptying contribute to the reflux of stomach contents.
  • Repeated acid exposure causes oesophageal inflammation (oesophagitis), and over time, may lead to complications such as Barrett's oesophagus or oesophageal stricture formation.
GORD Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Transient reflux episodes causing occasional heartburn, often triggered by dietary or lifestyle factors. Symptoms are usually self-limiting at this stage.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent reflux symptoms lasting weeks to months, with potential development of complications like oesophagitis, Barrett’s oesophagus, or strictures.
  • Progression: If untreated, GORD can become chronic and lead to long-term oesophageal damage or increased risk of oesophageal adenocarcinoma in some patients.
GORD Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Heartburn - A burning sensation behind the sternum, commonly occurring after meals or when lying down. This is caused by irritation of the oesophageal lining by gastric acid.
  • Regurgitation - The sensation of stomach contents rising into the throat or mouth, often associated with a sour or bitter taste. This occurs due to the failure of the LOS to prevent backflow.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing, often resulting from oesophageal inflammation, strictures, or motility disorders caused by chronic acid exposure.
  • Chest Pain - Sometimes described as burning or pressure-like, often mistaken for cardiac pain, resulting from acid irritation of the oesophagus.
  • Chronic Cough - Reflux can irritate the respiratory tract or trigger aspiration, leading to a persistent cough.
  • Laryngitis - Reflux into the larynx can cause hoarseness or throat discomfort due to acid damage to the laryngeal tissues.
GORD Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Oesophagitis - Endoscopic findings of oesophageal inflammation, commonly caused by prolonged acid exposure to the oesophagus.
  • Strictures - Narrowing of the oesophagus, seen on endoscopy, secondary to chronic acid exposure and oesophagitis, leading to fibrosis and scar formation.
  • Barrett's Oesophagus - Metaplastic changes in the oesophageal epithelium (normal squamous cells replaced by columnar cells), detected on biopsy, a precursor to oesophageal adenocarcinoma.
GORD Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May indicate malignancy (e.g., oesophageal or gastric cancer) or severe oesophagitis.
  • Haematemesis or melaena - Suggests upper gastrointestinal bleeding, which may be caused by erosive oesophagitis, ulcers, or malignancy.
  • Progressive dysphagia - Typically indicates a more advanced oesophageal stricture or malignancy.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Can be due to chronic blood loss from erosive oesophagitis, ulcers, or malignancy.
GORD Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough - Can be the only manifestation of GORD, particularly in patients without typical heartburn or regurgitation.
  • Laryngitis - Persistent hoarseness or throat clearing without obvious gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Asthma exacerbations - Reflux-induced bronchospasm or micro-aspiration can trigger asthma symptoms, especially in nocturnal or supine reflux.
GORD Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Upper GI endoscopy - Used to visualise oesophageal inflammation, strictures, or Barrett's oesophagus. Findings can include oesophagitis, ulcers, or metaplasia in Barrett’s oesophagus.
  • 24-hour pH monitoring - Assesses acid exposure in the oesophagus, confirming acid reflux episodes. A pH < 4 more than 4% of the time is indicative of GORD.
  • Barium swallow - Useful for visualising strictures or anatomical abnormalities such as hiatal hernia. Typically shows reflux of barium from the stomach into the oesophagus.
GORD Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of heartburn, regurgitation, and occasional dysphagia, particularly after large meals or when lying down. Symptoms are partially relieved with antacids but have been progressively worsening.
GORD Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Presents with epigastric pain, often relieved by food, and does not typically involve regurgitation.
  • Cardiac chest pain (angina/MI) - Chest pain may be exertional, not related to meals, and may radiate to the jaw or arm, unlike the retrosternal burning of GORD.
  • Achalasia - Dysphagia to both solids and liquids from the onset, often with regurgitation of undigested food, without heartburn.
  • Eosinophilic oesophagitis - Dysphagia, often in younger patients with a history of atopy, characterised by eosinophilic infiltration on biopsy.
GORD Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications - Weight loss, elevating the head of the bed, avoiding trigger foods (e.g., spicy foods, caffeine), and not eating close to bedtime.
  • Pharmacological treatment - Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) such as omeprazole or lansoprazole are the mainstay of treatment. H2 receptor antagonists (e.g., ranitidine) may also be used.
  • Surgical intervention - In refractory cases, anti-reflux surgery (e.g., Nissen fundoplication) may be considered, particularly in patients with anatomical abnormalities like a hiatal hernia.
GORD Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease"][/sc]
  • Barrett's oesophagus - A precancerous condition resulting from chronic acid exposure and leading to an increased risk of oesophageal adenocarcinoma.
  • Oesophageal strictures - Result from chronic inflammation and fibrosis, causing progressive dysphagia and the need for endoscopic dilatation.
  • Oesophageal adenocarcinoma - A rare but serious complication arising from Barrett’s oesophagus, with poor prognosis if diagnosed late.
