Mild Acne in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Mild acne (acne vulgaris) is an extremely common chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting the pilosebaceous follicles. In primary care, patients typically present with a combination of non-inflammatory lesions (open and closed comedones, i.e., blackheads and whiteheads) and a small number of inflammatory lesions (papules and pustules). There are no nodules or cysts in mild acne. The condition most frequently affects areas with a high density of sebaceous follicles, such as the face (particularly the T-zone), neck, upper chest, back, and shoulders.

The onset is most common during puberty, coinciding with hormonal changes that increase sebum production, but it can persist into or even start in adulthood. While often considered a cosmetic issue, it can cause significant psychological distress and impact quality of life. The clinical course can fluctuate, and while many cases resolve by the early twenties, it can be a persistent condition requiring long-term management. The primary care clinician's role is to confirm the diagnosis, assess the severity and psychological impact, and initiate appropriate first-line therapy.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While mild acne is managed in primary care, certain features warrant urgent review or dermatology referral to exclude more serious conditions or to manage severe disease variants.

  • Severe or nodulocystic acne: The presence of numerous, large, painful, deep nodules or cysts, extensive scarring, or failure to respond to multiple courses of appropriate therapy (including oral antibiotics and combined topicals) requires specialist management.
  • Acne fulminans: A rare, severe form of acne presenting with an abrupt onset of inflammatory and ulcerative nodules, accompanied by systemic symptoms such as fever, joint pain (arthralgia), and weight loss. This requires immediate hospital admission and specialist care.
  • Signs of hyperandrogenism: In female patients, the presence of acne alongside features like hirsutism (excessive male-pattern hair growth), significant alopecia, deepening of the voice, or clitoromegaly suggests an underlying endocrine disorder such as Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) or, rarely, an androgen-secreting tumour. This requires endocrinological investigation.
  • Significant psychological distress: Patients with symptoms of body dysmorphic disorder, social isolation, depression, or suicidal ideation related to their acne require urgent psychological or psychiatric assessment alongside dermatological management.
  • Atypical presentation: Sudden onset of monomorphic (all lesions look the same) inflammatory papules may suggest drug-induced acne (e.g., from corticosteroids, lithium, or anabolic steroids) or Pityrosporum folliculitis.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is key to determining the appropriate management plan and excluding differential diagnoses.

History Taking

  • Acne History: Ascertain onset, duration, and perceived triggers. Detail the types of spots (blackheads, white spots, red spots, pus-filled spots).
  • Previous Treatments: Document all previous over-the-counter and prescribed treatments, including the duration of use, effectiveness, and any side effects experienced. Poor adherence is a common reason for treatment failure.
  • Skincare Routine: Ask about current cleansers, moisturisers, and cosmetics. Harsh, oil-based, or comedogenic products can exacerbate acne.
  • Psychological Impact: Assess the effect of the acne on the patient's mood, self-esteem, and social functioning. This is crucial for gauging the overall severity and guiding management intensity.
  • Menstrual History (females): Enquire about regularity of periods and any premenstrual flare-ups, which are common. Ask about features of hyperandrogenism.

Clinical Examination

  • Lesion Type and Distribution: Examine the face, neck, chest, and back. Characterise the dominant lesions (comedonal, papulopustular). The presence of only comedones and a few papules/pustules confirms mild acne. Note the absence of nodules and cysts.
  • Signs of Scarring: Look for evidence of atrophic (ice-pick, boxcar) or hypertrophic/keloid scarring, and for post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (dark marks left after a lesion resolves). The presence of scarring may warrant a more aggressive treatment approach or earlier referral.
  • Signs of Hyperandrogenism: If the history is suggestive, look for signs such as hirsutism, male-pattern hair loss, or significant seborrhoea.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

It is important to distinguish acne vulgaris from other common facial dermatoses, as management differs significantly.

Common Differentials

  • Rosacea (papulopustular type): Often confused with acne. Key distinguishing features are the absence of comedones, a typical age of onset over 30, and the presence of background centro-facial erythema (redness), flushing, and telangiectasia.
  • Perioral/Periorificial Dermatitis: Characterised by small, erythematous papules and pustules clustered around the mouth, nose, or eyes. Crucially, it typically spares a narrow border around the vermilion of the lips. Comedones are absent.
  • Keratosis Pilaris: Presents as small, rough, keratotic follicular papules, typically on the upper arms, thighs, and sometimes the cheeks. It feels like sandpaper and is generally not inflamed.
  • Bacterial or Malassezia (Pityrosporum) Folliculitis: An inflammation of hair follicles. Presents as monomorphic (uniform) papules and pustules, often itchy, and can be spread across the chest, back or face. Bacterial folliculitis is often caused by Staphylococcus aureus.

Less Common Differentials to Consider

  • Drug-Induced Acne: Often has an abrupt onset of monomorphic inflammatory papules. Common culprits include systemic corticosteroids, anabolic steroids, lithium, certain anticonvulsants, and EGFR inhibitors.
  • Milia: These are small, hard, white, keratin-filled cysts just under the skin surface. They are not inflamed and do not have an opening like a comedone.
5. Management options in primary care

Management follows a stepwise approach, starting with education and first-line topical treatments. A 12-week trial is needed to assess efficacy.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Education: Explain that acne is a chronic condition and treatment takes time (8-12 weeks for visible improvement). Reassure them that it is not caused by poor hygiene.
  • Skincare: Advise using a gentle, non-alkaline cleanser twice daily. Patients should use non-comedogenic (non-pore-blocking) products. Over-washing or harsh scrubbing should be avoided as it can irritate the skin.
  • Avoid Picking/Squeezing: This can worsen inflammation and increase the risk of scarring and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation.
  • Diet: While the link is debated, some patients may find a link with high-glycaemic index foods or dairy. General advice on a healthy, balanced diet is appropriate.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO): Acts as an antimicrobial against C. acnes and is mildly comedolytic. It is a cornerstone of acne therapy and helps reduce antibiotic resistance when used in combination products. Can cause skin irritation and bleaching of clothes/bedding. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

NICE recommends a 12-week course of one of the following first-line treatments:

  • Topical Retinoids (e.g., Adapalene): Act by normalising follicular keratinisation, preventing the formation of microcomedones. They are highly effective for comedonal acne. Can cause skin dryness, redness, and photosensitivity. [Rx info]
  • Fixed Combination Topicals: These are often preferred for their synergistic effects and simplicity.
    • Adapalene + Benzoyl Peroxide: Combines a retinoid with BPO. Highly effective for both inflammatory and non-inflammatory lesions. [Rx info]
    • Topical Antibiotic + Benzoyl Peroxide (e.g., Clindamycin + BPO): Combines an anti-inflammatory antibiotic with BPO to prevent resistance. Topical antibiotic monotherapy is not recommended. [Rx info]
  • Azelaic Acid: Acts as a comedolytic and has antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties. It is a useful alternative for patients who cannot tolerate retinoids or BPO, or during pregnancy. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of acne in the UK is guided by the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline NG198 'Acne vulgaris: management'. The core principles are a structured, stepwise approach to treatment, patient education, and antimicrobial stewardship.

NICE recommends a 12-week trial of a first-line treatment for mild-to-moderate acne. These include topical adapalene combined with topical benzoyl peroxide, or topical azelaic acid. A fixed combination of topical clindamycin with benzoyl peroxide is another option. The choice depends on patient preference, lesion type, and tolerability.

A crucial point emphasised by NICE is the principle of antimicrobial stewardship. To minimise the risk of bacterial resistance, topical antibiotics (like clindamycin or erythromycin) should never be used as monotherapy. They should always be co-prescribed with benzoyl peroxide or a topical retinoid. Similarly, oral and topical antibiotics should not be used concurrently.

The guidelines advocate for regular review, typically at 12 weeks, to assess treatment efficacy and adherence. If a treatment is effective, it should be continued. If it is ineffective or not tolerated, the next step in the treatment pathway should be considered. The psychological impact of acne is also highlighted as a key consideration at all stages of management.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is essential for managing expectations, improving adherence, and achieving good clinical outcomes.

Counselling for Self-Care and All Treatments

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that acne is a chronic condition and that treatments control it rather than cure it. Inform them that it can take 8-12 weeks of consistent use before they see a significant improvement.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge the psychological and social impact of acne. Reassure them that it is a valid medical condition and that effective treatments are available.
  • Explain Treatment Rationale: Briefly explain how the treatment works. For combination products, explain the importance of using ingredients like benzoyl peroxide to prevent antibiotic resistance. This empowers the patient and improves adherence.
  • Application Technique: Advise applying a thin layer of topical treatment to the entire affected area of skin (e.g., the whole face), not just individual spots. This helps to treat existing lesions and prevent new ones from forming. Applications should be on clean, dry skin.

Counselling for Specific Treatments

  • Topical Retinoids/Benzoyl Peroxide: Warn about common initial side effects like redness, dryness, and stinging. Advise starting with application on alternate nights or for short-contact periods (e.g., wash off after an hour) to build tolerance. Stress the importance of using a non-comedogenic moisturiser and broad-spectrum sunscreen as these agents increase photosensitivity. Warn about BPO bleaching fabric.
  • Pregnancy and Contraception: When prescribing topical retinoids to females of childbearing potential, it is essential to confirm they are not pregnant and advise on the need for effective contraception.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting ensures patients know when and how to seek further help. A planned follow-up is a core part of the acne management pathway.

Clear verbal and written advice should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek advice if they experience any of the following:

  • No improvement after 12 weeks: If there has been no discernible benefit after three months of consistent use of a first-line therapy.
  • Severe skin irritation: If the skin becomes excessively red, sore, swollen, or cracked and is not manageable by reducing frequency or using moisturisers.
  • Worsening of acne: If the acne progresses to a more severe form, with the development of nodules, cysts, or scarring.
  • Significant psychological impact: If the acne is causing severe distress, low mood, or social withdrawal.
  • Signs of a systemic illness: In the rare event of developing fever, joint pains or feeling generally unwell in the context of rapidly worsening acne (suggesting acne fulminans).

A routine follow-up appointment should be arranged for 12 weeks after initiating a new treatment. This allows sufficient time to assess the full effect of the therapy. At this review, adherence, tolerability, and clinical response should be evaluated to decide whether to continue the current treatment or move to the next step in the management pathway.

9. Pathophysiology

Acne vulgaris is a multifactorial inflammatory disease of the pilosebaceous unit. Its development is understood to involve four key interconnected processes.

  1. Sebum Production: Sebaceous glands, particularly on the face and upper trunk, are sensitive to androgen hormones. During puberty, rising androgen levels cause these glands to enlarge and produce increased amounts of sebum (seborrhoea). This oily substance is a key substrate for bacterial growth and inflammation.
  2. Follicular Hyperkeratinisation: There is an abnormal proliferation and reduced desquamation of keratinocytes lining the upper part of the hair follicle. This leads to blockage of the follicular orifice, forming a plug known as a microcomedone. This is the primary precursor lesion of all forms of acne.
  3. Microbial Colonisation: The anaerobic, lipid-rich environment of the blocked follicle promotes the colonisation of the commensal bacterium Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes). C. acnes proliferates within the sebum.
  4. Inflammation and Immune Response: C. acnes stimulates an innate immune response by producing chemotactic factors and lipase enzymes that break down sebum triglycerides into pro-inflammatory free fatty acids. This triggers the influx of neutrophils and lymphocytes, leading to the development of inflammatory papules, pustules, and, in more severe cases, nodules and cysts.

The initial non-inflammatory lesions (comedones) can evolve into inflammatory lesions as the follicular wall ruptures, releasing keratin, lipids, and bacteria into the surrounding dermis and provoking a robust inflammatory reaction.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

Prescribers must be aware of key safety information for each treatment class.

Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO)

  • Side Effects: Common effects include skin dryness, erythema, peeling, and stinging, particularly at the start of treatment. These are usually manageable by using a lower concentration, reducing the frequency of application, and using a moisturiser.
  • Cautions: It can bleach hair, clothes, and bedding. Patients should be warned about this to avoid damage to fabrics.

Topical Retinoids (e.g., Adapalene, Tretinoin)

  • Contraindications: Absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy due to teratogenicity risk. Women of childbearing potential must have effective contraception in place. They should also be avoided during breastfeeding as a precaution.
  • Side Effects & Risks: Photosensitivity is a key side effect; patients must be counselled to use a high-factor, broad-spectrum sunscreen daily. Initial skin irritation (known as 'retinisation') with redness and peeling is common but usually subsides after a few weeks.

Topical Antibiotics (e.g., Clindamycin)

  • Resistance: The primary concern is the development of antimicrobial resistance. NICE guidance is explicit that topical antibiotics must NOT be used as monotherapy. They should always be used in a fixed combination product with BPO or alongside a separate topical BPO or retinoid.
  • Duration: Use should be reviewed at 12 weeks and stopped if there is no further improvement to limit long-term exposure.

Azelaic Acid

  • Side Effects: Generally very well tolerated. Can cause mild and transient skin irritation, stinging or itching. It may also cause hypopigmentation, which is more noticeable in patients with darker skin tones.
11. Management in special populations

Children & Adolescents

Acne is most prevalent in this group. Management follows standard NICE guidelines. It is particularly important to assess and address the psychological impact, as bullying and social anxiety are common. Adherence can be a challenge, so clear, simple instructions and managing expectations are vital. Pre-pubertal acne is rare and may warrant investigation for an underlying endocrine cause.

Older adults

The onset of acne in later life is less common and should prompt consideration of differentials like rosacea or drug-induced acne. Older skin can be thinner and more sensitive, so treatments like retinoids or BPO should be initiated cautiously, perhaps at a lower strength or frequency, with emphasis on moisturising to minimise irritation.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

This requires careful consideration of medication safety. Topical retinoids are absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy. First-line management is non-pharmacological advice. If medication is required, azelaic acid is a safe and effective option. High-strength benzoyl peroxide may also be used if the benefit outweighs the risk. Topical clindamycin or erythromycin can be considered under guidance. All decisions should be made in consultation with the patient, checking the latest BNF and MHRA guidance.

People with significant comorbidities

For most physical comorbidities, standard acne treatment is safe. However, in patients who are significantly immunosuppressed, bacterial folliculitis may be a more likely diagnosis. The most significant comorbidity is psychological distress. Patients with pre-existing depression, anxiety, or body dysmorphic disorder may have their condition worsened by acne, and a holistic management approach with mental health support is crucial.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Teenager with Comedonal Acne

  • Presentation: A 14-year-old boy presents with his mother. He has had "blackheads and some whiteheads" on his nose and forehead for about a year. He is starting to get a few red spots and feels self-conscious. He has tried various face washes with no improvement.
  • Assessment: Examination shows multiple open and closed comedones across the T-zone with approximately 5-6 small papules. There are no nodules or scars. His mood seems a little flat but he denies significant distress.
  • Diagnosis: Mild comedonal and papulopustular acne.
  • Management: The GP explains the cause of acne in simple terms. They discuss skincare and advise a trial of a first-line fixed combination topical gel containing adapalene and benzoyl peroxide. Counselling is given on starting with alternate-night application, using a pea-sized amount for the whole face, the risk of initial irritation and photosensitivity, and the need for a non-comedogenic moisturiser and sunscreen. The importance of consistency is stressed, explaining it will take 12 weeks to see the full effect.
  • Safety Netting & Follow-up: A follow-up review is booked for 12 weeks. He is advised to get in touch sooner if his skin becomes very sore or his mood worsens.

Case Study 2: The Young Woman with Persistent Acne

  • Presentation: A 25-year-old woman presents with persistent spots on her chin and jawline. She reports a premenstrual flare. She has been using an over-the-counter benzoyl peroxide 5% wash, which helps slightly but dries her skin. She is frustrated and it is affecting her confidence.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals a dozen inflammatory papules and a few pustules localised to the U-zone (jawline and chin). There are some comedones present. Her menstrual cycle is regular and she has no signs of hyperandrogenism.
  • Diagnosis: Mild persistent inflammatory acne.
  • Management: The GP acknowledges her frustration and validates her concerns. As she finds BPO drying, they discuss other first-line options. Azelaic acid 20% cream is chosen as a suitable alternative, known to be effective and generally well-tolerated. The GP counsels on its use twice daily and explains it can also help with any dark marks left by the spots (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation).
  • Safety Netting & Follow-up: She is told to expect results over 2-3 months and a review is scheduled for 12 weeks. She is advised to return if there is no improvement or if her skin becomes significantly irritated. They briefly discuss that if this is not effective, other options like combination topicals or the combined oral contraceptive pill could be considered at review.
