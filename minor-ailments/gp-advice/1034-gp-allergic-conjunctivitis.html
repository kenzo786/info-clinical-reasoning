Allergic Conjunctivitis in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Allergic conjunctivitis is a very common reason for consultation in primary care, particularly during spring and summer. It is a Type I hypersensitivity reaction of the conjunctiva to an allergen. Patients typically present with bilateral, acute onset of intense itching, which is the hallmark symptom. This is often accompanied by watery discharge, conjunctival redness (hyperaemia), and mild to moderate swelling of the conjunctiva (chemosis) and eyelids (oedema).

The condition is frequently associated with a personal or family history of atopy, such as allergic rhinitis (hay fever), asthma, or eczema. Symptoms can be seasonal, commonly triggered by pollen (from trees, grass, or weeds), or perennial, triggered by indoor allergens like house dust mites, animal dander, or moulds. Vision is not typically affected, though it may be momentarily blurry due to excessive tearing. The condition is not contagious. In most cases, it is a benign condition that responds well to simple management, although symptoms can be recurrent and significantly impact quality of life.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While allergic conjunctivitis is benign, it is crucial to identify red flags that suggest a more serious condition requiring urgent assessment by an ophthalmologist. An urgent referral to an eye casualty or ophthalmology service is warranted for:

  • Significant eye pain: Pain, as opposed to itching or grittiness, is not a typical feature of allergic conjunctivitis. Severe pain could indicate conditions like keratitis, scleritis, anterior uveitis, or acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Photophobia (light sensitivity): Marked sensitivity to light is a strong indicator of corneal or intraocular inflammation (e.g., keratitis or anterior uveitis) and is not typical of simple allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Marked reduction in visual acuity: Vision that is persistently blurred and does not clear with blinking is a significant red flag. This suggests a cause other than tears or mucus, such as corneal oedema or inflammation.
  • Corneal involvement: Any opacity, haziness, or ulceration seen on the cornea requires emergency referral. The use of fluorescein dye can reveal epithelial defects suggestive of keratitis.
  • Unilateral red eye: Simple allergic conjunctivitis is almost always bilateral. A persistent, unilateral red eye should raise suspicion for infective keratitis (especially in contact lens wearers), foreign body, or anterior uveitis.
  • Contact lens wearers: Any contact lens wearer with a red, painful eye must be urgently assessed to rule out microbial keratitis, which is a sight-threatening emergency.
  • History of trauma or recent eye surgery: A red eye in this context requires specialist evaluation to exclude complications like infection (endophthalmitis) or inflammation.
  • Abnormal pupil: A fixed, dilated, or irregular pupil is a major red flag pointing towards acute glaucoma or anterior uveitis.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is essential to confirm the diagnosis and exclude serious pathology.

History Taking

  • Symptom Profile: Establish the primary symptom – itching is key for allergy. Ask about watery discharge versus purulent discharge (suggests bacterial infection), and duration. Enquire about seasonality (pollen) versus perennial symptoms (dust mites, pets).
  • Associated Symptoms: Ask about other atopic symptoms like sneezing, nasal discharge (rhinitis), or wheeze (asthma).
  • Past Medical & Family History: Enquire about a personal or family history of atopy (eczema, asthma, hay fever).
  • Triggers: Ask about exposure to potential allergens like pollen, pets, dust, or new cosmetics/eye drops.
  • Contact Lens Use: This is a critical question. Ask about type of lenses, wearing schedule, and hygiene.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about severe pain, photophobia, changes in vision, and unilateral symptoms.

Clinical Examination

  • Visual Acuity: Measure using a Snellen chart. Should be normal; any reduction not cleared by blinking is a red flag.
  • General Inspection: Note any lid swelling (oedema). Look for bilateral, symmetrical signs.
  • Eye Examination: Inspect the conjunctiva for diffuse redness (hyperaemia) and swelling (chemosis). The discharge should be clear and watery. Evert the eyelids (if comfortable) to look for papillae (a bumpy appearance), particularly on the upper tarsal conjunctiva in more chronic cases. Check pupils for normal size, shape, and reaction to light.
  • Fluorescein Staining: Consider this if there is any suspicion of corneal involvement (e.g., severe symptoms, contact lens wearer, history of trauma). Any staining defect requires urgent referral.
  • Palpation: Check for pre-auricular lymph nodes; their presence is more suggestive of viral conjunctivitis.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The main task is to distinguish benign allergic conjunctivitis from other causes of red eye, particularly infective conjunctivitis and more serious, sight-threatening conditions.

Common Differentials

  • Viral Conjunctivitis: Often caused by adenovirus. Tends to be highly contagious, may be unilateral initially before spreading to the other eye. Discharge is watery, and a gritty or burning feeling is common. A key differentiator is often the presence of a recent URTI and palpable, tender pre-auricular lymph nodes.
  • Bacterial Conjunctivitis: Characterised by a purulent (pus-like), sticky yellow-green discharge that causes the eyelids to be stuck together, especially upon waking. Itching is less prominent than in allergic conjunctivitis. It is also contagious.
  • Dry Eye Syndrome (Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca): Can cause redness, grittiness, and a burning sensation. Symptoms often worsen throughout the day or with prolonged screen use. Itching is not a primary feature.
  • Blepharitis: Inflammation of the eyelid margins, leading to red, crusty, or flaky eyelids. It can cause secondary conjunctival irritation and redness.

Serious Differentials to Exclude (Red Flag Conditions)

  • Anterior Uveitis / Iritis: Presents with a painful, photophobic red eye, often with blurred vision. The redness may be most prominent around the cornea (ciliary flush), and the pupil may be small or irregular.
  • Infective Keratitis: A corneal infection, common in contact lens wearers. Marked by severe pain, photophobia, reduced vision, and potentially a visible opacity on the cornea. A sight-threatening emergency.
  • Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma: Presents with a severely painful, red eye, blurred vision (with haloes around lights), headache, nausea, and vomiting. The pupil is often fixed in a mid-dilated position. An ophthalmic emergency.
  • Foreign Body / Corneal Abrasion: Usually a clear history of injury. Leads to a painful, watery, and gritty-feeling eye. Typically unilateral.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is stepwise, starting with conservative measures and escalating to pharmacological treatments as needed. The approach should be tailored to symptom severity and patient preference.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Allergen Avoidance: The primary management step. Advise patients to stay indoors when pollen counts are high, keep windows closed, and wear wraparound sunglasses. For perennial allergies, advise on dust mite control measures or minimising contact with pets.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Cold compresses applied to the eyes can be soothing. Advise against rubbing the eyes, as this can worsen mast cell degranulation and inflammation.
  • Lubricants: Over-the-counter artificial tears can help to dilute allergens and flush them from the ocular surface. Refrigerating the drops can provide additional soothing.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Topical Mast Cell Stabilisers (e.g., Sodium Cromoglicate): These prevent mast cells from releasing histamine and other inflammatory mediators. They are best used prophylactically as they take several days to become fully effective. Suitable for all ages. [Rx info]
  • Topical Antihistamines (e.g., Antazoline with Xylometazoline): Provide rapid relief of itching by blocking H1 receptors. Often combined with a decongestant. Suitable for acute symptoms but not for long-term use. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Dual-Action Agents (e.g., Olopatadine, Ketotifen, Azelastine): These are highly effective first-line options for moderate-to-severe symptoms. They combine a potent antihistamine for rapid relief with a mast cell stabilising effect for longer-term control. [Rx info]
  • Oral Antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine, Fexofenadine): Useful when ocular symptoms are accompanied by significant allergic rhinitis or other systemic allergic symptoms. Non-sedating options are preferred. [Rx info]
  • Topical Corticosteroids: Highly effective for severe inflammation but should ONLY be initiated by an ophthalmologist due to the risk of serious side effects, including cataracts, glaucoma, and secondary infections. Their use in primary care is not recommended.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by sources such as the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) on Allergic Conjunctivitis and guidance from professional bodies like the College of Optometrists. The core principles are accurate diagnosis, exclusion of red flags, and a stepwise approach to management.

NICE CKS recommends a clinical diagnosis based on the hallmark symptom of bilateral itchy eyes, often with a history of atopy. The first step in management is always advising on allergen avoidance and non-pharmacological measures like cold compresses. For pharmacological treatment, a stepped approach is advised. First-line options include topical antihistamines or mast cell stabilisers. NICE suggests that dual-action agents (topical antihistamine and mast cell stabiliser) are also a suitable first-line choice. If symptoms are not controlled, treatment can be stepped up, for example by adding an oral antihistamine, particularly if nasal symptoms are also present.

The guidance strongly emphasises the importance of excluding red flags that would necessitate an urgent referral to ophthalmology. The use of topical corticosteroids is strictly reserved for specialist care due to the potential for significant adverse ocular effects. There is no role for antibiotics in the management of allergic conjunctivitis.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to empowering patients to manage this chronic, recurrent condition and ensuring they understand when to seek further help.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Condition: Clearly state that this is an allergy, not an infection, and it is not contagious. Use the analogy of "hay fever for the eyes."
  • Validate and Reassure: Acknowledge how frustrating and uncomfortable the itching can be, while reassuring that it is not sight-threatening.
  • Focus on Allergen Avoidance: Explain that medication helps symptoms, but avoiding the trigger is the most important part of management. Provide practical, specific tips (e.g., "Check the pollen forecast on your weather app").
  • Discourage Eye Rubbing: Emphasise that rubbing provides temporary relief but ultimately makes the inflammation worse by releasing more histamine. Suggest using a cold compress instead.

Counselling for Treatments

  • Explain the Role of Different Drops: For mast cell stabilisers (sodium cromoglicate), explain they are for prevention and need to be used regularly, even on good days, to be effective. For antihistamine or dual-action drops (olopatadine), explain they are for rapid symptom relief.
  • Demonstrate Correct Instillation: Show the patient how to correctly instil eye drops: wash hands, pull down the lower lid to create a pocket, and apply one drop without the bottle tip touching the eye.
  • Contact Lens Advice: Advise patients to avoid wearing soft contact lenses during a flare-up of symptoms and for 24 hours after finishing treatment. Explain that preservatives in many eye drops can accumulate in soft lenses. If drops are essential, preservative-free single-dose units are preferred.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients know when to re-consult if the condition deviates from its expected benign course. This advice should be clear and specific.

Advise the patient to seek an urgent review with their GP or an optometrist, or to attend an eye casualty department, if they develop any of the following red flag symptoms:

  • Worsening or significant eye pain (rather than just itching).
  • Increased sensitivity to light (photophobia).
  • Noticeably blurred or reduced vision that doesn't clear with blinking.
  • The redness becomes intense and localised in one eye.
  • A thick, pus-like discharge, suggesting a secondary bacterial infection.
  • Symptoms fail to improve within a week of starting appropriate treatment, or if they worsen significantly.

For routine cases, a follow-up is not usually required. However, if symptoms are severe or persistent despite initial treatment, a review may be appropriate to consider alternative therapies or to reinforce management strategies. For patients with very severe, persistent, or sight-affecting disease (vernal or atopic keratoconjunctivitis), referral to ophthalmology for specialist management should be considered.

9. Pathophysiology

Allergic conjunctivitis is a classic example of a Type I (immediate) hypersensitivity reaction. The process is initiated by the exposure of the conjunctival mucosa to a specific allergen (e.g., a pollen grain or house dust mite particle) in a genetically predisposed (atopic) individual.

On first exposure, the allergen is processed by antigen-presenting cells. This leads to the activation of T-helper 2 (Th2) cells, which in turn stimulate B-cells to produce allergen-specific Immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies. This is the sensitisation phase. These IgE antibodies then bind to the surface of mast cells, which are abundant in the conjunctival stroma.

Upon subsequent exposure, the allergen cross-links the IgE antibodies on the surface of these sensitised mast cells. This triggers mast cell degranulation, causing the explosive release of pre-formed inflammatory mediators, most notably histamine. Histamine binds to H1 receptors on conjunctival nerve endings, causing the characteristic intense itching, and to H1 receptors on blood vessels, causing vasodilation (redness) and increased vascular permeability (oedema/chemosis). This accounts for the immediate phase of the reaction. Hours later, a late-phase reaction can occur, driven by newly synthesised mediators (prostaglandins, leukotrienes) and the infiltration of inflammatory cells like eosinophils, perpetuating the inflammatory response.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When selecting a treatment, it is important to consider potential contraindications, side effects, and specific patient factors like contact lens wear.

Topical Mast Cell Stabilisers & Antihistamines

  • Side Effects: The most common side effect is transient local stinging or burning upon instillation. Blurred vision may occur briefly.
  • Contact Lenses: Many multi-dose bottles contain the preservative benzalkonium chloride, which can accumulate in soft contact lenses and cause corneal toxicity. Patients should be advised to remove soft lenses before instilling drops and wait at least 15 minutes before reinserting. Preservative-free single-dose vials (e.g., for ketotifen) are a better option for frequent users or contact lens wearers.
  • Age: Some preparations have specific age licensing. For example, olopatadine and ketotifen are licensed for children aged 3 and over, while sodium cromoglicate can be used in younger children.

Oral Antihistamines

  • Side Effects: While modern second-generation antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine, loratadine) are 'non-sedating', a small number of people may still experience drowsiness. Patients should be cautious about driving or operating machinery until they know how the medicine affects them. First-generation antihistamines (e.g., chlorphenamine) are highly sedating.
  • Interactions: Sedative effects can be potentiated by alcohol or other CNS depressant medications.

Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline)

  • Risks: These agents cause vasoconstriction. Prolonged use (more than 5-7 days) can lead to rebound hyperaemia (rhinitis or conjunctivitis medicamentosa), where the redness worsens upon stopping the drug. They should be used for short periods only. They are contraindicated in patients with narrow-angle glaucoma.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Allergic conjunctivitis is common in children, often alongside hay fever. Management prioritises non-pharmacological measures. Lubricating eye drops and cold compresses are safe first steps. For medication, topical sodium cromoglicate is a good first-line option and is licensed for all ages. Dual-action agents like olopatadine or ketotifen are licensed from age 3 upwards and are very effective. Oral non-sedating antihistamines can also be used. It is essential to ensure the diagnosis is correct and not confusing it with infectious conjunctivitis, which is also very common in this age group.

Older adults

The diagnosis remains clinical. It is important to consider co-morbidities. Dry eye syndrome is more prevalent in older adults and can present with similar symptoms of irritation and redness; sometimes both conditions coexist. A thorough medication history is important, as some systemic drugs can cause dry eyes. When prescribing oral antihistamines, non-sedating options are strongly preferred to minimise the risk of confusion, sedation, and falls.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Non-pharmacological advice is first-line. If medication is required, topical sodium cromoglicate or lodoxamide are considered safe for use during pregnancy and breastfeeding as systemic absorption is minimal. Oral antihistamines such as loratadine and cetirizine are generally considered low-risk but should be used if the benefits outweigh the risks, after discussion with the patient. Topical dual-action agents like olopatadine should be used with caution and only if necessary. Always consult the latest BNF or specialist resources for up-to-date advice.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with glaucoma, it is vital to avoid vasoconstrictor eye drops (e.g., xylometazoline) if they have, or are at risk of, angle-closure glaucoma. For patients who are contact lens wearers, they should be strongly advised to cease wear during any acute red eye episode until a clear diagnosis is made and symptoms have resolved, to mitigate the high risk of microbial keratitis. If eye drops are needed, preservative-free formulations are strongly recommended.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: Seasonal Allergic Conjunctivitis

  • Presentation: A 22-year-old university student presents in May with a three-day history of intensely itchy, watery, and red eyes, bilaterally. He also reports sneezing and a runny nose. He has a history of eczema as a child.
  • Assessment: Visual acuity is normal. There is bilateral conjunctival hyperaemia and mild lid oedema. The discharge is clear and watery. Pupils are equal and reactive. There is no photophobia or pain.
  • Diagnosis: Seasonal allergic conjunctivitis, with associated allergic rhinitis.
  • Management: The GP explains the allergic basis of his symptoms, linking it to the pollen season. He is advised on allergen avoidance strategies (e.g., wearing sunglasses, keeping windows shut). He is recommended to start an over-the-counter dual-action antihistamine/mast cell stabiliser eye drop (e.g. olopatadine, available via Pharmacy First schemes or prescription) for rapid and sustained relief, and a non-sedating oral antihistamine. Safety netting advice is given to return if he develops pain, light sensitivity, or blurred vision.

Case Study 2: A Potential Red Flag

  • Presentation: A 35-year-old office worker who wears monthly soft contact lenses presents with a 24-hour history of a red, uncomfortable right eye. She denies significant itching but reports a gritty, foreign-body sensation and some light sensitivity.
  • Assessment: Her vision in the right eye is slightly blurred. The right eye shows marked, diffuse redness. She is reluctant to open the eye in the consulting room light (photophobia). Her left eye is completely white and quiet.
  • Diagnosis & Action: This presentation has multiple red flags: unilateral symptoms, pain/grittiness rather than itch, photophobia, blurred vision, and contact lens wear. While it could be giant papillary conjunctivitis, the priority is to exclude sight-threatening microbial keratitis. The GP advises her to remove her contact lens immediately and refers her for an urgent, same-day assessment at the local eye casualty or with an accredited optometrist for full assessment, including slit-lamp examination and fluorescein staining.
