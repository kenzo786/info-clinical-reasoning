Vaginal Thrush in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Vaginal thrush, or vulvovaginal candidiasis, is a very common reason for consultation in primary care. It is caused by an overgrowth of yeast, most commonly Candida albicans, which is a normal commensal of the vaginal flora. It is not considered a sexually transmitted infection (STI), although sexual activity can sometimes trigger it.

Patients typically present with vulval itching (pruritus) as the predominant symptom. This is often accompanied by vulval soreness or irritation. A characteristic, non-offensive, thick, white, 'cottage cheese-like' vaginal discharge is common, but the discharge can also be watery or absent. Other symptoms can include superficial dyspareunia (pain during intercourse) and external dysuria (stinging on passing urine due to inflamed skin).

The condition is usually uncomplicated and responds well to short-course antifungal treatment. However, some individuals experience recurrent or persistent infections which require a more structured management approach. Common predisposing factors include recent antibiotic use, uncontrolled diabetes, pregnancy, and immunosuppression.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most vaginal thrush is straightforward to manage in primary care, it is crucial to identify features suggesting an alternative or more serious diagnosis, or that indicate complicated infection requiring a different approach.

  • Recurrent episodes: Four or more culture-verified episodes in 12 months. This is not an emergency but warrants a structured assessment, potential investigation (e.g., high vaginal swab), and a planned management regimen rather than ad-hoc treatment.
  • Treatment failure: Persistent symptoms despite completing a full course of appropriate first-line therapy. This may suggest an incorrect diagnosis, a resistant organism (e.g., non-albicans species), or poor adherence.
  • Signs of systemic illness or deep-seated infection: Fever, chills, lower abdominal pain, or deep dyspareunia. These are not features of uncomplicated thrush and may suggest pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) or another pelvic pathology.
  • Associated STI symptoms: Features such as a purulent or malodourous discharge, abnormal bleeding, or pelvic pain raise the possibility of a concurrent STI (e.g., chlamydia, gonorrhoea, trichomoniasis) or bacterial vaginosis.
  • Vulval skin changes: Features like ulceration, blisters, significant skin thinning (atrophy), or white plaques (leukoplakia) are red flags for other vulval dermatoses (e.g., lichen sclerosus, herpes simplex) or, rarely, vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN).
  • Post-menopausal or pre-pubertal presentation: Thrush is less common in these groups due to lower oestrogen levels. An alternative diagnosis (e.g., atrophic vaginitis, lichen sclerosus, threadworms in children) should be carefully considered. Post-menopausal bleeding requires urgent cancer pathway referral.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is key to confirming the diagnosis and excluding alternatives. Many cases can be managed based on a typical history alone.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Clarify the main symptom (e.g., itch vs. pain vs. discharge). Ask about the nature of any discharge (colour, consistency, odour) and associated symptoms like soreness, dyspareunia, or dysuria.
  • Episode Pattern: Is this a first episode, an occasional recurrence, or part of a pattern of frequent (≥4 per year) episodes? Ask about response to previous treatments.
  • Predisposing Factors: Enquire about recent antibiotic use, known diabetes (and glycaemic control), pregnancy status, and any conditions or medications causing immunosuppression.
  • Sexual and Contraceptive History: Ask about new sexual partners and whether the regular partner has any symptoms (e.g., balanitis). This helps to assess STI risk.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about abdominal pain, fever, abnormal bleeding, and concerning vulval skin changes.

Clinical Examination

  • When to examine: Examination is essential if the history is atypical, if there is treatment failure, or if red flags are present. It may not be necessary for every case of classic, uncomplicated thrush in a patient who has had it before.
  • Vulval Inspection: Look for erythema, oedema, fissures, and satellite lesions (small pustules) characteristic of candidiasis. Note any evidence of other dermatological conditions.
  • Speculum Examination: This allows visualisation of the vaginal walls and cervix. In thrush, the vaginal walls may be erythematous, and adherent white discharge may be visible. It also allows for swabs to be taken.
  • Investigations: A high vaginal swab (HVS) for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity is indicated in cases of recurrence, treatment failure, or atypical presentation to confirm the presence of yeast, identify the species (e.g., C. albicans vs. C. glabrata), and exclude other causes like bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis. Vaginal pH testing (if available) can be helpful; the pH is typically normal in thrush (≤4.5), whereas it is elevated in BV and trichomoniasis.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

While vulvovaginal candidiasis is a very common diagnosis, several other conditions can present with similar symptoms of discharge, itch, or soreness.

Common Differentials

  • Bacterial Vaginosis (BV): Often presents with a thin, grey/white, malodourous discharge with a characteristic 'fishy' smell. Itch is less prominent than in thrush. Caused by an alteration in vaginal flora, not an infection.
  • Trichomonas Vaginalis: A sexually transmitted infection caused by a protozoan. Typically causes a profuse, yellow-green, frothy discharge and vulvovaginal soreness. Itch can be present.
  • Allergic or Irritant Contact Dermatitis: Can cause vulval erythema, itch, and soreness due to a reaction to products like soaps, shower gels, laundry detergents, or sanitary products. Discharge is not a primary feature.
  • Atrophic Vaginitis (Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause): In post-menopausal women, oestrogen deficiency leads to vaginal dryness, soreness, and dyspareunia. Itch can occur. The discharge is typically scant.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs): Chlamydia or gonorrhoea can cause vaginal discharge and pelvic pain, but significant itch is less common. Herpes simplex virus causes painful blisters or ulcers.
  • Lichen Sclerosus: An inflammatory skin condition causing intense itch and soreness. Examination reveals pale, thin, or crinkled skin, often with fissures or bruising. It can lead to scarring and architectural changes (e.g., labial resorption).
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): An upper genital tract infection. The key features are lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, and potentially fever and malaise. A change in discharge may be present.
  • Vulval Intraepithelial Neoplasia (VIN) or Cancer: Rare but must be considered with any persistent, unexplained vulval lesion, ulcer, or lump, particularly if associated with itch or pain.
5. Management options in primary care

Management depends on whether the episode is uncomplicated, severe, or recurrent. The goal is to resolve symptoms and, in recurrent cases, prevent further episodes.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • General Vulval Care: Advise avoiding potential irritants such as perfumed soaps, bubble baths, and vaginal douching. Use a simple emollient wash substitute. Wear loose-fitting cotton underwear and avoid prolonged wearing of wet clothing (e.g., swimming costumes).
  • Symptomatic Relief: A saline bath or topical emollient can soothe inflamed skin.
  • Partner Treatment: Asymptomatic male partners do not require treatment. If a partner has symptoms of balanitis (redness, soreness, or irritation of the glans penis), they should be treated concurrently with an antifungal cream.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) / Standard First-Line Options

  • Topical Imidazoles: Clotrimazole or miconazole are available as pessaries (for vaginal use) and creams (for vulval use). A single pessary (e.g., clotrimazole 500mg) or a short course of lower-dose pessaries are effective for uncomplicated infection. A combination pack containing both a pessary and external cream is often recommended to treat both vaginal and vulval symptoms. These agents work by inhibiting fungal ergosterol synthesis, disrupting the cell membrane. [Rx info]
  • Oral Azoles: A single oral dose of Fluconazole is a convenient and effective alternative to topical treatment. It has a similar mechanism of action to topical imidazoles. It should generally be avoided in pregnancy. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs) / Second-Line Options

  • Longer Courses for Severe/Recurrent Disease: Management of severe (extensive erythema/oedema/fissures) or recurrent thrush often requires longer treatment courses. This may involve an induction regimen (e.g., more frequent oral or topical azoles) followed by a maintenance regimen for six months (e.g., a weekly oral dose or monthly pessary). [Rx info]
  • Alternative Antifungals: For cases confirmed to be caused by resistant strains or non-albicans species (e.g., C. glabrata), or where first-line treatments fail, alternative therapies may be needed. This might include Itraconazole (oral) or Nystatin (topical), often guided by culture sensitivities and specialist advice. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of vaginal thrush in the UK is primarily guided by NICE CKS (Clinical Knowledge Summaries) and guidance from the British Association for Sexual Health and HIV (BASHH). These guidelines emphasise a structured approach to diagnosis and treatment.

For uncomplicated vulvovaginal candidiasis, guidelines recommend treatment with either a short course of a topical imidazole (e.g., clotrimazole) or a single dose of oral fluconazole. The choice between them should be based on patient preference, cost, and potential contraindications (e.g., avoiding oral treatment in pregnancy). There is no significant difference in efficacy between the two approaches for uncomplicated cases.

For recurrent thrush (≥4 episodes/year), NICE recommends confirming the diagnosis with a high vaginal swab. Management involves an initial induction course to achieve mycological cure, followed by a 6-month maintenance regimen with regular prophylactic antifungal treatment. Patients should be reviewed at the end of the maintenance period.

Guidelines also stress the importance of providing advice on vulval skin care and ruling out or managing predisposing factors, such as optimising glycaemic control in women with diabetes mellitus. Partner treatment is only recommended if the partner is symptomatic.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to successful management, ensuring patient understanding, adherence to treatment, and appropriate help-seeking behaviour.

Counselling for Diagnosis and Self-Care

  • Explain the Diagnosis: Reassure the patient that thrush is very common and is caused by an overgrowth of a normal yeast, not poor hygiene. Crucially, explain that it is not considered an STI.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that the symptoms, particularly itch, can be very distressing and disruptive.
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions they can take. Advise on avoiding irritants like perfumed products and douching, and the benefits of using emollients as a soap substitute. Explain that these measures can help reduce the frequency of episodes.

Counselling for Treatments

  • How to Use Treatments: Provide clear instructions. For pessaries, advise insertion high into the vagina at night to ensure they remain in place. For creams, explain they are for the external vulval skin. For combination packs, explain the role of both components.
  • Treatment Expectations: Advise that symptoms should start to improve within a few days of starting treatment but may take up to a week to resolve completely.
  • Side Effects and Interactions: For topical treatments, warn that they can damage the latex in condoms and diaphragms, so alternative contraception should be used during treatment and for several days after. For oral fluconazole, briefly mention common side effects like nausea or headache and its potential for drug interactions.
  • Recurrent Thrush Plan: If starting a maintenance regimen, explain the rationale (suppressing yeast growth) and the importance of adherence for the full 6-month duration to maximise the chance of success.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is essential to ensure patients know when to seek further help, particularly if the diagnosis is uncertain or symptoms do not resolve as expected.

Clear verbal and, if appropriate, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek further advice if they experience any of the following:

  • Symptoms do not improve within 7-14 days after completing a course of treatment.
  • Symptoms recur within two months of treatment. (This may indicate the start of a pattern of recurrent thrush).
  • They develop any new, concerning symptoms such as lower abdominal pain, fever, abnormal vaginal bleeding, or a malodourous discharge.
  • They notice any concerning changes to the skin of the vulva, such as ulcers, lumps, or persistent white patches.
  • The treatment causes significant irritation or side effects.

For patients managed for recurrent thrush with a maintenance regimen, a planned review should be scheduled towards the end of the 6-month course to assess the response and decide on further management. For a first or uncomplicated episode, routine follow-up is not required if symptoms resolve completely.

9. Pathophysiology

Vulvovaginal candidiasis results from an overgrowth of Candida species, which are part of the normal commensal fungal flora of the vagina in up to 20% of asymptomatic women. The most common causative organism is Candida albicans (80-90% of cases). Other species, such as C. glabrata, are less common but may be associated with recurrent or resistant infections.

Symptomatic infection (candidiasis) is not a true infection from an external source, but rather a disturbance of the vaginal ecosystem that allows the yeast to proliferate from its commensal state to a pathogenic, invasive form. This involves the transformation of yeast cells into hyphae, which can invade the vaginal epithelial cells.

This overgrowth triggers a local inflammatory response from the host. The release of inflammatory mediators and cytokines in the vaginal and vulval tissues leads to the classic symptoms of erythema, oedema, and intense pruritus. The thick, white discharge is composed of exfoliated epithelial cells, inflammatory cells, and clumps of yeast hyphae.

Several factors can disrupt the delicate balance of the vaginal microbiome and predispose an individual to Candida overgrowth. These include hormonal factors (high oestrogen levels in pregnancy or with some forms of contraception), metabolic factors (poorly controlled diabetes mellitus leading to high glucose levels in vaginal secretions), and suppression of normal bacterial flora (e.g., lactobacilli) by broad-spectrum antibiotics, or general immunosuppression (e.g., use of corticosteroids, HIV).

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When selecting a treatment, it is important to consider contraindications, interactions, and specific safety points for each class of antifungal.

Topical Azoles (e.g., Clotrimazole, Miconazole)

  • Interactions: The most significant practical interaction is the ability of the cream/pessary base to damage latex condoms and diaphragms, rendering them ineffective. Patients must be counselled on this and advised to use alternative contraceptive methods.
  • Side Effects: Generally very well tolerated. A local burning or stinging sensation can occur but is usually mild and transient.
  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to the active ingredient or excipients is the only major contraindication. They are considered safe for use in pregnancy and breastfeeding.

Oral Azoles (e.g., Fluconazole, Itraconazole)

  • Contraindications: Oral azoles are generally contraindicated in pregnancy, especially in the first trimester and with high or multiple doses, due to a potential risk of teratogenicity. They should be avoided in patients with acute porphyria or severe liver disease.
  • Interactions: Fluconazole and itraconazole are inhibitors of cytochrome P450 enzymes (notably CYP3A4 and CYP2C9). They can significantly increase the concentration of many other drugs, including certain statins (risk of myopathy), warfarin (risk of bleeding), and some calcium channel blockers. A thorough drug interaction check using the BNF is mandatory before prescribing.
  • Side Effects & Risks: Common side effects include nausea, abdominal discomfort, and headache. Liver injury is a rare but serious potential adverse effect, particularly with longer courses of treatment (e.g., for maintenance therapy). Patients on long-term treatment may require baseline and periodic liver function tests.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Vulvovaginal candidiasis is rare before puberty. If suspected, diagnosis should be confirmed by swab. Vulvovaginitis in children is more commonly caused by irritants, other infections (like threadworms or Group A Streptococcus), or occasionally a foreign body. The possibility of sexual abuse must be considered in any child with a genital infection. Management should ideally be guided by a paediatric specialist.

Older adults

Thrush is less common in post-menopausal women due to lower oestrogen levels. Atrophic vaginitis is a very common mimic, presenting with soreness and dryness. A thorough assessment is needed to differentiate. If thrush is confirmed, standard treatments can be used, but be mindful of drug interactions with oral azoles in the context of polypharmacy. Any associated post-menopausal bleeding is a red flag for urgent investigation.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Thrush is very common in pregnancy due to hormonal changes. Management should be with topical treatments only. A 7-day course of a topical imidazole (e.g., clotrimazole) is recommended as first-line, as shorter courses may be less effective. Oral antifungals (fluconazole, itraconazole) must be avoided due to risks to the developing fetus. Topical treatments are considered safe during breastfeeding. A full drug-safety check is advised for any systemic therapy considered during lactation.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with uncontrolled diabetes mellitus are prone to severe and recurrent thrush; optimising glycaemic control is a cornerstone of management. In individuals who are significantly immunocompromised (e.g., due to chemotherapy or uncontrolled HIV), thrush can be more persistent. Longer courses of treatment may be required, and there is a higher risk of infection with non-albicans species. There should be a lower threshold for taking a swab for culture and sensitivities in these at-risk groups.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young Woman with a First Episode

  • Presentation: A 24-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of intense vulval itching and a thick, white, non-smelly discharge. She has never had this before. She finished a course of amoxicillin for a chest infection last week. There are no other symptoms.
  • Assessment: Based on the classic history and recent antibiotic use, a presumptive diagnosis of uncomplicated vulvovaginal candidiasis is made. Examination is not deemed necessary. STI risk is assessed as low.
  • Management: The GP discusses treatment options. The patient prefers a tablet, so a single dose of oral fluconazole is prescribed. Counselling is given that symptoms should improve in a few days. Safety netting advice is provided: return if symptoms do not resolve or if they recur.

Case Study 2: The Pregnant Patient

  • Presentation: A 30-year-old woman, who is 28 weeks pregnant, presents with vulval soreness and itching. She has had thrush before, but not during pregnancy.
  • Assessment: The symptoms are typical of thrush, which is common in pregnancy. A speculum examination is performed to confirm the appearance and rule out other issues like ruptured membranes. Adherent white plaques are seen on the erythematous vaginal walls.
  • Management: The GP explains that oral tablets must be avoided in pregnancy. They prescribe a 7-day course of clotrimazole pessaries and an associated cream for the vulva. The patient is counselled on how to use the treatment and reassured it is safe in pregnancy. Standard safety netting is provided.

Case Study 3: Recurrent Episodes

  • Presentation: A 42-year-old woman consults for her fifth episode of thrush in the last year. Each time it responds to a fluconazole capsule, but it always comes back within a couple of months. She is frustrated.
  • Assessment: The history is consistent with recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis. The GP explains this requires a more structured approach. A high vaginal swab is taken to confirm the diagnosis and check the candida species. A blood test (HbA1c) is arranged to screen for diabetes.
  • Management: Pending results, she is given an induction course of treatment. The swab confirms C. albicans and the HbA1c is normal. The GP discusses and starts a 6-month maintenance regimen with weekly fluconazole. She is counselled on the importance of completing the full course and given advice on general vulval care. A follow-up is planned for 6 months' time.
