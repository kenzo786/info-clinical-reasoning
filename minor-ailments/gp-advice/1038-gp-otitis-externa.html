Otitis Externa in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Otitis externa, or inflammation of the external ear canal, is a common condition presenting in primary care. Patients typically complain of a rapid onset (usually within 48 hours) of ear pain (otalgia), itching, a feeling of fullness or blockage in the ear, and sometimes a degree of hearing loss. The pain can be severe and is characteristically worsened by touching the tragus or pulling on the pinna.

A scanty discharge (otorrhoea) may be present, which can be thin and serous or thicker and purulent. It is often triggered by factors that disrupt the protective environment of the ear canal, such as swimming (hence the term 'swimmer's ear'), excessive cleaning with cotton buds, trauma, or underlying skin conditions like eczema. In most cases, otitis externa is unilateral and resolves within a week or two with appropriate topical treatment. The primary goal of the clinician is to control the pain, treat the inflammation and infection, and advise on preventing recurrence.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases are managed in the community, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious or complicated diagnosis requiring urgent review or referral to an Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) specialist.

  • Suspicion of malignant (necrotising) otitis externa: This is an invasive, osteomyelitic infection of the temporal bone, most often seen in elderly, diabetic, or immunocompromised patients. Key features are severe, deep, unrelenting pain that is often worse at night and out of proportion to the clinical findings. Granulation tissue may be visible on the floor of the ear canal at the bone-cartilage junction. This is a medical emergency.
  • Cranial nerve palsy: The development of facial weakness (facial nerve palsy) in the context of an ear infection is a major red flag for malignant otitis externa. Other cranial nerves can also be affected.
  • Spreading cellulitis: Redness, swelling, and tenderness that extends beyond the ear canal to involve the pinna, face, or neck. This suggests a more severe infection that may require systemic antibiotics.
  • Complete canal obstruction: If the ear canal is so swollen that it is completely occluded, it may not be possible to administer topical treatments effectively. These patients often require specialist assessment for aural toilet (microsuction) and insertion of an ear wick to deliver medication.
  • Vertigo: While not typical for otitis externa, the presence of true spinning dizziness could indicate labyrinthitis or other inner ear pathology, warranting a more detailed neurological assessment.
  • Sudden onset sensorineural hearing loss: This is not a feature of otitis externa and requires urgent ENT assessment.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is key to confirming the diagnosis and ruling out red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Profile: Clarify the onset, duration, and severity of ear pain. Ask about itching, discharge (colour, amount), and hearing loss. Tragal or pinna tenderness is a classic symptom.
  • Precipitating Factors: Enquire about recent swimming, use of cotton buds or other objects in the ear, hearing aid use, or recent air travel.
  • Past Medical History: Note any history of previous otitis externa, eczema, psoriasis, diabetes, or conditions causing immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, chemotherapy).
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about fever, severe nocturnal pain, facial weakness, dizziness, and spreading redness.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for systemic signs of illness.
  • Head and Neck Examination: Inspect the pinna and surrounding skin for cellulitis. Palpate the tragus and pull the pinna to elicit tenderness. Check for lymphadenopathy. Assess facial nerve function by asking the patient to raise their eyebrows, close their eyes tightly, and show their teeth.
  • Otoscopy: This is the key examination. The ear canal typically appears red, swollen (oedematous), and may contain debris or discharge. The tympanic membrane (eardrum) may be difficult to visualise due to swelling and debris, but if visible, it is usually normal. In contrast to otitis media, the eardrum is not typically bulging. A furuncle (localised abscess) may be visible.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for an acutely painful ear is broad, but the combination of tragal tenderness and a swollen external canal on otoscopy is highly suggestive of otitis externa.

Common Differentials

  • Acute Otitis Media with Perforation: This will present with otalgia, but the pain often classically resolves or improves with the onset of discharge as the eardrum perforates. The external canal itself is not typically inflamed, and tragal tenderness is less common.
  • Furunculosis: A small abscess or infected hair follicle in the outer cartilaginous part of the ear canal. It causes intense, localised pain but often less generalised canal swelling than diffuse otitis externa.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Can be caused by irritants or allergens in earrings, hearing aids, or topical preparations. Itching is often the predominant symptom, accompanied by a weeping rash.
  • Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM): Characterised by a persistent discharge through a perforated eardrum. It is typically painless.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Malignant (Necrotising) Otitis Externa: As described in the red flags section, this is an invasive infection. Suspect in any elderly or diabetic patient with severe pain, especially if granulation tissue is seen on otoscopy.
  • Mastoiditis: A complication of acute otitis media, presenting with pain, fever, and swelling/tenderness over the mastoid bone behind the ear. The pinna may be pushed forwards and downwards.
  • Ramsay Hunt Syndrome (Herpes Zoster Oticus): A reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus causing a severe unilateral earache, facial nerve palsy, and a vesicular rash in the ear canal or on the pinna.
  • Carcinoma of the Ear Canal: A rare but serious cause. Suspect in cases of persistent otitis externa that fail to respond to standard treatment, especially if there is bleeding or unusual granulation tissue.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is focused on four key areas: analgesia, aural toilet (cleaning), topical medication, and risk factor modification.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Analgesia: Advise on regular simple analgesia to control the often severe pain.
  • Keep the Ear Dry: Advise the patient to avoid getting the affected ear wet until the inflammation has fully resolved (typically 7-10 days). Suggest using a cotton wool ball coated in petroleum jelly placed in the outer ear during showering. Swimming should be avoided.
  • Avoid Canal Trauma: Instruct the patient not to insert anything into the ear canal, including cotton buds, fingers, or towels, as this can worsen irritation and delay healing.
  • Hearing Aid Use: Hearing aids should be left out of the affected ear until the infection has cleared.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol or ibuprofen are effective for pain control and should be recommended for regular use in the first few days.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Acetic Acid 2%: Acts as an astringent and has antibacterial and antifungal properties. It is often recommended as a first-line treatment for mild, diffuse otitis externa. [Rx info]
  • Topical Antibiotic +/- Corticosteroid: This is the mainstay of treatment for moderate to severe cases. The antibiotic targets common pathogens (e.g., Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus), and the steroid reduces inflammation, pain, and swelling. Options include drops or sprays containing an aminoglycoside (e.g., gentamicin), a quinolone (e.g., ciprofloxacin), or other agents combined with a steroid like hydrocortisone or dexamethasone. [Rx info]
  • Ear Wick Insertion: If the canal is very swollen, a ribbon gauze wick can be inserted into the canal to facilitate the delivery of topical drops to the site of infection. The wick is typically left in for 2-3 days.
  • Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin): These are NOT indicated for uncomplicated otitis externa. They should only be considered if there is evidence of spreading cellulitis beyond the ear canal or if the patient is systemically unwell or at high risk of severe infection. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is primarily guided by NICE CKS (Otitis Externa). The guidelines emphasise a step-wise approach based on the severity of the condition and the presence of risk factors.

The core principles are effective analgesia, topical treatment, and advice on future prevention. NICE recommends a 7-day course of topical treatment. For mild disease, topical acetic acid is a suitable first-line option. For moderate-to-severe disease, a topical preparation containing both an antibiotic and a corticosteroid is recommended.

Antibiotic stewardship is a key component. The guidance is clear that oral antibiotics should not be used for uncomplicated otitis externa, as topical therapy delivers much higher concentrations of antibiotic to the site of infection and avoids systemic side effects and the promotion of antimicrobial resistance. Oral antibiotics are reserved for cases with extra-auricular spread of infection or for systemically unwell or high-risk individuals.

Referral to an ENT specialist is recommended for cases that fail to respond to initial treatment, if the ear canal is occluded by swelling and debris requiring microsuction, or if a serious underlying cause like necrotising otitis externa is suspected.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is crucial for ensuring treatment adherence and preventing recurrence.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Diagnosis: Describe otitis externa as an infection of the skin of the ear canal, similar to an infection on the skin elsewhere. Reassure the patient that it is common and treatable.
  • Pain Management: Emphasise the importance of taking regular paracetamol or ibuprofen to control the pain, which can be severe initially.
  • Ear Drop Technique: Provide clear instructions on how to administer ear drops correctly. Advise the patient to lie on their side with the affected ear uppermost, instil the drops, and remain in that position for several minutes to allow the drops to penetrate the canal. Gently pressing the tragus can help.
  • Prevention is Key: Discuss measures to prevent recurrence, such as keeping ears dry (especially after swimming), avoiding the use of cotton buds, and managing any underlying eczema or psoriasis.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Treatment Duration: Advise the patient to complete the full course of treatment (typically 7 days), even if symptoms improve sooner.
  • Expected Progress: Explain that pain and swelling should start to improve within 2-3 days of starting treatment, but full resolution may take over a week.
  • Oral Antibiotics Explained: If not prescribing oral antibiotics, explain that the infection is on the surface and that drops deliver the medicine directly where it's needed most effectively, without the side effects of tablets.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting involves providing clear advice on when to seek further medical help if the condition does not improve as expected or worsens.

Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The pain becomes significantly worse, especially at night, despite regular analgesia and topical treatment.
  • The infection does not start to improve after 3-4 days of treatment, or is not resolved after one week.
  • Redness and swelling spreads from the ear to the surrounding skin on the face or neck.
  • They develop a fever or feel systemically unwell.
  • They notice any dizziness, new hearing loss, or weakness in their facial muscles.

A planned follow-up appointment is not usually necessary for uncomplicated cases that respond to treatment. However, a review should be arranged for patients who have had an ear wick inserted (for its removal in 2-3 days) or for those who were assessed as being at higher risk or had severe initial symptoms, to ensure resolution.

9. Pathophysiology

The external auditory canal has several innate defence mechanisms. It is lined with skin that produces cerumen (earwax), which is hydrophobic, acidic (pH 4-5), and contains lysozymes, creating a hostile environment for bacterial and fungal growth. The canal's anatomy, with its migrating epithelium, also facilitates natural cleaning.

Otitis externa occurs when these defences are breached. Common mechanisms include:

  • Moisture: Water from swimming or showering can raise the pH of the canal, creating a more favourable environment for bacterial growth (particularly Pseudomonas aeruginosa). This macerates the skin, breaking down the barrier.
  • Trauma: The use of cotton buds, hearing aids, or fingernails can cause micro-abrasions in the delicate canal skin, providing a portal of entry for pathogens.
  • Underlying Dermatoses: Conditions like eczema or psoriasis can cause inflammation and skin breakdown, predisposing to secondary infection.

Once the barrier is compromised, bacteria—most commonly Pseudomonas aeruginosa (in 20-60% of cases) and Staphylococcus aureus (10-70%)—colonise and invade the skin. This triggers an acute inflammatory response, leading to vasodilation, oedema (swelling), and pain. The accumulation of inflammatory exudate, desquamated epithelial cells, and pathogens creates the characteristic discharge and debris seen on otoscopy.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, it is essential to consider the properties and potential risks of the chosen agent.

Aminoglycoside-containing drops (e.g., Gentamicin, Neomycin)

  • Ototoxicity Risk: Aminoglycosides are potentially ototoxic and can cause irreversible sensorineural hearing loss and vestibular damage if they enter the middle ear. They are therefore contraindicated if the tympanic membrane is known to be perforated or if a perforation is suspected. If the eardrum cannot be visualised, it is safer to use a non-aminoglycoside preparation.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Neomycin, in particular, is a known sensitiser and can cause an allergic contact dermatitis.

Quinolone-containing drops (e.g., Ciprofloxacin, Ofloxacin)

  • Safety in Perforation: These agents are not ototoxic and are the preferred choice if a tympanic membrane perforation is present or suspected.

Corticosteroids (Topical)

  • Action: Reduce the inflammatory response, which is key to relieving pain and swelling.
  • Risks: May mask an underlying fungal infection or lead to one with prolonged use. The risk of systemic side effects with standard courses of topical ear drops is negligible.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Otitis externa is common in children, especially swimmers. The principles of management are the same as for adults, focusing on topical therapy and analgesia. It is important to rule out a foreign body in the ear canal as a cause of the symptoms. When prescribing, ensure the chosen preparation is licensed for the child's age. Quinolone drops are often preferred if the tympanic membrane cannot be clearly seen, to avoid the risk of ototoxicity from aminoglycosides.

Older adults

There must be a high index of suspicion for necrotising (malignant) otitis externa, especially if the patient is diabetic. Severe, unremitting pain should prompt urgent ENT referral. Otoscopy can be more challenging due to canal tortuosity or stenosis. Hearing aid use is a common predisposing factor; a review of hearing aid hygiene and fit may be required.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Topical preparations result in minimal systemic absorption. Acetic acid 2% spray is considered safe. Most topical antibiotic/corticosteroid preparations are also considered low risk, but it is always prudent to consult the latest BNF guidance or a specialist drug information service. Quinolone and aminoglycoside ear drops can generally be used where indicated. Systemic antibiotics should only be used if essential, with the choice of agent guided by safety in pregnancy or breastfeeding.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes or any cause of immunosuppression (e.g., on high-dose steroids, chemotherapy, or uncontrolled HIV) are at significantly higher risk of severe or invasive infection, particularly necrotising otitis externa. They require careful assessment, more aggressive management, and a much lower threshold for urgent specialist referral, even if symptoms initially appear non-specific.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young Swimmer

  • Presentation: A 19-year-old student and keen swimmer presents with a 2-day history of right ear pain and itching. The pain is worse when she lies on that side.
  • Assessment: Tugging her pinna reproduces the pain. Otoscopy reveals a red, mildly swollen ear canal with some white debris. The tympanic membrane is visible and looks normal.
  • Diagnosis: Mild diffuse otitis externa.
  • Management: The GP prescribes a 7-day course of a combination antibiotic and steroid ear drop. They advise regular paracetamol, keeping the ear dry, and avoiding swimming for at least a week. Safety netting advice is given to return if the pain worsens or does not improve in 3 days.

Case Study 2: The Hearing Aid User with Severe Swelling

  • Presentation: A 68-year-old man who wears hearing aids presents with 3 days of severe left ear pain and a blocked feeling. He has been unable to insert his hearing aid.
  • Assessment: The tragus is extremely tender to touch. On otoscopy, the ear canal is almost completely occluded by swelling and oedema, preventing visualisation of the eardrum.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Severe otitis externa with canal occlusion. The GP explains that drops will not get in effectively. They insert a Pope ear wick gently into the canal and prescribe a quinolone-based ear drop (a safe choice as the eardrum cannot be seen) to be applied to the wick three times a day. He is booked for a review in 2 days for wick removal and reassessment. He is given clear safety netting advice.

Case Study 3: The Elderly Diabetic Patient

  • Presentation: A 76-year-old man with type 2 diabetes presents with a 4-day history of excruciating left-sided ear pain, which is keeping him awake at night. He feels generally well.
  • Assessment: He looks distressed with pain. The pinna and tragus are tender. Otoscopy shows a swollen canal with some discharge. Crucially, a small area of granulation tissue is noted on the floor of the external canal.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The combination of diabetes, severe nocturnal pain, and granulation tissue raises a strong suspicion of necrotising (malignant) otitis externa. This is a red flag. The GP explains the concern about a more serious infection and immediately calls the on-call ENT registrar to arrange an emergency assessment.
