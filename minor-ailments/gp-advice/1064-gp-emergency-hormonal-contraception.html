Emergency Hormonal Contraception (EHC) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

A request for Emergency Hormonal Contraception (EHC) is a common consultation in primary care. It is not a presentation with symptoms, but rather a request to prevent an unplanned pregnancy following an episode of unprotected sexual intercourse (UPSI) or a contraceptive failure (e.g., a split condom or missed oral contraceptive pills).

The primary role of the clinician is to establish the time since the event, assess the individual's medical eligibility for the available methods, and provide the most effective treatment possible. The main oral options are Levonorgestrel (LNG) and Ulipristal Acetate (UPA). The copper intrauterine device (Cu-IUD) is the most effective method of emergency contraception overall and should be discussed as a primary option.

The consultation is an important opportunity to discuss ongoing contraceptive needs, assess for sexually transmitted infections (STIs), and address any safeguarding concerns. The goal is to provide timely and effective emergency contraception while also promoting long-term sexual health.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most consultations for EHC are straightforward, it is crucial to identify features that may indicate a need for urgent action or safeguarding. Referral or immediate action may be necessary in the following circumstances:

  • Suspected Sexual Assault or Coercion: If there is any concern that the intercourse was non-consensual or the person is at risk of harm. This requires sensitive handling, adherence to local safeguarding protocols, and potential involvement of specialist services.
  • Safeguarding issues in a young person (under 16): While a young person can consent to treatment under Fraser guidelines, concerns about child sexual exploitation, abuse, or the age of a sexual partner warrant adherence to child protection procedures. A person under 13 is unable to consent to sexual activity, and this requires an immediate safeguarding referral.
  • Symptoms of a possible existing pregnancy: Such as amenorrhoea, breast tenderness, or nausea. EHC is not effective if the patient is already pregnant. A pregnancy test should be performed.
  • Symptoms suggesting ectopic pregnancy or PID: Significant, acute lower abdominal pain, particularly if unilateral, or associated with fever, discharge, or bleeding. This requires urgent gynaecological assessment.
  • Patient outside the licensed window for oral EHC: If a patient presents more than 120 hours after UPSI, or if a Cu-IUD is indicated but cannot be fitted in primary care, an urgent referral to a specialist sexual health service is required.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured history is key to determining the most appropriate and effective method of emergency contraception.

History Taking

  • Timing and Nature of Risk: Establish the exact time (hours) since the first and any subsequent episodes of UPSI. Clarify the reason (e.g., no method used, condom failure, missed pills).
  • Menstrual History: Date of the last menstrual period (LMP) and typical cycle length to estimate the likely time of ovulation. Note that oral EHC is not effective post-ovulation.
  • Contraceptive History: Detail of current and past contraceptive methods used.
  • Past Medical History: Specifically ask about contraindications, such as severe liver disease, malabsorption syndromes (e.g., Crohn's), or a history of ectopic pregnancy. Check for severe asthma if considering UPA.
  • Drug History: A critical check for any interacting medicines, particularly liver enzyme-inducers (e.g., some antiepileptic drugs, St John's Wort, rifampicin/rifabutin), which can significantly reduce the efficacy of oral EHC.
  • Safeguarding Screen: For all patients, but particularly adolescents, sensitively enquire about the context of the sexual intercourse to screen for coercion or abuse. For those under 16, a formal Fraser competency assessment must be documented.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: A physical examination is not usually required for providing oral EHC.
  • BMI: Calculating the Body Mass Index is important. Evidence suggests the efficacy of oral EHC may be reduced in individuals with a higher BMI (>26 kg/m² or weight >70kg), making the Cu-IUD a more suitable option.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The primary 'diagnosis' is the need for emergency contraception to prevent an unplanned pregnancy. However, the consultation must exclude or consider other concurrent conditions.

Primary Consideration

  • Risk of Unplanned Pregnancy: The main reason for consultation. The assessment focuses on quantifying this risk based on cycle timing and providing the most effective intervention.

Conditions to Consider and Exclude

  • Existing Pregnancy: If the patient's period is overdue or they report symptoms of pregnancy, this must be excluded with a urine pregnancy test before providing EHC.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy: A rare but serious possibility, especially if there is a history of such, or if the patient presents with unilateral lower abdominal pain. This requires urgent investigation.
  • Sexually Transmitted Infection (STI): Any UPSI carries a risk of STIs. The consultation is an opportunity to discuss this risk, offer testing, and provide advice on prevention. Current symptoms like vaginal discharge or dysuria should prompt further assessment.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Consider if the patient presents with lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding, and/or vaginal or cervical discharge. This would require specific antibiotic treatment.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is guided by clinical effectiveness, time since UPSI, and patient preference. The Cu-IUD is the most effective method and should always be discussed.

Non-Hormonal Method

  • Copper Intrauterine Device (Cu-IUD): The most effective method of emergency contraception (>99% effective). It can be fitted up to 5 days after UPSI, or up to 5 days after the earliest predicted day of ovulation. It works by preventing fertilisation and may also prevent implantation. It offers the significant advantage of providing ongoing, highly effective contraception. [Rx info]

Over-the-Counter (OTC) / Pharmacy (P) Options

  • Levonorgestrel 1.5mg (LNG): A synthetic progestogen that primarily works by delaying or inhibiting ovulation. It is licensed for use up to 72 hours (3 days) after UPSI. Its efficacy decreases the longer the time from intercourse. It is available OTC from pharmacies. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Ulipristal Acetate 30mg (UPA): A selective progesterone receptor modulator that works by delaying or inhibiting ovulation, and is effective even when the LH surge has begun. It is licensed for use up to 120 hours (5 days) after UPSI and is more effective than LNG, particularly between 72 and 120 hours. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of emergency contraception in the UK is guided by the Faculty of Sexual and Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH) Clinical Guideline: Emergency Contraception, which is accredited by NICE.

The core principles of the guidance are:

  • Offer all methods: Clinicians should inform users about all three methods of emergency contraception (Cu-IUD, UPA, and LNG) and discuss their relative effectiveness and suitability.
  • Cu-IUD is first-line: The Cu-IUD is the most effective method and should be offered to all individuals who are eligible. It is not affected by BMI or interacting medications.
  • UPA is the most effective oral method: UPA is more effective than LNG and should be offered as the first-line oral option.
  • Assess for interacting drugs: A careful drug history is essential. For women taking liver enzyme-inducing medication, a Cu-IUD is the recommended option as oral methods are less effective. A double dose of LNG may be considered off-label if a Cu-IUD is declined or unavailable.
  • Future contraception: The consultation should be used as an opportunity to discuss and initiate a reliable, ongoing method of contraception.

These guidelines emphasise patient-centred care, ensuring the individual can make an informed choice based on effectiveness, convenience, and personal circumstances.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Clear and non-judgemental communication is essential for effective consultation and ensuring the patient makes an informed choice.

Counselling for Method Choice

  • Explain Mechanisms and Effectiveness: Clearly state that the Cu-IUD is the most effective method (>99%). Explain that oral methods (UPA and LNG) work by delaying ovulation and are not abortifacients. Explain that UPA is more effective than LNG.
  • Discuss Time Windows: Reinforce the time limits for each method (Cu-IUD and UPA up to 120 hours; LNG up to 72 hours) and that efficacy decreases with time.
  • Side Effects: Discuss common side effects of oral EHC, such as headache, nausea, and the potential for the next period to be earlier, later, or different than usual.
  • Action if Vomiting Occurs: Advise that if they vomit within 3 hours of taking an oral EHC tablet, they should seek advice as a repeat dose will be necessary.

Counselling for Follow-up and Future Prevention

  • Starting Ongoing Contraception: This is a key part of the consultation. Advise that after taking UPA, they must wait 5 days before starting hormonal contraception (and use barrier methods in between). After LNG, they can start hormonal contraception immediately.
  • Pregnancy Testing: Advise a pregnancy test in 3 weeks, or sooner if their next period is more than 7 days late, lighter than usual, or if they develop any symptoms of pregnancy.
  • STI Risk: Discuss the risk of STIs from UPSI and offer screening.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is crucial to ensure the patient knows when to seek further help and to manage potential treatment failure.

Clear verbal and written advice should be provided. Advise the patient to seek urgent medical attention if they experience any of the following:

  • Severe lower abdominal pain: Particularly if it is one-sided, as this could be a symptom of an ectopic pregnancy. This is very important, as although EHC reduces the overall risk of pregnancy, a higher proportion of pregnancies that do occur after EHC failure may be ectopic.
  • Their next period is more than 7 days late, or is unusually light or short. This may indicate pregnancy.
  • They have any other symptom they are concerned about or believe they may be pregnant.

Follow-Up Arrangements

  • A routine follow-up is not usually required. However, the patient should be advised to perform a pregnancy test no sooner than 3 weeks after the episode of UPSI.
  • If a Cu-IUD has been fitted, they should be counselled on checking threads and when to seek review.
  • It is good practice to arrange a follow-up appointment or signpost the patient to an appropriate service to discuss and start a long-term method of contraception.
9. Pathophysiology

Understanding the mechanism of EHC requires knowledge of the normal menstrual cycle. The cycle is governed by fluctuating hormones, primarily leading to the development of a dominant follicle, a surge in Luteinising Hormone (LH), and subsequent ovulation (release of the egg).

Oral emergency contraceptives work by interfering with this process before fertilisation can occur. Their primary mechanism is the inhibition or delay of ovulation.

  • Levonorgestrel (LNG): As a progestogen, LNG administered in a high dose before the LH surge begins can prevent or postpone the surge, thereby stopping the follicle from rupturing and releasing an egg. If the LH surge has already started, LNG is largely ineffective.
  • Ulipristal Acetate (UPA): As a selective progesterone receptor modulator (SPRM), UPA also acts to delay ovulation. Its key advantage is that it remains effective even when the LH surge has already begun, providing a longer window of efficacy than LNG. It binds to progesterone receptors, blocking the normal hormonal cascade that leads to ovulation.

Neither LNG nor UPA is effective once ovulation has occurred. They do not prevent implantation of a fertilised egg and are not abortifacients. The Copper IUD (Cu-IUD) has a different mechanism. The copper ions released into the uterus create an inflammatory environment that is toxic to both sperm and eggs, thus preventing fertilisation. It may also have a post-fertilisation effect by preventing implantation, although its primary action is pre-fertilisation.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When providing EHC, specific drug interactions, contraindications and effects on regular contraception must be considered.

Ulipristal Acetate (UPA)

  • Interactions: Efficacy can be reduced by drugs that increase gastric pH (e.g., omeprazole, antacids). Its efficacy is also significantly reduced by liver enzyme-inducers.
  • Hormonal Contraception: UPA can reduce the effectiveness of progestogen-containing contraceptives. Therefore, regular hormonal contraception (pill, patch, ring, injection) should not be started or resumed for 5 days after taking UPA. A barrier method must be used during this time.
  • Contraindications: Should not be used in women with severe asthma being treated with oral glucocorticoids.

Levonorgestrel (LNG)

  • Interactions: The primary concern is with liver enzyme-inducing drugs (e.g., carbamazepine, phenytoin, rifampicin, St John's Wort). These can significantly reduce LNG's effectiveness. FSRH guidance suggests offering a Cu-IUD as the first choice, or considering a double dose (3mg) of LNG (off-label use).
  • Hormonal Contraception: Regular hormonal contraception can be started immediately after taking LNG. Additional precautions (e.g., condoms) will be needed for a set period depending on the method started.
  • Repeat Use: Concomitant use of UPA and LNG is not recommended within the same cycle. If LNG has been taken, UPA should not be taken for 7 days. If UPA has been taken, LNG should not be taken for 5 days.
11. Management in special populations

Children (Under 16 years)

A consultation with a person under 16 requires a careful assessment of their competence to consent using the Fraser Guidelines. The assessment and decision-making must be meticulously documented. Safeguarding is paramount, with a need to sensitively explore the circumstances of the sexual contact and consider the possibility of exploitation or abuse. A child under 13 cannot legally consent to sex, and this situation would mandate an immediate child protection referral.

Older adults

This is generally not applicable, as EHC relates to pre-menopausal individuals. However, for women over 40 requiring contraception, a discussion about the benefits of long-acting reversible methods like the Cu-IUD may be particularly relevant.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

EHC is contraindicated if an existing pregnancy is known or suspected. It will not harm an established pregnancy if taken inadvertently. For breastfeeding individuals, Levonorgestrel (LNG) is considered safe with no need to interrupt breastfeeding. Following Ulipristal Acetate (UPA), official guidance recommends that breast milk should be expressed and discarded for one week after treatment, although recent FSRH statements suggest this may no longer be necessary; always check current guidance.

People with significant comorbidities or other factors

For individuals with a high BMI (>26 kg/m²) or weight (>70kg), the efficacy of oral EHC may be reduced. The Cu-IUD is the preferred and most effective option as its efficacy is not affected by weight. In those taking liver enzyme-inducing medications, oral EHC efficacy is significantly compromised; the Cu-IUD is the strongly recommended method. UPA is contraindicated in women with severe asthma controlled by oral steroids.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The University Student

  • Presentation: A 20-year-old student presents requesting EHC. A condom split approximately 40 hours ago. Her LMP was 10 days ago, and her cycle is regular (28-30 days). She has no significant past medical history and takes no regular medication.
  • Assessment: She is calm and coherent. Her BMI is 22. The timing places her in the high-risk peri-ovulatory window.
  • Management: The GP discusses all three options. The Cu-IUD is offered as the most effective method but the patient declines as she is hoping to travel soon. The GP explains that Ulipristal Acetate (UPA) is the most effective oral option. She is provided with UPA. She is counselled to wait 5 days before restarting her combined oral contraceptive pill and to use condoms until then. Safety netting advice is given regarding signs of pregnancy and abdominal pain, and she is advised to do a pregnancy test in 3 weeks.

Case Study 2: Patient on Interacting Medication

  • Presentation: A 28-year-old female requests EHC after missing two contraceptive pills and having UPSI 2 days ago. Her medical history includes epilepsy, for which she is stable on carbamazepine.
  • Assessment: The clinician identifies that carbamazepine is a potent liver enzyme-inducer, which will significantly reduce the effectiveness of both UPA and LNG.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP explains that because of her epilepsy medication, oral EHC is very unlikely to be effective. They strongly recommend the Copper IUD as the most effective method, explaining that it is not affected by her medication. The patient agrees this is the best option. The GP contacts the local sexual health clinic and arranges an urgent appointment for a Cu-IUD fitting that day, providing clear safety netting in the interim.

Case Study 3: The Younger Teenager

  • Presentation: A 15-year-old girl attends alone asking for the "morning after pill". She is visibly anxious and reports UPSI with her 16-year-old boyfriend last night.
  • Assessment: The GP uses the Fraser guidelines to assess competency, finding she understands the advice, the implications of treatment, and the reasons for wanting confidentiality. The GP gently explores the relationship and finds no safeguarding concerns.
  • Management: After discussing options, UPA is supplied. The GP spends time discussing the importance of reliable ongoing contraception and offers a follow-up appointment in one week to talk through options like the implant or pill. The importance of STI screening is also discussed, and she is given information on how to access this. Clear safety netting advice is provided, along with contact details for a young person's sexual health service. All aspects of the Fraser competency assessment and consultation are carefully documented.
