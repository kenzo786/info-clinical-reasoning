Acute Otitis Media in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute Otitis Media (AOM) is a very common condition in primary care, particularly in infants and young children. It is defined by inflammation of the middle ear, associated with a middle ear effusion and a rapid onset of symptoms.

Patients, most often children, typically present with a sudden onset of ear pain (otalgia), which may cause irritability, crying, poor feeding, and disturbed sleep. In pre-verbal children, pulling or rubbing the ear can be a key sign. A fever is present in about two-thirds of cases. Other associated symptoms may include cough, coryza, and transient, mild hearing loss due to fluid in the middle ear. The condition frequently follows or accompanies a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI). In most cases, AOM is a self-limiting illness, with symptoms typically improving within three days and resolving within a week without specific treatment.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While AOM is usually benign, it is crucial to identify signs of complications that require urgent assessment and potential hospital referral.

  • Signs of mastoiditis: Post-auricular swelling, erythema, tenderness, or protrusion of the pinna. This suggests the infection has spread to the mastoid bone behind the ear.
  • Facial nerve palsy: New onset of facial drooping or weakness on the affected side indicates the infection is impacting the facial nerve (CN VII) as it passes through the middle ear.
  • Symptoms of intracranial complications: Severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, altered consciousness, confusion, or focal neurological deficits. These are rare but could indicate meningitis or a brain abscess.
  • Systemic signs of severe illness: High fever (>39°C), rigors, or features of sepsis (e.g., tachycardia, hypotension, non-blanching rash). This warrants immediate emergency assessment.
  • Vertigo: Severe dizziness or a spinning sensation may suggest labyrinthitis, an inflammation of the inner ear.
  • Complete hearing loss: Sudden, total loss of hearing in the affected ear is a red flag.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A systematic approach is key to diagnosing AOM and excluding other pathology.

History Taking

  • Symptom characteristics: Ascertain onset, severity, and nature of the ear pain. Ask about fever, ear discharge (otorrhoea), hearing loss, and any preceding or concurrent URTI symptoms. In children, ask about behavioural changes like irritability, tugging at the ear, and poor feeding.
  • Past Medical History: Enquire about recurrent AOM (≥3 episodes in 6 months or ≥4 in 12 months), previous ear surgery (e.g., grommets), cleft palate or Down's syndrome (anatomical risk factors), and immunosuppression.
  • Social History: Note exposure to household smoking and attendance at nursery, which are known risk factors.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about swelling behind the ear, facial weakness, severe headache, neck stiffness, and any signs of systemic illness.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the patient's general wellbeing, looking for signs of systemic illness, pain, or (in children) irritability or lethargy.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature. Other vitals (pulse, respiratory rate) are important if sepsis is suspected.
  • Otoscopy: This is the key examination. Findings suggestive of AOM include a bulging, erythematous (red), or opacified tympanic membrane (TM), with loss of the normal light reflex. An air-fluid level may be visible. If the TM has perforated, purulent discharge may be seen in the ear canal.
  • Head and Neck: Check for tenderness or swelling over the mastoid process behind the ear. Assess facial nerve function if there is any clinical suspicion.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

Ear pain is a common presentation with several possible causes.

Common Differentials

  • Otitis Externa: Inflammation of the external ear canal. The key distinguishing features are pain on traction of the pinna or tragus, and a swollen, inflamed ear canal on otoscopy, often with debris. The tympanic membrane is usually normal.
  • Otitis Media with Effusion (OME) / 'Glue Ear': Fluid in the middle ear without acute inflammation. It typically presents with hearing loss rather than acute pain or fever. On otoscopy, the TM may be retracted, dull, or show an air-fluid level or bubbles, but it is not bulging or acutely inflamed.
  • Referred Pain: Pain felt in the ear but originating from another site. Common sources include dental problems (e.g., molar eruption, abscess), tonsillitis, or temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction. The ear examination will be normal.
  • Foreign Body: A possibility in young children. The object may be visible on otoscopy.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Mastoiditis: Presents with post-auricular pain, swelling and erythema. A key differential to exclude in any patient with AOM.
  • Cholesteatoma: A destructive skin growth in the middle ear. While often a chronic issue, it can present with discharge and secondary infection. A history of persistent discharge or hearing loss, and specific otoscopic findings (e.g., attic crust, retraction pocket) are suggestive.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is primarily focused on pain relief, as most infections resolve spontaneously. Antibiotic stewardship is a key consideration.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain the natural history of the condition, noting that pain and fever should settle within 3-4 days. Reassure that most cases are self-limiting and do not require antibiotics.
  • Analgesia: This is the cornerstone of management. Advise regular doses of paracetamol or ibuprofen to control pain and fever.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol and ibuprofen are the primary treatments for pain and fever associated with AOM.
  • Anaesthetic Eardrops: Eardrops containing an anaesthetic (e.g., lidocaine) may be considered for pain relief in patients over 2 years, provided there is no evidence of tympanic membrane perforation. [Rx info]
  • Decongestants/Antihistamines: Evidence shows these are not effective for AOM and are not recommended.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Clarithromycin): An immediate antibiotic prescription should be offered only to patients who are systemically very unwell, are at high risk of complications (e.g., significant comorbidities, immunosuppression), or have a complication like mastoiditis. For other patients, NICE guidance recommends a 'no antibiotic' or 'back-up/delayed antibiotic' prescribing strategy. A back-up prescription may be considered for children under 2 years with bilateral AOM, or for any child with otorrhoea (discharge), as they are more likely to benefit. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline NG91 (Otitis media (acute): antimicrobial prescribing).

The core principles of the guideline are to promote effective pain management and robust antimicrobial stewardship. NICE emphasises that most cases of AOM resolve without antibiotics and that analgesia is the primary treatment. The evidence shows that for most children, antibiotics have a limited effect on pain and do not reduce the rate of recurrence or long-term complications like OME ('glue ear'), but they do cause side effects like diarrhoea and rash and contribute to antimicrobial resistance.

NICE provides clear criteria for antibiotic prescribing. An immediate antibiotic prescription is recommended only for those who are systemically very unwell or at high risk of complications. For other groups, a 'no antibiotic' or a 'back-up' (delayed) prescription is advised. Specific groups who may benefit more from antibiotics and for whom a back-up prescription should be considered include children under 2 years with bilateral AOM and any child or young person with otorrhoea (discharge) due to TM perforation. The guideline strongly advises against the use of decongestants and antihistamines.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is essential for managing patient/parental expectations and ensuring safe and appropriate care.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that ear infections are very common, usually viral, and that the body's own immune system can clear them. Explain that pain and fever should improve within 3 days, although full resolution can take a week.
  • Prioritise Pain Relief: Emphasise that the most important treatment is regular paracetamol and/or ibuprofen to keep the child comfortable.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: Use a simple explanation like, "Since most ear infections are caused by viruses, antibiotics won't help clear the infection any faster. They can also cause side effects like tummy upset or a rash, so we only use them when absolutely necessary."

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Delayed Prescriptions: If giving a back-up prescription, provide very clear instructions. For example: "This prescription is for you to have ready just in case. Don't use it right away. Only get it from the pharmacy if your child's pain isn't getting any better after 3 days, or if they seem to be getting much more unwell."
  • Side Effects: When prescribing antibiotics, briefly counsel on common side effects (e.g., diarrhoea, rash) and what to do if they occur.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a critical component of the consultation, providing clear guidance on when to seek further help.

Clear verbal and/or written instructions should be given to the patient or their parent/carer. Advise them to seek an urgent review if the individual:

  • Does not start to improve within 3-4 days.
  • Becomes significantly more unwell (e.g., develops a persistent high temperature, becomes drowsy or very irritable).
  • Develops any new symptoms such as swelling, redness or tenderness behind the ear.
  • Develops any facial weakness or drooping.
  • Develops a stiff neck, severe headache, or dislike of bright lights.
  • Has any other symptom that they are worried about.

No routine follow-up is required for uncomplicated AOM. However, a review should be considered for children who have recurrent episodes of AOM to assess for underlying issues and consider referral to ENT services.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute otitis media is fundamentally a condition of Eustachian tube dysfunction, typically initiated by a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).

The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear space to the nasopharynx, and its functions are to equalise pressure, protect the middle ear from pathogens, and clear secretions. In young children, the tube is shorter, more horizontal, and less functionally effective than in adults, predisposing them to AOM. During a URTI, the mucosa of the nasopharynx and Eustachian tube becomes inflamed and oedematous, leading to obstruction.

This obstruction impairs ventilation and drainage of the middle ear, causing negative pressure to develop and allowing fluid to be drawn from the mucosal lining into the middle ear space (creating an effusion). This sterile effusion provides a rich culture medium for pathogens. Viruses (like RSV, Rhinovirus, Influenza) or bacteria (most commonly *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*, and *Moraxella catarrhalis*) can then proliferate, leading to an active infection. The subsequent inflammatory response causes the accumulation of purulent fluid (pus), leading to increased pressure, bulging of the tympanic membrane, and the characteristic symptoms of pain and fever.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing for AOM, specific contraindications and side effects must be considered.

Analgesics (Paracetamol, Ibuprofen)

  • Contraindications: Ibuprofen should be avoided in patients with a history of hypersensitivity to NSAIDs, active peptic ulcer disease, or severe renal impairment. Paracetamol should be used with caution in severe hepatic impairment.
  • Considerations: Advise parents on correct weight-based dosing for children and the importance of not exceeding the maximum daily dose.

Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Clarithromycin)

  • Contraindications: Penicillin allergy is an absolute contraindication for amoxicillin. Macrolides (e.g., clarithromycin) should be used with caution in patients with hepatic impairment or a history of QT interval prolongation.
  • Side Effects: Common side effects include gastrointestinal upset (diarrhoea, nausea) and skin rash. Inform patients/parents that a non-itchy, non-urticarial rash with amoxicillin can occur, particularly in the presence of a viral illness, and is not always indicative of a true penicillin allergy.

Anaesthetic Eardrops

  • Contraindications: These must not be used if there is a known or suspected perforation of the tympanic membrane, as they can be ototoxic. Use in children under 2 years is not routinely recommended.
11. Management in special populations

Children

AOM is predominantly a childhood illness. Management should strictly follow NICE NG91, with a strong emphasis on analgesia and restricted use of antibiotics. A lower threshold for a back-up antibiotic prescription exists for children under 2 with bilateral AOM and for any child with otorrhoea, as they are the groups most likely to derive benefit. Children with anatomical abnormalities (e.g., Down's syndrome, cleft palate) or immunosuppression are at higher risk of complications and may warrant immediate antibiotic therapy.

Older adults

AOM is less common in adults. When it does occur, it is important to have a higher index of suspicion for underlying pathology, such as nasopharyngeal tumours causing Eustachian tube obstruction, particularly with unilateral or recurrent episodes. Referred pain from dental or other sources should also be carefully excluded. The principles of management (analgesia first) remain the same.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Paracetamol is the preferred analgesic. Ibuprofen may be used in the first and second trimesters if necessary but should be avoided in the third. For antibiotic therapy, amoxicillin is considered safe in pregnancy and breastfeeding. For those with a penicillin allergy, erythromycin is the preferred alternative macrolide during pregnancy. Always check the latest BNF or specialist resources for up-to-date advice.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are immunosuppressed (e.g., due to chemotherapy or uncontrolled HIV) are at higher risk of severe infection and complications. There should be a low threshold for immediate antibiotic treatment in this group. For patients with cochlear implants, specialist advice should be sought, as AOM poses a higher risk of meningitis.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Toddler with Ear Pain

  • Presentation: An 18-month-old boy is brought in by his mother. He has had a cold for a few days and for the last 24 hours has been crying, irritable, pulling at his right ear, and has a temperature of 38.2°C.
  • Assessment: He is alert but grizzly. Otoscopy of the right ear shows a bulging, red tympanic membrane. The left ear is normal. There are no red flags.
  • Diagnosis: Unilateral acute otitis media.
  • Management: The GP explains that this is a common infection that usually gets better on its own. They advise regular paracetamol and ibuprofen for pain and fever. They provide a back-up prescription for amoxicillin with clear instructions to only use it if he is not improving after 3 days or gets worse. Clear safety netting advice is given.

Case Study 2: The Child with a Discharging Ear

  • Presentation: A 4-year-old girl presents with a 2-day history of left-sided ear pain and fever. This morning, her mother noticed some pus-like fluid on her pillow. The pain has now eased.
  • Assessment: The child seems much brighter now the pain has subsided. Vital signs are normal. Otoscopy reveals a perforation in the left tympanic membrane with a small amount of purulent discharge visible in the canal.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Acute otitis media with perforation and otorrhoea. According to NICE guidelines, this group is more likely to benefit from antibiotics. An immediate 5-day course of amoxicillin is prescribed. The GP explains that the perforation will almost always heal by itself and advises keeping the ear dry. Safety netting advice is provided.

Case Study 3: The Adult with Ear Pain

  • Presentation: A 45-year-old man presents with severe right-sided ear pain for 2 days. He is a keen swimmer.
  • Assessment: He is systemically well. The pain is significantly worsened when the outer ear (pinna) is pulled. On otoscopy, the ear canal is red and swollen, making it difficult to view the tympanic membrane, which appears normal.
  • Diagnosis: Otitis Externa, not AOM.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis is an infection of the skin of the ear canal. They prescribe topical antibiotic/steroid eardrops and advise on water precautions and analgesia. This highlights the importance of distinguishing AOM from its common differentials.
