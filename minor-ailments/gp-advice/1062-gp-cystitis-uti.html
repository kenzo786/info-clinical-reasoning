Cystitis (UTI in women) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Cystitis, or lower urinary tract infection (UTI), is an extremely common presentation in primary care, particularly among adult women. It refers to inflammation of the bladder, most often caused by a bacterial infection. Patients typically present with a rapid onset of a cluster of urinary symptoms.

The classic triad of symptoms includes: dysuria (burning, stinging pain on passing urine), urinary frequency (needing to urinate more often than usual), and urinary urgency (a sudden, compelling need to urinate). Patients may also report suprapubic discomfort, cloudy or strong-smelling urine, and visible haematuria (blood in the urine). Systemic symptoms like fever are usually absent in uncomplicated cystitis.

The condition is typically self-limiting in some women, with symptoms resolving within a few days. However, it often causes significant discomfort and can be effectively treated with a short course of antibiotics. Common triggers include recent sexual intercourse, dehydration, use of spermicides, and previous UTIs.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

It is crucial to differentiate uncomplicated cystitis from more complex conditions that require urgent action. The presence of red flags necessitates further investigation or escalation.

  • Signs of pyelonephritis (upper UTI): Loin or flank pain, costovertebral angle tenderness, fever (>38°C), rigors, nausea, and vomiting. This suggests the infection has ascended to the kidneys, which can lead to bacteraemia and sepsis. Urgent assessment and treatment are required.
  • Signs of sepsis: New-onset confusion (delirium), tachycardia, hypotension, or feeling generally very unwell. These are features of a systemic inflammatory response and require emergency hospital admission.
  • Pregnant patient: Any suspected UTI in pregnancy requires prompt urine culture and treatment to reduce the risk of complications such as pyelonephritis, premature labour, and low birth weight.
  • Recurrent or persistent symptoms despite treatment: Failure to improve within 48 hours of starting appropriate antibiotics may suggest antibiotic resistance or an alternative diagnosis. Recurrent UTIs (2 in 6 months, or 3 in 12 months) warrant investigation.
  • Visible haematuria in women aged 60 and over: While often due to infection, this can be a sign of urinary tract malignancy and requires investigation as per NICE guidelines, even if the UTI resolves.
  • Male patient: UTI in men is uncommon and considered complicated by definition, requiring different investigation and management pathways.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A focused history is the cornerstone of diagnosing uncomplicated cystitis, often making a physical examination unnecessary.

History Taking

  • Symptom Profile: Clarify the presence, severity, and onset of key symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency. Ask about suprapubic pain, haematuria, and the character of the urine.
  • Exclude Red Flags: Systematically enquire about fever, loin pain, vomiting, and feeling systemically unwell.
  • Gynaecological Symptoms: Ask about vaginal discharge, itch, or external soreness to help differentiate from vaginitis or sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
  • Relevant Medical History: Check for pregnancy, diabetes, known renal tract abnormalities, immunosuppression, or history of recurrent UTIs.
  • Medication History: Note any allergies and recent antibiotic use.

Clinical Examination & Investigations

  • Examination: A physical examination is usually not required for simple cystitis. If pyelonephritis is suspected, assess for fever, check pulse and blood pressure, and palpate for abdominal or flank tenderness.
  • Urine Dipstick: NICE guidance advises against routine urine dipstick testing for diagnosis in non-pregnant women under 65 who present with 2 or more key urinary symptoms, as it has limited diagnostic value in this group. It may be useful in older women or where the diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Urine Culture (MSU): A mid-stream urine sample for culture and sensitivity testing is not needed for a first presentation of uncomplicated cystitis. It is recommended for pregnant women, patients with treatment failure, recurrent UTIs, or where pyelonephritis is suspected.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

While uncomplicated cystitis is very common, several other conditions can present with similar symptoms.

Common Differentials

  • Urethral Syndrome / Urethritis: Symptoms are often limited to dysuria ('scalding' pain localised to the urethra). This can be associated with sexually transmitted infections like Chlamydia trachomatis or Neisseria gonorrhoeae, or sometimes postmenopausal vaginal atrophy. The absence of frequency and urgency makes cystitis less likely.
  • Vaginitis (e.g., Candidiasis, Bacterial Vaginosis): Can cause external dysuria due to urine passing over inflamed labia. A key distinguishing feature is the presence of vaginal discharge, itch, or odour, which are absent in simple cystitis.
  • Overactive Bladder (OAB): Characterised primarily by urinary urgency, often with frequency and nocturia, but typically without dysuria or haematuria. The onset is usually more gradual than the acute presentation of a UTI.
  • Interstitial Cystitis / Bladder Pain Syndrome: A chronic condition characterised by suprapubic pain related to bladder filling, alongside frequency and urgency. The symptoms are long-standing, unlike an acute infection.
  • Urolithiasis (Kidney or Bladder Stones): Can cause frequency, urgency, and haematuria. Pain is often a prominent feature, which may be colicky and radiate from the loin to the groin if a stone is in the ureter.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is guided by symptom severity, patient preference, and adherence to antimicrobial stewardship principles.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Symptomatic Relief: Advise simple analgesia such as paracetamol or ibuprofen to manage pain.
  • Hydration: While often recommended, there is limited evidence for increasing fluid intake. Advise the patient to drink enough fluids to avoid dehydration.
  • Delayed Prescription: For those with mild or non-bothersome symptoms who prefer to avoid antibiotics, a self-care approach with a back-up prescription can be considered, with clear advice on when to start treatment.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Urine Alkalising Agents (e.g., Potassium or Sodium Citrate): These work by reducing the acidity of the urine, which may help to relieve the symptom of dysuria. Evidence for their efficacy is limited. They should be used with caution in patients with renal impairment or on certain antihypertensive medications. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

The choice of antibiotic should be guided by local formulary and resistance patterns.

  • First-line Antibiotics (e.g., Nitrofurantoin): These are recommended for uncomplicated cystitis in women. Nitrofurantoin works by being metabolised by bacterial enzymes into reactive intermediates that inhibit multiple microbial processes. A short course is typically effective. [Rx info]
  • Second-line Antibiotics (e.g., Trimethoprim or Pivmecillinam): Trimethoprim inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, a key enzyme in the bacterial folic acid pathway. It is considered second-line in many UK areas due to high rates of E. coli resistance. Pivmecillinam is a penicillin with good activity against common uropathogens. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management of lower UTIs in women is primarily directed by the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline NG109: 'Urinary tract infection (lower): antimicrobial prescribing'. The guidance emphasises accurate diagnosis, patient-centred care, and responsible antibiotic stewardship.

A key recommendation is to diagnose uncomplicated cystitis in non-pregnant women under 65 based on clinical symptoms alone (if two or more key symptoms are present), without the need for a urine dipstick test. For those with milder symptoms, NICE supports shared decision-making, offering either an immediate antibiotic prescription or a back-up prescription with advice on self-care.

For treatment, NICE recommends a 3-day course of nitrofurantoin or trimethoprim (where resistance rates are low, <10%). Pivmecillinam is also a potential choice. The guidance specifically advises against prescribing amoxicillin or quinolones (like ciprofloxacin) for uncomplicated cystitis due to high resistance rates and unfavourable risk-benefit profiles, respectively. Antibiotic choice should always be tailored using local microbiology data where available.

The guideline also provides specific recommendations for managing UTIs in different populations, including pregnant women and older adults, reinforcing the principles of antimicrobial stewardship across all patient groups to minimise the development of resistance.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Clear communication is essential for managing patient expectations and ensuring safe and effective treatment.

Counselling for Self-Care & Delayed Prescribing

  • Acknowledge Symptoms: Validate the patient's discomfort and explain that while very unpleasant, simple cystitis is not usually a serious condition.
  • Explain Rationale: If offering a delayed prescription, explain that many UTIs resolve on their own and this approach helps reduce antibiotic use. Provide clear criteria for when to start the medication (e.g., "if your symptoms haven't started to improve in 48 hours, or if they get significantly worse").
  • Symptom Management: Advise on using simple analgesia like paracetamol for pain relief. Inform them that symptoms should start to improve within a few days.

Counselling for Prescribed Antibiotics

  • Expected Response: Inform the patient they should start to feel better within 48 hours of starting the antibiotic.
  • Side Effects: Provide key counselling for the specific antibiotic. For example, with nitrofurantoin, warn patients that their urine may turn a dark yellow or brown colour, which is normal and harmless.
  • Completion of Course: Advise them to complete the full, short course of antibiotics as prescribed to ensure the infection is fully treated.
  • Prevention: Briefly discuss simple preventative measures such as wiping front to back, voiding urine after intercourse, and maintaining adequate hydration, although the evidence base for some of these is variable.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Effective safety netting is vital to ensure patients seek timely help if their condition deviates from the expected course of an uncomplicated UTI.

Patients should be given clear, specific instructions to seek further medical advice if they experience any of the following:

  • Symptoms do not start to improve within 48 hours of starting antibiotics (or within 3-4 days if managing with self-care).
  • They become systemically unwell (e.g., developing a fever, chills, or rigors).
  • They develop flank or loin pain, or severe back pain, suggesting the infection may have spread to the kidneys (pyelonephritis).
  • They develop nausea or vomiting which prevents them from keeping down fluids or medications.
  • They notice blood in the urine that persists after the infection has cleared (follow-up required).

For most cases of uncomplicated cystitis, no routine follow-up is necessary. However, a follow-up review should be considered for patients with recurrent infections or those whose symptoms do not resolve as expected. In these cases, a mid-stream urine (MSU) sample should be sent for culture to guide further management.

9. Pathophysiology

Cystitis is predominantly caused by an ascending infection. Uropathogenic bacteria, most commonly Escherichia coli (UPEC) originating from the host's own gut flora, colonise the periurethral area. Due to the short length of the female urethra and its proximity to the anus, these bacteria can ascend into the bladder.

Once in the bladder, bacteria must overcome host defence mechanisms, including the flushing action of micturition and the antimicrobial properties of the urothelial surface. Pathogenic bacteria like E. coli possess virulence factors, such as fimbriae or pili, which allow them to adhere to and invade the urothelial cells lining the bladder wall. This adherence is a critical step, preventing them from being washed out with urine.

The bacterial invasion triggers a potent inflammatory response from the host's innate immune system. The urothelium releases cytokines and chemokines, which recruit neutrophils to the site of infection. This inflammatory cascade leads to the characteristic symptoms of cystitis: vasodilatation and sensory nerve activation cause pain (dysuria) and a feeling of bladder fullness, while inflammation of the trigone leads to irritation and the sensation of urgency and frequency. The presence of bacteria and inflammatory cells results in pyuria, causing the urine to appear cloudy.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

Prescribers must be aware of key contraindications, interactions, and safety alerts for antibiotics used to treat UTIs.

Nitrofurantoin

  • Renal Function: It is contraindicated in patients with an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of less than 45 mL/min/1.73m². It is ineffective in these patients as it requires concentration in the urine, and there is a risk of systemic toxicity.
  • Pregnancy: Can be used during the first and second trimesters but should be avoided at term (36 weeks onwards) due to the risk of neonatal haemolysis.
  • Long-term use: Chronic use (e.g., for prophylaxis) requires monitoring and carries a risk of rare but serious adverse effects, including pulmonary fibrosis and chronic hepatitis. MHRA advises careful monitoring for these.
  • Counselling: Warn patients that it can colour urine dark yellow or brown.

Trimethoprim

  • Resistance: National and local resistance rates for E. coli are high, which is why it is now often considered a second-line agent. Check local guidelines.
  • Pregnancy: It is a folate antagonist and is contraindicated in the first trimester due to the risk of neural tube defects.
  • Interactions: Significant interactions exist. It should be avoided with other folate antagonists like methotrexate. It can also cause hyperkalaemia, especially when co-prescribed with ACE inhibitors, ARBs, or spironolactone; caution and monitoring are required.
11. Management in special populations

Children

This guidance is not for use in children. A suspected UTI in a child under 16 requires a different management approach as per specific NICE guidelines (NG224). It often warrants urine sampling, prompt treatment, and consideration of further investigation to rule out underlying structural abnormalities.

Older adults (especially >65)

Diagnosis can be challenging. Asymptomatic bacteriuria (bacteria in the urine without symptoms) is common and does not require antibiotic treatment. Symptoms may be non-specific or atypical, such as new-onset confusion (delirium), falls, or a general decline in function. Urine dipsticks have very low diagnostic value in this group. A clear clinical picture of infection should be present before considering antibiotics. There is a lower threshold for suspecting sepsis and requiring hospital admission.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

There is a lower threshold for treatment. A mid-stream urine (MSU) sample should always be sent for culture and sensitivity testing. Choice of antibiotic must be safe for pregnancy; nitrofurantoin (avoiding at term) and amoxicillin (if sensitivities are known) are often used. Trimethoprim is contraindicated in the first trimester. Asymptomatic bacteriuria detected during routine antenatal screening should also be treated to reduce risks of pyelonephritis and adverse pregnancy outcomes.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes may be more prone to UTIs and have poorer outcomes. Those with indwelling urinary catheters frequently have bacteriuria; treatment should only be initiated if they are systemically unwell or have clear signs of infection (catheter-associated UTI), not just based on cloudy urine or a positive dipstick. Immunocompromised patients are at higher risk of developing complicated infections and sepsis, warranting a lower threshold for investigation and treatment.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young, Healthy Adult

  • Presentation: A 24-year-old woman presents with a 2-day history of severe stinging on urination and needing to pass urine every hour. She reports no vaginal discharge, fever, or flank pain. She is otherwise well.
  • Assessment: Based on the two classic symptoms (dysuria, frequency) and absence of red flags or vaginal symptoms, a diagnosis of uncomplicated cystitis is made. No dipstick or examination is performed.
  • Management: After discussing options, she is prescribed a 3-day course of nitrofurantoin. The GP counsels her about potential side effects (dark urine) and provides clear safety netting advice to return if symptoms don't improve in 48 hours or if she develops signs of a kidney infection.

Case Study 2: The Ambiguous Presentation

  • Presentation: A 29-year-old woman reports a week of mild dysuria and some suprapubic discomfort. She has also noticed a new vaginal discharge. She has a new sexual partner.
  • Assessment: The presence of vaginal discharge makes a simple UTI less certain. The history raises suspicion for a sexually transmitted infection (e.g., chlamydia) or vaginitis (e.g., thrush, BV).
  • Diagnosis & Action: The clinician explains the diagnostic uncertainty. A urine sample is sent for culture (MSU) and a full STI screen, including vaginal swabs, is offered. While awaiting results, the patient is given advice on simple analgesia. Management is then tailored to the results of the investigations.

Case Study 3: The Older Adult with Atypical Symptoms

  • Presentation: A care home calls about an 82-year-old woman with known dementia who has become acutely more confused and agitated over the past 24 hours. She has not been eating and has been incontinent of urine.
  • Assessment: The GP assesses her immediately. She has a temperature of 38.1°C, a pulse of 110 bpm, and a respiratory rate of 22/min. Her blood pressure is stable. While a urine dipstick is positive for leucocytes and nitrites, the diagnosis is based on the clinical picture of new-onset delirium with a likely source of infection.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The presentation suggests sepsis secondary to a UTI (urosepsis). A urine sample is obtained for culture. Given the signs of sepsis, the GP arranges for urgent hospital admission for intravenous antibiotics and further management.
