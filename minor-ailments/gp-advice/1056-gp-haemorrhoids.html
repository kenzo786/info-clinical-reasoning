Haemorrhoids in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Haemorrhoids (piles) are a very common reason for consultation in primary care. They are pathologically described as the symptomatic enlargement and distal displacement of the normal anal vascular cushions. Many are asymptomatic, but patients typically present due to bleeding, discomfort, pruritus, or the sensation of a lump.

The cardinal feature of haemorrhoids is painless, bright red rectal bleeding, often seen on toilet paper after wiping or noticed dripping into the toilet bowl after defecation. Internal haemorrhoids can prolapse, presenting as a reducible or irreducible lump. External haemorrhoids may present as a painful, thrombosed lump. Symptoms are often intermittent and can be triggered by constipation, straining during defecation, or prolonged sitting.

In most instances, haemorrhoids are a benign condition that can be managed with lifestyle changes and simple treatments. The role of the clinician is to confirm the diagnosis, exclude more serious pathology through a focused assessment, and provide advice on self-care and management options.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most rectal bleeding in younger patients is due to benign causes, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying pathology, primarily colorectal cancer. An urgent 2-week wait referral is indicated for suspected colorectal cancer.

  • Age-related criteria: Unexplained rectal bleeding in a person aged 40 or over with loose stools or increased stool frequency for >6 weeks. Anyone aged 50 or over with unexplained rectal bleeding. Anyone aged 60 or over with a change in bowel habit or signs of anaemia.
  • Change in bowel habit: Any persistent change in bowel habit, especially to looser stools or increased frequency, lasting for 6 weeks or more, particularly in older adults.
  • Weight loss and appetite: Unexplained and unintentional weight loss, often accompanied by loss of appetite.
  • Abdominal or rectal mass: A palpable mass in the abdomen or rectum identified on clinical examination is a significant red flag.
  • Anaemia: Unexplained iron deficiency anaemia (low haemoglobin, low ferritin) or microcytic anaemia.
  • Severe or persistent symptoms: Very heavy or continuous rectal bleeding, or symptoms that persist despite primary care treatment. Severe pain may suggest an alternative diagnosis like a fissure or abscess.
  • Tenesmus: A persistent feeling of needing to pass stool, even after the bowel is empty. This requires investigation.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is essential to differentiate benign haemorrhoids from more serious conditions.

History Taking

  • Bleeding characteristics: Confirm it is fresh, bright red blood. Ask about quantity (streaks on paper, in the pan) and its relation to defecation. Dark, altered blood (melaena) suggests an upper GI source.
  • Associated Symptoms: Ask about pain (haemorrhoids are usually painless unless thrombosed; severe pain suggests a fissure), itching (pruritus ani), lumps (prolapse), and discharge.
  • Bowel Habit: Explicitly ask about any recent or persistent changes in frequency, consistency, or calibre of stools. Note any history of constipation or straining.
  • Systemic Enquiry: Screen for red flag symptoms like unintentional weight loss, abdominal pain, fatigue, and loss of appetite.
  • Past Medical and Family History: Enquire about personal or family history of bowel cancer, IBD, or polyps.

Clinical Examination

  • Abdominal Examination: To assess for any palpable masses or tenderness.
  • Perianal Inspection: Look for external haemorrhoids, skin tags, fissures, or other lesions. Asking the patient to bear down (Valsalva manoeuvre) may demonstrate prolapsing internal haemorrhoids.
  • Digital Rectal Examination (DRE): Should be performed unless acutely painful. Assess for sphincter tone, palpable masses within the rectum, and tenderness. Note the presence of blood or stool on the glove.
  • Proctoscopy: Can be useful in primary care to directly visualise internal haemorrhoids but is not mandatory if the history and examination are reassuring.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for rectal bleeding and anal symptoms is broad. The clinical assessment aims to distinguish common benign causes from serious pathology.

Common Benign Causes

  • Anal Fissure: A tear in the anal canal lining. Characterised by severe, sharp pain during and after defecation, often with minimal bright red bleeding. Pain is the dominant symptom.
  • Pruritus Ani: Intense itching around the anus. It can be idiopathic or secondary to haemorrhoids, poor hygiene, or skin conditions like eczema.
  • Skin Tags: Benign fleshy growths around the anus, often residual from previous thrombosed external haemorrhoids.
  • Proctitis: Inflammation of the rectal lining, can be due to IBD or infection, causing bleeding, discharge, and tenesmus.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Colorectal Cancer: The most important differential. More likely with red flags: age >40, change in bowel habit, weight loss, abdominal mass, or iron deficiency anaemia. Bleeding may be mixed in with the stool.
  • Rectal Polyps: Benign growths that can bleed and have malignant potential. Indistinguishable from cancer based on symptoms alone.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis can present with bloody diarrhoea, abdominal pain, weight loss, and systemic symptoms.
  • Anal Cancer: A rare cause, may present as an anal lump, pain, bleeding, or persistent ulceration. Often associated with HPV infection.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is stepwise, starting with conservative measures. Re-evaluate if symptoms do not resolve after initial treatment.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Dietary Modification: Increase intake of dietary fibre (fruit, vegetables, wholegrains) and ensure adequate fluid intake (1.5-2L/day) to soften stools and prevent straining.
  • Toilet Habits: Advise against straining and prolonged sitting on the toilet. Encourage attending to the urge to defecate as soon as it is felt.
  • Anal Hygiene: Recommend gentle cleaning after bowel movements with water or moist wipes instead of dry toilet paper, and patting the area dry.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Soaking in a warm bath can soothe discomfort and pruritus.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Soothing Preparations: Ointments, creams, and suppositories containing mild astringents (e.g., zinc oxide) or emollients can provide symptomatic relief. [Rx info]
  • Local Anaesthetics: Preparations containing lidocaine or cinchocaine can relieve pain and discomfort, but should only be used for short periods (up to 7 days) to avoid skin sensitisation. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Corticosteroids: Combination products containing a mild corticosteroid (e.g., hydrocortisone) and a local anaesthetic are effective for reducing inflammation and pruritus. Use should be limited to 7-14 days to minimise risk of skin atrophy. [Rx info]
  • Laxatives: A bulk-forming laxative (e.g., ispaghula husk) can be prescribed if dietary fibre is insufficient to achieve soft stools. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of haemorrhoids in the UK is primarily guided by NICE CKS (Clinical Knowledge Summaries). The guidance emphasises a conservative-first approach.

The core principles are to manage constipation and straining through lifestyle and dietary advice. If this is insufficient, topical preparations can be used for symptomatic relief. NICE guidance is clear on the need to exclude colorectal cancer, providing specific criteria for an urgent 2-week wait referral for suspected cases. These criteria are based on symptoms, age, and clinical findings.

For persistent or severe symptomatic haemorrhoids (e.g., second or third-degree haemorrhoids that remain symptomatic despite primary care treatment), NICE recommends referral to a colorectal surgeon for consideration of procedures like rubber band ligation, sclerotherapy, or surgery.

There is no role for antibiotics in the management of uncomplicated haemorrhoids. The evidence for most topical treatments is based on symptom relief, and they do not 'cure' the underlying haemorrhoid.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to managing haemorrhoids, particularly in providing reassurance and encouraging lifestyle changes.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Condition: Describe haemorrhoids as normal vascular cushions that have become swollen, often due to straining. Reassure the patient that they are very common and usually benign.
  • Lifestyle is Key: Emphasise that the most effective long-term strategy is managing diet and toilet habits to prevent constipation and straining. Explain that creams and ointments provide temporary relief but do not address the root cause.
  • Address Cancer Concerns: If red flags have been excluded, it is important to explicitly reassure the patient that their symptoms are not suggestive of cancer. This can significantly reduce anxiety.
  • Explain Treatment Use: Advise on how to apply topical treatments correctly (e.g., using a nozzle for internal application) and for how long, especially for those containing steroids or local anaesthetics.

Counselling for Referral

  • Explain Referral Reason: If referring under the 2-week wait pathway, explain clearly that this is to rule out a serious condition like bowel cancer and that for most people, the tests will be normal. Provide written information about the referral and subsequent investigations.
  • Managing Expectations for Procedures: If referring for a procedure like banding, explain what it involves in simple terms and the potential outcomes and risks.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients seek help appropriately if their condition changes or fails to improve.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The bleeding becomes significantly heavier, is dark in colour, or is mixed in with the stool.
  • The symptoms do not improve or worsen after 2-3 weeks of active treatment.
  • They develop any new 'red flag' symptoms, such as:
    • A persistent change in their bowel habit (lasting more than a few weeks).
    • Unexplained weight loss or loss of appetite.
    • Severe or worsening abdominal pain.
    • Feeling unusually tired, dizzy, or breathless (symptoms of anaemia).
  • They develop severe, acute anal pain, which could indicate a thrombosed haemorrhoid or an anal fissure.

For patients who have been managed conservatively in primary care, a routine follow-up is not usually required unless symptoms persist. Patients referred under a 2-week wait pathway should be advised to expect contact from the hospital within that timeframe.

9. Pathophysiology

Haemorrhoids are not varicose veins. They are a result of the disruption of the normal anatomy of the anal cushions. These cushions are discrete submucosal pads containing blood vessels, smooth muscle (Treitz's muscle), and connective tissue, located in the anal canal. They are part of the normal continence mechanism, contributing to the complete closure of the anal canal at rest.

The primary theory behind haemorrhoid development is the deterioration of the supporting connective tissues. Chronically elevated intra-abdominal pressure, most commonly from straining during defecation due to constipation, but also from pregnancy or chronic coughing, leads to venous engorgement of the cushions. This sustained pressure causes stretching and eventual fragmentation of the supporting connective and muscular fibres.

This process leads to the downward displacement and prolapse of the anal cushions into the anal canal or externally. The thin-walled blood vessels within the cushions become susceptible to trauma from passing hard stools, resulting in the characteristic bright red bleeding. If prolapsed tissue becomes trapped by the anal sphincter, venous return can be obstructed, leading to acute, painful thrombosis.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is used, it is important to be aware of limitations and potential side effects.

Topical Corticosteroids (with Local Anaesthetics)

  • Duration of Use: These preparations should not be used for prolonged periods. NICE CKS recommends limiting use to 7 days. Prolonged use of topical steroids in the perianal region can cause skin atrophy, thinning, and contact dermatitis, which can worsen symptoms like itching.
  • Contraindications: Avoid using in the presence of untreated fungal, bacterial, or viral skin infections, as steroids can mask and worsen the condition. Use with caution if the skin is broken or ulcerated.

Local Anaesthetics (e.g., Lidocaine, Cinchocaine)

  • Risk of Sensitisation: Prolonged use can lead to allergic contact dermatitis (skin sensitisation), presenting as worsening redness and itching. Their use should be limited to a maximum of 7 days.
  • Masking Symptoms: While providing effective pain relief, they can mask the worsening of a condition. It is important that safety netting advice is robust.

Bulk-Forming Laxatives (e.g., Ispaghula husk)

  • Administration: Must be taken with plenty of fluid to avoid causing oesophageal or intestinal obstruction.
  • Side Effects: Can initially cause bloating and flatulence. They may take 2-3 days to exert their full effect.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Haemorrhoids are very rare in children. Rectal bleeding in a child should always be taken seriously and warrants a thorough assessment. Commoner causes include anal fissure (often from constipation) or juvenile polyps. Referral to paediatrics is usually indicated to establish a firm diagnosis and rule out more serious pathology.

Older adults

There must be a high index of suspicion for colorectal cancer in older adults presenting with rectal bleeding or other anorectal symptoms. The threshold for a 2-week wait referral should be low and guided by NICE NG12 criteria. Polypharmacy is common, so consider medications that may cause constipation (e.g., opioids, calcium channel blockers). DRE is a key part of the assessment.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Haemorrhoids are extremely common during pregnancy and the post-partum period, due to hormonal effects on connective tissue and increased pressure from the gravid uterus. Management should focus on non-pharmacological measures first. Most topical treatments, including those with mild corticosteroids and local anaesthetics, are considered safe for short-term use as systemic absorption is minimal. However, patients should always be advised to check with a pharmacist or doctor. Dietary management of constipation is paramount.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients on anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy (e.g., warfarin, apixaban, clopidogrel), rectal bleeding may be more pronounced. The clinical significance of this needs careful assessment. For patients with IBD, new or changing anorectal symptoms may signify a flare of their underlying condition or a complication, and discussion with their gastroenterology team may be appropriate.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young Woman with Post-Natal Piles

  • Presentation: A 30-year-old woman presents 6 weeks post-partum. She reports painful defecation and bright red blood on the toilet paper. She has noticed a small, tender lump externally.
  • Assessment: She is otherwise well with no red flag symptoms. Inspection reveals a small, thrombosed external haemorrhoid. Given the acute pain, a DRE is deferred.
  • Diagnosis: Acutely thrombosed external haemorrhoid, secondary to pregnancy and childbirth.
  • Management: The GP reassures her this is very common. She is advised on a high-fibre diet and increased fluid intake. She is also advised on warm salt baths for soothing, and is prescribed a short course (7 days) of a combination topical preparation containing a local anaesthetic and corticosteroid. Clear safety netting is given to return if symptoms don't resolve or worsen.

Case Study 2: The Older Man with New Symptoms

  • Presentation: A 65-year-old man presents with a 2-month history of seeing blood on the toilet paper. He also mentions his stools have been looser than normal over this period and he has lost about 4kg in weight without trying.
  • Assessment: He appears pale. Abdominal examination reveals some vague tenderness in the left iliac fossa but no discrete mass. A DRE is performed and is unremarkable, with no palpable rectal mass.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The combination of age (>60), rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, and unintentional weight loss constitutes multiple red flags for colorectal cancer. The GP explains the need for urgent investigation to rule out a serious problem and makes an urgent 2-week wait referral to colorectal surgery for a colonoscopy, as per NICE guidelines. Blood tests including FBC and ferritin are also arranged.
