Contact Dermatitis in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Contact dermatitis is a common inflammatory skin condition resulting from direct contact with a particular substance. It presents as a localised eczematous reaction. There are two main types: irritant contact dermatitis (ICD) and allergic contact dermatitis (ACD). ICD is the most common form, caused by damage to the skin's barrier by substances like soaps, detergents, solvents, or even frequent contact with water. ACD is a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction (Type IV) that occurs when a sensitised individual is re-exposed to an allergen, such as nickel, fragrances, preservatives, or certain plants.

Patients typically present with symptoms of redness, itching, and a burning or stinging sensation in the affected area. The skin may become vesicular (blistered), dry, scaly, cracked, or fissured. In lighter skin, the rash is typically red, while in darker skin tones, it may appear as dark brown, purple, or grey patches. The distribution of the rash is a key clue, often corresponding directly to the area of exposure (e.g., on the hands from "wet work", under jewellery, or on the face from cosmetics). While ICD symptoms can appear quickly after contact with a strong irritant, ACD usually develops 24-72 hours after exposure. The condition is not contagious but can significantly impact a patient's quality of life and ability to perform occupational tasks.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of contact dermatitis can be managed in primary care, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious condition or complication.

  • Signs of severe secondary bacterial infection: Such as rapidly spreading erythema, significant pain, purulent discharge, systemic fever, or malaise. This may indicate cellulitis or impetigo requiring systemic antibiotics.
  • Eczema herpeticum: A widespread, painful, vesicular rash, often with "punched-out" erosions and systemic illness. This is a viral infection (herpes simplex) superimposed on eczema and is a dermatological emergency requiring antiviral treatment.
  • Severe or extensive reaction: Dermatitis covering a large surface area of the body, causing significant blistering, or associated with facial or eyelid swelling that could compromise vision or breathing.
  • Failure to respond to potent topical therapy: If the dermatitis is severe and does not improve with appropriate management, including potent topical corticosteroids, referral to dermatology for consideration of patch testing and alternative diagnoses is warranted.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the diagnosis is unclear and conditions like cutaneous T-cell lymphoma, psoriasis, or bullous pemphigoid are considered, referral is necessary.
  • Severe occupational dermatitis: Cases causing significant functional impairment or preventing an individual from working may require specialist occupational dermatology assessment and management.
  • Contact urticaria with systemic symptoms: If immediate swelling or wheals occur upon contact with a substance (e.g., latex) and are associated with systemic symptoms like breathlessness or wheezing, this suggests a Type I hypersensitivity reaction (anaphylaxis) and is a medical emergency.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A thorough history is the cornerstone of diagnosing contact dermatitis and identifying the causative agent.

History Taking

  • Symptom characteristics: Ascertain onset, duration, and site of the rash. Ask about itch, pain, or burning. Clarify if symptoms are intermittent or persistent.
  • Distribution and evolution: Map the rash's location and ask if it has spread or changed. The pattern often points to the cause (e.g., linear streaks from plants, demarcation at clothing lines).
  • Potential triggers: Take a detailed "allergy-focused" history. Ask about new products (soaps, shampoos, cosmetics, perfumes), jewellery, clothing, and hobbies.
  • Occupational history: This is critical. Enquire about the patient's job, specific tasks, substances handled, and use of personal protective equipment (PPE) like gloves. Ask if the rash improves when away from work (e.g., on weekends or holidays).
  • Past Medical History: Note any personal or family history of atopy (asthma, hay fever, atopic eczema), as this predisposes to irritant dermatitis.
  • Treatments tried: Ask about all previous treatments, including OTC preparations, prescribed medications, and complementary therapies, and their effect.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Note the patient's comfort and any signs of systemic illness.
  • Skin Examination: Carefully assess the affected skin, noting morphology (erythema, papules, vesicles, scaling, lichenification, fissuring) and distribution. Check for signs of secondary bacterial infection (weeping, crusting, pustules).
  • Examine relevant areas: Inspect hands (including web spaces and under rings), wrists, face, eyelids, neck, and feet, as these are common sites.

Scoring Tools

Unlike pneumonia, there are no universally adopted simple scoring tools like CRB-65 for contact dermatitis in primary care. Assessment is based on clinical history and examination findings. In secondary care, a dermatologist may use severity scores like the Eczema Area and Severity Index (EASI), but this is not routine in general practice.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The clinical picture of contact dermatitis can overlap with several other common skin conditions. The history and distribution are key to differentiation.

Common Differentials

  • Atopic Eczema: Often has a typical distribution in flexures (e.g., antecubital and popliteal fossae), face, and neck. It usually starts in childhood and is associated with a personal or family history of atopy.
  • Seborrhoeic Dermatitis: Affects areas rich in sebaceous glands, such as the scalp, face (eyebrows, nasolabial folds), and central chest. It typically presents with greasy, yellowish scale on an erythematous background.
  • Discoid (Nummular) Eczema: Presents as well-demarcated, coin-shaped, weeping or dry plaques, often on the limbs. It can be very itchy.
  • Psoriasis: Characterised by well-defined, erythematous plaques with silvery scale, typically on extensor surfaces (knees, elbows), scalp, and lower back. Nail changes like pitting may be present.
  • Tinea (Fungal Infection): Presents as an annular (ring-like) lesion with a raised, active, scaling edge and central clearing. Skin scrapings for mycology can confirm the diagnosis.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Cellulitis: A bacterial skin infection presenting with spreading erythema, warmth, swelling, and pain. The patient is often systemically unwell with fever. Borders are typically less well-defined than in dermatitis.
  • Scabies: An intensely itchy infestation, with pruritus often worse at night. Look for burrows, particularly in finger web spaces, wrists, and genitalia. Other family members are often affected.
  • Cutaneous T-cell Lymphoma (Mycosis Fungoides): A rare skin cancer that can mimic eczema for many years. It presents as persistent, itchy, scaly patches that may progress to plaques and tumours. Suspect in cases of chronic, treatment-resistant "eczema".
  • Dermatitis Herpetiformis: An intensely itchy, blistering rash associated with coeliac disease. It typically affects extensor surfaces symmetrically.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is centred on identifying and avoiding the causative agent, restoring the skin barrier, and reducing inflammation.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Allergen/Irritant Avoidance: This is the most crucial step. Provide specific advice based on the suspected cause (e.g., using nickel-free jewellery, changing cleaning products).
  • Skin Protection: Advise on the use of appropriate gloves for wet work or when handling chemicals. For prolonged glove use, cotton liners can prevent sweat-induced irritation.
  • Emollients and Soap Substitutes: Encourage frequent and liberal use of leave-on emollients (creams, ointments) to moisturise and repair the skin barrier. Advise using an emollient wash or soap substitute, as regular soaps are drying and alkaline.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Emollients: A wide range of creams, ointments, and lotions are available. Ointments are more occlusive and best for very dry, thickened skin, while creams are less greasy and often preferred for daytime use or inflamed areas. [Rx info]
  • Mild Topical Corticosteroids: Hydrocortisone 1% cream or ointment can be effective for mild dermatitis on the face or thin-skinned areas. It acts by reducing inflammation. Patients should be advised on correct application (thinly, to affected areas only, for a limited time). [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Corticosteroids: These are the mainstay for reducing inflammation in active dermatitis. Potency is chosen based on the severity of the dermatitis and the body site (e.g., moderate potency for the trunk/limbs, potent for palms/soles). They should be applied once daily for a defined period until the flare settles. [Rx info]
  • Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (e.g., Tacrolimus, Pimecrolimus): These are second-line agents prescribed by specialists or GPs with a specialist interest for use on sensitive sites like the face and eyelids where steroid side effects are a concern. They modulate the immune response to reduce inflammation. [Rx info]
  • Oral Corticosteroids: Rarely used in primary care but may be considered by a specialist for very severe, widespread acute contact dermatitis. [Rx info]
  • Antibiotics: Only indicated for the treatment of clinically evident secondary bacterial infection, not for uncomplicated dermatitis.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by NICE CKS (Clinical Knowledge Summaries) on Contact Dermatitis and Atopic Eczema, and guidance from the British Association of Dermatologists. The core principles are consistent across these resources.

The cornerstone of management is identifying and removing the causative agent (irritant or allergen). This is emphasised as the single most important intervention. Following this, the restoration of the skin barrier function through liberal use of emollients and soap substitutes is fundamental. Guidelines stress that this should be ongoing, even when the skin appears clear.

For active inflammation, guidelines recommend a stepped approach to topical corticosteroids. The principle is to use the least potent steroid that is effective, for the shortest possible time, to clear the flare-up ("get in, get it cleared, get out"). The choice of potency should be tailored to the patient's age, the body site affected, and the severity of the dermatitis. NICE advises on the appropriate potencies for different scenarios.

The guidelines do not recommend the routine use of antihistamines for the itch of contact dermatitis, as the itch is not primarily histamine-driven. Similarly, antibiotics should only be used where there are clear clinical signs of bacterial infection, in line with antimicrobial stewardship principles. Referral for patch testing by a dermatologist is recommended if an allergic cause is suspected and the allergen is not obvious from the history, or if the dermatitis is failing to respond to treatment.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to successful management, empowering the patient to take control of their condition.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Diagnosis: Clearly differentiate between irritant and allergic dermatitis if possible. Explain that it is an external reaction, not an internal disease, and is not contagious.
  • Emphasise Avoidance: Stress that avoiding the trigger is the most important part of treatment. Work with the patient to identify likely culprits in their daily routine, work, or hobbies.
  • Teach Emollient Use: Explain the 'why' and 'how'. Emollients repair the skin's barrier. Advise them to apply liberally and frequently (at least 2-3 times a day) all over, not just on the rash. Demonstrate using a soap substitute for all washing. Warn that emollient-soaked fabrics are a fire hazard.
  • Manage Expectations: Inform patients that improvement takes time and diligence. The skin can remain sensitive for several months even after the rash has cleared, and continued emollient use is vital to prevent recurrence.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Topical Steroids: Explain that these are safe and effective when used correctly. Address any patient fears about 'steroids'. Demonstrate the correct amount to use (e.g., the Fingertip Unit). Advise applying a thin layer only to the red, inflamed areas once a day until the flare settles, and then stopping. Explain the 'emollient first, wait 30 minutes, then steroid' rule.
  • Side Effects: Briefly counsel on potential side effects of potent steroids if used incorrectly or for too long, such as skin thinning.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is essential to ensure patients know when to seek further help and to confirm the diagnosis and treatment effectiveness.

Clear verbal and written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The rash spreads rapidly or becomes much more painful.
  • The skin starts to weep pus, develops yellow crusts, or they feel feverish and unwell (signs of significant infection).
  • They develop blisters that are widespread or painful (potential eczema herpeticum).
  • The skin is not starting to improve after 1-2 weeks of using the prescribed treatment correctly.
  • The dermatitis clears with treatment but quickly returns when it is stopped.
  • They develop any other symptom that they are concerned about.

A planned follow-up appointment, either face-to-face or via telephone, is often appropriate 2-4 weeks after initiating treatment. This allows the clinician to assess the response, reinforce education on avoidance and emollient use, review topical steroid use, and consider referral if the condition is not resolving as expected.

9. Pathophysiology

Contact dermatitis is broadly divided into two distinct pathophysiological processes: irritant and allergic.

Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD): This is a non-immunological, inflammatory reaction resulting from direct cytotoxic damage to keratinocytes. Irritants disrupt the skin's lipid barrier, increase transepidermal water loss, and damage cell membranes. This leads to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (like IL-1 and TNF-α) from skin cells, causing inflammation, erythema, and impaired barrier function. The reaction is dose-dependent; strong irritants (e.g., acids) cause an acute reaction, while weaker, cumulative irritants (e.g., water, detergents) cause chronic damage. It does not require prior sensitisation and can affect anyone.

Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD): This is a classic Type IV (delayed) T-cell mediated hypersensitivity reaction. It occurs in two phases. In the sensitisation phase, a low-molecular-weight chemical (hapten) penetrates the skin and binds to an endogenous protein, forming a hapten-protein complex. This complex is recognised as foreign by Langerhans cells (antigen-presenting cells), which process it and migrate to regional lymph nodes. Here, they present the antigen to naive T-cells, leading to their activation and proliferation, creating a population of memory T-cells. This phase is asymptomatic.

The elicitation phase occurs upon re-exposure to the same allergen. The allergen is processed by Langerhans cells, which present it to the sensitised memory T-cells in the skin. These T-cells are rapidly activated and release pro-inflammatory cytokines (like interferon-gamma and IL-17), which recruit other inflammatory cells. This results in the clinical signs of dermatitis, typically appearing 24-72 hours after re-exposure.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, it is important to be aware of specific contraindications and best practices for common treatments.

Emollients

  • Fire Hazard: Emollients are not flammable in themselves, but when they soak into fabrics (dressings, bedding, clothing) that fabric can ignite more easily. Counsel patients not to smoke or go near naked flames when using them.
  • Formulation Sensitivity: Occasionally, a patient may be sensitive to a component within an emollient, such as a preservative or fragrance (in cosmetic moisturisers). If an emollient appears to be irritating the skin, advise switching to an alternative, plainer formulation. Aqueous cream is no longer recommended as a leave-on emollient as it can irritate the skin.
  • Cross-contamination: Advise patients not to share pots of emollient and to use a clean spoon or spatula to remove the product, rather than their fingers, to reduce the risk of contamination.

Topical Corticosteroids

  • Contraindications: Do not use on untreated bacterial, fungal, or viral skin infections as they can worsen the condition. Avoid on areas of ulcerated skin.
  • Adverse Effects: The main concern is skin atrophy (thinning), which can lead to striae and telangiectasia. This risk is highest with potent steroids used for long periods, under occlusion, or on thin-skinned areas like the face, flexures, and genitalia. Other potential local side effects include perioral dermatitis and acneiform eruptions. Systemic absorption is rare with appropriate use but can occur with prolonged use of potent steroids over large areas.
  • Application: Advise applying once daily, as evidence shows this is as effective as twice daily application for most preparations. Apply a thin layer to inflamed skin only. The Fingertip Unit (FTU) is a useful guide for the quantity to apply.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Contact dermatitis is less common than atopic eczema in young children but should be considered, especially for localised rashes (e.g., nappy area dermatitis from irritants, or facial rash from food contact). Nickel allergy from jean studs or buckles is also seen. Management principles are similar: avoidance, emollients, and judicious use of topical corticosteroids. Use the lowest effective potency of steroid for the shortest time; mild-to-moderate potency is usually sufficient. Referral for patch testing may be considered in persistent cases.

Older adults

Older adults have thinner, more fragile skin, making them more susceptible to irritant dermatitis and skin trauma. Polypharmacy increases the risk of drug-induced rashes that can mimic dermatitis. Management should focus heavily on gentle skin care, soap substitutes, and liberal emollient use. Use topical steroids with caution, preferring lower potencies where possible to minimise the risk of skin atrophy and purpura. Assess for and manage any co-existing conditions like venous stasis which can cause gravitational eczema.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Management focuses on avoidance and emollients as first-line. If required, topical corticosteroids are considered safe for use during pregnancy and breastfeeding, as systemic absorption is minimal with correct application. Mild to moderate potency steroids are preferred. Advise applying to the smallest possible area for the shortest time. If applying to the nipple/areola area when breastfeeding, it should be wiped off thoroughly before feeding the infant. High potency steroids should only be used under specialist advice.

People with significant comorbidities

In patients with conditions like diabetes or peripheral vascular disease, skin integrity is already compromised, increasing the risk of both dermatitis and secondary infection. A lower threshold for investigating and treating secondary infection is appropriate. For immunocompromised patients (e.g., on chemotherapy or biologics), skin rashes are common, and an accurate diagnosis is vital. Be aware that their inflammatory response may be altered. Any new or persistent rash in an immunocompromised patient warrants careful evaluation to exclude infection or a cutaneous manifestation of their underlying disease.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Healthcare Worker

  • Presentation: A 35-year-old practice nurse presents with a 3-month history of dry, cracked, and itchy skin on the dorsum of both hands. It is red and sore, with some minor fissuring over the knuckles. It improves slightly on her days off.
  • Assessment: History reveals frequent handwashing and use of alcohol gel at work. She has a history of childhood eczema. Examination shows erythema, lichenification, and scaling on the back of her hands, consistent with chronic dermatitis. There are no signs of infection.
  • Diagnosis: Chronic irritant contact dermatitis, secondary to occupational "wet work", on a background of atopic diathesis.
  • Management: The GP explains the cause is damage to the skin barrier from frequent washing. They prescribe a moderate potency topical corticosteroid to use once daily for 2 weeks to settle the inflammation. They stress the importance of ongoing, liberal use of an emollient cream throughout the day and using a soap substitute. She is advised to dry her hands thoroughly after washing and apply emollient immediately. Safety netting advice is given to return if it fails to improve or shows signs of infection.

Case Study 2: The New Earrings

  • Presentation: A 19-year-old student presents with an intensely itchy, weeping rash on both earlobes that started two days ago. She recently bought some cheap fashion earrings from a market stall and wore them for the first time three days prior.
  • Assessment: On examination, there is a well-demarcated area of erythema, oedema, and vesiculation on both earlobes, corresponding exactly to where the earrings were located.
  • Diagnosis: Acute allergic contact dermatitis, most likely due to nickel in the earrings.
  • Management: The GP reassures her this is a common allergy. She is advised to immediately stop wearing the earrings and avoid all non-precious metal jewellery in future. A mild-to-moderate potency topical corticosteroid cream is prescribed for once-daily use for 7-10 days. She is advised to use a soap substitute for washing. The GP explains the reaction may take a few days to settle and to return if it worsens or spreads. The lifelong nature of the allergy is explained.
