Earache (Otalgia) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Earache (otalgia) is a very common reason for consultation in primary care, particularly in children. It can be caused by pathology in the ear itself (primary otalgia) or be referred from other anatomical sites (secondary otalgia). The two most common primary causes are acute otitis media (AOM) and otitis externa (OE).

AOM typically presents with rapid-onset ear pain, often following symptoms of a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI). In young children, this may manifest as non-specific signs like fever, pulling at the ear, irritability, and poor feeding. The condition is usually self-limiting, with most cases resolving within 3-7 days. OE presents with pain, itching, discharge, and a feeling of fullness in the ear canal. It is often triggered by swimming ('swimmer's ear'), trauma from cotton buds, or eczematous skin conditions.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of earache are benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent review or immediate hospital admission to an ENT specialist.

  • Signs of mastoiditis: Swelling, tenderness, or redness over the mastoid process (the bone behind the ear), with displacement of the pinna forwards and outwards. This suggests spread of infection from the middle ear to the mastoid bone and requires emergency admission.
  • Facial nerve palsy (cranial nerve VII): New onset of facial asymmetry or weakness (e.g., drooping of one side of the face) in the context of an ear infection is a sign of serious complication and needs urgent assessment.
  • Sudden onset sensorineural hearing loss: This is an otological emergency. If not clearly conductive loss from effusion or canal swelling, it requires immediate referral.
  • Necrotising (malignant) otitis externa: Suspect in immunocompromised (especially diabetic) or elderly patients with severe, deep, nocturnal pain disproportionate to clinical signs. Granulation tissue may be visible in the ear canal. This is an aggressive infection of the skull base requiring emergency admission.
  • Systemic signs of severe illness: High fever (>39°C), rigors, or altered mental state may indicate sepsis or intracranial complications like meningitis or a brain abscess.
  • Perichondritis: Inflammation of the cartilage of the pinna, often red, swollen and very tender. This can lead to cartilage necrosis ('cauliflower ear') if not treated promptly.
  • Foreign body in the ear: Particularly if it is a battery, which can cause rapid tissue necrosis, or if it cannot be easily removed in primary care.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is essential to determine the cause of earache and rule out serious pathology.

History Taking

  • Pain Characteristics: Ascertain onset, duration, severity, and character (e.g., sharp, dull, deep). Ask about relieving/exacerbating factors. Pain on moving the outer ear (pinna/tragus) is typical of otitis externa.
  • Associated Symptoms: Enquire about fever, ear discharge (otorrhoea), hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo, cough, or coryzal symptoms.
  • Recent Events: Ask about recent URTIs, swimming, air travel, or any trauma to the ear, including use of cotton buds.
  • Past Medical History: Note any previous ear problems, eczema/psoriasis, diabetes, or immunosuppression.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about swelling behind the ear, facial weakness, severe headache, neck stiffness, and systemic upset.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of systemic illness or distress.
  • Head and Neck Examination: Look at the pinna for signs of perichondritis. Palpate the mastoid process for tenderness and the tragus for pain. Check for facial nerve symmetry. Palpate for tender cervical lymph nodes.
  • Otoscopy:
    • Otitis Externa: The ear canal appears red, swollen, and may be filled with debris or discharge. The tympanic membrane (TM) may be obscured.
    • Acute Otitis Media: The TM is typically red, bulging, and may lose its normal light reflex. An air-fluid level or bullae may be visible. Perforation can lead to discharge in the canal.
    • Normal TM: If the ear canal and TM are normal, consider referred pain from teeth, the temporomandibular joint (TMJ), or the pharynx.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature, particularly if the patient appears unwell.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for earache is broad, but can be narrowed by a focused history and examination, particularly otoscopy.

Common Primary Causes (Abnormal Otoscopy)

  • Acute Otitis Media (AOM): Very common in children. Associated with URTI symptoms. Otoscopy shows a red, bulging eardrum.
  • Otitis Externa (OE): Common in adults, especially swimmers. Characterised by pain on moving the pinna/tragus and a swollen, inflamed ear canal.
  • Eustachian Tube Dysfunction: Often causes a blocked feeling or 'popping' rather than severe pain. May follow a cold. Otoscopy may show a retracted TM.
  • Earwax impaction: Can cause discomfort, fullness, and conductive hearing loss.

Common Secondary Causes (Normal Otoscopy)

  • Referred Pain: Pain is referred to the ear via shared nerve pathways. Common sources include:
    • Dental problems: Caries, abscesses, or erupting wisdom teeth.
    • Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) dysfunction: Associated with jaw clicking, clenching, and tenderness over the joint.
    • Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis: A sore throat is the primary symptom.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Mastoiditis: Post-auricular swelling, tenderness, and pinna displacement.
  • Necrotising Otitis Externa: Severe pain in an at-risk individual (diabetic/immunocompromised).
  • Temporal Arteritis: In older adults (>50), consider if associated with headache, scalp tenderness, or jaw claudication.
  • Nasopharyngeal or Laryngeal Cancer: Persistent, unexplained unilateral earache in an older adult, especially with other red flags like dysphagia or hoarseness, requires urgent investigation.
5. Management options in primary care

Management depends on the underlying cause. For common conditions like AOM and OE, the focus is on analgesia and targeted antimicrobial use.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain the likely diagnosis and natural history (e.g., AOM usually resolves in 3 days).
  • Pain Relief: Advise that regular, adequate analgesia is the most important treatment.
  • Ear Canal Care (for OE): Advise keeping the ear dry during treatment (e.g., use cotton wool with petroleum jelly when showering). Avoid swimming and inserting anything into the ear canal.
  • Warm Compress: A warm flannel held against the ear can be soothing.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol or ibuprofen should be advised first-line for pain relief in both AOM and OE.
  • Acetic Acid 2% Spray: Can be effective for mild otitis externa, helping to restore the canal's natural acidic pH. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Antibiotic/Corticosteroid Preparations: First-line for moderate-to-severe otitis externa. The steroid reduces inflammation and swelling, while the antibiotic treats infection. Options include those with an aminoglycoside or a quinolone. [Rx info]
  • Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Clarithromycin): Not routinely recommended for AOM. Consider an immediate or delayed prescription in specific circumstances (e.g., child <2 with bilateral AOM, otorrhoea, or systemically unwell), in line with NICE guidance. For OE, oral antibiotics are only indicated if the infection is spreading (cellulitis) or the patient is systemically unwell. [Rx info]
  • Topical Analgesic Drops (e.g., Phenazone with Lidocaine): May be considered for pain relief in AOM in those over 6 months, provided the eardrum is not perforated. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by NICE guideline NG91 (Otitis media (acute): antimicrobial prescribing) and the CKS topic for Otitis Externa.

For AOM, NICE guidance is clear: do not routinely offer antibiotics. The focus is on effective analgesia. An antibiotic prescription (immediate or delayed) should only be considered for certain populations: those with otorrhoea, children under 2 years with bilateral infection, or those who are systemically unwell or at high risk of complications. This is because AOM is often viral and even when bacterial, most cases resolve spontaneously. Antibiotics provide only a small benefit in pain reduction after 24 hours and do not prevent future recurrence or hearing loss, but do cause side effects and drive antimicrobial resistance.

For otitis externa, NICE CKS recommends a topical preparation containing an antibiotic and a corticosteroid as first-line treatment for 7 days. If the canal is occluded with swelling, an ear wick may need to be inserted to facilitate delivery of the drops. Oral antibiotics are not recommended for uncomplicated OE. If fungal infection (otomycosis) is suspected (e.g., fine hyphae visible, failure to respond), a topical antifungal should be used.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to managing earache, particularly parental anxiety regarding AOM in children and ensuring correct treatment administration for OE.

Counselling for Self-Care (especially AOM)

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that AOM is a self-limiting illness that usually gets better on its own in about 3 days.
  • Prioritise Analgesia: Emphasise that the most important thing is to manage the pain effectively with regular paracetamol or ibuprofen.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: Use a clear, simple explanation. "As most ear infections are caused by viruses, antibiotics won't help them get better faster. They can also cause side effects like diarrhoea or a rash."

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Delayed Prescriptions (AOM): Give explicit instructions on when to use it (e.g., "Only use this if the pain isn't getting any better after 3 days, or if your child becomes much more unwell.").
  • Ear Drop Technique (OE): Provide clear instructions. "Lie on your side with the sore ear facing up. Put the drops in and stay lying down for a few minutes to let them soak in properly. Gently pressing on the tragus can help the drops travel down the canal." Advise on keeping the ear dry.
  • Side Effects: Briefly counsel on common side effects of any prescribed medicines.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step. It involves providing clear, specific advice on when and how to seek further medical help if the condition worsens or does not follow the expected course.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient or parent to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The pain becomes significantly worse or does not start to improve within 3-4 days despite adequate analgesia.
  • The patient becomes systemically very unwell (e.g., high fever, drowsiness, persistent vomiting).
  • Swelling, redness, or tenderness develops in the bone behind the ear (mastoid).
  • Any new weakness or drooping of the face develops.
  • The patient develops a severe headache, neck stiffness, or dislike of bright lights.
  • For otitis externa, if symptoms are not improving after one week of treatment.

For patients managed with a delayed prescription for AOM, advise them to seek a review if they start the antibiotics but see no improvement after 2-3 days.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute Otitis Media (AOM): This is an infection of the middle ear space, located behind the tympanic membrane. It typically begins with a viral URTI, which causes inflammation and oedema of the nasopharynx and the Eustachian tube. This tube, which connects the middle ear to the back of the throat to equalise pressure, becomes blocked. Negative pressure develops in the middle ear, leading to an effusion (fluid collection). This stagnant fluid can then become secondarily infected by bacteria (commonly *Streptococcus pneumoniae* or *Haemophilus influenzae*), leading to the formation of pus. The pressure of this purulent fluid against the tympanic membrane causes it to bulge outwards, resulting in intense pain.

Otitis Externa (OE): This is an inflammatory condition of the skin lining the external auditory canal. The canal has a protective layer of earwax (cerumen), which is acidic and has antimicrobial properties. This barrier can be disrupted by factors like moisture (swimming, humidity), trauma (cotton buds, scratching), or underlying skin conditions (eczema). When the barrier is breached, bacteria (most commonly *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* or *Staphylococcus aureus*) or, less commonly, fungi (*Aspergillus*, *Candida*) can invade the skin, causing inflammation, swelling, and pain. The narrow, enclosed space of the canal exacerbates the pain as the swelling increases.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is used, it is essential to be aware of contraindications, interactions, and specific safety guidance.

Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin)

  • Contraindications: Penicillin allergy is an absolute contraindication for amoxicillin.
  • Side Effects: Common side effects include diarrhoea, nausea, and skin rashes. Can disrupt gut flora.

Topical Ear Preparations for Otitis Externa

  • Aminoglycoside-containing drops (e.g., gentamicin, neomycin): These are contraindicated if the tympanic membrane is known to be perforated or if a perforation is suspected (e.g., recent mucoid discharge). They carry a risk of ototoxicity (damage to the cochlea or vestibular system) if they enter the middle ear. Neomycin can also cause contact dermatitis.
  • Quinolone-containing drops (e.g., ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin): These are considered safe to use in the presence of a tympanic membrane perforation and are not ototoxic. They are a good choice when perforation cannot be ruled out.
  • Acetic Acid: Can cause significant stinging on inflamed skin, which may limit compliance.
11. Management in special populations

Children

AOM is extremely common. The threshold for prescribing antibiotics is lower in certain groups: systemically unwell children, those under 2 with bilateral AOM, and those with otorrhoea. Analgesia is the cornerstone of management. Safety netting with parents is vital. For OE, ensure the child can tolerate the application of ear drops; a spray preparation may be easier. Aural toilet may be required if the canal is full of debris, often requiring referral.

Older adults

There should be a higher index of suspicion for secondary otalgia (e.g., from dental disease, TMJ, or malignancy). Persistent, unilateral earache with a normal otoscopy requires careful evaluation and possible urgent referral. Elderly patients with diabetes are at high risk for necrotising otitis externa, which is a medical emergency. Be mindful of their ability to self-administer ear drops.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Simple analgesia (paracetamol) is safe. For AOM requiring an antibiotic, amoxicillin is safe. Penicillin-allergic pregnant patients should be prescribed erythromycin. For OE, topical acetic acid is safe. Many topical antibiotic/steroid drops can be used with caution after discussion; quinolone drops are often preferred due to minimal systemic absorption. Always check the latest BNF or specialist resources.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes or who are otherwise immunocompromised (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids) are at high risk of severe or complicated infections, particularly necrotising otitis externa. There should be a much lower threshold for investigation, treatment with systemic antibiotics (for OE), and specialist referral.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Toddler with AOM

  • Presentation: An 18-month-old boy is brought in by his mother. He has had a cold for a few days and for the last 12 hours has been crying, irritable, pulling his left ear, and has a temperature of 38.5°C.
  • Assessment: He is miserable but alert. Otoscopy of the right ear is normal. The left tympanic membrane is red and bulging. There are no signs of mastoiditis.
  • Diagnosis: Unilateral acute otitis media.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis and its self-limiting nature. They stress the importance of regular paracetamol and ibuprofen for pain and fever. They provide a delayed prescription for amoxicillin, advising the mother to only use it if he is not improving after 3 days or gets worse. Clear safety netting advice is given regarding signs of serious illness.

Case Study 2: The Swimmer with OE

  • Presentation: A 34-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of severe right ear pain and a watery discharge. She is a keen swimmer. The pain is worse when she lies on her right side or touches her ear.
  • Assessment: Tugging on her tragus reproduces the pain. Otoscopy reveals a swollen, red external canal, partially obscured by moist debris. The tympanic membrane cannot be fully visualised.
  • Diagnosis: Acute otitis externa.
  • Management: The GP diagnoses OE and, as the eardrum cannot be seen, prescribes a 7-day course of topical ciprofloxacin/dexamethasone drops (a non-ototoxic quinolone). The GP counsels her on the correct administration technique and advises her to keep the ear strictly dry and to avoid swimming for at least a week. Safety netting is provided.

Case Study 3: The Older Man with Referred Pain

  • Presentation: A 68-year-old man presents with a 4-week history of a dull ache in his left ear. He is a smoker. There is no discharge or hearing loss.
  • Assessment: Vital signs are normal. Examination of the pinna, mastoid, and ear canal is entirely normal. The tympanic membrane is healthy. Palpation of the TMJ is non-tender. A basic check of his dentition is unremarkable.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Unexplained persistent unilateral earache with normal otoscopy in an older smoker is a red flag for referred pain from a head and neck malignancy. The GP explains the need for further investigation to find the source of the pain and makes an urgent 2-week-wait referral to the ENT department for panendoscopy.
