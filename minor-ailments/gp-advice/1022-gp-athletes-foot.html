Athlete's Foot (Tinea Pedis) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Athlete's foot, or tinea pedis, is a very common fungal infection and a frequent reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It is caused by dermatophyte fungi that thrive in the warm, moist environments created by occlusive footwear and sweating.

Customers typically present with itching, scaling, and maceration in the interdigital spaces, most commonly between the fourth and fifth toes. Another common presentation is the 'moccasin' type, characterised by fine, dry, silvery scaling and erythema affecting the soles, heels, and sides of the feet. Less commonly, patients can present with an acute vesicular form with small blisters. The condition is often bilateral. While bothersome, it is usually a benign condition that responds well to appropriate OTC treatment and advice.

Common triggers include wearing trainers or tight-fitting shoes for long periods, not drying feet properly after washing, and using communal areas like swimming pools and changing rooms. The pharmacy team's role is to correctly identify the condition, rule out red flags, recommend appropriate treatment, and provide comprehensive self-care advice.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication, warranting urgent referral to a GP.

  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection (cellulitis): Spreading, painful erythema extending beyond the sole of the foot, with associated warmth, swelling, and tenderness. The person may report feeling unwell or feverish. This requires urgent GP referral for antibiotics.
  • Ulceration or significant skin breakdown: This is a major red flag, particularly in patients with diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, or immunosuppression. Refer urgently to a GP.
  • Failure to respond to appropriate OTC treatment: If a customer has correctly used a first-line topical antifungal for the recommended duration (e.g., 2-4 weeks) with no improvement, they should be referred to their GP.
  • Widespread or severe disease: Extensive moccasin-type tinea covering the whole sole or a severe, blistering rash is unlikely to respond to OTC treatment alone and warrants GP assessment.
  • Patient is immunocompromised or has poorly controlled diabetes: These patients are at high risk of complications and should be referred to their GP for assessment and management.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the rash has atypical features suggesting an alternative diagnosis (e.g., inflammatory conditions like psoriasis or eczema), referral to a GP is the safest course of action.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the age of the person, as many OTC antifungals are not licensed for use in children. This also establishes if the patient is present or if it's a third-party consultation, which requires extra care.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the diagnosis. Ask about itching, scaling, or peeling. Ask where on the foot it is (between toes, on the sole). Are there any blisters or breaks in the skin? Is it painful? Is there any spreading redness? This helps differentiate from other conditions and screen for red flags like cellulitis.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand the history. Most simple cases are acute. If symptoms have been present for many weeks or months despite treatment, it may indicate a resistant infection or an incorrect diagnosis, warranting GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments the person has already tried. This avoids recommending a product that has already failed. It's important to ask how they used it (e.g., for how long) as improper use is a common cause of treatment failure.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify any underlying medical conditions. Key conditions to ask about are diabetes, circulation problems, or any condition (or medicine) that weakens the immune system. This is crucial for identifying high-risk patients who need referral.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary, as several conditions can mimic the scaly rash of tinea pedis.

Common Differentials to Consider

  • Eczema (Dermatitis): Pompholyx (dyshidrotic) eczema can cause intensely itchy blisters on the soles and sides of the feet. Contact dermatitis from footwear can also cause a red, itchy rash. Eczema is often more vesicular and weeping than typical tinea.
  • Psoriasis: Plantar psoriasis can look very similar to moccasin-type tinea, with well-demarcated red plaques and silvery scale. A history of psoriasis elsewhere is a key clue.
  • Erythrasma: A bacterial infection that causes maceration and fine scaling in the toe webs, often without the significant itch of tinea pedis.

When to Suspect an Alternative

If the rash is symmetrical, extremely inflamed from the outset, or completely fails to respond to a correctly used antifungal, an alternative diagnosis should be considered, and the patient referred to their GP. The presence of symptoms elsewhere on the body (e.g., hands, elbows, knees) also points away from a simple tinea pedis diagnosis.

5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management in the pharmacy focuses on fungicidal/fungistatic treatments combined with crucial self-care advice.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This is a vital part of the treatment. Advise washing feet daily and drying thoroughly, especially between the toes. Recommend wearing clean, cotton socks daily and avoiding tight footwear. Advise use of flip-flops in communal changing areas.

Topical Antifungal Products

  • Topical Imidazoles (e.g., Clotrimazole 1%, Miconazole 2%):
    • Mechanism: Fungistatic. They stop fungi from growing and multiplying.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available as creams, sprays, and powders. Check product for specific age license.
    • Counselling: Apply to the affected area and surrounding skin twice daily. Crucially, advise to continue for two weeks after the skin has returned to normal to prevent recurrence. [Rx info]
  • Topical Terbinafine 1%:
    • Mechanism: Fungicidal. It actively kills the fungi.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Typically licensed for adults over 16 or 18.
    • Counselling: Offers a shorter treatment course, often once daily for one week. Advise that skin may continue to peel and improve even after the 7-day treatment has finished. Some formulations are single-dose applications. [Rx info]
  • Antifungal Powders (e.g., Miconazole):
    • Mechanism: Antifungal action and helps to absorb moisture.
    • Counselling: Useful for dusting inside socks and shoes to treat the environment as well as the foot, helping to prevent re-infection.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for managing customer expectations and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Oral Antifungals (e.g., Terbinafine, Itraconazole):
    Context: A GP will consider prescribing these oral medicines for tinea pedis if the infection is very widespread (e.g., moccasin type), severe, has not responded to correct use of topical treatments, or if there is also significant fungal nail infection (onychomycosis). [Rx advice]
  • Combination Topical Steroids/Antifungals (e.g., Hydrocortisone/Miconazole):
    Context: If the fungal infection is very inflamed and itchy, a GP may prescribe a combination cream containing a mild steroid. This is used for a short period (e.g., 7 days) to settle the inflammation before continuing with a plain antifungal. It is important not to recommend a simple steroid cream (like hydrocortisone 1%) alone, as this can worsen the fungal infection. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team to explain why a GP referral is necessary for certain presentations and what treatments the doctor might consider, reinforcing the pharmacist's clinical role.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for managing athlete's foot and preventing its return.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Condition Simply: Reassure the customer that it's a common infection caused by a bug that likes warm, damp places, and is not due to being unclean.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame hygiene advice as an active and essential part of the treatment. Provide a few key, memorable tips: "Dry between your toes with a separate towel," "Treat your shoes with an antifungal powder," and "Wear flip-flops in the gym changing room."
  • Explain the Treatment Course: This is a critical point. For imidazoles (clotrimazole/miconazole), stress the importance of continuing for 1-2 weeks after the rash disappears. For terbinafine, explain that one week of treatment is usually enough but the skin will continue to heal afterwards.
  • Manage Expectations: Advise that while the itch may improve quickly, the skin peeling and scaling will take longer to resolve fully.
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've explained it well, how long will you continue using the cream for?" can confirm the key message has been understood.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a customer when they should seek further help from their GP. This ensures that a person with a self-limiting illness knows when to act if their condition worsens or does not follow the expected course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the customer to consult their GP if:

  • The rash has not improved or has worsened after using the OTC treatment correctly for 2 weeks.
  • The infection clears but then comes back quickly.
  • The infection spreads to the toenails, causing them to become thick, yellow, or crumbly.
  • They have diabetes and develop any new foot problem.

Advise the customer to seek urgent medical advice from their GP if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • The foot becomes painful, hot, and swollen, with redness spreading up the foot.
  • There are any breaks in the skin, pus, or weeping (ulceration).
  • They feel generally unwell, for example with a fever.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or their symptoms change.

9. Pathophysiology

Tinea pedis is a superficial fungal infection caused by a group of fungi known as dermatophytes. The most common causative organisms are *Trichophyton rubrum*, *Trichophyton interdigitale*, and *Epidermophyton floccosum*.

These fungi produce enzymes called keratinases, which allow them to digest the protein keratin. This is the primary structural component of the outermost layer of the epidermis, the stratum corneum. The infection is limited to this superficial, non-living layer of skin. The fungi thrive in environments that are warm and moist.

Infection is acquired through direct contact with fungal spores, often in communal areas like showers. When spores land on the skin, they can germinate and proliferate, particularly if the skin is damp and warm inside occlusive footwear. This maceration effect damages the skin's barrier function, making it easier for the fungi to invade. The host's inflammatory response to the fungal presence causes the characteristic clinical features of itching, erythema, and scaling.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific details and ensuring correct use.

Topical Antifungal Creams and Sprays

  • Application Area: Counsel the user to apply the product not just to the obviously affected skin, but also to a margin of surrounding healthy skin (e.g., 1-2 cm) to treat the spreading edge of the infection.
  • Local Irritation: All topical products can occasionally cause stinging or skin irritation. If this is mild, it may resolve. If it is severe, the person should stop using the product and seek further advice.
  • Creams vs. Sprays: Creams are moisturising and suitable for dry, scaly areas. Sprays can be easier to apply over larger areas or in hard-to-reach places and are less messy. Powders are best for preventing recurrence by keeping feet and shoes dry.

Treatment Duration is Key

  • Compliance: The most common reason for treatment failure is non-compliance. People often stop treatment as soon as the itch subsides, rather than completing the full course needed to eradicate the fungus.
  • Clear Instructions: Always give a specific endpoint for the treatment, e.g., "Use it for one week" (for terbinafine) or "Use it for two weeks after the rash has gone" (for clotrimazole/miconazole).
11. Use in special populations

Children

Tinea pedis is uncommon in young children. Always check the specific product licence, as many antifungals are not licensed for children (e.g., under 16). If a child presents with a suspected fungal foot rash, a GP referral is often the most appropriate action for a definitive diagnosis and management plan.

Older adults

Consider physical limitations that might make applying cream to their feet difficult. Check for comorbidities like diabetes or peripheral vascular disease, which would necessitate a GP referral. Ensure any recommendation is simple and easy to follow.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care and hygiene advice is first-line. For treatment, topical imidazoles (clotrimazole, miconazole) are considered safe for OTC sale as systemic absorption is minimal. Other agents, including terbinafine cream, are generally not recommended without medical advice. The safest option is to advise use of a topical imidazole or to refer to the GP if the person is concerned.

People with significant comorbidities

This is a critical group for referral. Anyone with diabetes, poor circulation (peripheral vascular disease), or a weakened immune system (e.g., on steroid tablets, chemotherapy) should be referred to their GP for any new foot problem, including suspected athlete's foot. The risk of severe infection, ulceration, and delayed healing is too high for OTC management.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Product Sale with Counselling

  • Customer: "Hi, I think I've got athlete's foot. My feet are really itchy between the toes."
  • Pharmacist: "I can certainly help with that. Who is it for?"
  • Customer: "It's for me, I'm 25."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. And what are the symptoms exactly, just itching and peeling between the toes?"
  • Customer: "Yes, that's it. It started a week or so ago. The skin looks a bit white and cracked."
  • Pharmacist: "Have you tried anything for it yet, and do you take any regular medication?"
  • Customer: "No, nothing yet, and I don't take any medicines."
  • Recommendation: "It does sound like a classic case of athlete's foot. A good option is this terbinafine cream. You apply it once a day for just one week. It's important to keep up the good foot hygiene too - so dry your feet really well after washing. Even though you only use the cream for a week, your skin will continue to heal afterwards. If it's not getting better after the treatment, or if it gets red and painful, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Comorbidity

  • Customer: "Can I get some cream for my dad's feet? They're very dry and scaly."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. To make sure I recommend the right thing, can I ask who it's for?"
  • Customer: "My dad, he's 70."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and what are his symptoms exactly? Is it itchy?"
  • Customer: "He says not really, but the skin on his soles is very thick and keeps cracking."
  • Pharmacist: "And has he tried anything for it?"
  • Customer: "Just E45, but it's not helping."
  • Pharmacist: "This is the most important question - does your dad have any medical conditions, like diabetes?"
  • Customer: "Yes, he's a diabetic."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you, that's really important information. Because your dad has diabetes, it's not safe for me to sell you a product over the counter. Any new foot problem in someone with diabetes needs to be checked by a doctor or a podiatrist. Could you make an appointment for him with his GP so they can assess it properly? It's really important to look after the feet when you have diabetes."
