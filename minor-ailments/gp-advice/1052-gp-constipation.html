Constipation in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Constipation is a common reason for consultation in primary care, affecting individuals of all ages. It is not a disease in itself but a symptom. Presentation varies, but is typically defined by infrequent bowel movements (fewer than three per week) and difficulty passing stools. The Rome IV criteria can be used to standardise the diagnosis of functional constipation, requiring at least two symptoms such as straining, lumpy or hard stools (types 1-2 on the Bristol Stool Chart), a sensation of incomplete evacuation, a sensation of anorectal obstruction, or manual manoeuvres to facilitate defecation.

Patients often report associated symptoms like abdominal bloating, cramping, and general discomfort. While for most it is a functional issue related to diet, lifestyle, or medication, it can cause significant distress and impact quality of life. The primary care clinician's role is to identify the likely cause, exclude serious underlying pathology, and initiate a stepwise management plan, beginning with conservative measures.

Common triggers include a low-fibre diet, inadequate fluid intake, reduced physical activity, and certain medications (e.g., opioids, anticholinergics, iron supplements). In many cases, constipation is self-limiting and responds well to lifestyle advice and simple laxatives. However, it can also be a chronic problem requiring long-term management.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of constipation are benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants further investigation and may require an urgent referral, typically via a '2-week wait' pathway for suspected cancer in the UK.

  • Persistent, unexplained rectal bleeding: Any bleeding not clearly attributable to benign anal causes like fissures or haemorrhoids is a red flag for colorectal cancer.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Significant, unintentional weight loss alongside a change in bowel habit is a key warning sign for malignancy.
  • New onset in older adults: A persistent change in bowel habit to constipation lasting more than 6 weeks in a person aged over 50 years warrants investigation for colorectal cancer (as per NICE NG12).
  • Palpable abdominal or rectal mass: A mass felt on abdominal examination or digital rectal examination (DRE) requires urgent investigation to exclude malignancy.
  • Unexplained iron-deficiency anaemia: Anaemia in the context of constipation or altered bowel habit can be a sign of occult gastrointestinal bleeding, often from a colorectal tumour.
  • Severe, persistent pain with vomiting and bloating: These features may indicate intestinal obstruction, which is a surgical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission.
  • Failure to respond to treatment: Persistent constipation that does not respond to an escalating regimen of laxatives may require specialist investigation.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is essential to differentiate functional constipation from more serious causes and to guide management.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Ascertain onset, duration, and frequency of bowel movements. Use the Bristol Stool Chart to objectively assess stool consistency. Ask about straining, sensation of incomplete evacuation, bloating, and pain.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about rectal bleeding, weight loss, abdominal pain, and family history of bowel cancer or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Lifestyle Factors: Enquire about dietary fibre intake, daily fluid consumption, and level of physical activity.
  • Medication Review: A thorough review of all current medications (prescribed and over-the-counter) is critical to identify potential pharmacological causes (e.g., opioids, calcium channel blockers, antidepressants, iron supplements).
  • Past Medical History: Note any history of hypothyroidism, diabetes, hypercalcaemia, or neurological conditions such as Parkinson's disease or multiple sclerosis.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for pallor or cachexia.
  • Abdominal Examination: Inspect for distension. Palpate for any masses or tenderness. Faecal loading may be palpable in the left iliac fossa.
  • Digital Rectal Examination (DRE): This should be considered, particularly if red flags are present, symptoms are persistent, or faecal impaction is suspected. Assess anal tone, presence and consistency of stool in the rectum (impaction), and for any rectal masses.

Assessment Tools

The Bristol Stool Chart is a key tool for objectively assessing stool form and helping patients describe their symptoms. Types 1 and 2 are indicative of constipation. While not a scoring tool for severity, the Rome IV criteria are used clinically and in research to diagnose functional constipation and IBS-C.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for constipation is broad, spanning from simple lifestyle-related issues to serious organic disease. The clinical assessment aims to distinguish between these possibilities.

Common Causes

  • Functional Constipation: The most common cause, related to a combination of low dietary fibre, poor fluid intake, and sedentary lifestyle. Examination is usually unremarkable apart from potential faecal loading.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C): Characterised by recurrent abdominal pain that is related to defecation, alongside constipation. Bloating is also a very common feature.
  • Drug-Induced Constipation: A frequent side effect of many common medications. A clear temporal link between starting a new drug and the onset of constipation is a key clue.
  • Faecal Impaction: Common in the elderly and immobile, presenting as overflow diarrhoea, rectal discomfort, and sometimes confusion. A DRE is diagnostic.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Colorectal Cancer: This must be considered in patients with red flag symptoms such as rectal bleeding, weight loss, iron-deficiency anaemia, or a persistent change in bowel habit in those over 50.
  • Hypothyroidism: May present with constipation alongside other systemic features like fatigue, weight gain, hair loss, and cold intolerance.
  • Bowel Obstruction: Presents acutely with severe abdominal pain, distension, vomiting, and absolute constipation (no passage of faeces or flatus). This is a medical emergency.
  • Neurological Disease: Conditions like Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, or spinal cord injury commonly cause severe, slow-transit constipation.
  • Hypercalcaemia or Hypokalaemia: Metabolic disturbances can affect gut motility and lead to constipation.
5. Management options in primary care

Management follows a stepwise approach, starting with conservative advice and escalating through different classes of laxatives if required. The goal is to achieve a soft, well-formed stool (Bristol Stool Chart type 4) without straining.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Dietary Modification: Advise a gradual increase in dietary fibre to a target of 30g per day. Sources include fruits, vegetables, wholegrains, pulses, and seeds.
  • Fluid Intake: Recommend adequate hydration, aiming for 1.5-2 litres of fluid per day.
  • Physical Activity: Encourage regular exercise as it can help stimulate bowel function.
  • Toileting Habits: Advise establishing a regular routine, responding to the body's natural urge to defecate, and allowing sufficient time. Using a footstool can help with anorectal angle and ease passage.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) and Prescribed Laxatives

  • Bulk-forming laxatives (e.g., Ispaghula husk): First-line pharmacological treatment. They increase faecal mass and retain water, stimulating peristalsis. Must be taken with plenty of fluid. [Rx info]
  • Osmotic laxatives (e.g., Macrogols, Lactulose): Second-line, or first-line if stools are hard. They draw water into the bowel, softening stool. Macrogols are often preferred as they cause less bloating than lactulose. [Rx info]
  • Stimulant laxatives (e.g., Senna, Bisacodyl): Third-line. They increase peristalsis by stimulating colonic nerves. Intended for short-term use only, as long-term use can cause abdominal cramps and electrolyte imbalance. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Prucalopride: A selective 5-HT4 receptor agonist that stimulates colonic motility. An option for chronic constipation in adults if at least two different types of laxatives have failed. [Rx info]
  • Linaclotide: Licensed for moderate-to-severe IBS-C. It increases intestinal fluid secretion and transit speed. [Rx info]
  • Faecal Softeners (e.g., Docusate Sodium): Limited role and not recommended for maintenance by NICE. May be used for painful anal conditions like fissures.
  • Co-danthramer/Co-danthrusate: Use is restricted to palliative care for opioid-induced constipation in terminally ill patients, due to concerns about genotoxicity of the danthron component.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of constipation in the UK is primarily guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) on Constipation. The guidance emphasises a structured, stepwise approach to treatment.

The core principle is to begin with conservative management, including dietary and lifestyle modifications. If these are insufficient, pharmacological intervention should be initiated. The recommended sequence for laxative use is: 1. Start with a bulk-forming laxative. 2. If stools remain hard, add or switch to an osmotic laxative. Macrogols are recommended as the first-choice osmotic laxative. 3. If the response is inadequate, add a stimulant laxative for short-term use.

NICE advises against the routine long-term use of stimulant laxatives. Faecal softeners are not generally recommended. For chronic constipation that persists for at least 6 months despite optimal use of at least two different laxatives, specialist options like prucalopride can be considered. For faecal impaction, macrogols are recommended as the first-line treatment, often at a high dose initially.

The guidelines highlight the importance of identifying and managing any underlying secondary causes and reviewing medications that may be contributing to the constipation. There is a strong emphasis on patient education, setting realistic expectations, and regular review of treatment effectiveness.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to successfully managing constipation, empowering patients to make sustainable lifestyle changes and use treatments correctly.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Diagnosis: Reassure the patient that functional constipation is common and not usually a sign of serious disease once red flags are excluded. Explain the role of diet, fluids, and activity.
  • Manage Expectations: Advise that it can take several days for laxatives to work and that lifestyle changes need to be long-term. The goal is comfortable, regular bowel movements, not necessarily daily ones.
  • Use of Tools: Introduce the Bristol Stool Chart to help patients monitor their progress towards a type 4 stool. Provide written dietary information or direct to reliable sources like the NHS website.
  • Fibre Intake: Counsel patients to increase fibre intake gradually to avoid worsening bloating and flatulence.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Laxative Use: Give clear instructions. For bulk-forming laxatives, stress the importance of taking them with plenty of water to prevent obstruction. For macrogols, explain how to prepare the sachets. For stimulants, clarify they are for short-term rescue use.
  • Side Effects: Briefly explain common side effects, such as bloating with lactulose or cramps with senna, and advise the patient on how to manage them (e.g., adjusting the dose).
  • Titrating Treatment: Empower the patient to adjust their laxative dose based on their stool consistency, aiming for a soft, comfortable motion.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step in the consultation. It involves providing clear, specific advice on when and how to seek further medical help if the condition worsens or does not follow the expected course.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • Development of any red flag symptoms, particularly rectal bleeding, unexplained weight loss, or a palpable lump in the abdomen.
  • Severe abdominal pain, especially if accompanied by vomiting, fever, or abdominal swelling (potential obstruction).
  • The constipation fails to improve despite following advice and using laxatives as prescribed for 2-4 weeks.
  • The development of any other symptom that they are concerned about.

For most patients with simple constipation, a routine follow-up is not required if symptoms resolve. However, for those with more complex issues, such as faecal impaction, opioid-induced constipation, or in frail elderly patients, a planned review (e.g., in 1-2 weeks) is appropriate to assess the response to treatment and adjust the management plan accordingly. The goal after disimpaction is to establish a stable maintenance regimen to prevent recurrence.

9. Pathophysiology

Constipation results from stool moving too slowly through the colon (slow-transit) or from an inability to effectively evacuate stool from the rectum (defecatory disorder). In many cases, these overlap, and colonic transit time is normal (normal-transit constipation), where symptoms are driven by perception and may overlap with IBS.

The colon's primary role is to absorb water and electrolytes from digested food to form stool. The transit of contents is controlled by a complex interplay of enteric nerves, hormones, and colonic smooth muscle contractions (peristalsis). If transit time is prolonged (slow-transit constipation), excessive water is absorbed, leading to hard, dry stools that are difficult to pass. This can be idiopathic or secondary to metabolic conditions (hypothyroidism), neurological disorders (Parkinson's), or drugs (opioids) that reduce gut motility.

Defecatory disorders involve a functional problem with the pelvic floor or anal sphincter. In pelvic floor dyssynergia, the abdominal, recto-anal, and pelvic floor muscles do not coordinate correctly during attempted defecation. Instead of the pelvic floor relaxing to allow passage of stool, it may paradoxically contract, creating a functional outlet obstruction. This leads to excessive straining, a feeling of incomplete evacuation, and the need for manual manoeuvres.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing laxatives, it is essential to be aware of contraindications, key side effects, and specific patient groups.

Bulk-forming Laxatives (e.g., Ispaghula husk)

  • Contraindications: Intestinal obstruction, colonic atony, or severe swallowing difficulties.
  • Key Considerations: Must be taken with sufficient fluid (at least 150ml per dose) to avoid causing oesophageal or intestinal obstruction. Can take 2-3 days to work. May cause bloating and flatulence initially.

Osmotic Laxatives (e.g., Macrogols, Lactulose)

  • Contraindications: Intestinal obstruction or perforation; inflammatory bowel disease flare.
  • Key Considerations: Macrogols are generally considered more effective and produce less flatulence than lactulose. Lactulose should be used with caution in patients with lactose intolerance. Electrolyte disturbances are rare with standard doses but can occur with high doses or in renal impairment.

Stimulant Laxatives (e.g., Senna, Bisacodyl)

  • Contraindications: Intestinal obstruction.
  • Risks & Side Effects: Main side effect is abdominal cramping. Not recommended for long-term continuous use as this may lead to colonic atony and electrolyte imbalance (hypokalaemia). Melanosis coli (pigmentation of the colonic mucosa) is a benign, reversible finding with long-term use.

Prucalopride

  • Contraindications: Severe renal impairment (eGFR <30), intestinal obstruction/perforation, severe inflammatory conditions like Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis.
  • Side Effects: Common side effects include headache, nausea, and diarrhoea, which are usually transient.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Constipation is common, and often triggered by toilet training, dietary changes, or painful defecation. The mainstay of treatment is an osmotic laxative (macrogols are first-line). Disimpaction may require high initial doses. Stimulant laxatives may be added for short periods under specialist advice. Dietary and behavioural interventions are key. A chronic course (>8 weeks) warrants careful assessment.

Older adults

Prevalence is high due to polypharmacy, reduced mobility, and comorbidities. Faecal impaction is a significant risk and may present atypically with confusion, urinary retention, or overflow diarrhoea. Medication review is vital. A stepwise approach to laxatives should be used, starting with low doses. Bulk-forming laxatives may be difficult for frail, bedbound patients with poor fluid intake. Macrogols are often a good choice.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Constipation is very common due to hormonal changes and pressure from the growing uterus. Lifestyle and dietary advice are first-line. If medication is needed, bulk-forming (e.g., Fybogel) and osmotic laxatives (lactulose, macrogols) are safe to use. Senna (stimulant) may be used if other measures fail but should be avoided near term. Docusate sodium can also be used. Most laxatives are considered safe in breastfeeding as they are minimally absorbed.

People with significant comorbidities

In patients taking opioids, prophylactic laxatives (an osmotic plus a stimulant) should be co-prescribed from the outset. For refractory opioid-induced constipation, peripherally-acting mu-opioid receptor antagonists (PAMORAs) like naloxegol may be considered. In patients with neurological conditions like Parkinson's disease or MS, constipation is often severe and requires a proactive, long-term combination of laxatives and specialist input.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young Professional

  • Presentation: A 29-year-old marketing executive presents with a 6-month history of infrequent, hard stools, bloating, and straining. She often misses breakfast and has a busy, sedentary job.
  • Assessment: She is systemically well with no red flag symptoms. Abdominal examination is unremarkable. The history is highly suggestive of functional constipation.
  • Diagnosis: Functional constipation.
  • Management: The GP discusses the importance of diet, fluids, and regular meals. They advise aiming for a 30-minute walk at lunchtime. They recommend starting a bulk-forming laxative, explaining it must be taken with a large glass of water. They provide a Bristol Stool Chart to monitor progress. Safety netting for red flags is given.

Case Study 2: The Older Adult with Faecal Impaction

  • Presentation: A practice nurse requests a home visit for an 82-year-old housebound man who has been passing small amounts of liquid stool for 5 days and seems more confused. He has a history of dementia and osteoarthritis.
  • Assessment: He is agitated. His abdomen is soft but distended. A digital rectal examination reveals a rectum packed with hard stool.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Faecal impaction with overflow diarrhoea. The GP prescribes a disimpaction regimen using high-dose macrogols, with clear written instructions for the carers. A follow-up call is arranged in 3 days to check progress, with a plan to establish a maintenance laxative regimen once the impaction has cleared to prevent recurrence.

Case Study 3: The Patient with Red Flags

  • Presentation: A 68-year-old man presents with a 2-month history of worsening constipation. He has never had bowel problems before. He has also noticed streaks of dark blood mixed with his stool on a few occasions and feels his trousers have become looser.
  • Assessment: He has no palpable abdominal mass. A blood test is arranged which later reveals a new iron-deficiency anaemia.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The combination of new-onset change in bowel habit >6 weeks, rectal bleeding, and iron-deficiency anaemia in a person over 60 constitutes significant red flags for colorectal cancer. The GP makes an urgent 2-week-wait referral to the colorectal team for further investigation, as per NICE guidelines.
