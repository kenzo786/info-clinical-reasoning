Cold Sores (Herpes Labialis) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Cold sores (herpes labialis) are a common condition in primary care, caused by the reactivation of the Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV). Most cases are caused by HSV-1. Patients typically present with a characteristic sequence of symptoms. It often begins with a prodrome of tingling, itching, or burning at a specific site on or around the lips, lasting several hours to a day. This is followed by the eruption of small, fluid-filled vesicles.

These vesicles may coalesce, then burst and weep, forming a painful ulcer which subsequently crusts over. The entire episode is self-limiting and typically resolves without scarring within 7 to 10 days. The condition is recurrent, with the virus lying dormant in the trigeminal ganglion between outbreaks. Common triggers for reactivation include ultraviolet (UV) light exposure, fatigue, stress, febrile illness, trauma to the area, or immunosuppression.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While typically a benign, self-limiting condition, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious complication that may require urgent specialist assessment or admission.

  • Ocular Involvement (Herpetic Keratitis): Any symptoms affecting the eye, such as eye pain, grittiness, redness, photophobia, blurred vision, or vesicles on the eyelid or tip of the nose (Hutchinson's sign). This indicates potential corneal involvement and is an ophthalmological emergency requiring same-day referral.
  • Eczema Herpeticum: A widespread, painful, vesicular rash that is often 'punched-out' and crusted, superimposed on areas of pre-existing eczema or other skin conditions. The patient may be systemically unwell with fever. This is a dermatological emergency requiring urgent specialist advice and systemic antiviral treatment.
  • Severe or Disseminated Infection: Multiple lesions spreading beyond the perioral area, or severe, non-healing ulcers, particularly in an immunocompromised patient. This can indicate systemic disease and requires a low threshold for specialist discussion and potential admission.
  • Neurological Symptoms: The presence of severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, fever, or new focal neurological signs or confusion is a red flag for herpes simplex encephalitis. This is a rare but life-threatening complication requiring immediate emergency hospital admission.
  • Neonatal Herpes: Any suspected herpes lesion in a newborn (up to 28 days old) is a medical emergency. This requires immediate paediatric admission due to the high risk of severe disseminated disease.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A focused history and examination are usually sufficient to make a clinical diagnosis and exclude red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Confirm the presence of a prodrome (tingling/burning). Ascertain the timing, location, and appearance of the lesions.
  • Frequency and Triggers: Ask about the frequency of recurrence and any known triggers. This helps to establish the impact on the patient and informs management, particularly regarding suppressive therapy.
  • Past Medical History: Specifically enquire about immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, chemotherapy, high-dose steroids), atopic eczema, and previous severe herpetic infections.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about eye symptoms (pain, redness, visual change), systemic symptoms (fever, severe headache), and whether the rash is widespread or unusually severe.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the patient's general state of health. Note any signs of systemic illness or distress.
  • Lesion Inspection: Examine the location and stage of the lesions (e.g., vesicles, ulcers, crusts). The diagnosis is usually clinical and swabs for viral PCR are rarely needed in uncomplicated cases.
  • Regional Examination: Check for tender regional lymphadenopathy (submandibular/cervical), which can be present. Examine the eyes for redness or signs of involvement. Inspect the rest of the skin, especially in patients with eczema, for dissemination.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The diagnosis of recurrent herpes labialis is often straightforward based on the classic history and appearance. However, other conditions can present similarly.

Common Differentials

  • Impetigo: A bacterial skin infection that can occur on the face. Typically presents with golden, honey-coloured crusts rather than vesicles. It does not usually have a prodrome and is not confined to the vermilion border.
  • Aphthous Ulcers: These occur on the non-keratinised mucosa *inside* the mouth (e.g., inner cheeks, tongue). They present as ulcers from the outset and are not preceded by vesicles.
  • Contact Dermatitis: An allergic or irritant reaction can cause redness, blistering, and crusting around the lips, but is often related to a new cosmetic or product and may be more diffuse.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC): A persistent, non-healing, crusted, or ulcerated lesion on the lip, especially in an older patient with a history of significant sun exposure, should be considered malignant until proven otherwise. It will not follow the typical 7-10 day healing cycle.
  • Eczema Herpeticum: As described in red flags. The key is the rapid evolution of widespread, monomorphic lesions in a patient with a history of atopic eczema.
  • Syphilitic Chancre: A primary chancre can present as a single, painless, indurated ulcer on the lip. A sexual history may be relevant.
5. Management options in primary care

Management depends on the severity, frequency, and immune status of the patient. For most, reassurance and self-care are sufficient.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance: Explain the benign, self-limiting nature of the condition.
  • Infection Control: Advise on hand washing after touching the lesion, and avoiding sharing items like cutlery, glasses, or towels to prevent transmission. Patients should avoid kissing and oral sex during an outbreak until lesions have fully healed.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Simple analgesia can be used for pain. Applying a cool, damp cloth may be soothing.
  • Trigger Avoidance: Advise using a high-factor UV-blocking lip balm to prevent sun-induced recurrences.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Topical Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir 5% cream): These work by inhibiting viral replication. Evidence suggests they provide modest benefit, shortening the duration of the episode by about half a day. Their main value is if applied early during the prodromal phase. [Rx info]
  • Hydrocolloid Patches: These non-medicated patches cover the lesion, creating a moist healing environment, reducing crusting, and providing a discreet barrier to reduce contamination.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir, Valaciclovir): These inhibit viral DNA polymerase, halting viral replication. They are not recommended for routine, uncomplicated outbreaks. They are reserved for specific situations such as a severe primary infection, to prevent complications in at-risk groups (e.g., immunocompromised), or for managing frequent and distressing recurrences, either episodically at the first sign of a prodrome, or as daily suppressive therapy. 6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) for Herpes Simplex - oral. The guidance emphasises a stratified approach based on patient factors and severity.

For most immunocompetent adults with uncomplicated, infrequent recurrences, the guidelines support self-care and reassurance as the mainstay of management. The use of OTC topical antivirals is an option, with the caveat that they must be started very early to have any meaningful effect, and the overall benefit is marginal.

Guidelines do not recommend routine prescribing of oral antivirals for sporadic cold sores. Their use is reserved for specific indications, including:

  • Primary herpetic gingivostomatitis.
  • Immunocompromised patients.
  • Prevention of recurrent infection (suppressive therapy) if outbreaks are frequent (e.g., more than 6 per year) and causing significant distress.
  • Treatment of complications such as eczema herpeticum or ocular herpes, always under specialist guidance.
The guidance clearly outlines red flags that necessitate urgent referral to prevent serious morbidity.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to managing patient expectations and promoting self-care.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Condition: Reassure the patient that this is a very common and usually harmless condition caused by a dormant virus. Explain the concept of triggers.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that cold sores can be painful and cosmetically distressing.
  • Transmission: Clearly explain that the virus is contagious through direct contact. Emphasise the importance of hand hygiene and avoiding kissing or sharing utensils during an active outbreak to protect others, especially babies, pregnant women, and immunocompromised individuals.
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions they can take, such as using sunblock on their lips, managing stress, and using OTC remedies early if they choose to.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Topical Antivirals: Stress that the cream must be applied at the very first sign of the tingle, and that it will only have a modest effect on the duration.
  • Oral Antivirals: If providing episodic treatment, give clear instructions to start the course of tablets as soon as the prodrome begins. If starting suppressive therapy, explain the rationale, the need for daily adherence, and the plan for future review to reassess the need for ongoing treatment. Briefly counsel on common side effects like headache or nausea.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial part of the consultation, ensuring the patient knows when to seek further help.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The sore spreads to involve the eye, or they develop any eye pain or changes in their vision.
  • The sores become very widespread, numerous, or unusually painful.
  • The sore has not started to heal within 7-10 days or is not fully healed by 14 days.
  • They develop a high fever, severe headache, or feel generally very unwell.
  • The area becomes red, hot, swollen, and increasingly painful, which may suggest a secondary bacterial infection.

For patients initiated on suppressive antiviral therapy, a planned follow-up (e.g., after 6-12 months) is appropriate to review its effectiveness, discuss side effects, and make a joint decision about whether to continue or trial a period off treatment.

9. Pathophysiology

Cold sores are the result of reactivation of the Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV). The vast majority of oral-facial lesions are caused by HSV-1.

The process begins with a primary infection, which often occurs in childhood and may be subclinical or present as acute herpetic gingivostomatitis. Following this initial infection, the virus is not cleared from the body. Instead, it ascends along a sensory nerve axon to the corresponding dorsal root ganglion, where it establishes a lifelong state of latency. For herpes labialis, this is typically the trigeminal ganglion.

During latency, the viral genome persists within the neuron, but replication is suppressed. Various stimuli can trigger viral reactivation. These include local factors (UV radiation, trauma) and systemic factors (illness, psychological stress, hormonal changes). Upon reactivation, the virus replicates and travels back down the sensory nerve axon to the epithelial cells of the skin or mucosa supplied by that nerve. Here, it causes a productive, lytic infection, destroying host cells and leading to the characteristic inflammatory response and formation of vesicles. This cycle of latency and reactivation is the hallmark of HSV infection.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, it is important to consider the safety profile of the chosen agent.

Topical Antivirals (Aciclovir, Penciclovir)

  • Safety: Systemic absorption is minimal, and they are considered very safe for most people.
  • Side Effects: Local skin reactions such as burning, stinging, or mild drying can occur but are uncommon.

Oral Antivirals (Aciclovir, Valaciclovir, Famciclovir)

  • Contraindications & Cautions: Dose reduction is essential in patients with renal impairment, as these drugs are renally excreted. Caution is advised in older adults, who are more susceptible to renal impairment and neurotoxicity. Maintaining good hydration is important to reduce the risk of renal toxicity.
  • Side Effects: Generally well-tolerated for short courses. Common side effects include headache, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea. Neuropsychiatric reactions (e.g., confusion, hallucinations, drowsiness), though rare, are a risk, particularly in the elderly or those with renal impairment.
  • Interactions: Significant interactions are uncommon with standard-dose episodic therapy. Probenecid can increase aciclovir levels. Caution is needed when co-prescribing with other nephrotoxic drugs.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Primary infection can manifest as severe herpetic gingivostomatitis, with high fever and painful oral lesions that may impair feeding and hydration, sometimes requiring hospitalisation. Recurrent cold sores are typically managed with self-care advice. Topical antivirals can be used. Oral antivirals are reserved for severe cases or immunocompromised children, usually following specialist advice.

Older adults

Be vigilant for persistent, non-healing lesions, as the risk of squamous cell carcinoma of the lip is higher. When prescribing oral antivirals, be cautious due to the higher prevalence of renal impairment and increased risk of neurotoxicity. It is prudent to check renal function before starting suppressive therapy and to use age-appropriate dosing.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

First-line management is non-pharmacological. Topical aciclovir is considered safe to use during pregnancy and breastfeeding as systemic absorption is minimal. Oral aciclovir should only be used if the potential benefit outweighs the risk (e.g., severe primary infection) and is considered compatible with breastfeeding. Always consult the latest BNF or specialist sources. A key concern is preventing neonatal herpes, so hygiene advice is paramount.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are significantly immunocompromised (e.g., post-transplant, on chemotherapy, advanced HIV) are at risk of more severe, frequent, and atypical presentations which may be persistent and ulcerative. There should be a very low threshold for using oral antiviral therapy (episodic or suppressive) and for seeking specialist advice. In patients with atopic eczema, there is a higher risk of developing eczema herpeticum, and they should be counselled about this risk.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Infrequent Outbreak

  • Presentation: A 24-year-old man presents with a 1-day history of a tingling on his upper lip followed by a small crop of blisters. He gets this once every year or two. He is otherwise well.
  • Assessment: Clinical examination confirms a small cluster of vesicles at the vermilion border. No red flags.
  • Diagnosis: Uncomplicated recurrent herpes labialis.
  • Management: The GP reassures him of the benign nature of the condition. They explain that as the blisters are already present, antiviral cream is unlikely to be of significant benefit now but could be tried at the 'tingle stage' in future. Advice is given on hygiene to prevent transmission and using sunblock lip balm as UV light may be a trigger. Clear safety netting advice is provided.

Case Study 2: The Frequent and Distressing Recurrence

  • Presentation: A 38-year-old woman consults because she is having outbreaks of cold sores every 4-6 weeks. She finds them painful and is very self-conscious about her appearance as she works in a customer-facing role.
  • Assessment: History reveals classic prodrome and lesions healing within 10 days. No red flags. Examination is normal between episodes.
  • Diagnosis: Frequent recurrent herpes labialis causing significant psychosocial impact.
  • Management: The GP discusses the management options. Given the frequency and impact, they agree on a trial of suppressive therapy with oral aciclovir. The rationale, dosage, potential side effects, and the need for good hydration are explained. A plan is made to review in 6 months to assess the benefit and decide on continuing treatment.

Case Study 3: The Red Flag Presentation

  • Presentation: A 6-year-old boy with known moderate atopic eczema is brought in by his mother. He had a small cold sore on his lip two days ago, but over the last 24 hours has become feverish and developed multiple painful, crusted spots over his cheeks and neck in the areas where his eczema is usually worst.
  • Assessment: The child is febrile (38.5°C) and distressed. Examination reveals numerous 'punched-out' erosions and vesicles on an erythematous base across his cheeks.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP immediately suspects eczema herpeticum. This is a dermatological emergency. The GP contacts the on-call paediatric or dermatology team at the local hospital, explains the situation, and arranges for the child's immediate assessment for admission and initiation of systemic antiviral therapy.
