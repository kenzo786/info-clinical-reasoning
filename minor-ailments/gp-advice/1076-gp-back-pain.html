Acute Low Back Pain in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Low back pain is an extremely common reason for consultation in primary care. It is typically defined as pain in the lumbosacral region, between the bottom of the ribs and the top of the legs. The vast majority of cases are 'non-specific' or 'mechanical' back pain, meaning there is no identifiable serious underlying pathology and the pain originates from the structures of the back, such as muscles, ligaments, and joints.

Patients usually present with a sudden or gradual onset of pain, which may be localised or radiate into the buttocks or thigh. The pain is often described as an ache or stiffness, and is typically worsened by movement or certain postures. An episode of acute, non-specific low back pain usually resolves within a few days to weeks. Sciatica, where pain radiates down the leg below the knee in a dermatomal pattern due to nerve root irritation, may take longer to resolve, often over 4-6 weeks but sometimes longer. The clinician's role is to exclude serious causes, provide reassurance and self-management advice, and manage patient expectations about recovery.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

Whilst most back pain is benign, it is crucial to screen for red flags that suggest a serious underlying cause. The presence of these features requires urgent investigation or emergency referral.

  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: Bilateral sciatica, severe or progressive bilateral neurological deficit, difficulty urinating or urinary retention, loss of anal sphincter tone or faecal incontinence, and saddle anaesthesia (numbness around the buttocks, perineum, and inner thighs). This is a neurosurgical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission.
  • Spinal Malignancy/Metastasis: A personal history of cancer (especially breast, lung, prostate, kidney, thyroid), unexplained weight loss, pain that is worse at night or when lying down, and being generally unwell.
  • Spinal Infection (e.g., discitis, osteomyelitis): Fever, chills, recent bacterial infection (e.g., UTI), history of intravenous drug use, or being immunocompromised. The pain is often severe, constant, and unremitting.
  • Vertebral Fracture: History of significant trauma (e.g., a fall from height, road traffic accident), or minor trauma in a patient with osteoporosis or on long-term steroids. There may be a structural deformity or localised bony tenderness.
  • Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA): A pulsatile abdominal mass, severe pain that may radiate to the back, and known atherosclerotic vascular disease. This requires emergency vascular assessment.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is key to identifying red flags, establishing the nature of the pain, and assessing its impact.

History Taking

  • Pain Characteristics: Use SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving factors, Severity). Specifically ask about radiation below the knee and any numbness or weakness.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about bladder/bowel changes, saddle sensation, bilateral leg symptoms, history of cancer, fevers, and recent trauma.
  • Neurological Symptoms: Enquire about altered sensation (paraesthesia or numbness), muscle weakness (e.g., foot drop), and changes in gait.
  • Psychosocial Factors (Yellow Flags): Assess for factors that predict poor outcomes, such as unhelpful beliefs about pain (e.g., "hurt equals harm"), fear avoidance behaviour, low mood, anxiety, and social or work-related problems.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the patient's posture, gait, and ease of movement.
  • Spinal Examination: Inspect for deformity (e.g., scoliosis, loss of lumbar lordosis). Palpate the spinous processes for localised tenderness. Assess range of motion.
  • Neurological Examination (Lower Limbs): Assess power (hip flexion, knee extension, ankle dorsiflexion/plantarflexion), sensation in the dermatomes, and reflexes (knee and ankle).
  • Special Tests: The straight leg raise test can be used to assess for nerve root irritation in cases of suspected sciatica.

Scoring Tools

The STarT Back Tool can be used to stratify patients into low, medium, or high risk of a poor outcome. This helps to inform shared decision-making about the intensity of management needed.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The main goal is to differentiate between common, benign causes and the rare but serious spinal pathologies.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Non-specific Low Back Pain: The most common diagnosis. Pain is localised to the back and is mechanical in nature (varies with activity and time). Examination is neurologically normal.
  • Sciatica (Lumbar Radiculopathy): Characterised by unilateral leg pain, often radiating below the knee, which is typically worse than the back pain. May be associated with numbness, tingling, or weakness in the distribution of a specific nerve root.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: Suggested by bilateral leg symptoms and sphincter disturbance.
  • Malignancy/Infection/Fracture: Indicated by specific red flags in the history and systemic signs. Pain is often non-mechanical.
  • Inflammatory Arthritis (e.g., Ankylosing Spondylitis): Consider in younger adults (<40) with pain that is worse in the morning, improves with exercise but not rest, and associated with prolonged morning stiffness.
  • Referred Pain: Pain originating from other organs, such as pancreatitis, kidney stones, or abdominal aortic aneurysm. The nature of the pain and associated symptoms will differ from mechanical back pain.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is centred on education, promoting activity, and simple analgesia. A stratified approach based on risk of chronicity (e.g., using the STarT Back tool) is recommended.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain that the pain is not serious and is likely to improve significantly over a few weeks.
  • Remain Active: Advise patients to continue with normal activities as much as possible and avoid bed rest. A gradual return to work and usual hobbies is encouraged.
  • Exercise: Recommend simple exercises such as walking, stretching, or yoga. Referral for a structured group exercise programme can be beneficial.
  • Heat/Cold Therapy: Application of a hot water bottle or a cold pack can provide short-term symptomatic relief.
  • Manual Therapy: Consideration of referral to a physiotherapist, osteopath, or chiropractor for a course of manipulation, mobilisation or soft tissue techniques may be appropriate as part of a treatment package including exercise.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs): Ibuprofen is a suitable first-line choice. It reduces pain and inflammation, enabling the patient to remain active. Advise taking with or after food. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time. Check for contraindications including peptic ulceration, renal impairment, heart failure, and asthma.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Oral NSAIDs: Naproxen can be considered if ibuprofen is insufficient. Co-prescribe a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) in at-risk individuals. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible time. Avoid in renal impairment, heart failure, and established cardiovascular disease.
  • Weak Opioids (e.g., Codeine): Consider for acute low back pain only when an NSAID is ineffective, contraindicated, or not tolerated. Use for the shortest duration necessary. Counsel on risks of constipation, drowsiness, and dependence. Advise not to drive if drowsy. Use with caution in older adults and those with a history of substance misuse.
  • Note: NICE guidance does not recommend offering paracetamol alone, benzodiazepines, or gabapentinoids for managing low back pain.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of low back pain and sciatica in the UK is primarily guided by NICE guideline [NG59].

The core principles of NG59 are to promote self-management and physical activity. The guideline emphasises a 'no-do' list, advising against practices that have been shown to be ineffective. Routine imaging (X-ray, MRI) in a non-specialist setting for patients without red flags is strongly discouraged, as findings often correlate poorly with symptoms and can lead to unnecessary anxiety and intervention.

Pharmacological management is recommended as an adjunct to non-pharmacological advice. NICE recommends considering oral NSAIDs at the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible time. Weak opioids should only be considered if NSAIDs are contraindicated, not tolerated or have been ineffective. Paracetamol alone is not recommended. Gabapentinoids and strong opioids are not recommended for managing low back pain.

For patients with sciatica, the same principles apply. The guideline advises against the use of gabapentinoids, benzodiazepines, and oral corticosteroids. It supports a stratified approach to care, using a tool like STarT Back to tailor the intensity of support to the patient's risk of poor outcome. This may involve referral for exercise programmes, manual therapy (as part of a treatment package), or psychological therapies for those at higher risk.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is paramount to empower patients and prevent chronicity.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Diagnosis: Clearly explain that the pain is 'non-specific' or 'mechanical', which means it's not caused by anything serious and is very common. Use analogies like a 'sprain of the back'.
  • Address Concerns: Acknowledge the pain is real and distressing. Actively listen to and address fears about damage or disability. Explain why imaging like an X-ray is not helpful in most cases.
  • Promote Positive Action: Frame advice positively. Emphasise that "motion is lotion" and that hurt does not necessarily mean harm. Encourage a gradual return to valued activities and work.
  • Manage Expectations: Advise that recovery is expected but can take time. Provide a realistic timeframe (e.g., significant improvement in 2 weeks, but full resolution may take 6 weeks or more).

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • NSAIDs: Explain they are to reduce pain and inflammation to enable activity, not as a standalone cure. Advise on taking them with food and for the shortest duration necessary.
  • Opioids: If prescribed, be explicit that it is for short-term use for severe pain only. Counsel on side effects like constipation and drowsiness, and the risk of dependence if used long-term.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients seek help appropriately if their condition changes.

Clear verbal and written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to seek urgent medical review (via their GP, NHS 111, or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, including:

  • Numbness or tingling around the genitals or buttocks (saddle anaesthesia).
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control, or inability to pass urine.
  • Progressive weakness in both legs or numbness in both legs.
  • New and significant trauma to the back.
  • Feeling systemically unwell with a fever.

Advise the patient to re-consult if the pain is worsening, not improving as expected after 2-3 weeks, or if they develop new, concerning symptoms like leg weakness or widespread numbness. A planned follow-up may be appropriate for patients assessed as being at high risk of chronicity.

9. Pathophysiology

The lumbar spine is a complex structure of vertebrae, intervertebral discs, facet joints, ligaments, and muscles. Non-specific low back pain arises from strain or sprain of these structures. It is considered a 'nociceptive' pain source, where local tissue injury or inflammation activates pain receptors.

In most acute cases, a precise pathoanatomical diagnosis is not possible or necessary. The pain is thought to be driven by a combination of minor tears in muscles or ligaments, inflammation of facet joints, or irritation of the outer layers of the intervertebral disc. This leads to protective muscle spasm, which itself can be painful and limit movement.

Sciatica is a neuropathic pain state. It occurs when a lumbar or sacral nerve root is compressed or chemically irritated, most commonly by a prolapsed intervertebral disc. The compression and associated inflammation of the nerve root leads to pain, numbness, and tingling felt in the specific dermatome supplied by that nerve. Other causes of nerve root irritation include spinal stenosis (narrowing of the spinal canal) or, rarely, a spinal tumour.

The transition from acute to chronic back pain is complex and involves psychosocial factors ('yellow flags') as much as biological ones. Central sensitisation, where the central nervous system becomes amplified and hypersensitive to pain signals, is thought to play a key role in chronic pain states.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, it is vital to consider individual patient factors and safety guidance.

Oral NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen, Naproxen)

  • Contraindications & Cautions: Avoid in active peptic ulcer disease. Use with caution in patients with a history of GI bleeding, renal impairment, heart failure, hypertension, and asthma. Avoid in the third trimester of pregnancy.
  • Interactions: Increased risk of bleeding with anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin, DOACs) and SSRIs. Can reduce the effectiveness of antihypertensives.
  • MHRA Alerts: All NSAIDs are associated with a small increased risk of thrombotic events (myocardial infarction, stroke). Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration. Diclofenac has a higher cardiovascular risk profile compared to naproxen or ibuprofen.

Weak Opioids (Codeine)

  • Regulation: Prescribers should be aware of the potential for misuse and dependence. Codeine-containing products for cough and cold have been reclassified as POM.
  • Contraindications & Cautions: Avoid in patients with significant respiratory compromise (e.g., acute severe asthma, COPD). Use with caution in older adults (risk of confusion, falls) and in those with a history of substance misuse.
  • Side Effects & Risks: Common side effects include constipation, nausea, and drowsiness. Patients should be warned not to drive if they feel drowsy. Risk of dependence with prolonged use.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Back pain in children is uncommon and should be taken seriously. There is a higher likelihood of a specific underlying cause (e.g., spondylolisthesis, discitis, tumour). A lower threshold for investigation and referral to paediatrics or orthopaedics is warranted. Management should focus on identifying and treating the underlying cause.

Older adults

Older adults have a higher prevalence of red flag conditions such as vertebral fractures (often osteoporotic), spinal malignancy, and AAA. They may present atypically. Polypharmacy is common, increasing the risk of drug interactions. NSAIDs should be used with extreme caution due to the high risk of GI and renal toxicity. Opioids increase the risk of confusion, falls, and constipation. Non-pharmacological management is preferred.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Low back pain is very common in pregnancy due to hormonal changes and altered biomechanics. Management should prioritise non-pharmacological methods like exercise, postural advice, and physiotherapy. Paracetamol is considered the safest simple analgesic if required. NSAIDs should be avoided, particularly in the first and third trimesters. Codeine should be avoided, especially near term and during breastfeeding, due to risks to the infant.

People with significant comorbidities

Management must be tailored to the individual. For patients with renal impairment, heart failure, or established cardiovascular disease, NSAIDs should generally be avoided. For those with a history of peptic ulcer disease, an NSAID with PPI co-prescription may be considered with caution, but other options are preferable. The risk-benefit of any pharmacological agent must be carefully weighed against the patient's existing health status.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: Acute Non-Specific Low Back Pain

  • Presentation: A 35-year-old builder presents with a 2-day history of central low back pain after lifting a heavy object. The pain is a 6/10 ache, worse on movement. No leg pain, weakness, or altered sensation.
  • Assessment: He is systemically well. Red flag screening is negative. Examination shows some lumbar muscle tenderness and reduced flexion, but a normal neurological exam.
  • Diagnosis: Acute non-specific (mechanical) low back pain. STarT Back score is 2 (low risk).
  • Management: The GP reassures him that no serious damage has occurred. They advise him to stay active, modify his duties at work temporarily, and use over-the-counter ibuprofen. Clear safety netting advice for red flags is given. A follow-up is not routinely planned, but he is advised to return if not improving in 2-3 weeks.

Case Study 2: Acute Sciatica

  • Presentation: A 52-year-old office worker presents with a 1-week history of right-sided back pain that now radiates down the back of her leg to her calf. The leg pain is sharp, rated 8/10, and associated with some tingling in her foot.
  • Assessment: She is distressed by the pain. Red flag screen for cauda equina is negative. Neurological exam reveals reduced power (4/5) in ankle dorsiflexion on the right and a positive straight leg raise test at 40 degrees.
  • Diagnosis: Acute sciatica (lumbar radiculopathy), likely L5 nerve root.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis is a 'trapped nerve'. They provide clear safety netting advice about cauda equina symptoms. They prescribe a short course of naproxen with omeprazole and advise on self-management strategies. They refer her to physiotherapy for assessment and an exercise programme. They schedule a telephone review in 2 weeks.
