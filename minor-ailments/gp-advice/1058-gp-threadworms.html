Threadworms (Enterobiasis) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Threadworm infection (enterobiasis), caused by the helminth Enterobius vermicularis, is the most common worm infection in the UK and is a very frequent reason for consultation in primary care, particularly concerning children. The most common presentation is intense perianal or perineal pruritus (itching), which is characteristically worse at night. This nocturnal itching occurs as the female worm migrates to the perianal skin to lay her eggs, which can disrupt sleep, leading to irritability and fatigue.

While many infections are asymptomatic, patients or parents may report seeing small, white, thread-like worms (8-13mm long) on the surface of stools or around the anus, especially at night about two hours after falling asleep. In girls, the worms can migrate to the vulval area causing irritation, vaginal discharge, or urinary tract infections. Transmission is faecal-oral; ingestion of microscopic eggs results from direct contact with a contaminated surface (e.g., bedding, toys, towels) or person. The condition is highly infectious, and it is common for entire households to be affected.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While threadworm infection is almost always a benign and self-limiting condition, certain features are atypical and should prompt consideration of an alternative diagnosis or the presence of a rare complication.

  • Severe or persistent abdominal pain: While mild, transient abdominal discomfort can occasionally occur, severe, localised, or persistent pain is a red flag. It may indicate an alternative diagnosis such as appendicitis, which can rarely be caused by occlusion of the appendix by worms.
  • Blood in the stool: The presence of blood is not a feature of threadworm infection and requires investigation for other causes, such as an anal fissure, inflammatory bowel disease, or colorectal pathology.
  • Altered bowel habit or significant weight loss: Persistent diarrhoea, constipation, or unexplained weight loss are not typical of threadworms and warrant investigation for other gastrointestinal conditions.
  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection: Intense scratching can lead to excoriation and secondary cellulitis of the perianal skin, presenting with worsening erythema, pain, swelling, and possibly a purulent discharge. This may require antibiotic treatment.
  • Symptoms persisting despite adequate treatment: Failure to respond to two appropriately spaced doses of medication alongside strict hygiene measures should prompt a re-evaluation of the diagnosis. Other causes of perianal itch should be considered.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

The diagnosis of threadworms is usually made on the basis of the classic clinical history alone.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Enquire about the nature of the symptoms, specifically perianal itching. Establish the timing (nocturnal itching is pathognomonic). Ask about sleep disturbance and irritability. In girls, ask about vulval itch or discharge.
  • Visual Confirmation: Ask if the patient or parent has seen the worms (on stool or around the anus at night) or the smaller white eggs.
  • Household & Close Contacts: Ask about symptoms in other family members or close contacts (e.g., at nursery or school), as infection within a household is extremely common.
  • Previous Episodes & Treatments: Enquire about past episodes and what treatments, if any, have been used. This helps assess adherence to hygiene measures.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about abdominal pain, blood in the stool, weight loss, or signs of skin infection.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: A physical examination is often not necessary if the history is typical.
  • Perianal Inspection: If the diagnosis is in doubt, visual inspection of the perianal area may reveal excoriation from scratching or, rarely, a visible worm. This is best done at night or first thing in the morning.
  • The Sticky Tape Test: This can be used to confirm the diagnosis if worms have not been seen. A piece of clear adhesive tape is pressed against the perianal skin first thing in the morning (before bathing) to collect eggs. The tape is then applied to a glass slide for microscopic examination. This is rarely required in routine primary care.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The main differential diagnosis is for other causes of perianal itching (pruritus ani).

Common Differentials

  • Perianal Dermatitis/Eczema: Can be atopic or irritant (e.g., from inadequate cleaning or excessive moisture). The skin may appear red, chapped, or lichenified. Itching is not typically confined to night-time.
  • Haemorrhoids: A common cause of pruritus in adults. Patients may also report pain, bleeding, or a palpable lump.
  • Anal Fissure: A small tear in the anal mucosa, typically causing sharp pain on defecation and bright red bleeding. Itching can be a feature as it heals.
  • Skin Conditions: Psoriasis or lichen sclerosus can affect the anogenital skin and cause intense itching. Look for characteristic skin changes or lesions elsewhere on the body.
  • Fungal Infections (Candidiasis): Can cause an itchy, red rash, particularly in skin folds. Often associated with satellite lesions.

Less Common Differentials

  • Other Worms: Infections with other worms like Ascaris lumbricoides (roundworm) or Taenia saginata (tapeworm) are rare in the UK and present with different symptoms, usually more significant gastrointestinal upset.
  • Scabies: While typically affecting finger webs, wrists, and axillae, severe infestations can affect any part of the body and cause intense nocturnal itching. Look for burrows and a history of contact.
5. Management options in primary care

Management involves a dual approach: pharmacological treatment to kill the worms and rigorous hygiene measures to break the cycle of re-infection. All household members should be treated at the same time, even if asymptomatic.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice (Crucial for success)

  • Hygiene Measures (for 6 weeks): Explain that these measures are essential to eliminate eggs from the environment and prevent re-infection. Advise meticulous hand hygiene (especially after using the toilet and before eating), keeping fingernails short, and discouraging scratching. Recommend a morning shower or bath to wash away eggs laid overnight. Change and hot-wash (at 60°C) underwear, pyjamas, and bed linen daily if possible for the first few days, then regularly. Damp-dust the house, especially bedrooms.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Mebendazole: This is the first-line treatment. It is an anthelmintic that works by preventing the worms from absorbing glucose, causing them to die within a few days. It is available OTC for adults and children over 2 years. A second dose is recommended after 2 weeks to treat any worms that have hatched from eggs since the first dose. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Mebendazole: It is also available as a POM, which is necessary if prescribing for children under 2 years old (this is an off-label use that may require specialist advice), or for complex cases. The principles of treatment remain the same. [Rx info]
  • Piperazine: This was previously used but is no longer recommended in UK guidelines and has been largely superseded by mebendazole due to its lower efficacy and different mechanism (it paralyses the worms).
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of threadworms in the UK is guided primarily by the NICE CKS (Clinical Knowledge Summaries) topic on Threadworm. The guidance is clear and consistent across the UK.

The core principles are:

  • Confirmation and Communication: Diagnosis is usually clinical. It is important to reassure patients and parents that threadworm infection is extremely common, not a sign of poor hygiene, and is easily treated.
  • Dual-Action Treatment: Emphasise that pharmacological treatment and hygiene measures are equally important. Medication alone is likely to fail due to re-infection from eggs in the environment.
  • Treat the Household: All members of the household should be treated with an anthelmintic at the same time on the same day, regardless of whether they have symptoms.
  • Two-Dose Regimen: A second dose of medication two weeks after the first is recommended to kill any worms that have hatched in the interim, thus breaking the life cycle.
  • Special Populations: Specific guidance is provided for managing infection in pregnancy, breastfeeding, and in children under two years of age, where hygiene methods are first-line and medication is used with caution.
7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to de-stigmatising the condition and ensuring adherence to the crucial hygiene measures.

Counselling for Self-Care and Treatment

  • Reassurance & Normalisation: Start by explaining that threadworms are extremely common, especially in primary school-aged children, and that having them is not a reflection of poor cleanliness. This reduces embarrassment and encourages open discussion.
  • Explain the Life Cycle: A simple explanation helps patients understand the rationale for treatment and hygiene. For example, "The medicine kills the live worms, but not the tiny eggs. The strict cleaning for six weeks is to get rid of all the eggs in the house so you don't get re-infected."
  • Provide a Concrete Hygiene Plan: Give clear, actionable instructions. Providing a patient information leaflet can be very helpful. Emphasise that this is the most important part of the treatment.
  • Explain 'Treat the Household': Clearly state that everyone living in the house needs to take the medication at the same time, because it's very easy to have the infection without any symptoms.
  • Set Expectations: Explain that itching may not stop for a few days after treatment as the dead worms are passed. The second dose in two weeks is vital to complete the cure.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting for threadworms involves giving clear advice about when to seek further help if the problem does not resolve as expected.

Advise the patient or parent to seek a further review if:

  • Symptoms of itching persist for more than a week or two after the second dose of medication (assuming hygiene measures have been strictly followed).
  • New symptoms develop, such as severe or persistent abdominal pain, blood in the stool, or unexplained weight loss.
  • The skin around the anus becomes very red, painful, or broken, suggesting a secondary bacterial infection.
  • The patient or family are struggling to comply with the treatment and hygiene regimen and require further support.

Routine follow-up is not usually necessary. The two-dose treatment combined with a six-week hygiene regimen is curative in most cases. If the infection recurs, it is typically a new infection, and the treatment and hygiene cycle should be repeated.

9. Pathophysiology

The causative organism for threadworm infection is Enterobius vermicularis, an intestinal nematode (roundworm). Infection begins with the ingestion of its microscopic eggs. The eggs are resilient and can survive for up to two weeks in the environment, for example in dust, on clothing, or on toys.

Once ingested, the eggs travel to the small intestine (duodenum) where they hatch into larvae. The larvae continue to migrate down the gastrointestinal tract, maturing into adult worms in the caecum and large intestine. The adult worms are small and white, with males measuring 2-5mm and females 8-13mm.

Approximately one month after ingestion, the gravid female worm, full of eggs, migrates down the colon and out of the anus. This migration typically occurs at night. The female worm lays thousands of microscopic eggs on the perianal skin. A sticky, gelatinous substance secreted with the eggs, and the movement of the worm itself, causes the characteristic intense itching (pruritus ani). The subsequent scratching contaminates the fingertips with eggs, which are then transferred to the mouth (auto-infection) or to surfaces, where they can infect others (cross-infection). This completes the life cycle.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is used, it is essential to be aware of contraindications and specific guidance, particularly for Mebendazole, the primary therapeutic agent.

Mebendazole

  • Mechanism: It is a broad-spectrum anthelmintic of the benzimidazole class. It inhibits the formation of worm microtubules and causes a lethal blockage of glucose uptake.
  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to mebendazole is an absolute contraindication. It should be avoided during the first trimester of pregnancy due to teratogenic risk in animal studies. While systemic absorption is low, it is metabolised in the liver and should be used with caution in patients with severe hepatic impairment.
  • Interactions: Co-administration with metronidazole has been associated with a risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome/toxic epidermal necrolysis in very rare case reports, and this combination should be avoided. Systemic absorption is minimal, so other significant interactions are uncommon.
  • Administration: The tablet can be chewed, swallowed whole, or crushed and mixed with food for young children who cannot manage a whole tablet.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Mebendazole is licensed for use in children aged 2 years and over. For children under 2, NICE CKS advises that treatment with mebendazole is off-label. While it may be considered in severe or troublesome cases, hygiene methods should be the primary strategy. The decision to prescribe for infants aged 6 months to 2 years should be made on a case-by-case basis, balancing the risks and benefits. Specialist advice may be sought. Dosing for those under 6 months is not recommended.

Older adults

Management is the same as for other adults. However, it is important to consider the differential diagnosis for pruritus ani more broadly in this population, as conditions like haemorrhoids, dermatitis, or malignancy become more prevalent. Ensure they are physically able to undertake the rigorous cleaning required.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Hygiene measures for 6 weeks are the recommended first-line approach. Mebendazole is contraindicated in the first trimester of pregnancy. If drug treatment is considered essential after the first trimester, the potential risks and benefits must be discussed with the patient. According to the UK Teratology Information Service (UKTIS), there is no evidence of an increased malformation risk with use in the second or third trimesters. Mebendazole is minimally absorbed into the bloodstream, and levels in breast milk are expected to be very low, so it is considered compatible with breastfeeding.

People with significant comorbidities

Threadworm infection does not typically pose a specific risk to individuals with comorbidities. Treatment with mebendazole is generally safe as it has very low systemic absorption. Caution is advised in severe liver disease. There are no specific considerations for conditions like asthma, COPD, or diabetes.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Primary School Child

  • Presentation: A 35-year-old mother calls for advice. Her 6-year-old daughter has been waking up crying in the night for the past three nights, complaining of an "itchy bottom". The mother looked last night with a torch and saw "tiny white wiggly threads". The rest of the family, including a 4-year-old son, are asymptomatic.
  • Assessment: The history is classic for threadworms. No red flags are present.
  • Diagnosis: Threadworm infection.
  • Management: The GP reassures the mother that this is a very common and easily treated condition. They explain the need to treat the whole household simultaneously to prevent re-infection. They advise her to buy mebendazole over the counter for everyone in the family, and to give a second dose to everyone in two weeks. The crucial role of the 6-week hygiene regimen is explained and a patient information leaflet is recommended.

Case Study 2: Persistent Symptoms in an Adult

  • Presentation: A 42-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of perianal itching. He has tried over-the-counter mebendazole twice, two weeks apart, on two separate occasions, and has been "fairly good" with hygiene. The itching is intermittent but sometimes wakes him at night. He has not seen any worms.
  • Assessment: On questioning, he reports no abdominal pain, bleeding, or weight loss. Examination of the perianal area reveals erythematous, slightly thickened skin with some excoriation, but no visible fissures or haemorrhoids.
  • Diagnosis & Action: While threadworms are a possibility, the persistence despite treatment and the clinical appearance raise suspicion of an alternative diagnosis like perianal eczema. The GP explains that the itch may now be due to a skin condition. A trial of a mild topical steroid and emollient is prescribed, alongside advice on avoiding soaps and excessive wiping. He is safety-netted to return if symptoms do not improve or worsen.
