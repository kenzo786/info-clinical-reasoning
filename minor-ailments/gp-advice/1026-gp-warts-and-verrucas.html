Warts and Verrucas in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Warts and verrucas are benign epidermal neoplasms caused by the Human Papillomavirus (HPV). They are extremely common in the general population, particularly in school-aged children. Verruca (plantar wart) is the term used for a wart on the sole of the foot.

Patients present with single or multiple firm, hyperkeratotic papules. Their appearance varies by location:

  • Common warts (verruca vulgaris): Dome-shaped papules with an irregular, papillomatous surface. Typically on hands, knuckles, and knees.
  • Plantar warts (verrucas): Flattened by pressure, growing inwards. Often tender or painful on walking. Paring the surface reveals thrombosed capillaries appearing as black dots, a key diagnostic sign.
  • Plane warts: Smooth, flat-topped, skin-coloured papules, usually seen in multiples on the face, neck, and back of hands.
  • Filiform warts: Long, slender growths, often on the face and neck.

Transmission occurs through direct skin-to-skin contact or via contaminated surfaces, such as floors in communal changing rooms. The natural history is one of spontaneous resolution, with around 50% disappearing within one year and two-thirds within two years, especially in children. However, in adults or immunocompromised individuals, they can be more persistent.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While almost always benign, it is essential to consider alternative diagnoses and refer when certain features are present. An urgent referral (e.g., a 2-week-wait cancer pathway in England) should be made for:

  • Suspicion of malignancy: A lesion that is rapidly growing, ulcerated, bleeding without trauma, persistently painful, or has an atypical appearance (e.g., irregular pigmentation, induration). This could represent a squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) or, rarely, an amelanotic melanoma.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the diagnosis is unclear and malignancy cannot be confidently excluded.
  • Anogenital warts: Patients with warts on the anogenital region should be referred to a specialist genitourinary medicine (GUM) or sexual health clinic for diagnosis, management, and screening for other sexually transmitted infections.
  • Extensive or recalcitrant warts in an immunocompromised patient: Organ transplant recipients or those with HIV, for example, have an increased risk of HPV-associated dysplasia and SCC and may benefit from specialist dermatological assessment.
  • Severe functional impact: A wart causing severe pain that significantly limits mobility or daily activities and has not responded to primary care treatments.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach helps to confirm the diagnosis and rule out red flags.

History Taking

  • Lesion History: Ascertain onset, duration, and any change in size or appearance. Ask about symptoms such as pain, itching, or bleeding.
  • Treatment History: Note any previous treatments (OTC or prescribed), how they were used, for how long, and their effect.
    • - Past Medical History: Specifically ask about conditions or medications that cause immunosuppression (e.g., organ transplant, chemotherapy, biologics, HIV). Check for diabetes or peripheral vascular disease, as this may influence treatment choices.
  • Social History: Ask about swimming or use of communal showers. Note if other family members are affected.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the location, number, size, and type of the lesions.
  • Dermoscopy: If available, dermoscopy can aid diagnosis, showing papillomatous structures and thrombosed capillaries (black dots).
  • Confirmation of Verruca: For plantar lesions, it may be necessary to gently pare away the overlying hyperkeratotic skin with a scalpel blade. This will reveal the characteristic pinpoint black dots of a verruca and differentiate it from a corn, which typically has a translucent core and is painful on direct pressure.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The diagnosis is usually straightforward, but several other skin lesions can mimic warts and verrucas.

Common Benign Differentials

  • Corns and Calluses: Hyperkeratotic areas caused by friction or pressure. Typically found on feet over bony prominences. They do not interrupt skin lines and do not have thrombosed capillaries. Pain is elicited on direct pressure, whereas verrucas are often more painful on lateral pressure.
  • Seborrhoeic Keratosis: Common in middle-aged and older adults. Have a greasy, "stuck-on" appearance and a variable brown colour.
  • Molluscum Contagiosum: Caused by a poxvirus. Presents as small, pearly, dome-shaped papules, often with central umbilication. Common in young children.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC): Can present as a persistent, keratotic, or ulcerated nodule. More common in older, sun-exposed, or immunosuppressed individuals. An urgent referral is warranted if suspected.
  • Actinic Keratosis: A pre-malignant lesion on sun-exposed skin, typically appearing as a scaly, rough patch.
  • Amelanotic Melanoma: A rare but important differential. Presents as a firm, growing pink or red nodule. Any new, growing, or atypical non-pigmented lesion should raise suspicion.
5. Management options in primary care

Management should be guided by a shared decision with the patient, considering the high rate of spontaneous resolution against the potential for treatment-related side effects and the time commitment required.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Active Monitoring / No Treatment: Explain the benign nature and high likelihood of spontaneous resolution, especially in children. This is a valid first-line strategy.
  • Prevention of Spread: Advise against scratching or picking warts. Encourage the use of waterproof plasters, flip-flops in communal showers, and not sharing towels, shoes or socks.
  • Duct Tape Occlusion: Involves covering the wart with duct tape for six days, removing it, soaking the area, and debriding with an emery board or pumice stone. The cycle is repeated for up to 2-3 months. Evidence is mixed, but it is a low-risk option.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Salicylic Acid: The first-line chemical treatment. It acts as a keratolytic, destroying the virus-infected epidermis. Available as gels, paints, or plasters. Requires consistent, daily application for up to 3 months. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs) and Clinic Procedures

  • Cryotherapy: Application of liquid nitrogen to freeze and destroy the wart tissue. Treatment can be painful and may cause blistering or pigmentary changes. It is typically repeated every 2-4 weeks. Evidence suggests it is no more effective than properly applied salicylic acid but is often perceived as a more definitive treatment by patients. [Rx info]
  • Other Keratolytics: Preparations containing higher concentrations of salicylic acid, or combinations with lactic acid or formaldehyde, may be used for stubborn warts. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is primarily guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) for Warts and Verrucas. The guidance emphasises a patient-centred approach.

The core recommendations are:

  • For cutaneous warts, offer either no treatment or topical salicylic acid as first-line options. The choice should be based on shared decision-making, considering patient preference and the likelihood of spontaneous resolution.
  • Salicylic acid should be applied daily for at least 12 weeks. Patients should be advised on the correct application technique, including protecting surrounding skin and debriding the wart surface.
  • Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen is a potential second-line option in primary care if salicylic acid is ineffective or unsuitable. However, NICE CKS notes that its effectiveness is uncertain compared to salicylic acid, and it is more likely to cause adverse effects.
  • Many treatments historically offered in primary care, such as curettage, cautery, or other topical agents, are not routinely recommended due to a lack of strong evidence or the risk of adverse events like scarring. Referral to dermatology should be considered for warts that are extensive, causing significant distress, or have failed to respond to a full course of both salicylic acid and cryotherapy.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to managing expectations and ensuring treatment adherence.

Counselling for Self-Care and OTC Treatment

  • Explain the Cause and Natural History: Clarify that warts are caused by a common virus and are not a sign of poor hygiene. Emphasise that they are harmless and very likely to resolve on their own, although this can take up to two years.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that warts can be cosmetically displeasing or painful, but reassure that they are not a serious medical problem.
  • Guide Treatment Application: If recommending salicylic acid, provide clear instructions: "Soak the wart in warm water for 5 minutes, gently file the surface with a pumice stone, apply the product carefully to the wart only, protecting the surrounding skin with petroleum jelly, and let it dry. Do this every evening."
  • Set Realistic Expectations: Stress that treatment requires persistence, will take up to three months, and may not be successful. It can also cause skin irritation.

Counselling for Cryotherapy

  • Explain the Procedure: Describe what will happen, including the intense cold sensation and stinging pain during and for a few minutes after the freeze.
  • Warn About After-Effects: Inform the patient that a blister may form within 24 hours. Advise them not to burst it; if it breaks, they should apply a clean dressing. Mention the possibility of pain, scarring, and skin discolouration.
  • Clarify the Treatment Course: Explain that multiple sessions, typically 2-4 weeks apart, are usually necessary for success.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is essential to ensure patients seek help appropriately if the lesion behaves unusually or treatment causes problems.

Advise the patient to re-consult if they notice any of the following:

  • Features of malignancy: The lesion starts to grow quickly, changes colour (especially becoming pigmented), bleeds spontaneously, becomes ulcerated, or feels hard and fixed.
  • Signs of infection: The area becomes very red, hot, swollen, painful, or discharges pus (this is a complication of treatment rather than the wart itself).
  • Severe treatment side effects: Excessive pain or blistering that the patient is unable to manage.
  • Failure to resolve: The wart has not responded after a full 3-month course of salicylic acid, or after 4-6 sessions of cryotherapy. At this point, the diagnosis should be reconsidered and referral could be discussed.

For patients undergoing cryotherapy, a scheduled follow-up for the next treatment session in 2-4 weeks is standard. For those embarking on self-treatment, a review can be arranged after 3 months if there has been no improvement.

9. Pathophysiology

Warts are caused by infection of the epidermal basal keratinocytes by the Human Papillomavirus (HPV), a non-enveloped DNA virus. Different HPV genotypes have a predilection for specific body sites and cause different types of warts; for example, HPV-1 is commonly associated with plantar warts, while HPV-2 and HPV-4 cause common warts.

The virus gains entry to the basal layer of the epidermis through minor skin trauma or maceration. Once inside the host cell, the viral DNA replicates. The virus expresses proteins (e.g., E6 and E7) that interfere with normal cell cycle regulation, promoting keratinocyte proliferation. This results in the characteristic epidermal thickening (acanthosis) and surface hyperkeratosis seen clinically as a wart.

The virus also has mechanisms to evade the host's innate and adaptive immune systems, allowing it to establish a persistent infection. The classic black dots seen within warts, especially verrucas, are thrombosed, dilated capillaries within the dermal papillae, which grow up into the thickened epidermis. Spontaneous resolution occurs when the host's cell-mediated immune system eventually recognises the HPV-infected cells and mounts a successful cytotoxic T-cell response to clear them.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication or a procedure is used, it is essential to be aware of contraindications and potential adverse effects.

Topical Salicylic Acid

  • Contraindications: Should not be used on the face, anogenital areas, moles, or birthmarks. Use with caution in patients with diabetes or peripheral vascular disease due to the increased risk of skin ulceration and impaired healing.
  • Side Effects: The most common side effect is local skin irritation, peeling, and redness. Systemic absorption and salicylate toxicity are not a concern when used as directed on small areas.
  • Counselling Point: Advise patients to protect the surrounding healthy skin with soft paraffin (Vaseline) or a corn plaster before applying the acid.

Cryotherapy (Liquid Nitrogen)

  • Contraindications: Relative contraindications include very young children (due to pain), patients with significant peripheral vascular disease, cryoglobulinaemia, or Raynaud's phenomenon. Avoid use over superficial nerves (e.g., the side of the finger, posterior to the fibular head) and around the nail matrix.
  • Side Effects & Risks: Pain is expected. Blistering is common and can sometimes be large and haemorrhagic. Other risks include permanent hypopigmentation or hyperpigmentation (more common in darker skin types), scarring, and, rarely, nerve damage leading to numbness.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Spontaneous resolution is very common (up to two-thirds resolve within 2 years). A "watch and see" approach is often the most appropriate management. If treatment is desired, topical salicylic acid is first-line. Cryotherapy is often poorly tolerated due to pain and should be used with caution, if at all. Duct tape occlusion is a painless alternative.

Older adults

It is crucial to maintain a higher index of suspicion for malignancy (SCC, melanoma) mimicking a wart. Warts may be more persistent. Caution is required when treating lesions on the lower legs due to the potential for poorer healing and ulceration. Check for comorbidities like diabetes and peripheral vascular disease before initiating treatment with salicylic acid or cryotherapy.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Non-pharmacological advice and active monitoring are first-line. Cryotherapy is considered a safe treatment option during pregnancy and breastfeeding. The use of topical salicylic acid is generally considered low risk if applied to a small, single area for a limited duration, but absorption is possible, and it may be prudent to avoid its use, particularly in the third trimester. Always consult the latest BNF guidance.

People with significant comorbidities

In immunocompromised patients (e.g., organ transplant recipients, uncontrolled HIV), warts can be numerous, widespread, and very resistant to treatment. There is also an increased risk of malignant transformation to squamous cell carcinoma within these lesions. There should be a low threshold for referral to a dermatologist for assessment and management planning.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: Child with multiple hand warts

  • Presentation: An 8-year-old boy presents with his mother. He has had several warts on the fingers of both hands for about a year. They are not painful, but he is being teased at school. His mother has tried various OTC remedies intermittently without success.
  • Assessment: Examination confirms multiple common warts (verruca vulgaris) on his fingers. There are no red flag features.
  • Management: The GP explains the viral cause and high chance of spontaneous resolution. They discuss the pros and cons of treatment. They agree on a trial of consistent daily treatment with a salicylic acid gel. The GP provides detailed counselling on correct application and the importance of persistence for 3 months. They also provide safety netting advice about skin irritation.

Case Study 2: The painful verruca

  • Presentation: A 42-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of a painful lesion on the ball of his foot. It hurts when he walks. He has tried an OTC freezing spray which was very painful but did not help.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals a 1cm plantar wart. The GP gently pares the surface, which reveals pinpoint black dots, confirming the diagnosis of a verruca.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The diagnosis is a plantar wart. Given the pain and failure of OTC treatment, the GP discusses options. They explain that both salicylic acid and cryotherapy are options. The patient opts for cryotherapy. The GP counsels on the pain of the procedure, likely blistering, and the need for several sessions. They perform the first treatment and arrange a follow-up in 3 weeks.

Case Study 3: The 'wart' in an older person

  • Presentation: A 75-year-old woman with a history of extensive sun exposure from living abroad presents with a "wart" on her temple that has been present for 4 months. She reports that it has recently grown larger and has bled on a few occasions after towelling her face.
  • Assessment: On examination, there is a 1.2cm firm, pink, indurated nodule with a central keratotic crust and some surrounding telangiectasia. It does not look like a typical viral wart.
  • Action: The GP identifies several red flag features (rapid growth, bleeding, atypical appearance) concerning for a squamous cell carcinoma. They explain the need for an expert opinion and make an urgent 2-week-wait referral to the dermatology department for assessment and biopsy.
