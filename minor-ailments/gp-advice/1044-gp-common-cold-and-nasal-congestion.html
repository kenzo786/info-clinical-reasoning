Common Cold (Nasal Congestion) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

The common cold is one of the most frequent reasons for primary care consultation, particularly in autumn and winter. It is an acute, self-limiting viral infection of the upper respiratory tract.

Patients typically present with a combination of symptoms including a blocked or runny nose (rhinorrhoea), sneezing, sore throat, and often a mild cough. The nasal discharge may initially be clear, becoming thicker and yellow or green after a few days; this change reflects inflammatory cell influx and does not reliably indicate bacterial infection. A low-grade fever and general malaise are common. Symptoms usually peak within 2-3 days and resolve within 7-10 days, although a residual cough may persist longer.

Common triggers are numerous viral pathogens, most commonly Rhinoviruses. The condition is benign and typically resolves without medical intervention. The clinician's role is to exclude more serious pathology, provide reassurance and self-care advice, and manage expectations regarding the natural duration of symptoms.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While the common cold is benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants further investigation or urgent review.

  • Systemic signs of severe illness: Such as rigors, persistent high fever (>38°C), marked breathlessness, or chest pain. This may indicate pneumonia or sepsis.
  • Signs of meningitis: Stiff neck, photophobia, non-blanching rash, or new-onset confusion require emergency assessment.
  • Unilateral facial pain and swelling: Severe, localised pain over the sinuses, particularly if associated with facial swelling, fever, and purulent discharge, may suggest severe acute bacterial sinusitis or a complication like orbital cellulitis.
  • Severe sore throat with stridor or drooling: May indicate a deep neck space infection or epiglottitis (rare in adults due to Hib vaccination), which are airway emergencies.
  • Marked shortness of breath: A respiratory rate >30 breaths/min, inability to complete sentences, or cyanosis suggests significant respiratory compromise (e.g., pneumonia, acute exacerbation of asthma/COPD).
  • Symptoms in an at-risk individual: Immunocompromised patients, the very elderly, or those with significant cardiorespiratory comorbidity who appear systemically unwell require a lower threshold for concern and further assessment.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is key to differentiating a simple cold from more significant conditions.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Clarify the nature of the nasal discharge, presence of sneezing, sore throat, cough, and myalgia. Ask about the chronology and progression of symptoms.
  • Past Medical History: Enquire about pre-existing conditions like asthma, COPD, and allergic rhinitis. Check for immunosuppression.
  • Social History: Note smoking status. Ask about sick contacts.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about fever severity, breathlessness, chest pain, facial pain, headache, and neck stiffness.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of respiratory distress, toxicity, or confusion.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation (SpO2). Pulse and blood pressure are important if sepsis is a possibility.
  • ENT Examination: Inspect the pharynx for inflammation or exudate. Check for nasal mucosal swelling or polyps. Palpate the sinuses for tenderness.
  • Chest Examination: Auscultate the chest for focal signs (crepitations suggesting pneumonia) or generalised wheeze (suggesting bronchospasm).

Scoring Tools

No specific scoring tools are used for a common cold. However, if pneumonia is suspected, the CRB-65 score should be used. If sepsis is a concern, tools like NEWS2 may be relevant in guiding the urgency of referral.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for nasal congestion and rhinorrhoea is broad, but the context of an acute, infectious presentation narrows the possibilities.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Common Cold (Viral URTI): The most common cause. Characterised by a mix of nasal symptoms, sneezing, sore throat, and mild malaise over 7-10 days.
  • Influenza: While sharing some symptoms, influenza is typically marked by a more abrupt onset with high fever, significant myalgia, headache, and prostration.
  • COVID-19: Presentation is highly variable but can be indistinguishable from a common cold. Anosmia/ageusia are more specific but less common with newer variants. A history of exposure and local prevalence are relevant.

Other Common Differentials

  • Acute Bacterial Sinusitis: Consider if symptoms last >10 days with no improvement, or worsen after an initial improvement ('double-sickening'). Features include purulent discharge, severe localised facial pain (often unilateral), and fever.
  • Allergic Rhinitis: Often has a clear temporal pattern (e.g., seasonal). Associated with prominent itching of the nose and eyes, and sneezing. Patients often have a personal or family history of atopy.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Pneumonia: Suggested by high fever, productive cough, breathlessness, pleuritic pain, and focal chest signs.
  • Meningitis: Suggested by headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, and altered mental state.
  • Nasal Foreign Body: Consider in children or vulnerable adults with persistent, unilateral, foul-smelling nasal discharge.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is centred on reassurance, education about the condition's natural history, and advice on symptomatic relief. Antibiotics are not indicated for an uncomplicated common cold.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain the viral nature of the illness, that it will resolve on its own, and that antibiotics are ineffective and unnecessary.
  • Rest and Hydration: Advise adequate fluid intake and rest as needed.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Steam inhalation (with caution to avoid scalds), saline nasal sprays or rinses can help to clear congestion.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol or ibuprofen can help with associated fever, headache, and myalgia. [Rx info]
  • Decongestants (Topical): Agents like xylometazoline or oxymetazoline cause local vasoconstriction in the nasal mucosa, providing rapid relief from congestion. Advise use for a maximum of 5-7 days to avoid rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa). [Rx info]
  • Decongestants (Oral): Pseudoephedrine or phenylephrine act systemically. They are effective but carry risks and contraindications (e.g., in hypertension, heart disease). [Rx info]
  • Saline Preparations: Isotonic or hypertonic saline sprays can help to thin mucus and moisturise the nasal passages. They have an excellent safety profile.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

These have no role in the management of an uncomplicated common cold. Antibiotics should only be considered if a secondary bacterial infection like acute bacterial sinusitis is strongly suspected, in line with NICE guidance.

6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of the common cold is addressed within several UK guidelines, primarily the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) on "Common Cold" and guidelines on antimicrobial prescribing (e.g., NICE NG120 on cough).

Guidance is unequivocal: the common cold is a viral, self-limiting illness for which antibiotics are not beneficial. The core principles of management are to promote self-care, provide advice on symptomatic relief, and ensure robust antimicrobial stewardship. Evidence shows that antibiotics do not alter the course of the illness, prevent complications, or reduce symptom duration, but do cause side effects and contribute to antimicrobial resistance.

For symptomatic relief, the evidence for many OTC remedies is limited, but simple analgesia and topical decongestants (for short-term use) are recognised as effective. Saline nasal sprays are also supported. Guidelines emphasise the importance of clear safety netting advice, instructing patients when to re-consult if their condition does not follow the expected course.

The regulation of oral decongestants like pseudoephedrine is also relevant, with MHRA guidance highlighting their sale is restricted due to misuse in illicit methamphetamine production, and more recently, a review of the small risk of posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) and reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS).

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is paramount in managing the common cold, primarily to manage expectations and reduce the demand for unnecessary antibiotics.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that a cold is caused by a virus and typically lasts for 7-10 days. Explain that the colour of nasal discharge is not an indicator for antibiotics.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that the symptoms are unpleasant and disruptive but provide reassurance that it is not a serious illness for most people.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: Use a clear, simple analogy. For example, "Antibiotics are for infections caused by bacteria, but colds are caused by viruses. Taking an antibiotic won't help you get better and could cause side effects." The TARGET patient information leaflet can support this conversation.
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions they can take, such as getting rest, drinking plenty of fluids, and using specific OTC treatments like paracetamol for pain or saline sprays for congestion.

Counselling for OTC Treatments

  • Decongestant Nasal Sprays: When recommending these, it is vital to explicitly advise using them for no more than 5-7 consecutive days to prevent rebound congestion, which can be difficult to manage.
  • Oral Decongestants: Advise patients to check with the pharmacist before taking them, especially if they have any other health conditions (like high blood pressure) or are taking other medicines.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step in the consultation for any self-limiting condition. It involves providing clear, specific advice on when and how to seek further medical help.

Clear verbal and, if appropriate, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • Symptoms do not show any sign of improvement after 10 days, or significantly worsen.
  • They develop a persistent high temperature (>38°C) and feel systemically very unwell.
  • They become significantly breathless or develop chest pain.
  • They develop a severe headache, stiff neck, or an aversion to bright lights.
  • They develop severe, one-sided facial pain or swelling around the eye.
  • They have any other symptom that they are seriously concerned about.

For most healthy individuals, no specific follow-up is required. For higher-risk patients (e.g., frail, significant comorbidities) who are managed in the community, it may be appropriate to advise them to call for a review if they are not starting to improve within a specified timeframe (e.g., 3-5 days).

9. Pathophysiology

The common cold is an acute viral infection of the upper respiratory tract epithelium, primarily affecting the nose and nasopharynx. Rhinoviruses are responsible for 30-50% of cases, with other pathogens including coronaviruses, influenza viruses, and adenoviruses.

The virus invades the mucosal cells, leading to direct cell damage and triggering a local inflammatory response. Inflammatory mediators such as bradykinin and prostaglandins are released. This leads to vasodilation of blood vessels in the nasal submucosa and increased vascular permeability, resulting in oedema (nasal congestion) and transudation of fluid (rhinorrhoea). There is also stimulation of mucous glands, further contributing to the nasal discharge.

Sneezing is a reflex stimulated by histamine release from mast cells. A sore throat is caused by inflammation of the pharyngeal mucosa. Systemic symptoms like malaise and low-grade fever are mediated by cytokines (e.g., interleukins, TNF-alpha) that are released as part of the host's innate immune response to the viral infection. The change in sputum or nasal discharge colour from clear to yellow/green is primarily due to the influx of neutrophils, and is not in itself a sign of bacterial superinfection.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When advising on OTC medications for symptomatic relief, it is essential to be aware of contraindications and specific safety guidance.

Oral Decongestants (e.g., Pseudoephedrine)

  • Mechanism: Sympathomimetic agent that causes systemic vasoconstriction.
  • Contraindications: Avoid in patients with severe hypertension, coronary artery disease, or those taking monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs).
  • Cautions: Use with caution in patients with diabetes, hyperthyroidism, glaucoma, and benign prostatic hyperplasia (can cause urinary retention).
  • MHRA Alerts: A recent review highlighted a small risk of posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) and reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS). Patients should be advised to stop using these medicines and seek medical advice if they develop symptoms of a severe headache, nausea, or seizures.

Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline)

  • Mechanism: Direct alpha-adrenergic agonist causing local vasoconstriction.
  • Side Effects & Risks: The primary risk is rhinitis medicamentosa (rebound congestion) if used for longer than 7 days. This is caused by downregulation of adrenergic receptors, leading to dependence on the spray for nasal patency.

Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs, e.g., Ibuprofen)

  • Contraindications: Avoid in patients with a history of hypersensitivity to NSAIDs, active peptic ulcer disease, severe heart failure, or severe renal impairment. Use with caution in asthma.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Most colds are benign. OTC cough and cold medicines containing decongestants are not recommended for children under 6 years, and should be used only on pharmacist advice for children aged 6 to 12, due to a lack of evidence of efficacy and risk of side effects. Simple analgesics like paracetamol or ibuprofen (if appropriate) can be used. Saline nasal drops or sprays are safe and can be effective for relieving congestion, especially before feeds in infants. A warm drink of lemon and honey can be soothing for an associated cough in children over 1 year old (honey is contraindicated in infants under 12 months due to the risk of infant botulism).

Older adults

Older adults may present atypically and are at higher risk of complications like pneumonia. Oral decongestants should be used with significant caution due to the increased prevalence of cardiovascular disease and hypertension, and the risk of inducing urinary retention in men with prostatism. Drug interactions are more likely due to polypharmacy. There should be a lower threshold for clinical assessment if symptoms are severe or prolonged.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Non-pharmacological advice is first-line. Saline nasal sprays are safe. Simple paracetamol is the analgesic of choice. Most oral decongestants are best avoided during pregnancy (especially the first trimester) and breastfeeding. Short-term use of topical decongestant sprays may be considered if essential, but patients should consult with a pharmacist or GP. Many combination OTC products contain multiple ingredients which must be checked individually for safety.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with hypertension or cardiovascular disease, oral decongestants should be avoided due to their pressor effects. In patients with asthma, a viral cold is a common trigger for an exacerbation. They should be advised to monitor their symptoms closely and follow their asthma action plan. In patients with diabetes, oral decongestants can affect blood glucose levels and should be used with caution.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The University Student

  • Presentation: A 19-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of a runny nose, sneezing, and a "scratchy" throat. She feels tired but is otherwise well. She is worried because her flatmates have all had the same thing.
  • Assessment: She is afebrile (37.2°C), respiratory rate is 14/min, and looks well. Examination of her throat shows mild pharyngeal erythema. Her chest is clear.
  • Diagnosis: Uncomplicated common cold (viral URTI).
  • Management: The GP reassures her that this is a typical viral cold. They explain that antibiotics are not needed and advise on self-care: rest, fluids, and paracetamol for her throat. They provide clear safety netting advice about when to re-consult, such as developing a high fever or shortness of breath.

Case Study 2: The Office Worker with Prolonged Symptoms

  • Presentation: A 48-year-old male, a non-smoker, presents saying his "cold won't go away". He had a typical cold 2 weeks ago which initially improved, but for the last 5 days, he has developed worsening right-sided facial pain, headache, and a thick, green nasal discharge. He feels feverish.
  • Assessment: Temp: 38.1°C. There is marked tenderness on palpation over the right maxillary sinus. Pharynx is unremarkable. Chest is clear. No signs of systemic sepsis.
  • Diagnosis: Acute bacterial sinusitis, secondary to an initial viral URTI. The history of 'double-sickening' and focal, unilateral signs are key.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis. Following NICE guidance for sinusitis, as the patient has features of more severe illness, a prescription for an antibiotic (e.g., phenoxymethylpenicillin) is considered. Safety netting advice is given to return if symptoms worsen, do not improve in 3 days, or if he develops swelling around the eye.
