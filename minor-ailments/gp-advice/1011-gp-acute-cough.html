Acute Cough in Primary Care
Context: Acute Cough in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute cough is one of the most common reasons for consultation in primary care. It is defined as a cough lasting for less than three weeks. In most cases, it is a symptom of an acute, self-limiting viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).

Patients typically present with a sudden onset of a dry or productive cough, often accompanied by other URTI symptoms such as sore throat, nasal congestion, sneezing, and malaise. A low-grade fever may be present. The cough itself, while the other symptoms may resolve within a week, can persist. It is important to advise patients that a post-viral cough can commonly last for two to three weeks.

Common triggers are viral infections like the common cold, influenza, or COVID-19. The condition is usually benign and resolves without specific medical intervention. The primary role of the clinician is to exclude more serious pathology through a focused assessment and to provide advice on self-care, managing patient expectations regarding the cough's natural duration.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most acute coughs are benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants further investigation, urgent review, or immediate hospital admission.

  • Systemic signs of severe illness: Such as rigors, significant fever (>38°C), or marked breathlessness. This may indicate severe pneumonia or sepsis.
  • Chest pain: Pleuritic pain may suggest pneumonia, pulmonary embolism (PE), or pneumothorax. A dull, persistent, or central crushing pain requires urgent cardiac assessment.
  • Haemoptysis: Coughing up blood, especially if more than a minimal streak, is a red flag for conditions including PE, tuberculosis, bronchiectasis, or malignancy.
  • Marked shortness of breath: A respiratory rate >30 breaths/min, inability to complete sentences, or use of accessory muscles suggests significant respiratory compromise. Consider pneumonia, PE, or acute exacerbation of asthma/COPD.
  • Signs of sepsis: Confusion (new onset), cyanosis, hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg), or tachycardia. These indicate a systemic inflammatory response to infection and require emergency admission.
  • Suspected foreign body: A history of sudden onset of choking or coughing, particularly in children or vulnerable adults, requires urgent assessment.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Individuals with significant immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, or uncontrolled HIV) are at high risk of severe or opportunistic infections and require a lower threshold for investigation and admission.
  • Persistent unilateral wheeze: This could indicate an inhaled foreign body or an endobronchial lesion.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is essential to differentiate benign from serious causes of acute cough.

History Taking

  • Cough Characteristics: Ascertain onset, duration, and nature (dry vs. productive, colour of sputum). Ask about associated symptoms like fever, myalgia, and coryza.
  • Past Medical History: Enquire about pre-existing respiratory conditions (asthma, COPD), cardiac disease, GORD, immunosuppression, or history of VTE.
  • Social History: Smoking status is a critical factor. Also consider occupational or environmental exposures.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about chest pain, haemoptysis, breathlessness at rest, and systemic symptoms.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of respiratory distress, cyanosis, and confusion.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature, respiratory rate, pulse, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation (SpO2).
  • Chest Examination: Auscultate for focal crepitations (pneumonia), generalised wheeze (viral-induced bronchospasm, asthma/COPD), or areas of reduced air entry. Percussion may reveal dullness over consolidation.

Scoring Tools

If pneumonia is suspected based on focal chest signs, the CRB-65 score should be used in primary care to assess mortality risk and guide decisions on place of care:

  • Confusion (new onset)
  • Respiratory Rate (≥30/min)
  • Blood Pressure (systolic <90 mmHg or diastolic ≤60 mmHg)
  • 65 (age ≥65 years)

A score of 0 suggests low risk (home care). A score of 1-2 suggests intermediate risk (consider hospital admission). A score of 3-4 indicates high risk (urgent hospital admission). The CURB-65 score (with Urea >7 mmol/L) is used in the hospital setting.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for acute cough is broad, but most cases seen in general practice are self-limiting viral infections. The clinical assessment aims to exclude more serious pathology.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI): The most frequent cause. Characterised by coryzal symptoms, malaise, and a cough that may be dry or productive. Examination is often unremarkable.
  • Acute Bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchi, almost always viral. Presents with a prominent cough, which may produce clear or coloured sputum. Wheeze can be present. Systemic upset is usually mild.
  • Post-viral Cough: A persistent cough following an initial URTI, due to bronchial hyper-responsiveness. Can last several weeks and is typically dry.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Pneumonia: Suggested by focal chest signs (crepitations, bronchial breathing), fever, and systemic symptoms (e.g., rigors, pleuritic pain). A high CRB-65 score increases the likelihood.
  • Acute Exacerbation of Asthma/COPD: A history of the condition is key. Look for increased breathlessness, wheeze, and use of accessory muscles.
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE): Consider in patients with pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, or unexplained dyspnoea, especially with risk factors for VTE (e.g., immobility, recent surgery, active cancer).
  • Heart Failure: May present with a cough (sometimes with pink, frothy sputum), orthopnoea, and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea. Look for basal crepitations and peripheral oedema.
  • Lung Cancer: Unlikely to present as an acute cough but should be considered if there is haemoptysis, unexplained weight loss, or persistent symptoms (>3 weeks), especially in a smoker.
  • Whooping Cough (Pertussis): Consider in cases of a prolonged, paroxysmal cough followed by an inspiratory gasp ('whoop'), and sometimes post-tussive vomiting.
5. Management options in primary care

Management focuses on reassurance, education, and symptomatic relief. Antibiotics are rarely indicated. All advice should be framed within the context of antimicrobial stewardship.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain that the cough is typically viral, self-limiting, and that antibiotics are ineffective. Advise that a cough can persist for 3-4 weeks.
  • Rest and Hydration: Advise adequate fluid intake and rest.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Simple measures like inhaling steam (with caution to avoid scalds) and drinking hot lemon and honey can be soothing.
  • Smoking Cessation: Provide advice and support for smoking cessation.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

Advise patients that the evidence for the effectiveness of most OTC cough remedies is limited.

  • Simple Linctus: Contains citric acid monohydrate. Acts as a demulcent, soothing the throat. Available as a GSL medicine. Evidence of efficacy is limited but it is well tolerated and considered safe in pregnancy.
  • Expectorants (e.g., Guaifenesin): Claim to increase the volume and reduce the viscosity of sputum. Their clinical efficacy is debated and evidence of benefit is limited; advise patients accordingly to manage expectations.
  • Suppressants (e.g., Dextromethorphan): Acts on the cough centre in the brain. May offer modest relief for a troublesome dry cough. Avoid in patients taking MAOIs. Not appropriate for productive coughs where expectoration is beneficial.
  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol or ibuprofen can help with associated fever, headache, and myalgia.
  • Withdrawn Medicines: Pholcodine-containing products were withdrawn in March 2023 due to risks of anaphylaxis to neuromuscular blockers used in general anaesthesia. Patients should be advised to not use any remaining products.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

These have a very limited role in uncomplicated acute cough.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Doxycycline): Only consider if the patient is systemically unwell, at high risk of complications (e.g., significant comorbidity), or has suspected pneumonia with a CRB-65 score ≥1. Do not prescribe for simple acute bronchitis. Follow NICE NG120 and local antimicrobial guidance; prescribe the shortest appropriate course and check for allergy before prescribing.
  • Codeine Linctus: A weak opioid cough suppressant. Following MHRA advice (Feb 2024), it is now a POM due to risks of dependence and overdose. Its use should be reserved for a troublesome, dry, persistent cough over the short term where other measures have failed. Avoid in children under 12, patients with significant respiratory compromise, and during breastfeeding. Counsel on drowsiness and dependence risk.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of acute cough in the UK is guided primarily by NICE guideline NG120 (Cough (acute): antimicrobial prescribing) and the associated CKS topic summary. The core principles are rooted in promoting self-care and robust antimicrobial stewardship.

NICE guidance is unequivocal: do not routinely offer an antibiotic for an acute cough (including acute bronchitis) in patients who are not systemically very unwell or at higher risk of complications. The evidence shows that antibiotics have minimal impact on symptom duration or severity in uncomplicated cases but do contribute to adverse effects and antimicrobial resistance.

For patients not requiring immediate antibiotics, guidelines recommend providing self-care advice. A back-up or delayed prescription for antibiotics may be considered for patients at higher risk of complications, with clear instructions on when to use it. If pneumonia is suspected, the CRB-65 score should be used to guide management and place of care. NICE also advises against offering oral or inhaled corticosteroids for acute bronchitis.

The MHRA plays a key role, issuing safety updates that influence prescribing. Recent examples include the withdrawal of pholcodine from the market and the reclassification of codeine linctus to a prescription-only medicine, both of which must be reflected in current clinical practice.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is central to managing acute cough, particularly in managing patient expectations and reducing pressure for inappropriate antibiotic prescribing.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that a cough is a normal response to a viral infection and can take 3-4 weeks to resolve fully. This can be a key piece of information that prevents re-consultation.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that a persistent cough is frustrating and disruptive but reassure them that it is usually not a sign of serious illness.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: Use a clear, simple explanation. For example, "Antibiotics work on bacteria, but this cough is caused by a virus. Using an antibiotic won't help you get better any faster and could give you side effects like a rash or diarrhoea."
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions they can take, such as rest, fluids, and using honey or simple linctus for symptomatic relief. The TARGET patient information leaflet can be a useful resource.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Delayed Prescriptions: If providing a back-up prescription, give explicit instructions on when to use it (e.g., "Only start this if you become significantly more breathless or develop a high fever and feel very unwell") and when not to (e.g., "Don't use it just because the cough is still there in a week").
  • Side Effects: When prescribing any medicine, including antibiotics or codeine, briefly counsel on common and important potential side effects.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step in the consultation for any self-limiting condition. It involves providing clear, specific advice on when and how to seek further medical help if the condition worsens or does not follow the expected course.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The cough worsens significantly or does not improve after 3-4 weeks.
  • They become breathless at rest or find their breathing is rapid.
  • They develop sharp chest pain, especially if it is worse on breathing in.
  • They cough up any blood.
  • They start to feel systemically very unwell (e.g., high fever, rigors, confusion, drowsiness).
  • They have any other symptom that they are concerned about.

For patients who were assessed as higher risk but managed in the community, a scheduled follow-up may be appropriate, either via telephone or a face-to-face review in 2-3 days, depending on the clinical scenario.

9. Pathophysiology

The cough reflex is a vital protective mechanism for the respiratory system, clearing the airways of foreign material, microbes, and excess secretions. It is a complex reflex arc involving sensory receptors, afferent pathways, a central cough centre, and efferent pathways.

Sensory receptors (nociceptors and mechanoreceptors) are located throughout the respiratory tract, from the larynx down to the terminal bronchioles. In an acute viral URTI, the primary trigger is inflammation. Viral pathogens cause epithelial damage and inflammatory cell infiltration. This releases inflammatory mediators such as bradykinin, prostaglandins, and tachykinins.

These mediators sensitise the afferent nerve endings of the vagus nerve (cranial nerve X). This leads to an exaggerated response to stimuli like cold air or inhaled particles—a phenomenon known as bronchial hyper-responsiveness. The afferent signals travel via the vagus nerve to the cough centre in the medulla oblongata of the brainstem. The cough centre processes these signals and initiates the efferent response. This involves coordinated signals to the diaphragm, abdominal muscles, and larynx, resulting in the characteristic explosive expiratory manoeuvre of a cough. The persistence of cough after the initial infection has cleared (post-viral cough) is thought to be due to this ongoing airway inflammation and neuronal hypersensitivity.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is considered, it is essential to be aware of contraindications, interactions, and specific safety guidance.

Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Doxycycline)

  • Contraindications: Penicillin allergy is an absolute contraindication for amoxicillin. Doxycycline is generally contraindicated in pregnancy, breastfeeding, and children under 12 due to effects on teeth and bones.
  • Interactions: Doxycycline absorption can be impaired by antacids, calcium, iron, and zinc supplements.
  • MHRA Alerts: Be mindful of general antibiotic safety updates, such as the risks associated with fluoroquinolones (not first-line for cough) and promoting awareness of antimicrobial resistance.

Opioid Cough Suppressants (Codeine)

  • Regulation: Codeine linctus is now a Prescription-Only Medicine (POM).
  • Contraindications: Avoid in patients at risk of respiratory failure (e.g., severe COPD), in children under 12 years, and during breastfeeding. Use with caution in older adults due to risks of confusion and falls.
  • Side Effects & Risks: Common side effects include constipation and drowsiness. There is a risk of dependence and misuse with prolonged use. Patients should be warned not to drive if they feel drowsy.

Withdrawn Medications

  • Pholcodine: All products containing pholcodine were withdrawn from the UK market in March 2023. This was due to evidence linking its use within the 12 months prior to general anaesthesia with an increased risk of anaphylaxis to neuromuscular blocking agents. It is vital this is no longer recommended or prescribed.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Most coughs are due to self-limiting viral URTIs. Over-the-counter cough and cold medicines are not recommended for children under 6, and for children aged 6 to 12 only with caution, due to a lack of efficacy and risk of side effects. A warm drink of lemon and honey is a safe and soothing option for children over 1 year old (honey should be avoided in infants under 12 months due to the risk of infant botulism). Red flags such as stridor, grunting, severe respiratory distress, or suspected foreign body require urgent referral. A chronic cough (>4 weeks) in a child warrants further investigation.

Older adults

Older adults are more susceptible to complications like pneumonia and may present atypically (e.g., with confusion or falls instead of fever). There should be a lower threshold for performing a CRB-65 score and considering hospital assessment. Polypharmacy is common, so prescribers must be cautious of drug interactions and side effects (e.g., sedative effects of codeine increasing fall risk).

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Non-pharmacological advice is first-line. If medication is needed, simple paracetamol is safe. Simple linctus is considered safe. For troublesome coughs, dextromethorphan may be considered during pregnancy after the first trimester, but check the latest BNF guidance. Codeine should be avoided, particularly near term and during breastfeeding. Many OTC remedies contain multiple ingredients; each must be checked for safety. Doxycycline is contraindicated. The Breastfeeding Network provides useful resources on drug safety.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with conditions like severe asthma, COPD, heart failure, or immunosuppression are at higher risk of complications. An acute cough may signify an exacerbation of their underlying condition. There should be a lower threshold for prescribing antibiotics in line with NICE guidance for this 'at-risk' group. Any treatment must consider potential interactions with their existing medications and the impact on their underlying disease.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young, Healthy Adult

  • Presentation: A 28-year-old non-smoking male presents with a 5-day history of a harsh cough, productive of yellow sputum. He also reports a runny nose and feeling "a bit achy". He is worried he needs antibiotics.
  • Assessment: He is afebrile (37.1°C), respiratory rate is 16/min, SpO2 is 98% on air. Chest auscultation reveals a few scattered rhonchi but no focal signs. He is alert and systemically well.
  • Diagnosis: Acute viral bronchitis. The yellow sputum is explained as inflammatory cells, not necessarily indicating bacterial infection.
  • Management: The GP explains the viral nature of the illness and that antibiotics are not indicated. They provide advice on self-care (fluids, rest, paracetamol for aches) and the natural history, noting the cough may last 2-3 weeks. Clear safety netting advice is given regarding signs of worsening illness.

Case Study 2: The Older Adult with Comorbidities

  • Presentation: A 78-year-old female with known COPD and type 2 diabetes calls for a home visit. She has had a cough for 3 days which is worsening, and she feels more breathless than usual.
  • Assessment: On examination, she is drowsy and confused (new finding). Temp: 38.3°C, RR: 32/min, BP: 100/60 mmHg, SpO2: 89% on air. Chest auscultation reveals coarse crepitations in the right lower lobe.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP calculates a CRB-65 score of 3 (Confusion, RR >30, Age >65), indicating high-risk pneumonia. The GP immediately arranges for emergency hospital admission via a 999 call, providing a detailed handover to the ambulance crew.

Case Study 3: The Persistent Post-Viral Cough

  • Presentation: A 45-year-old female consults for a dry, irritating cough that has been present for four weeks. It started after a bad cold. It is particularly troublesome at night. She is otherwise well and a non-smoker.
  • Assessment: Vital signs are normal. Chest is clear on auscultation. There are no red flags.
  • Diagnosis: Post-viral cough.
  • Management: The GP reassures her that this is common and due to airway hyper-responsiveness. They advise simple self-care measures like sipping water and trying a simple linctus or honey and lemon. They explain it will settle with time but to return if it persists beyond 8 weeks or if new symptoms (like wheeze or breathlessness) develop. The option of a short-term trial of an inhaled corticosteroid could be considered if symptoms are severe and persistent, but this is off-label.
