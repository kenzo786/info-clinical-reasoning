Athlete's Foot (Tinea Pedis) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Athlete's foot, or tinea pedis, is a very common fungal infection of the skin on the feet. It is one of the most frequent dermatophyte infections seen in primary care. The condition is caused by fungi that thrive in warm, moist environments created by occlusive footwear and sweating.

Patients typically present with itching, scaling, and maceration in the interdigital spaces, most commonly between the fourth and fifth toes. Another common presentation is the 'moccasin' type, characterised by fine, dry, silvery scaling, erythema, and hyperkeratosis affecting the soles, heels, and sides of the feet. Less commonly, patients can present with an acute vesicular form (tinea pedis acutum) with small blisters, often on the instep. The condition is often bilateral. While bothersome, it is usually a benign condition that responds well to appropriate treatment.

Common triggers include wearing trainers or tight-fitting shoes for long periods, not drying feet properly after washing, and using communal areas like swimming pools and changing rooms. Most cases can be managed effectively with advice and topical treatments.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While tinea pedis is typically benign, it is crucial to identify signs of complication or diagnostic uncertainty that require further action.

  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection (cellulitis): Spreading, painful erythema extending beyond the sole of the foot, with associated warmth, swelling, and tenderness. The patient may have systemic symptoms such as fever and malaise. This requires urgent antibiotic treatment.
  • Ulceration or significant skin breakdown: This is particularly concerning in patients with diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, or immunosuppression, as it can lead to serious complications including diabetic foot ulcers or osteomyelitis.
  • Failure to respond to appropriate first- and second-line treatments: If a patient has correctly used a topical and then an oral antifungal with no improvement, the diagnosis should be reconsidered. Referral to dermatology may be needed for further investigation (e.g., skin scrapings for mycology) or alternative diagnoses.
  • Widespread or severe disease at presentation: Extensive moccasin-type tinea or severe vesicular tinea may warrant specialist advice from the outset, particularly if the patient is immunocompromised.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the rash has atypical features suggesting an alternative diagnosis (e.g., inflammatory conditions like psoriasis or eczema) that cannot be confidently distinguished in primary care, referral may be appropriate.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is key to confirming the diagnosis and excluding red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Ascertain onset, duration, and specific symptoms (itch, scaling, pain, blisters). Ask which areas of the feet are affected.
  • Treatments Tried: Enquire about any previous or current treatments, including over-the-counter preparations and prescription medicines, and how they were used (e.g., duration of application).
  • Past Medical History: Specifically ask about diabetes, immunosuppression (e.g., due to HIV, chemotherapy, or steroids), psoriasis, eczema, and peripheral vascular disease.
  • Social and Lifestyle History: Ask about occupation (e.g., jobs requiring safety boots), hobbies (e.g., swimming, athletics), and typical footwear. This helps identify contributing factors.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about spreading redness, pain, fever, or any breaks in the skin.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the patient's general health and note any signs of systemic illness.
  • Foot Examination: Inspect all areas of the feet, including soles, heels, and between all toes. Note the pattern of the rash (interdigital, moccasin, vesicular). Look for maceration, scaling, erythema, and skin fissures.
  • Nail Examination: Carefully inspect the toenails for signs of associated fungal nail infection (onychomycosis), such as thickening, discolouration, and subungual hyperkeratosis, as co-existing infection is common and may require systemic treatment.
  • Signs of Complication: Check for signs of cellulitis (warmth, swelling, tender spreading erythema) or ulceration.

In primary care, diagnosis is usually clinical. Skin scrapings for microscopy and culture are rarely performed but can be useful in cases of diagnostic uncertainty or treatment failure, typically arranged via dermatology.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for an itchy, scaly rash on the feet is broad. The clinical history and distribution pattern are key to diagnosis.

Common Differentials

  • Eczema (Dermatitis): Can be indistinguishable. Pompholyx (dyshidrotic) eczema typically presents with intensely itchy vesicles on the sides of the fingers, palms, and soles of the feet. Contact dermatitis may be caused by an allergen in footwear (e.g., leather chemicals, dyes). Eczema is often more vesicular and weepy than tinea.
  • Psoriasis: Plantar psoriasis can mimic moccasin-type tinea with well-demarcated, erythematous plaques and silvery scale. Look for clues elsewhere, such as nail pitting or psoriatic plaques on the scalp, elbows, and knees.
  • Erythrasma: A bacterial infection (caused by *Corynebacterium minutissimum*) that affects intertriginous areas. In the toe webs, it causes maceration and fine scaling, similar to tinea. It may fluoresce coral-pink under a Wood's lamp.
  • Pitted Keratolysis: A bacterial infection causing malodorous, "punched-out" pits on the soles of the feet, particularly in the context of hyperhidrosis. It is typically not as itchy or scaly as tinea.

Distinguishing Features

Tinea pedis is often asymmetrical, at least initially, and typically involves the toe webs. The presence of onychomycosis strongly supports a fungal cause. A trial of appropriate antifungal treatment can be both diagnostic and therapeutic. If there is significant doubt or co-existing pathology is suspected, a dermatology opinion may be required.

5. Management options in primary care

Management focuses on eradicating the fungus with antifungal medicines and modifying environmental factors with self-care advice.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Foot Hygiene: Wash feet daily and dry them thoroughly, especially between the toes. Use a separate towel for the feet and wash it regularly.
  • Socks and Footwear: Wear clean cotton or moisture-wicking socks daily. Avoid tight, occlusive footwear; opt for leather or breathable materials. Allow shoes to dry completely before wearing them again.
  • Avoid Spreading: Do not share towels, shoes, or socks. Wear flip-flops or pool sandals in communal areas like changing rooms and showers.
  • At Home: Go barefoot at home where possible to allow feet to air.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

These are the first-line treatment for uncomplicated interdigital tinea pedis.

  • Topical Imidazoles (e.g., Clotrimazole, Miconazole): These are fungistatic, stopping the fungus from growing. They are available as creams, sprays, and powders. Should be applied to the affected area and surrounding skin and continued for 1-2 weeks after the rash has cleared. [Rx info]
  • Topical Terbinafine: This is fungicidal, killing the fungus. It is often effective with a shorter treatment course, typically one week. Some formulations are designed as a single application. [Rx info]
  • Tolnaftate / Undecenoic Acid: Older antifungal preparations, also available OTC.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Combination Topical Preparations (e.g., Miconazole/Hydrocortisone): A combination of an antifungal and a mild topical steroid can be useful for short-term treatment if there is marked inflammation and itch. Use should be limited to 7 days to avoid skin atrophy. [Rx info]
  • Oral Antifungals (e.g., Terbinafine, Itraconazole): Reserved for extensive moccasin-type tinea, infection that is resistant to topical therapy, or if there is co-existing onychomycosis requiring systemic treatment. The choice depends on the causative organism, patient comorbidities, and potential drug interactions. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of tinea pedis in the UK is guided by NICE CKS (Fungal skin infection - foot) and the British Association of Dermatologists (BAD) guidelines.

The core principles are to first establish a confident clinical diagnosis and provide comprehensive advice on foot hygiene to prevent recurrence. Guidelines recommend topical antifungal therapy as the first-line treatment for most cases of athlete's foot. Terbinafine cream is often recommended due to its fungicidal action and shorter treatment duration, though imidazoles like clotrimazole and miconazole are also effective first-line options.

For moccasin-type tinea pedis, which responds poorly to topical agents alone, guidelines support the use of oral antifungal therapy (e.g., terbinafine or itraconazole). Systemic treatment is also indicated for severe, extensive, or refractory infections, or where there is significant onychomycosis. Prescribing of oral antifungals requires careful consideration of potential side effects and drug interactions.

The use of combination creams containing a mild steroid and an antifungal is supported for inflamed presentations, but treatment should be brief (maximum 7-14 days) before switching to a topical antifungal alone. The evidence does not support the use of antibiotics unless there are clear clinical signs of a secondary bacterial infection like cellulitis.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to successful treatment and preventing recurrence.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Condition: Reassure the patient that athlete's foot is a very common and treatable fungal infection, not a sign of poor personal hygiene. Explain that the fungus thrives in warm, damp conditions.
  • Empower with Hygiene Advice: Clearly explain the key self-care measures (thorough drying, breathable footwear, sock rotation, use of flip-flops in communal areas). This is crucial for both treating the current episode and preventing future ones.
  • Manage Expectations: Advise that while symptoms may improve quickly, treatment should be continued for the recommended duration (often 1-2 weeks after the skin appears normal) to fully eradicate the fungus and prevent relapse.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Topical Treatment Application: Demonstrate or explain how to apply the cream: a thin layer to the affected skin and about 1-2cm of the surrounding normal skin. Advise them to wash and dry hands before and after application.
  • Oral Antifungals: If prescribing oral terbinafine or itraconazole, explain that this is because the infection is extensive or has not responded to creams. Counsel on the course length and key potential side effects (e.g., taste disturbance or GI upset for terbinafine; drug interactions for itraconazole) and what to do if they occur. Provide the relevant patient information leaflet.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is essential to ensure patients know when to re-consult if the condition does not resolve or worsens.

Clear verbal advice should be given, and it can be helpful to supplement this with a patient information leaflet.

  • When to return: Advise the patient to seek further medical advice if the condition has not started to improve after completing a full course of topical treatment (e.g., 2-4 weeks).
  • Signs of spreading infection: Instruct them to seek urgent review if they notice the redness spreading up the foot or leg, if the foot becomes hot, swollen, and painful, or if they feel unwell with a fever. This could indicate developing cellulitis.
  • Skin breakdown: Patients, particularly those with diabetes, should be advised to return if they notice any breaks, weeping, or ulcers developing in the skin.
  • Treatment side effects: Advise them to stop treatment and consult if they develop a severe rash or irritation from a topical agent, or any concerning symptoms while on oral medication.

For most uncomplicated cases of tinea pedis managed with topical therapy, no routine follow-up is required. If oral antifungals are prescribed, follow-up may be arranged to assess efficacy and monitor for side effects, in line with local and national guidance (e.g., regarding LFT monitoring).

9. Pathophysiology

Tinea pedis is a superficial fungal infection caused by a group of fungi known as dermatophytes. The most common causative organisms are *Trichophyton rubrum*, *Trichophyton interdigitale* (formerly *T. mentagrophytes*), and *Epidermophyton floccosum*.

These fungi have a unique ability to infect keratinised tissues—the skin, hair, and nails. They produce enzymes called keratinases, which digest the protein keratin, the primary structural component of the outermost layer of the epidermis, the stratum corneum. The infection is limited to this superficial layer as the fungi cannot penetrate deeper into living tissue in an immunocompetent host.

Infection is acquired through direct contact with fungal spores, often shed from an infected person into a shared environment like a shower floor or changing room. The spores germinate and the fungi proliferate in a suitable environment, which is typically warm and moist. Occlusive footwear creates the ideal microclimate of heat and humidity (maceration) which damages the barrier function of the stratum corneum, facilitating fungal invasion. The resulting inflammatory response from the host's immune system, combined with the fungal activity, leads to the characteristic clinical features of itching, erythema, and scaling.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When selecting a treatment, it is important to consider contraindications, interactions, and specific safety guidance for each agent.

Topical Antifungals (Imidazoles, Terbinafine)

  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to the active ingredient or any excipients is the only major contraindication.
  • Side Effects: Generally very well tolerated. May occasionally cause local skin irritation, stinging, or redness. If severe, treatment should be discontinued.
  • Use: Advise patients to apply thinly and to continue use for 1-2 weeks after clinical resolution to prevent relapse.

Oral Terbinafine

  • Contraindications: Avoid in patients with chronic or active liver disease. Use with caution in patients with renal impairment and psoriasis.
  • Interactions: Terbinafine is a CYP2D6 inhibitor, so it can increase levels of drugs metabolised by this enzyme (e.g., certain beta-blockers, tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs).
  • Side Effects & Risks: Can cause gastrointestinal upset, headache, rash, and taste disturbance. Rare but serious side effects include hepatotoxicity, severe skin reactions (e.g., Stevens-Johnson syndrome), and haematological disorders. LFTs should be checked before starting and monitored if treatment continues beyond 6 weeks or if the patient develops symptoms of liver dysfunction.

Oral Itraconazole

  • Contraindications: Avoid in patients with evidence of ventricular dysfunction such as congestive heart failure (negative inotropic effect). Also contraindicated in pregnancy.
  • Interactions: Itraconazole is a potent CYP3A4 inhibitor and has numerous clinically significant drug interactions. It must not be co-prescribed with certain drugs, including some statins (e.g., simvastatin), calcium channel blockers, and oral anticoagulants. Always check the BNF for interactions before prescribing.
  • Side Effects & Risks: Common side effects include nausea, abdominal pain, and headache. The most significant risk is hepatotoxicity (rare) and congestive heart failure. LFTs should be monitored.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Tinea pedis is less common in pre-pubertal children but can occur. Management is similar to adults, with an emphasis on foot hygiene. Topical antifungals (e.g., clotrimazole, miconazole) are the treatment of choice. Oral antifungals are rarely required and should typically be initiated only after specialist consultation. Dosing is weight-based. Diagnostic confirmation with skin scrapings is more often considered in children before committing to oral therapy.

Older adults

Older adults may have comorbidities like diabetes or peripheral vascular disease that increase their risk of complications such as cellulitis. Physical limitations may make self-care and application of topical treatments difficult. Moccasin-type distribution and onychomycosis are more common. There should be a lower threshold for considering oral therapy, but careful attention must be paid to potential drug interactions with polypharmacy and renal/hepatic function.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Non-pharmacological advice on foot hygiene is the first step. For pharmacological treatment, topical imidazoles (clotrimazole, miconazole) are considered safe to use during pregnancy and breastfeeding as systemic absorption is minimal. Topical terbinafine has limited data and is not routinely recommended. Oral antifungal agents (terbinafine, itraconazole) are contraindicated during pregnancy and generally avoided during breastfeeding. Always consult the latest BNF guidance or a specialist resource.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes are at high risk of secondary bacterial infection and developing foot ulcers. They require aggressive management of tinea pedis to maintain skin integrity. A lower threshold for using oral antifungals may be appropriate, and any signs of ulceration or cellulitis warrant urgent attention. Immunocompromised patients (e.g., on chemotherapy or high-dose steroids) may present with more extensive or atypical infections and may not mount a significant inflammatory response. They are at higher risk of disseminated disease and often require systemic antifungal therapy.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young Swimmer

  • Presentation: A 19-year-old university student who swims several times a week presents with a 3-week history of an intensely itchy and peeling rash between his fourth and fifth toes on his right foot.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals white, macerated, and scaly skin in the right fourth toe web. The rest of the foot and nails appear normal. He is otherwise fit and well.
  • Diagnosis: Uncomplicated interdigital tinea pedis.
  • Management: The GP diagnoses a fungal infection, likely acquired from the communal showers at the pool. They advise on strict foot hygiene: careful drying between the toes after showering, wearing flip-flops in the changing rooms, and using cotton socks. They recommend an over-the-counter topical antifungal, such as terbinafine 1% cream once daily for one week. Clear safety netting advice is given to return if the rash spreads, becomes painful, or does not resolve after treatment.

Case Study 2: The Older Adult with Diabetes

  • Presentation: A 68-year-old man with type 2 diabetes and peripheral neuropathy presents for his annual foot check. He mentions in passing that the skin on the soles of both feet has been dry and cracking for months. He has tried various moisturisers with no effect.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals fine, silvery scaling and mild erythema covering the entire sole, heel, and sides of both feet in a 'moccasin' distribution. His toenails are thickened and yellow. There are no signs of cellulitis or ulceration.
  • Diagnosis: Chronic hyperkeratotic (moccasin) tinea pedis with associated onychomycosis.
  • Management: The Practice Nurse explains that this is a fungal infection that requires more than just moisturiser. Given the extensive moccasin pattern and nail involvement, topical therapy alone is unlikely to be successful. The nurse liaises with the GP. After confirming no contraindications and checking baseline LFTs, the GP prescribes a course of oral terbinafine. The patient is counselled on the importance of completing the course and the key side effects to watch for. He is also given hygiene advice and scheduled for a review to monitor treatment efficacy and safety.
