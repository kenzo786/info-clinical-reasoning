Insect Bites and Stings in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Insect bites and stings are a very common reason for consultation in primary care, especially during spring and summer. Most reactions are mild and localised. In the UK, common culprits include mosquitoes, midges, fleas, bedbugs, horseflies, wasps, and bees. Patients typically present with a skin reaction characterised by an itchy papule or weal, surrounded by an area of erythema. The bite or sting may be painful, particularly from wasps, bees, or horseflies.

The onset of the reaction is usually rapid, occurring within minutes of the event. The majority of simple, localised reactions are self-limiting and resolve within a few hours to several days. The key role for the clinician is to differentiate these benign reactions from more severe local reactions, secondary bacterial infections, and rare but life-threatening systemic allergic reactions such as anaphylaxis. It is also important to consider the possibility of tick bites and the associated risk of Lyme disease.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

Whilst most insect bites and stings are minor, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a need for urgent action or emergency hospital admission.

  • Signs of Anaphylaxis: This is a medical emergency requiring an immediate 999 call. Features include sudden onset of wheeze or stridor, respiratory distress, angioedema (swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat), widespread urticaria, hypotension, tachycardia, or collapse.
  • Severe or Spreading Cellulitis: Signs of secondary bacterial infection such as rapidly expanding erythema, severe warmth, bulla formation, ascending lymphangitis (red tracks on the skin), or significant systemic upset (fever, rigors, confusion). This requires urgent assessment and may necessitate intravenous antibiotics.
  • Bites in a High-Risk Location: Any sting inside the mouth or throat, or significant swelling around the eyes, can compromise the airway or vision and warrants urgent assessment.
  • Systemic Symptoms with a Tick Bite: While an erythema migrans rash itself requires prompt treatment rather than emergency referral, the presence of fever, neck stiffness, severe headache, or neurological signs following a known tick bite would warrant urgent review to exclude neuroborreliosis or other tick-borne diseases.
  • Unusually Severe and Extensive Local Reaction: A large local reaction (swelling >10 cm in diameter) that crosses a joint, is extremely painful, or shows signs of blistering may require more intensive treatment than simple self-care measures.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is necessary to identify the nature of the reaction and exclude serious pathology.

History Taking

  • Event Details: If known, what type of insect was it? Was a stinger seen (suggests a bee)? Where and when did the bite/sting occur (UK or abroad)?
  • Symptom Progression: Timing of onset and evolution of the local reaction. Ask about both skin symptoms (itch, pain, swelling) and systemic symptoms.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about shortness of breath, wheeze, swelling of the face, tongue or throat, dizziness, abdominal pain, and any history of fainting.
  • Past Medical History: Previous reactions to bites or stings? A personal or family history of atopy (asthma, eczema, hay fever) or known allergies? Previous diagnosis of anaphylaxis?
  • Tetanus Status: Ensure the patient is up-to-date, particularly if the wound is contaminated with soil.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of respiratory distress (wheeze, stridor), angioedema, or generalised rash (urticaria).
  • Vital Signs: Measure respiratory rate, oxygen saturation (SpO2), pulse, and blood pressure if there is any concern about a systemic reaction or severe infection. Measure temperature if sepsis is suspected.
  • Skin Examination: Inspect the bite site. Note the size and characteristics of the reaction (e.g., papule, weal, bulla). Look for a retained stinger or the presence of a tick. Assess for signs of secondary infection: warmth, tenderness, spreading erythema, pus, or lymphangitis.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The clinical assessment aims to distinguish between a simple local reaction and conditions requiring specific management.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Simple Localised Reaction: The most common outcome. A small, itchy, red swelling at the site of the bite or sting that resolves within a few days.
  • Large Local Reaction: An extensive area of swelling and erythema (>10 cm) that peaks at 24-48 hours and resolves over 5-10 days. It is an exaggerated allergic reaction, not an infection.
  • Papular Urticaria: Common in children. Presents as crops of very itchy papules, often on the limbs, that can persist for days or weeks. It represents a hypersensitivity reaction to bites from insects like fleas or midges.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Cellulitis: A secondary bacterial infection of the skin, usually caused by scratching. Key features are spreading, tender, warm erythema, often with systemic signs like fever. A large local reaction is often mistaken for cellulitis, but is typically more itchy than painful and lacks significant systemic upset.
  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, rapid-onset, multi-system allergic reaction. Characterised by airway, breathing or circulatory problems. A purely cutaneous reaction is not anaphylaxis.
  • Lyme Disease: Should be considered after a tick bite. The characteristic rash, erythema migrans, is a slowly expanding, annular (often 'bulls-eye') lesion that appears 3-30 days after the bite. It is not typically hot or painful.
  • Urticaria / Angioedema (other causes): Consider other triggers such as foods, drugs, or viral illness if there is no clear history of a bite or sting.
  • Herpes Zoster (Shingles): Can present with pain and a unilateral, dermatomal rash, but this is vesicular and typically preceded by a prodrome of pain or tingling.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is focused on symptomatic relief for simple reactions, while identifying and treating complications appropriately.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Stinger Removal: If a bee stinger is visible in the skin, advise scraping it out sideways with a fingernail or a credit card edge. Avoid using tweezers, which can squeeze more venom into the skin.
  • Clean and Cool: Wash the affected area with soap and water. Apply a cold compress or ice pack (wrapped in a cloth) for at least 10 minutes to reduce pain and swelling.
  • Elevation: If the bite or sting is on a limb, keeping it elevated can help reduce swelling.
  • Avoid Scratching: Advise patients to avoid scratching as this can damage the skin and introduce infection. Keeping fingernails short can be helpful, particularly in children.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Oral Antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine): Acts by blocking histamine H1 receptors to reduce itching, weal, and flare. A sedating antihistamine (e.g., Chlorphenamine) can be useful at night. [Rx info]
  • Mild Topical Corticosteroid (e.g., Hydrocortisone 1% cream): A mild anti-inflammatory that can be applied to the skin to reduce local inflammation and itch. Should be avoided on broken or infected skin. [Rx info]
  • Crotamiton cream/lotion: An antipruritic agent that can provide symptomatic relief from itching. [Rx info]
  • Simple Analgesia (e.g., Paracetamol, Ibuprofen): Can be used to manage pain associated with the bite or sting. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Potent Topical Steroids (e.g., Betamethasone): May be considered for more severe, non-infected local reactions to provide stronger anti-inflammatory effects. [Rx info]
  • Oral Steroids (e.g., Prednisolone): A short course is highly effective for treating severe large local reactions. [Rx info]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin, Clarithromycin): Reserved for cases with clear clinical evidence of a secondary bacterial infection (cellulitis). They have no role in treating simple or large local reactions. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of insect bites and stings in the UK is primarily guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS). The guidance emphasises a focus on symptomatic relief and robust antimicrobial stewardship.

NICE guidance is clear that antibiotics should not be offered for uncomplicated insect bites, including large local reactions, as they are inflammatory or allergic in nature, not infectious. Antibiotics are only recommended when there are clear clinical signs of a secondary bacterial infection, such as cellulitis. This approach minimises the risk of antibiotic side effects and the development of antimicrobial resistance.

For patients with a large local reaction, guidelines support the use of oral antihistamines and, for more severe cases, a short course of oral corticosteroids. For patients who have experienced a systemic allergic reaction, NICE recommends referral to a specialist allergy service and consideration of an adrenaline auto-injector. For tick bites, separate NICE guidance (NG95) on Lyme disease should be followed, outlining the management of erythema migrans and other presentations.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to reassuring the patient, managing their expectations, and ensuring they know when to seek further help.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Reassurance: Explain that redness, swelling and itching are a normal reaction to the foreign proteins in the insect's saliva or venom.
  • Explain the Natural History: Advise that a simple bite will usually settle in a few days, but a large local reaction can look quite dramatic, continue to swell for 24-48 hours, and take up to a week to resolve. Clearly differentiate this allergic reaction from an infection.
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions they can take, such as applying a cold compress, elevating the limb, and using OTC antihistamines for itch.
  • Prevention: For future prevention, discuss the use of insect repellents containing DEET, wearing long sleeves and trousers in high-risk areas, and avoiding use of strong perfumes.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Oral Steroids: If prescribing prednisolone for a large local reaction, explain that it is a strong anti-inflammatory to reduce the severe swelling and that it is only needed for a short period.
  • Anaphylaxis Management: For patients prescribed an adrenaline auto-injector, comprehensive training on recognising symptoms and correct device usage is mandatory. An allergy action plan should be provided.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients know when to re-consult or seek emergency care.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • Symptoms of a severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis): Advise an immediate 999 call for any difficulty breathing, wheezing, swelling of the face, mouth or throat, feeling faint or dizzy, or a widespread rash that appears suddenly.
  • Signs of spreading infection: If the redness around the bite starts to spread significantly, the area becomes very hot and tender, red lines appear tracking up the limb, or if the person develops a fever and feels generally unwell.
  • Failure to improve: If the local symptoms are not starting to improve after a few days or are getting progressively worse.
  • New 'bulls-eye' rash: If a circular, expanding rash appears around a bite site anytime in the following month, as this may be a sign of Lyme disease.

Routine follow-up for simple bites is not required. If antibiotics have been started for cellulitis, a review might be appropriate after 2-3 days to assess treatment response, depending on the clinical scenario.

9. Pathophysiology

The skin's reaction to an insect bite or sting is an inflammatory response to proteins in the insect's saliva or venom. This response can be non-allergic or allergic.

In a simple local reaction, pharmacologically active substances in the venom or saliva directly trigger mast cells in the skin to degranulate. This releases inflammatory mediators, primarily histamine, which causes local vasodilation (erythema), increased vascular permeability (the weal or oedema), and stimulation of sensory nerve endings (itch and pain).

Allergic reactions are mediated by the immune system. A large local reaction is an IgE-mediated type I late-phase hypersensitivity reaction. In a previously sensitised individual, insect antigens cross-link IgE antibodies on mast cells, causing immediate histamine release (the initial weal and flare) followed by a later influx of eosinophils and other inflammatory cells. This cellular infiltrate is responsible for the more extensive, indurated, and prolonged swelling that peaks at 24-48 hours. Anaphylaxis is a systemic manifestation of this same type I hypersensitivity, with widespread mast cell and basophil degranulation causing systemic vasodilation, bronchoconstriction, and increased mucus secretion.

In contrast, papular urticaria is thought to be a delayed-type (type IV) hypersensitivity reaction, where T-cells react to persistent insect antigens in the skin, leading to the formation of discrete, firm, and intensely pruritic papules.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is used, it is important to be aware of potential issues.

Oral Antihistamines

  • Sedation: First-generation antihistamines (e.g., chlorphenamine, hydroxyzine) can cause significant drowsiness. Patients must be warned not to drive or operate heavy machinery if affected. Modern, non-sedating antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine, loratadine) are generally preferred for daytime use.

Corticosteroids

  • Topical Steroids: Should be applied thinly and for a limited time. They should not be used on ulcerated or infected skin, as they can mask or worsen infection. They should also be avoided on the face or eyelids unless specifically advised.
  • Oral Steroids: A short course of prednisolone (e.g., 3-5 days) for a large local reaction has few absolute contraindications but should be used with caution in patients with diabetes, severe heart failure, or active peptic ulceration. Advise taking with food to reduce gastric irritation.

Antibiotics

  • Choice of Agent: If treating secondary cellulitis, the antibiotic choice should cover the likely causative organisms, *Staphylococcus* and *Streptococcus*. Flucloxacillin is the first-line choice in the UK for non-penicillin-allergic patients.
  • Allergy: Always confirm allergy status before prescribing. Clarithromycin or doxycycline are common alternatives for those with a penicillin allergy.

Insect Repellents (for prevention)

  • DEET: Products containing 50% DEET are most effective but require careful use in children and pregnancy. Lower concentrations are available. It can damage plastics and synthetic fabrics.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Children are prone to vigorous scratching, increasing the risk of secondary impetiginisation and cellulitis. Papular urticaria is also much more common. Management involves paediatric-appropriate doses of oral antihistamines and mild topical steroids. Prevention with repellents requires care; DEET can be used from 2 months of age, but lower concentrations should be used and applied by an adult, avoiding the child's hands and face.

Older adults

The skin can be more fragile in older adults, so care should be taken with scratching and application of topical treatments. There should be a lower threshold for suspecting cellulitis, as immune function may be lower. Caution is required when prescribing sedating antihistamines due to the increased risk of confusion and falls. Polypharmacy is common, so potential drug interactions must be considered.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care measures are first-line. If medication is needed, hydrocortisone cream and most oral antihistamines (loratadine and cetirizine are preferred) are considered safe. A short course of prednisolone can be used if benefits outweigh risks. DEET-based repellents can be used, but some may prefer non-DEET alternatives. Always check the latest BNF guidance or specialist resources.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy or uncontrolled diabetes) are at higher risk of developing severe cellulitis from a simple bite and require prompt assessment. Patients with a known history of atopy or previous severe reactions may have more dramatic responses. For patients with known mast cell disorders or a prior history of anaphylaxis to stings, any new sting should be treated as a potential emergency.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Large Local Reaction

  • Presentation: A 52-year-old man presents 36 hours after being stung by a wasp on his left calf. The entire lower leg from knee to ankle is now red, swollen, and feels warm. It is very itchy but only mildly tender. He feels perfectly well in himself and is afebrile.
  • Assessment: Vital signs are normal. The affected area is approximately 20cm in diameter, erythematous and oedematous, but not markedly tender. There is no lymphangitis or blistering.
  • Diagnosis: Large local reaction. It is distinguished from cellulitis by the prominent itch, lack of significant tenderness, and absence of systemic symptoms.
  • Management: The GP reassures him this is an allergic, not infectious, reaction. He is advised to keep the leg elevated when resting. He is prescribed a 5-day course of prednisolone and a non-sedating oral antihistamine. Clear safety netting advice is given to return if he develops a fever, feels unwell, or if the pain significantly worsens.

Case Study 2: The Infected Bite

  • Presentation: A 6-year-old girl is brought in by her mother. She has had multiple midge bites on her ankles for several days. One area has become increasingly painful, and she has been feeling hot and miserable for the last 24 hours.
  • Assessment: The child appears flushed and has a temperature of 38.4°C. On her right lateral malleolus is a 5cm area of intense, tender, hot erythema with a central pustule and some golden crusting.
  • Diagnosis: Secondary bacterial infection (impetiginisation and cellulitis) of an insect bite.
  • Management: The GP prescribes a course of oral flucloxacillin suspension. The parent is advised on wound care (gentle cleaning), simple analgesia for pain and fever, and to ensure the child drinks plenty of fluids. Safety netting advice is given to seek urgent review if she becomes more unwell, stops drinking, or if the redness spreads rapidly.
