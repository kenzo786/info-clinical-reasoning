Impetigo in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Impetigo is a common and highly contagious superficial bacterial skin infection, most frequently seen in children but affecting all ages. It is primarily caused by Staphylococcus aureus and sometimes Streptococcus pyogenes. The infection typically occurs on areas of pre-existing skin damage, such as a cut, insect bite, or in skin affected by eczema or scabies (secondary impetigo).

There are two main clinical forms:

  • Non-bullous impetigo: This is the most common form (around 70% of cases). It starts as red macules or papules that quickly evolve into vesicles or pustules. These rupture to release a serous fluid that dries to form characteristic thick, adherent, "golden" or "honey-coloured" crusts. Lesions are often found around the nose and mouth.
  • Bullous impetigo: This form is caused exclusively by certain strains of S. aureus that produce exfoliative toxins. It presents with flaccid, fluid-filled blisters (bullae), often 1-2 cm in diameter. The bullae rupture easily, leaving a moist, red base with a thin, varnish-like crust. The trunk and limbs are common sites.

The condition is usually not systemically serious and may be itchy but is not typically painful. Without treatment, it often resolves within two to three weeks, but treatment is advised to speed up recovery and reduce contagion.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While impetigo is generally a benign, localised infection, it is crucial to identify features that suggest a more serious condition or complication requiring urgent review or hospital admission.

  • Signs of systemic illness: Fever, malaise, rigors, or hypotension may indicate the infection has become invasive, leading to bacteraemia or sepsis.
  • Spreading cellulitis: If the erythema, warmth, swelling, and tenderness extend rapidly into the deeper dermis around the impetigo lesions, this indicates cellulitis, which requires systemic antibiotic therapy.
  • Widespread or rapidly progressing bullous impetigo: Particularly in an infant, this can be a precursor to Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS), a dermatological emergency where toxins cause widespread epidermal detachment.
  • Deep, painful, or "punched-out" ulcers: This may suggest ecthyma, a deeper ulcerative form of impetigo that penetrates the dermis and can lead to scarring.
  • Facial infection with periorbital swelling: Any infection around the eyes with associated swelling, pain on eye movement, or visual disturbance requires urgent assessment to rule out periorbital or orbital cellulitis.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Individuals with significant immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy or uncontrolled HIV) are at higher risk of severe, disseminated infection and require a lower threshold for systemic treatment and specialist advice.
  • Suspected post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis: Although rare in the UK, if a patient develops haematuria (cola-coloured urine), oedema, and hypertension 1-2 weeks after a skin infection, this is a red flag for this renal complication.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A focused history and examination are usually sufficient to diagnose impetigo and determine the appropriate management plan.

History Taking

  • Lesion characteristics: Ask about the onset, location, and evolution of the spots (e.g., from small spots to blisters to crusts).
  • Associated symptoms: Enquire about itch or pain. Ask about systemic symptoms like fever or feeling generally unwell.
  • Context and Contacts: Ask if other family members or school contacts have a similar rash. Note any preceding skin trauma, insect bites, or underlying skin conditions like atopic eczema or scabies.
  • Previous Episodes: Recurrent impetigo may warrant further investigation, including nasal swabs to check for staphylococcal carriage.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess if the patient, particularly a child, appears systemically well or unwell.
  • Skin Examination:
    • Non-bullous: Look for the pathognomonic golden, honey-coloured crusts on an erythematous base, typically on the face or extremities.
    • Bullous: Look for intact or recently ruptured flaccid bullae with a thin, shiny crust, often on the trunk or in intertriginous areas.
    • Extent: Assess whether the infection is localised (a few lesions in one area) or widespread (multiple lesions over several body areas), as this guides management.
    • Complications: Check for surrounding cellulitis (expanding erythema and warmth) or regional lymphadenopathy.

Further Investigations

Skin swabs for microbiological culture and sensitivities are not routinely required for typical cases. Consider taking a swab from a moist lesion or de-roofed blister if:

  • The infection is failing to respond to first-line treatment.
  • The patient has recurrent episodes.
  • Meticillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA) is suspected (e.g., due to healthcare-associated risk factors).
  • The diagnosis is uncertain.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The characteristic appearance of impetigo often makes it a straightforward clinical diagnosis. However, several other conditions can present similarly.

Common Differentials

  • Herpes Simplex (Cold Sores): Typically presents as a tight cluster of uniform vesicles on an erythematous base, commonly around the lips. It can crust over but often follows a history of tingling or burning prodrome. A Tzanck smear or viral PCR swab can confirm the diagnosis if needed.
  • Infected Eczema: Atopic eczema can become secondarily infected with bacteria, leading to weeping, crusting, and pustules superimposed on typical eczematous skin. The history of chronic, itchy, flexural dermatitis is a key differentiator.
  • Tinea (Ringworm): Fungal infections can present with crusted, erythematous plaques. However, tinea lesions typically have a raised, active, and sometimes scaly border with central clearing, which is not characteristic of impetigo.
  • Insect Bites: Can become secondarily infected, mimicking impetigo. However, the initial presentation is typically a pruritic papule at a bite site.
  • Shingles (Herpes Zoster): Presents as a vesicular rash in a unilateral dermatomal distribution. It is usually preceded by pain or altered sensation in the affected dermatome.
  • Contact Dermatitis: An allergic or irritant reaction can cause vesicles, weeping, and crusting, but the distribution will correspond to the area of contact with the offending substance and is often intensely itchy.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is guided by the extent and severity of the infection and aims to resolve symptoms and reduce transmission. Antimicrobial stewardship is a key consideration.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Hygiene Measures: Emphasise hand washing with soap and water, especially after touching lesions. Use separate towels, flannels, and bedding, and wash them on a hot cycle.
  • Avoid Scratching: Keep fingernails short and encourage the patient to avoid touching or scratching the lesions to prevent spread and scarring.
  • Cleaning Lesions: Gently soak and remove crusts with warm water and a clean cloth to allow topical treatments to penetrate.
  • School/Nursery/Work Exclusion: Advise that the individual should stay away from school, nursery, or work until the lesions are crusted over and dry, or for 48 hours after commencing antibiotic treatment.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

Antiseptic preparations are preferred over topical antibiotics for localised non-bullous impetigo to reduce resistance pressure.

  • Hydrogen Peroxide 1% cream: Acts as a topical antiseptic. This is the first-line treatment recommended by NICE for localised non-bullous impetigo. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

Topical antibiotics are reserved for when hydrogen peroxide is unsuitable or ineffective. Oral antibiotics are used for widespread or bullous infection, or if the patient is systemically unwell.

  • Topical Antibiotics (e.g., Fusidic acid, Mupirocin): Inhibit bacterial protein synthesis. NICE guidance restricts the use of topical fusidic acid due to high rates of staphylococcal resistance. It should only be used if hydrogen peroxide is unsuitable, for a short duration. Mupirocin is often reserved for suspected or confirmed MRSA. [Rx info]
  • Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin, Clarithromycin): A 5-day course is usually sufficient. Flucloxacillin (a penicillin) is the first-line choice for widespread non-bullous impetigo or bullous impetigo. Clarithromycin (a macrolide) is an alternative for those with a penicillin allergy. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of impetigo in the UK is primarily guided by the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline NG153, "Impetigo: antimicrobial prescribing", and the associated CKS topic summary. The core principle is to minimise the use of antibiotics to curb antimicrobial resistance while ensuring effective treatment.

NICE recommends a stratified approach based on the extent of the infection:

  • For localised non-bullous impetigo: NICE advocates for a "topical antiseptic first" approach. Hydrogen peroxide 1% cream is recommended as the first-line treatment. A topical antibiotic (e.g., fusidic acid) should only be offered if hydrogen peroxide is unsuitable.
  • For widespread non-bullous impetigo, or any bullous impetigo: NICE recommends an oral antibiotic. Flucloxacillin is the first-line agent, with clarithromycin as the alternative in cases of penicillin allergy.

The guidance strongly advises against the routine use of fusidic acid to treat impetigo. This is a direct response to Public Health England data showing high and increasing rates of resistance in S. aureus to this agent. Short-course prescriptions (typically 5 days for both topical and oral antibiotics) are recommended to reduce selection pressure for resistant bacteria.

The guidance also reiterates the importance of providing advice on hygiene measures to all patients to limit the spread of the infection and prevent recurrence.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective patient and parent communication is key to successful management, particularly for reinforcing hygiene measures and managing expectations.

Counselling for Self-Care and Hygiene

  • Explain the Condition: Describe impetigo as a common, contagious skin infection that usually isn't serious but needs careful handling to stop it from spreading.
  • Emphasise Contagion: Clearly explain how it spreads via direct contact and shared items. Advise on exclusion from school, nursery, or work (48 hours after starting antibiotics or until lesions are dry and crusted).
  • Hygiene is Key: Give specific instructions: "Wash your hands with soap and water frequently, especially after touching the sores. Don't share towels, flannels, or bedding with the infected person."
  • How to Apply Creams: Advise to wash hands before and after application. Explain that crusts can be gently soaked off with warm water to help the cream reach the infected skin.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Reason for Choice: Explain why a particular treatment was chosen (e.g., "We are using an antiseptic cream first, as recommended, because it works well for a few spots and helps prevent antibiotic resistance."). If prescribing an oral antibiotic, explain this is because the infection is more widespread.
  • Completing the Course: Stress the importance of completing the full course of antibiotics, even if the skin looks better, to ensure the infection is fully cleared.
  • Side Effects: Briefly mention common side effects (e.g., "Flucloxacillin can sometimes cause an upset stomach").
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a critical component of the consultation, ensuring the patient or their carer knows when and how to seek further help.

Clear verbal and written advice should be provided. Advise the patient or their parent/carer to seek a further review if:

  • The impetigo is spreading or worsening rapidly despite starting treatment.
  • The number of lesions significantly increases after 48-72 hours of treatment.
  • The skin around the lesions becomes increasingly red, hot, swollen, and painful (suggesting developing cellulitis).
  • The person becomes systemically unwell, developing a fever, chills, or feeling generally ill.
  • The impetigo has not resolved or is not showing clear signs of improvement by the end of the treatment course.
  • New symptoms develop, such as blistering in an infant (suggesting SSSS) or dark urine and swelling (suggesting post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis).

A routine follow-up is not typically necessary for uncomplicated impetigo that is expected to respond to treatment. However, if there are concerns, such as in a frail patient or if the diagnosis is uncertain, a telephone or face-to-face review in 2-3 days might be appropriate.

9. Pathophysiology

Impetigo is a superficial infection of the epidermis, the outermost layer of the skin. The infection is established when bacteria, primarily Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes, breach the skin's protective barrier. This breach can be a minor cut, an insect bite, or a pre-existing skin condition like atopic dermatitis.

In non-bullous impetigo, the bacteria colonise the stratum corneum. The host's immune system responds, leading to inflammation and the formation of vesicles or pustules filled with neutrophils, bacteria, and serous fluid. When these fragile pustules rupture, the exudate dries on the skin surface, mixing with skin debris and bacteria to form the characteristic thick, honey-coloured crusts.

Bullous impetigo is caused by specific strains of S. aureus that produce exfoliative toxins (ETA and ETB). These toxins are proteases that target and cleave a specific protein called desmoglein 1. Desmoglein 1 is a key component of desmosomes, which are structures that hold epidermal cells (keratinocytes) together. By breaking down desmoglein 1, the toxins cause a loss of adhesion between keratinocytes in the superficial epidermis (specifically, the stratum granulosum layer). This results in acantholysis (separation of epidermal cells) and the formation of large, flaccid bullae filled with sterile, serous fluid. Because the cleavage is very superficial, the blisters are thin-walled and rupture easily, and healing occurs without scarring.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, clinicians must consider the specific properties and risks of each agent.

Hydrogen Peroxide 1% Cream

  • Mechanism: Releases oxygen upon contact with catalase in the skin, which has a weak antiseptic effect against bacteria.
  • Side Effects: Can cause mild skin irritation or a stinging sensation on application. Significant adverse effects are rare with this low concentration.

Topical Antibiotics (Fusidic Acid)

  • Mechanism: Bacteriostatic agent that inhibits bacterial protein synthesis.
  • Resistance: There are high rates of staphylococcal resistance to fusidic acid in the UK. Its use should be limited to 5-day courses and only when antiseptics are not appropriate, to preserve its efficacy for other conditions.
  • Side Effects: Generally well-tolerated but can cause local skin reactions.

Oral Penicillins (Flucloxacillin)

  • Contraindications: Absolute contraindication in patients with a history of penicillin allergy. Use with caution in patients with a history of flucloxacillin-induced cholestatic jaundice.
  • Administration: Best taken on an empty stomach (e.g., 30-60 minutes before food) to maximise absorption.
  • Side Effects: Common side effects include gastrointestinal upset (nausea, diarrhoea). Cholestatic hepatitis is a rare but serious idiosyncratic reaction that can occur several weeks after treatment has stopped.

Oral Macrolides (Clarithromycin)

  • Interactions: Clarithromycin is a potent inhibitor of the CYP3A4 enzyme and has significant interactions with many drugs, most notably certain statins (e.g., simvastatin - risk of rhabdomyolysis), calcium channel blockers, and anticoagulants. A thorough drug history is essential.
  • Side Effects: Common side effects include GI upset, taste disturbance, and a risk of QT interval prolongation.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Impetigo is most common in this group. The principles of management are the same, but communication must be tailored to the parents/carers. Emphasise hygiene and school exclusion rules (48 hours post-treatment start or until lesions are crusted). Oral antibiotic dosing for widespread infection is weight-based. Bullous impetigo in an infant requires extra vigilance for signs of SSSS.

Older adults

Older adults may have more fragile skin or comorbidities like diabetes, which can increase susceptibility and severity. There should be a lower threshold for using oral antibiotics. It is crucial to check for potential drug interactions with existing polypharmacy, especially when considering a macrolide like clarithromycin.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Topical treatments like hydrogen peroxide or fusidic acid are considered safe as systemic absorption is minimal. For oral therapy, flucloxacillin is safe to use during pregnancy and breastfeeding. The preferred macrolide in pregnancy, if required, is erythromycin, although clarithromycin can be used if appropriate. Always consult the latest BNF guidance for up-to-date advice.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with pre-existing skin conditions (e.g., atopic eczema, psoriasis, scabies) are prone to secondary impetigo (impetiginisation). In these cases, it is vital to treat both the infection and the underlying skin disorder. Immunocompromised patients (e.g., on chemotherapy, biologics, or high-dose steroids) may develop more severe or atypical infections and should be considered for systemic antibiotic treatment earlier. There should be a lower threshold for taking skin swabs in this group.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Schoolchild with Facial Sores

  • Presentation: A 6-year-old boy presents with his mother. He has a 3-day history of a few crusty, yellow spots around his left nostril. He is otherwise well and afebrile. His mother is concerned it is spreading slowly.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals three small, crusted lesions with a honey-coloured appearance and slight underlying erythema. There are no bullae or signs of surrounding cellulitis. He appears well. The diagnosis is localised, non-bullous impetigo.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis and its contagious nature. They advise on hygiene measures (hand washing, not sharing towels) and school exclusion for 48 hours after starting treatment. Following NICE guidance, they prescribe hydrogen peroxide 1% cream to be applied three times daily for 5 days.
  • Safety Netting: The mother is advised to return if the spots spread significantly, if he develops a fever, or if the skin is not improving after the 5-day course.

Case Study 2: The Teenager with Widespread Lesions

  • Presentation: A 15-year-old girl presents with multiple spots on both shins that started a week ago after she grazed her legs playing hockey. The lesions are now weepy and crusty, and some new blisters have appeared on her trunk.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals over ten lesions across her shins and abdomen. Some are classic non-bullous crusted plaques, while others are small, flaccid bullae. She is systemically well. The diagnosis is widespread, mixed non-bullous and bullous impetigo.
  • Management: Due to the widespread nature and bullous features, the GP prescribes a 5-day course of oral flucloxacillin, after checking for penicillin allergy. They provide counselling on hygiene, avoiding contact sports until healed, and the importance of completing the antibiotic course.
  • Safety Netting: The patient is advised to seek urgent review if she feels unwell, develops a high temperature, or if the skin becomes hot, red, and painful.

Case Study 3: Recurrent Infection in an Adult

  • Presentation: A 34-year-old man with atopic eczema presents with his third episode of impetigo in the last year. He has crusted lesions in his antecubital fossae, superimposed on his eczema. His previous episode was treated with fusidic acid cream.
  • Assessment: The clinical picture is of infected eczema. Given the recurrence, the GP suspects staphylococcal carriage or antibiotic resistance.
  • Management: The GP takes a bacterial swab from a crusted lesion and a nasal swab to check for carriage and sensitivities. While awaiting results, they prescribe a 5-day course of oral flucloxacillin. They also reinforce the importance of aggressive emollient therapy and provide a suitable topical steroid to manage the underlying eczema once the infection is under control.
  • Safety Netting and Follow-up: The patient is advised to book a follow-up appointment to discuss the swab results, which will guide future management, potentially including nasal decolonisation therapy if he is a carrier.
