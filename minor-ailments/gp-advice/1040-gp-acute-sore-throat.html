Acute Sore Throat in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute sore throat is an extremely common reason for consultation in primary care, characterised by pharyngeal pain that is often worse on swallowing. It is most frequently a symptom of acute pharyngitis or tonsillitis. In over 90% of cases in adults, the cause is viral and self-limiting.

Patients typically present with a rapid onset of throat pain, which may be associated with pyrexia, headache, and malaise. On examination, there is typically pharyngeal and/or tonsillar erythema. Tonsillar swelling or exudates may be present. The condition is usually benign, with most symptoms resolving within one week, regardless of the underlying cause (viral or bacterial).

Common triggers are viral infections, such as rhinovirus, adenovirus, and coronavirus. The most common bacterial cause is Group A beta-haemolytic streptococcus (GABHS), also known as Streptococcus pyogenes. The clinician's role is to identify features suggestive of a more serious diagnosis, assess the likelihood of a bacterial cause to guide antibiotic stewardship, and provide advice on self-care and safety netting.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most sore throats are benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a condition requiring urgent assessment or emergency hospital admission.

  • Airway compromise: Any signs of stridor, respiratory distress, drooling, or a muffled 'hot potato' voice suggest potential airway obstruction from causes like epiglottitis or a severe peritonsillar abscess (quinsy). This requires immediate emergency admission.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy): Characterised by severe unilateral throat pain, trismus (difficulty opening the mouth), deviation of the uvula away from the affected side, and a visible swelling or fullness of the soft palate. This requires urgent ENT assessment.
  • Systemic sepsis: Features such as marked fever (>38°C), rigors, tachycardia, hypotension, or new-onset confusion indicate a systemic response to infection that warrants urgent hospital evaluation.
  • Inability to swallow fluids: If a patient, particularly a child or frail adult, is unable to swallow their own saliva or maintain hydration, they may require admission for intravenous fluids and observation.
  • Suspected Scarlet Fever: A sore throat accompanied by a characteristic fine, sandpapery rash on the torso, a flushed face, and a 'strawberry tongue'. This is a notifiable disease and requires antibiotic treatment to reduce the risk of complications.
  • Immunocompromised patient: A sore throat in a patient with significant immunosuppression (e.g., on chemotherapy or certain DMARDs like carbimazole) may signal neutropenic sepsis or agranulocytosis and requires an urgent assessment including a full blood count.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is essential to assess severity, exclude red flags, and guide management decisions regarding antibiotics.

History Taking

  • Symptom Analysis: Clarify the onset, duration, and severity of pain. Ask about associated symptoms like fever, cough, coryza, headache, and abdominal pain (common in children with streptococcal infection).
  • Functional Impact: Ask about ability to swallow food and fluids. Note any change in voice or difficulty opening the mouth (trismus).
  • Past Medical History: Enquire about previous episodes of tonsillitis, rheumatic fever, or immune system problems.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about breathing difficulties, drooling, neck stiffness, severe unilateral pain, and skin rashes.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of distress, stridor, and hydration status.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature, pulse, and respiratory rate.
  • Oropharyngeal Examination: Inspect the pharynx and tonsils for erythema, swelling, exudates, or petechiae on the palate. Note any uvular deviation or unilateral swelling.
  • Neck Examination: Palpate for tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy.

Scoring Tools

To assess the likelihood of a bacterial (streptococcal) cause and guide antibiotic prescribing, the FeverPAIN score is recommended by NICE:

  • Fever (during previous 24 hours)
  • Purulence (pus on tonsils)
  • Attend rapidly (illness duration of 3 days or less)
  • Inflamed tonsils (severely)
  • No cough or coryza

Each criterion scores 1 point. A score of 0-1 is associated with a low likelihood of streptococcus; 2-3 an intermediate likelihood; and 4-5 a high likelihood (~65%).

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for acute sore throat is broad, but the majority of cases are simple infections. The clinical assessment aims to distinguish these from less common but more serious causes.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis: The most frequent cause. Often accompanied by other URTI symptoms like cough and coryza. FeverPAIN score is typically low (0-2).
  • Bacterial Tonsillitis (GABHS): More likely with a high FeverPAIN score (4-5). Clinically, it is often difficult to distinguish from viral causes on inspection alone.

Serious or Specific Differentials to Exclude

  • Infectious Mononucleosis (Glandular Fever): Caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). Suspect in adolescents or young adults with a severe sore throat, prominent cervical lymphadenopathy, profound fatigue, and palatal petechiae. A maculopapular rash can occur, especially if amoxicillin is mistakenly given.
  • Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy): A collection of pus between the tonsil capsule and pharyngeal muscles. The key signs are unilateral swelling, uvular deviation, and trismus.
  • Scarlet Fever: A GABHS infection that produces an erythrogenic toxin, causing the characteristic rash and strawberry tongue in addition to a sore throat.
  • Epiglottitis: A cellulitis of the epiglottis, now rare in children due to the Hib vaccine but still occurs in adults. Presents with severe throat pain, dysphagia, drooling, and stridor.
  • Agranulocytosis: A severe drug-induced neutropenia (e.g., from carbimazole, clozapine) can present with a severe, necrotic sore throat and fever.
  • Kawasaki Disease: A medium-vessel vasculitis in children, presenting with a high fever for ≥5 days plus a sore throat, rash, conjunctivitis, and other signs.
5. Management options in primary care

Management focuses on symptomatic relief for most patients, with antibiotics reserved for specific situations based on clinical assessment and scoring tools, in line with antimicrobial stewardship principles.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain that the illness is usually viral and self-limiting, typically resolving within a week.
  • Rest and Hydration: Advise adequate fluid intake to avoid dehydration.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Suggest gargling with warm, salty water or sucking on ice cubes or ice lollies. Avoiding smoking and smoky environments is also helpful.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol or ibuprofen are effective for pain and fever. They should be advised as the first-line treatment for symptomatic relief.
  • Medicated Lozenges and Sprays: Products containing a local anaesthetic (e.g., benzocaine, lidocaine) or an anti-inflammatory (e.g., flurbiprofen) can provide temporary relief. Evidence for their overall effectiveness is limited.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Phenoxymethylpenicillin, Clarithromycin): Reserved for patients who are systemically unwell, at high risk of complications, or have a high FeverPAIN score (e.g., 4 or 5). They can reduce symptom duration by about 24 hours and decrease the small risk of suppurative complications. Phenoxymethylpenicillin is first-line for GABHS. [Rx info]
  • Corticosteroids: A single dose of oral corticosteroid (e.g., dexamethasone) is sometimes used to reduce pain in severe cases, but this is not routine practice in primary care and is an off-label use.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of acute sore throat in the UK is primarily guided by NICE guideline NG84 (Sore throat (acute): antimicrobial prescribing). The core principle is to support self-care and limit antibiotic use to reduce antimicrobial resistance.

NICE guidance is clear that for most people, a sore throat is a self-limiting viral infection for which antibiotics are ineffective. Antibiotics should not be offered to patients with a FeverPAIN score of 0 or 1. A back-up (delayed) prescription may be considered for those with a score of 2 or 3, with clear advice on when to use it.

For patients with a FeverPAIN score of 4 or 5, an immediate antibiotic prescription may be offered, following a discussion about the potential benefits (modest symptom reduction, small reduction in complication risk) versus the risks (side effects, antimicrobial resistance). The guidance also applies to children and young people over 3 years old.

Guidelines emphasise the importance of identifying patients with signs of more serious illness who require urgent referral and those with scarlet fever who require immediate antibiotics. For pain relief, paracetamol or ibuprofen are recommended.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to managing patient expectations, improving satisfaction, and ensuring appropriate use of antibiotics.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that most sore throats are caused by viruses and will get better on their own within about a week.
  • Justify the Management Plan: Explain the rationale for not prescribing antibiotics, referencing the likely viral cause and the risks of antibiotic use. Using the FeverPAIN score can be a helpful, objective tool to share with the patient. For example, "Your score is low, which means there is a very small chance this is a bacterial infection that would respond to antibiotics."
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions they can take, such as using regular analgesia, drinking plenty of fluids, and other self-care measures.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Delayed Prescriptions: Provide explicit verbal and written instructions on when to use the prescription (e.g., "Only use this if your symptoms are not starting to improve after 3-5 days, or if you become significantly more unwell") and the importance of not using it if they improve as expected.
  • Immediate Prescriptions: If prescribing antibiotics, explain the expected benefits are modest and counsel on potential side effects like diarrhoea or rash. Emphasise the importance of completing the course as prescribed.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients know when to seek further medical help. Advice should be clear, specific, and ideally backed up by a patient information leaflet.

Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The sore throat worsens significantly or does not start to improve within 3-5 days.
  • They develop difficulty breathing (e.g., stridor or shortness of breath).
  • They have difficulty swallowing their own saliva or are unable to drink.
  • They develop severe pain on one side of the throat or have difficulty opening their mouth fully (trismus).
  • They become systemically very unwell with high fever, rigors, or confusion.
  • They develop a skin rash, particularly the sandpaper-like rash of scarlet fever.
  • They have any other symptom that they are seriously concerned about.

For most patients managed with self-care advice, no specific follow-up is required. For higher-risk patients or where there is diagnostic uncertainty, a planned review might be appropriate.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute pharyngitis is the inflammation of the pharynx, the part of the throat behind the mouth and nasal cavity. It is a direct result of an inflammatory response to an invading pathogen, most commonly a virus (e.g., rhinovirus, adenovirus) or less commonly a bacterium (e.g., Streptococcus pyogenes).

The pathogen adheres to and invades the epithelial cells of the pharyngeal mucosa. This triggers the innate immune system, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and mediators like prostaglandins and bradykinin. This cascade results in vasodilation and increased permeability of local blood vessels, leading to the classic signs of inflammation: erythema (redness), oedema (swelling), and heat.

The oedema and the direct stimulation of afferent nerve fibres (nociceptors) by inflammatory mediators cause the sensation of pain (odynophagia). In bacterial tonsillitis, a significant influx of neutrophils can lead to the formation of pus, which appears as a visible tonsillar exudate. Systemic symptoms like fever and malaise are caused by the systemic circulation of cytokines like Interleukin-1 (IL-1), IL-6, and TNF-alpha.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is prescribed, it is essential to be aware of contraindications, significant interactions, and specific safety guidance.

Analgesics (Paracetamol, Ibuprofen)

  • Paracetamol: Very safe when used at the correct dose. Risk of severe hepatotoxicity in overdose.
  • Ibuprofen (NSAIDs): Use with caution in patients with asthma, a history of peptic ulcer disease, renal impairment, or heart failure. Contraindicated after 30 weeks of pregnancy. Can interact with anticoagulants, ACE inhibitors, and diuretics.

Antibiotics (Phenoxymethylpenicillin, Clarithromycin)

  • Phenoxymethylpenicillin: The drug of choice for GABHS. Absolutely contraindicated in patients with a penicillin allergy. Common side effects include nausea and diarrhoea.
  • Clarithromycin (Macrolides): An alternative for those with a true penicillin allergy. It has numerous significant drug interactions (e.g., with statins, warfarin, some calcium channel blockers) due to its inhibition of the CYP3A4 enzyme. It can also cause QT interval prolongation and should be used with caution in patients with risk factors.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Bacterial tonsillitis and its complication, scarlet fever, are more common in children aged 3-14. Assessment should focus on identifying red flags, particularly dehydration and respiratory distress. Viral-induced rashes are common and need to be distinguished from the rash of scarlet fever. Glandular fever should also be considered. Simple analgesia is the mainstay of treatment. Aspirin should be avoided due to the risk of Reye's syndrome.

Older adults

Older adults may present with more severe symptoms or complications. Atypical presentations, such as delirium or falls, can occur. There should be a lower threshold for suspecting serious pathology. Prescribers must be cautious of polypharmacy, particularly the renal effects of NSAIDs and drug interactions with antibiotics like clarithromycin.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care advice is first-line. Paracetamol is the preferred analgesic. Ibuprofen should be avoided in the third trimester. Most throat lozenges are considered safe for short-term use, but patients should check individual product licenses. If an antibiotic is required, phenoxymethylpenicillin and cephalosporins are considered safe. Clarithromycin should be used with caution in pregnancy. Tetracyclines are contraindicated.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are immunosuppressed (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, or post-transplant) are at high risk of severe or opportunistic infections. A sore throat could be a sign of a serious underlying problem like neutropenia and warrants urgent investigation. For patients on carbimazole or clozapine, a sore throat is a red flag for agranulocytosis and requires an immediate FBC.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The University Student

  • Presentation: A 19-year-old student presents with a 2-day history of a very painful sore throat and fever. He has no cough or runny nose.
  • Assessment: He looks tired but not toxic. Temp: 38.4°C. On examination, his tonsils are very large and red with visible pus. He has tender cervical nodes. FeverPAIN score is 4 (Fever, Purulence, Attend rapidly, Inflamed tonsils).
  • Diagnosis & Management: Likely bacterial tonsillitis. The GP discusses the pros and cons of antibiotics, explaining they may shorten the illness by about a day. An immediate prescription for phenoxymethylpenicillin is offered. Safety netting advice is given, including watching for signs of glandular fever (profound fatigue, abdominal pain) or quinsy.

Case Study 2: The Working Mother

  • Presentation: A 42-year-old woman presents with a 4-day history of a scratchy sore throat, a cough, and a runny nose. She is worried as she has an important work meeting and wants antibiotics to "nip it in the bud".
  • Assessment: She is well-appearing and afebrile. Her pharynx is mildly red, but tonsils are not inflamed and there is no exudate. FeverPAIN score is 0.
  • Diagnosis & Management: Viral URTI. The GP explains that the symptoms strongly suggest a viral cause, for which antibiotics are ineffective. They use the low FeverPAIN score to help explain this. They provide counselling on self-care, focusing on regular analgesia, and give clear safety netting advice to return if symptoms worsen or don't improve after a week.

Case Study 3: The Child with a Rash

  • Presentation: A 6-year-old boy is brought in with a 24-hour history of a sore throat, fever, and a new rash.
  • Assessment: He is flushed, with a temperature of 38.8°C. His tongue is red and swollen ('strawberry tongue'). His tonsils are red and enlarged. There is a widespread, fine, pinkish-red rash across his chest and abdomen which feels like sandpaper.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Scarlet fever. The GP explains the diagnosis to the parent and that it requires antibiotic treatment to prevent complications. They prescribe a 10-day course of phenoxymethylpenicillin, provide advice on school exclusion (until 24 hours after starting antibiotics), and notify Public Health England as it is a notifiable disease.
