Shingles (Herpes Zoster) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Shingles (herpes zoster) is the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), the same virus that causes chickenpox. It typically presents with a prodrome of pain, burning, tingling, or itching in a specific, unilateral dermatomal distribution. This neuropathic pain can precede the rash by several days and may be accompanied by systemic symptoms like headache, photophobia, and general malaise.

A few days after the onset of pain, a characteristic vesicular rash erupts in the same dermatome. The rash begins as erythematous macules and papules which quickly progress to form clusters of vesicles on an erythematous base. These vesicles may become pustular or haemorrhagic before crusting over within 7-10 days. The rash is classically unilateral and does not cross the midline. The entire episode, from prodrome to crusts falling off, typically lasts 2-4 weeks.

The most common site is a thoracic dermatome, but it can affect any dermatome, including cranial nerves. While it can occur at any age in someone who has had chickenpox, it is most common in older adults (over 50) and in individuals with a weakened immune system. The primary goal of management is to shorten the illness, relieve pain, and prevent complications, most notably post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN).

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of shingles can be managed in primary care, certain features are red flags necessitating urgent advice or referral to secondary care (usually ophthalmology, ENT, or an admissions unit).

  • Ophthalmic Involvement (Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus): Any shingles affecting the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve (V1 dermatome), which includes the forehead, scalp, and around the eye. Hutchinson's sign (vesicles on the tip, side, or root of the nose) indicates nasociliary branch involvement and significantly increases the risk of serious eye complications like keratitis, uveitis, and glaucoma, which can threaten sight. All cases require urgent ophthalmology referral.
  • Ear Involvement (Ramsay Hunt Syndrome): A history of deep ear pain accompanied by a vesicular rash in the external auditory canal, pinna, or palate. If associated with ipsilateral facial nerve palsy, this constitutes Ramsay Hunt syndrome (herpes zoster oticus). This requires urgent ENT referral or discussion.
  • Motor Weakness: The development of motor weakness in the affected dermatome (e.g., limb weakness, abdominal wall paresis) suggests motor neuropathy and warrants specialist assessment.
  • Signs of CNS Involvement: New onset of severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, confusion, or altered consciousness may indicate aseptic meningitis or encephalitis, a neurological emergency requiring immediate hospital admission.
  • Disseminated Zoster: The presence of more than 20 vesicles outside the primary and adjacent dermatomes. This suggests widespread viral replication and is more common in immunocompromised individuals. This requires urgent admission for intravenous antiviral therapy.
  • Severely Immunocompromised Patient: Patients with significant immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy, high-dose systemic steroids, uncontrolled HIV, or recent organ transplant) are at high risk of severe or disseminated disease and require a low threshold for urgent specialist advice and likely admission.
  • Signs of Secondary Bacterial Infection: Increasing erythema, warmth, swelling, and purulent discharge from lesions, especially if associated with fever, may indicate cellulitis or impetigo requiring antibiotic treatment.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

Diagnosis is usually made clinically based on the characteristic history and examination findings. A structured assessment is key to confirming the diagnosis and ruling out red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Onset and Evolution: Clarify the timeline, starting with the initial pain/paraesthesia and the appearance of the rash. Antiviral treatment is most effective if started within 72 hours of rash onset.
  • Pain Assessment: Characterise the pain (e.g., burning, stabbing, aching) and its severity using a pain score.
  • Rash Characteristics: Confirm the unilateral, dermatomal distribution. Ask about specific locations like the nose tip, eye area, or in the ear.
  • Associated Symptoms: Enquire about fever, headache, malaise, hearing loss, vertigo, and facial drooping.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about visual symptoms (blurry vision, red eye, pain), facial weakness, severe headache, or confusion.
  • Past Medical History: Specifically ask about previous chickenpox infection, immunosuppressive conditions (HIV, cancer) or medications (steroids, biologics), and shingles vaccination status.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess if the patient appears systemically unwell.
  • Skin Examination: Inspect the rash, confirming its unilateral nature and dermatomal boundary. Note the stage of the lesions (macules, vesicles, pustules, crusts). Specifically check for Hutchinson's sign (lesion on nose tip) and vesicles in the ear canal.
  • Neurological Examination: If facial involvement, assess the cranial nerves, particularly facial nerve (VII) for weakness (ask patient to raise eyebrows, close eyes tightly, smile) and eye movements. If ophthalmic involvement is suspected, a basic eye assessment for visual acuity and red eye is crucial before urgent referral.
  • No specific scoring tools are used for routine diagnosis, but pain scales are useful for monitoring treatment response.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

While the classic vesicular rash in a dermatome is highly specific, the differential diagnosis can be broader, especially in the pre-eruptive phase or with atypical presentations.

Common Differentials to Consider

  • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): Can present with clustered vesicles, but rashes are more typically recurrent in the same non-dermatomal site (e.g., oral or genital). Widespread or dermatomal HSV infection ('zosteriform simplex') can mimic shingles and may require viral swabs to differentiate, especially in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Can cause a localised, blistering rash. The distribution may follow contact with an irritant or allergen (e.g., plant sap like phytophotodermatitis) and is often intensely itchy rather than primarily painful. The shape may be linear or bizarre, not conforming to a dermatome.
  • Impetigo: A bacterial infection that causes vesicles and "honey-coloured" crusts. It is typically not confined to a dermatome and is less painful than shingles. It can also be a secondary infection of shingles lesions.
  • Insect Bites: Can present as localised, itchy, or painful papules and vesicles, but the distribution is usually random rather than dermatomal.

Pre-Eruptive Pain Differentials (Zoster Sine Herpete)

  • In the prodromal phase, before the rash appears, the unilateral pain can be mistaken for other conditions depending on the dermatome involved. This includes pleurisy, myocardial infarction, biliary colic, renal colic, or sciatica. The subsequent eruption of the rash clarifies the diagnosis.
5. Management options in primary care

Management goals are to control pain, limit the severity and duration of the rash, and reduce the risk of complications, especially post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN).

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Rash Care: Advise the patient to keep the rash clean and dry to prevent secondary bacterial infection. They should wear loose-fitting clothing made of natural fibres to reduce irritation.
  • Cool Compresses: Applying cool, wet compresses to the lesions for 5-10 minutes several times a day can be soothing.
  • Covering the Rash: A non-adherent dressing can be used to cover the rash. This reduces irritation and prevents transmission of the virus to non-immune contacts.
  • Infectivity: Explain that they are infectious (can transmit chickenpox, not shingles) to people who have not had chickenpox before, via direct contact with open vesicles. Advise avoidance of pregnant women, neonates, and immunocompromised individuals until all lesions have crusted over.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol and/or ibuprofen can be used for mild to moderate pain, provided there are no contraindications.
  • Topical Preparations: Calamine lotion can have a soothing, cooling effect on the rash.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir, Valaciclovir, Famciclovir): These drugs inhibit viral DNA replication. They are recommended for individuals aged 50 or over, those with non-truncal involvement, moderate to severe pain or rash, or who are immunocompromised. Treatment should be initiated within 72 hours of rash onset for maximal benefit but may be considered up to one week after if new vesicles are still forming or in high-risk groups. [Rx info]
  • Neuropathic Analgesia for Acute Neuritis: For severe acute pain not controlled by simple analgesia, consider initiating a low-dose neuropathic agent like amitriptyline or gabapentin. This is particularly important for older patients at higher risk of PHN. [Rx info]
  • Stronger Analgesia: In cases of severe pain, short-term use of opioid analgesics may be necessary, alongside laxatives.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management of shingles is primarily guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) on Shingles and guidance on post-herpetic neuralgia. The core principles are timely antiviral treatment for at-risk groups and effective pain management.

NICE CKS recommends offering oral antiviral treatment to people with shingles who present within 72 hours of rash onset and fall into specific categories: aged 50 or over, any age with non-truncal (e.g. limb, neck, perineal) involvement, any age with ophthalmic involvement (requiring urgent referral), any age with moderate or severe pain or rash, or if the person is immunocompromised. The evidence shows that for these groups, antivirals can reduce the duration of the rash and may reduce the duration and severity of acute pain and the incidence of PHN.

For pain management, a stepwise approach is recommended, starting with paracetamol and NSAIDs, moving to neuropathic agents (amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin) for neuropathic pain, and considering opioids for severe acute pain. For established post-herpetic neuralgia (pain persisting >3 months), NICE guideline CG173 provides detailed recommendations on pharmacological management.

The UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) oversees the national shingles immunisation programme, which aims to reduce the incidence and severity of shingles and PHN in older adults. The non-live vaccine, Shingrix, is now used. Eligibility has been expanded from September 2023 for immunocompetent individuals turning 65 or 70, and for severely immunosuppressed individuals from age 50.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Clear communication is essential for managing expectations, ensuring adherence to treatment, and providing effective safety netting.

Counselling for Diagnosis and Self-Care

  • Explain the Diagnosis: Describe shingles as a reactivation of the chickenpox virus that affects a single nerve. Reassure the patient that it is a common condition.
  • Natural History: Explain that the rash will blister and then crust over, typically taking 2-4 weeks to heal. Mention that some skin discolouration or scarring may remain.
  • Infectivity Explained: Clearly state, "You cannot give shingles to someone else, but you can give chickenpox to someone who has never had it before." Advise them to avoid contact with pregnant women who haven't had chickenpox, babies under one month old, and people with very weak immune systems until all their blisters have crusted over. Covering the rash helps prevent transmission.
  • Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN): Inform patients, particularly those over 50, about the risk of PHN – pain that can persist for months or even longer after the rash has healed. Explain that timely antiviral treatment and good pain control can help reduce this risk.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Antivirals: If prescribing, explain that the medicine works best when started early and that its main purpose is to speed up healing and reduce the risk of long-term pain. Emphasise the importance of completing the full course.
  • Analgesia: Explain the rationale for using neuropathic pain agents if prescribed. Counsel on the need to titrate the dose upwards slowly and on common side effects, such as drowsiness or dizziness, especially with amitriptyline or gabapentinoids. Advise caution with driving.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Effective safety netting is vital to ensure patients seek help appropriately if complications develop.

Provide clear verbal and, if possible, written advice on when to seek further medical attention. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they develop any of the following:

  • Eye Symptoms: Any new redness in the eye, eye pain, blurred or reduced vision, or sensitivity to light, particularly if the rash is on their forehead or nose. They should seek same-day medical advice.
  • Facial Weakness: If they notice one side of their face starting to droop.
  • Worsening Headache or Confusion: Development of a severe headache, stiff neck, or if they start to feel confused or very drowsy.
  • Spreading Rash: If the rash spreads across the midline of their body or if they notice new blisters appearing in areas far from the original rash.
  • Severe Pain: If their pain is not controlled by the prescribed medication.
  • Signs of Bacterial Infection: If the rash becomes increasingly red, hot, swollen, or starts weeping pus, or if they develop a fever.

A follow-up review may be appropriate, particularly for older or more frail patients, to assess pain control and check for complications. A telephone review after a week could be considered. All patients should be advised that the pain may take some time to settle and to return for review if pain persists for more than 3 months after the rash has healed (for management of PHN).

9. Pathophysiology

Shingles is caused by the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a double-stranded DNA virus from the human herpesvirus family. The primary infection with VZV causes chickenpox (varicella), usually in childhood. After the resolution of the primary infection, the virus is not cleared from the body but becomes latent, ascending from skin lesions via sensory nerves to establish a dormant infection in the dorsal root ganglia, cranial nerve ganglia, or autonomic ganglia.

The virus remains latent for decades, suppressed by a robust cell-mediated immune response. Reactivation occurs when this specific VZV-specific T-cell immunity wanes, a process associated with increasing age, immunosuppressive therapy, or certain illnesses (e.g., HIV, malignancy). Upon reactivation, the virus replicates within the ganglion, causing intense inflammation, neuronal necrosis, and neuralgia.

The replicated virus then travels anterograde down the sensory nerve to the area of skin supplied by that ganglion (the dermatome). Viral replication in the epidermal cells leads to the characteristic vesicular rash. The associated acute neuritic pain is a direct result of the inflammatory process within the ganglion and peripheral nerve. If this nerve damage is severe and healing is incomplete, it can lead to the chronic, often debilitating, pain state known as post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN), which is maintained by mechanisms of peripheral and central sensitisation.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing for shingles, it is crucial to consider the specific profiles of the medications used, particularly antivirals and neuropathic agents.

Oral Antivirals (Aciclovir, Valaciclovir, Famciclovir)

  • Renal Function: All oral antivirals for shingles require dose adjustment in patients with renal impairment (reduced eGFR). It is essential to check renal function before prescribing and adjust the dose according to the BNF. Dehydration can increase the risk of renal toxicity, so patients should be advised to maintain good fluid intake.
  • Interactions: Significant interactions are uncommon, but caution is advised with other nephrotoxic drugs.
  • Aciclovir vs Valaciclovir/Famciclovir: Valaciclovir (a pro-drug of aciclovir) and famciclovir have improved bioavailability and less frequent dosing schedules compared to aciclovir, which can improve adherence.

Neuropathic Analgesics (Amitriptyline, Gabapentin, Pregabalin)

  • Amitriptyline: Contraindicated in the acute recovery period after myocardial infarction and in patients with arrhythmias or severe heart disease. Use with caution in older adults due to anticholinergic side effects (confusion, urinary retention) and risk of falls. Start at a very low dose and titrate slowly.
  • Gabapentinoids (Gabapentin, Pregabalin): Also require dose titration and adjustment for renal impairment. Common side effects include drowsiness, dizziness, and peripheral oedema. There is a risk of misuse and dependence with pregabalin. MHRA guidance highlights the risk of severe respiratory depression, especially when co-prescribed with opioids.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Shingles is uncommon in immunocompetent children. When it occurs, it is typically mild with minimal pain and a low risk of PHN. Antiviral treatment is not routinely recommended for uncomplicated cases in healthy children. Management focuses on simple analgesia and rash care. However, antiviral treatment should be considered for children with non-truncal shingles or those who are immunocompromised, often following specialist advice.

Older adults

This group has the highest incidence of shingles and is at the greatest risk of severe acute pain and debilitating post-herpetic neuralgia. Antiviral therapy is strongly recommended for all patients over 50. Proactive and effective pain management, often with early introduction of neuropathic agents, is critical. Prescribers must be cautious, starting with low doses and titrating slowly due to increased susceptibility to side effects (e.g., confusion, falls from amitriptyline or gabapentinoids) and the likelihood of polypharmacy and renal impairment. Shingles vaccination should be encouraged as per the national programme.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Shingles in pregnancy does not pose a significant risk of congenital varicella syndrome, but specialist advice should be sought regarding management. If treatment is deemed necessary, aciclovir is the antiviral of choice due to more extensive safety data. Valaciclovir and famciclovir should be used only if the potential benefit justifies the potential risk. Systemic aciclovir is considered compatible with breastfeeding.

People with significant comorbidities

Immunocompromised patients (e.g., due to HIV, high-dose steroids, chemotherapy, biologics) are at high risk of severe, complicated, or disseminated shingles. They require urgent antiviral therapy and often need specialist consultation or hospital admission for higher doses or intravenous treatment. Standard oral antiviral doses may be insufficient. The threshold for suspecting complications (e.g., CNS or visceral involvement) should be very low. Dose adjustments for all medicines may be required in patients with renal or hepatic impairment.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Older Adult with Thoracic Shingles

  • Presentation: A 72-year-old man presents with a 2-day history of a painful, blistering rash on the right side of his chest. The pain started 4 days ago and he describes it as a constant burning. He is otherwise well.
  • Assessment: Examination reveals a cluster of vesicles on an erythematous base in the T4 dermatome on the right, not crossing the midline. He is afebrile and systemically well. His eGFR from 3 months ago was 68.
  • Diagnosis: Acute shingles (herpes zoster).
  • Management: Given his age (>50) and presentation within 72 hours, he is prescribed a 7-day course of oral valaciclovir [Rx info]. He is advised on simple analgesia (paracetamol) and self-care for the rash. The risk of PHN is explained. Clear safety netting advice is given regarding signs of secondary infection or uncontrolled pain. A follow-up is planned if the pain does not settle.

Case Study 2: The Red Flag Referral

  • Presentation: A 58-year-old woman phones the surgery. For 24 hours she has had a tingling forehead and now has some painful spots on the right side of her forehead and on the tip of her nose. Her right eye feels a bit gritty.
  • Assessment: The history of a rash on the forehead (V1 dermatome) and, crucially, on the tip of the nose (Hutchinson's sign) is a major red flag for herpes zoster ophthalmicus. The gritty feeling suggests early eye involvement.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP explains the serious risk to her eye. She is advised to attend the hospital's acute eye service immediately for assessment by an ophthalmologist. The GP faxes a referral letter and also speaks to the on-call ophthalmologist. She is prescribed oral antivirals to start immediately as per local protocol, pending her specialist review.

Case Study 3: Established Post-Herpetic Neuralgia

  • Presentation: An 81-year-old woman returns for review. She had shingles affecting her left flank four months ago. The rash has completely healed, but she is left with severe, persistent pain, which she describes as "like electric shocks" and says her clothes brushing against the skin is unbearable (allodynia). Simple analgesia is ineffective.
  • Assessment: The history is classic for post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN). Her mood is low due to the constant pain.
  • Diagnosis: Post-herpetic neuralgia.
  • Management: The GP validates her symptoms and explains the diagnosis. Following NICE guidance for neuropathic pain, they discuss treatment options. They decide to start a low dose of amitriptyline [Rx info], to be taken at night, with a plan to titrate the dose up slowly every week depending on response and side effects. She is counselled on potential side effects like a dry mouth and drowsiness. A review is scheduled in 2 weeks to assess progress.
