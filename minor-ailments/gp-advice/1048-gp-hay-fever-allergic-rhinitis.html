Allergic Rhinitis (Hay Fever) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal mucosa, affecting a significant portion of the UK population. It is one of the most common reasons for consultation in primary care, particularly during spring and summer. Patients typically present with a combination of nasal and ocular symptoms. The classic tetrad of symptoms includes anterior rhinorrhoea (runny nose, usually clear), paroxysmal sneezing, nasal congestion, and nasal itching. Ocular symptoms (allergic conjunctivitis) such as red, itchy, and watery eyes are also very common.

The condition can be classified based on timing and severity. Seasonal allergic rhinitis (hay fever) is triggered by airborne allergens like grass, tree, or weed pollens. Perennial allergic rhinitis occurs year-round and is typically caused by indoor allergens such as house dust mites, pet dander, or moulds. Symptoms can range from mild and intermittent, causing minor disruption, to moderate-severe and persistent, significantly impacting quality of life, sleep, and performance at work or school. It frequently co-exists with other atopic conditions, notably asthma and eczema.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While allergic rhinitis is a benign condition, it is crucial to identify red flags that may indicate more serious pathology requiring further investigation or urgent referral to secondary care (ENT or ophthalmology).

  • Unilateral symptoms: Persistent one-sided nasal blockage, discharge, or pain can be a sign of a structural abnormality (e.g., deviated septum, foreign body) or, rarely, a sinonasal tumour.
  • Persistent blood-stained nasal discharge: While minor epistaxis can occur with nasal inflammation or spray use, persistent or heavy bleeding is a red flag for malignancy or a granulomatous disorder.
  • Anosmia (loss of smell): While common with severe congestion, a complete and persistent loss of smell, especially if unilateral, warrants investigation to exclude structural or neurological causes.
  • Severe or persistent facial pain: This is not typical for allergic rhinitis and may suggest acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, dental abscess, or trigeminal neuralgia.
  • Periorbital swelling or erythema: Significant swelling or redness around the eye suggests periorbital cellulitis, a potential complication of sinusitis that requires emergency assessment.
  • Visual disturbance: Any change in vision, such as reduced acuity or double vision, associated with nasal symptoms is a red flag requiring urgent ophthalmological review.
  • Nasal crusting or septal perforation: This may indicate a granulomatous disease (e.g., GPA/Wegener's), cocaine abuse, or complications from inappropriate long-term use of nasal sprays.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is key to confirming the diagnosis and assessing the impact of allergic rhinitis.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Clarify the nature of symptoms (sneezing, itching, rhinorrhoea, blockage, eye symptoms). Ask about timing—seasonal, perennial, or episodic. Enquire about triggers (e.g., pollen seasons, pets, dust).
  • Severity and Impact: Use the ARIA classification (Intermittent vs. Persistent; Mild vs. Moderate-Severe) to gauge impact on sleep, daily activities, and work/school performance.
  • Associated Conditions: Systematically ask about symptoms of asthma (cough, wheeze, breathlessness), eczema, and food allergies (e.g., oral allergy syndrome). Poorly controlled rhinitis can worsen asthma control.
  • Past Treatments: Document previous use of OTC and prescribed medications, their effectiveness, and any side effects. Crucially, ask about nasal spray technique.
  • Social/Occupational History: Consider occupational rhinitis if symptoms improve when away from work (e.g., exposure to flour, wood dust, latex).
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically screen for unilateral symptoms, bleeding, facial pain, or loss of smell.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Look for signs like mouth breathing, allergic shiners (dark circles under the eyes), and a nasal crease from upward rubbing ('allergic salute').
  • Nasal Examination: Using an otoscope with a large speculum, inspect the nasal mucosa. In allergic rhinitis, turbinates are typically swollen, pale, and boggy, with a clear discharge. Look for septal deviation or nasal polyps (glistening, grape-like structures).
  • Eye Examination: Check for conjunctival injection and swelling.
  • Chest Examination: Auscultate the chest for wheeze if there is any suspicion of co-existing asthma.

Scoring Tools

Formal scoring tools are not routinely used in primary care diagnosis, but the ARIA (Allergic Rhinitis and its Impact on Asthma) classification helps to stratify management based on symptom duration and severity.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for rhinitis symptoms is broad. The history is often the key to distinguishing between them.

Common Differentials

  • Infective Rhinitis (Common Cold): Usually short-lived (less than 10 days) and often accompanied by systemic features like fever, myalgia, and sore throat. Nasal discharge may become purulent.
  • Non-Allergic Rhinitis: A group of conditions with various triggers.
    • Vasomotor Rhinitis: Nasal congestion and rhinorrhoea triggered by environmental factors like changes in temperature, humidity, or strong odours, without evidence of allergy.
    • Rhinitis Medicamentosa: Rebound nasal congestion caused by the overuse of topical nasal decongestant sprays (e.g., xylometazoline) for more than 5-7 days.
  • Hormonal Rhinitis: Nasal congestion associated with pregnancy, puberty, or use of the oral contraceptive pill.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Sinonasal Tumours: Rare, but must be considered with unilateral symptoms, blood-stained discharge, persistent facial pain, or cranial nerve signs.
  • Nasal Polyps: Often associated with asthma and aspirin sensitivity (Samter's triad). Can cause significant obstruction and anosmia. While often benign, they require ENT assessment.
  • Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA): A systemic vasculitis that can present with nasal crusting, bleeding, and septal perforation. Patients are often systemically unwell.
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Leak: Rare, typically follows head trauma. Presents as persistent, unilateral, clear watery discharge that tastes salty. Requires emergency referral.
5. Management options in primary care

Management follows a stepwise approach, aiming to control symptoms and improve quality of life. Education and allergen avoidance are the foundation of treatment.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Allergen Avoidance: Advise on practical measures. For pollen: monitor pollen counts, keep windows closed, wear wrap-around sunglasses. For dust mites: use allergen-proof bedding, wash bedding at 60°C, increase ventilation.
  • Nasal Douching: Rinsing the nasal passages with a saline solution can help clear mucus and allergens. This can be done with pre-made solutions or a sinus rinse bottle.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Oral Antihistamines: Second-generation, non-sedating antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine, Fexofenadine) are first-line for mild-intermittent symptoms, particularly sneezing and itching. [Rx info]
  • Intranasal Corticosteroid (INCS) Sprays: The most effective treatment for moderate-to-severe symptoms, especially nasal blockage. (e.g., Beclometasone, Fluticasone propionate). Patient education on correct technique is vital for efficacy. [Rx info]
  • Sodium Cromoglicate: Available as nasal spray and eye drops. Less effective than other options but has a good safety profile, particularly for pregnant people and children. Requires frequent administration. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Intranasal Corticosteroid (INCS) Sprays: More potent preparations are available on prescription (e.g., Mometasone furoate, Fluticasone furoate). These are the mainstay of treatment for persistent or moderate-severe rhinitis. [Rx info]
  • Combination Nasal Sprays: A combination of an intranasal antihistamine and a corticosteroid (e.g., Azelastine/Fluticasone propionate) can be used if monotherapy is insufficient. [Rx info]
  • Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs): (e.g., Montelukast). Can be useful as an add-on therapy, particularly in patients with co-existing asthma. [Rx info]
  • Short-course Oral Corticosteroids: Rarely indicated. May be considered as a short rescue course for severe, debilitating symptoms to gain control while long-term treatments take effect. Use should be brief due to systemic side effects. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by NICE CKS (Allergic Rhinitis) and guidelines from the British Society for Allergy and Clinical Immunology (BSACI). The core principles are a stepwise approach to treatment, patient education, and regular review.

NICE guidance recommends a stepped-care model based on symptom severity. For mild/intermittent symptoms, oral or intranasal antihistamines are suggested. For moderate/severe or persistent symptoms, intranasal corticosteroids (INCS) are the first-line treatment due to their superior efficacy, especially for nasal congestion. Guidelines stress that INCS should be used regularly for maximal effect, which may take up to two weeks to develop.

If monotherapy with an INCS is not sufficient, guidelines support adding an oral antihistamine or switching to a combination nasal spray containing both a corticosteroid and an intranasal antihistamine. Leukotriene receptor antagonists like montelukast are positioned as an add-on therapy, particularly for patients with comorbid asthma.

Injectable corticosteroids (e.g., Kenalog) are not recommended in UK guidelines for the routine treatment of hay fever due to an unfavourable risk-benefit profile, with potential for serious systemic side effects. The MHRA has also issued warnings regarding the neuropsychiatric risks associated with montelukast, advising clinicians to be alert for mood or behaviour changes.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is essential for successful management, ensuring patient adherence and realistic expectations.

Counselling for Self-Care and Diagnosis

  • Explain the Condition: Describe allergic rhinitis as an overreaction of the immune system to harmless substances like pollen. Explain that the goal is symptom control, not a cure.
  • Allergen Avoidance: Provide specific, practical advice tailored to their likely triggers (e.g., "On high pollen days, try to keep your car windows shut and shower when you get home").
  • Manage Expectations: Inform patients that treatments like intranasal corticosteroids are not a "quick fix." They need to be used regularly and may take 1-2 weeks to reach their full effect.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Nasal Spray Technique: This is the single most important counselling point for INCS. Demonstrate the correct technique: gently shake the bottle, look down, use the right hand for the left nostril and vice-versa (to aim away from the septum), and sniff gently, not snort. This maximises efficacy and minimises side effects like irritation or bleeding.
  • Antihistamines: Advise patients to use non-sedating antihistamines for daytime use. Warn them that even these can cause drowsiness in some individuals and to be cautious when first driving or operating machinery.
  • Montelukast: Counsel patients and caregivers about the potential for neuropsychiatric side effects (e.g., sleep disturbances, mood changes, agitation) and advise them to stop the medication and seek medical advice if these occur.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting ensures patients understand when to seek further help. A follow-up plan allows for assessment of treatment efficacy and adjustment of the management plan.

Clear verbal and written advice should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult if they experience any of the following:

  • Failure to improve: Symptoms do not improve after 2-4 weeks of consistent, correct use of first-line prescribed treatment.
  • Development of Red Flag symptoms: Specifically mention unilateral blockage or discharge, persistent blood in discharge, facial pain, or loss of smell.
  • Worsening of co-existing asthma: If they notice an increase in their asthma symptoms or use of their reliever inhaler.
  • Suspected treatment side effects: Such as persistent nasal irritation, bleeding from nasal sprays, or mood changes with montelukast.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If symptoms become perennial or the trigger is unclear.

A routine follow-up can be scheduled for 4-6 weeks after initiating a new treatment to review its effectiveness and the patient's quality of life. For patients with seasonal symptoms, an annual review before the season begins can be helpful to optimise their treatment plan.

9. Pathophysiology

Allergic rhinitis is a classic example of a Type 1 hypersensitivity reaction. The process occurs in two main phases.

The first stage is sensitisation. On initial exposure to an allergen (e.g., a grass pollen grain), an antigen-presenting cell processes the allergen and presents it to a T-helper cell (Th2). These Th2 cells stimulate B lymphocytes to differentiate into plasma cells, which then produce allergen-specific Immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies. This specific IgE circulates and binds to the surface of mast cells and basophils in the nasal mucosa, sensitising the individual.

Upon subsequent exposure to the same allergen, the allergen binds to and cross-links the IgE antibodies on the surface of these sensitised mast cells. This triggers mast cell degranulation, releasing a cascade of pre-formed and newly synthesised inflammatory mediators. This constitutes the early-phase reaction (occurring within minutes) and is driven by mediators like histamine, tryptase, and leukotrienes, causing the characteristic symptoms of sneezing, itching, and clear rhinorrhoea.

The late-phase reaction occurs 4-6 hours later. It is characterised by the recruitment of other inflammatory cells, including eosinophils, neutrophils, and T-lymphocytes, to the nasal mucosa. These cells release further pro-inflammatory mediators, leading to a more sustained inflammatory response. This phase is primarily responsible for the prolonged and troublesome symptom of nasal congestion.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When selecting treatments, it is essential to consider their specific safety profiles, interactions, and contraindications.

Intranasal Corticosteroids (INCS)

  • Side Effects: Local side effects are most common and include nasal irritation, stinging, and epistaxis. These are often related to poor technique (spraying onto the nasal septum). Systemic absorption is generally low with modern preparations, but long-term use at high doses, especially with concurrent inhaled steroids for asthma, requires theoretical consideration of systemic effects.
  • Contraindications: Caution is needed in patients with recent nasal surgery or trauma. There is a very small risk of septal perforation, usually linked to poor technique.

Antihistamines

  • Sedation: First-generation antihistamines (e.g., chlorphenamine, promethazine) cause significant sedation and are generally not recommended for regular use. Second-generation agents (e.g., cetirizine, loratadine) are "non-sedating" but can still cause drowsiness in a minority of individuals. Patients should be warned about this, especially when driving.
  • Interactions: Caution is needed when co-prescribing with other CNS depressants.

Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs - Montelukast)

  • MHRA Alert: There is a known risk of neuropsychiatric reactions (e.g., agitation, sleep disturbance, depression, and rarely, suicidal ideation). Patients and their caregivers must be counselled on this risk and advised to stop treatment and seek advice if they occur.

Decongestants (Topical e.g. Xylometazoline)

  • Rhinitis Medicamentosa: These agents, often bought OTC, should not be used for more than 5-7 consecutive days. Prolonged use leads to rebound vasodilation and worsening nasal congestion, creating a cycle of dependency.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Allergic rhinitis is common in children and can impact school performance and quality of life. A stepwise approach is used, with licensing varying by age. Non-sedating oral antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine, loratadine) are available in liquid form for younger children. Intranasal corticosteroids like mometasone and fluticasone are licensed for children (check specific product license for age). Correct spray technique and device choice are particularly important. Montelukast is also licensed for paediatric use but requires careful counselling regarding neuropsychiatric side effects.

Older adults

Older adults may be more susceptible to the anticholinergic and sedative side effects of first-generation antihistamines, which can increase the risk of confusion and falls; these should be avoided. Second-generation antihistamines are safer. Polypharmacy is common, so check for potential drug interactions. Ensure they are physically able to use a nasal spray device correctly.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Allergen avoidance and saline nasal sprays are first-line. If medication is required, a stepwise approach is used, prioritising drugs with established safety records. According to NICE CKS, intranasal corticosteroids (e.g. beclometasone, budesonide, fluticasone) are considered safe as systemic absorption is very low. If an antihistamine is needed, loratadine or cetirizine are generally preferred. Oral decongestants should be avoided. Always consult the latest BNF or specific resources on drug use in pregnancy and breastfeeding.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with asthma, treating co-existing allergic rhinitis is crucial as it can improve asthma control. LTRAs (montelukast) can be a particularly useful treatment option in this group. Be cautious with antihistamines in patients with severe renal impairment and adjust doses accordingly. Ensure any new treatment does not interact with their existing medication regimen.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The University Student

  • Presentation: A 19-year-old student presents in June with a 3-week history of intense sneezing fits, itchy, watery eyes, and a constantly running nose. It is disrupting his ability to concentrate on his exams. He has tried an OTC antihistamine tablet which helps "a little bit".
  • Assessment: He is afebrile and systemically well. Examination shows boggy, pale nasal turbinates and injected conjunctivae. Chest is clear. Diagnosis is seasonal allergic rhinitis.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis. As symptoms are moderate-severe and impacting his studies, an intranasal corticosteroid spray is prescribed. The GP demonstrates the correct "cross-body" spray technique. He is advised to continue the oral antihistamine as needed and to try allergen avoidance measures. Safety netting advice is given to return if symptoms don't improve in 2-3 weeks.

Case Study 2: The Patient with Year-Round Symptoms

  • Presentation: A 42-year-old woman presents with chronic nasal blockage. She says she "can't breathe through her nose" at night, which disturbs her sleep. She also has a history of mild asthma. She has been buying a decongestant nasal spray and using it most nights for months.
  • Assessment: History reveals perennial symptoms, worse indoors. Examination shows inflamed, swollen nasal mucosa. Her overuse of decongestants suggests rhinitis medicamentosa complicating underlying perennial allergic rhinitis (likely dust mite allergy).
  • Management: The GP explains both diagnoses. She is advised to stop the decongestant spray completely, warning her that congestion may worsen for a week or two. A prescription for a regular intranasal corticosteroid spray is given to manage the underlying allergy and help with the rebound congestion. Given her co-existing asthma, adding montelukast is also discussed as a future option if the spray alone is insufficient. Follow-up is arranged for 4 weeks.

Case Study 3: The Red Flag Presentation

  • Presentation: A 65-year-old man presents with a 2-month history of a blocked right nostril and a blood-tinged discharge from that side only. He has no other typical hay fever symptoms.
  • Assessment: The unilateral nature and bloody discharge are immediate red flags. He is a non-smoker. Vital signs are normal. Examination of the right nostril is difficult due to blockage but appears abnormal.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP explains that while this could be something simple, the one-sided symptoms require specialist investigation. An urgent 2-week-wait referral to the ENT department is made to rule out a sinonasal malignancy.
