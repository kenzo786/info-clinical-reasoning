Fever in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Fever, or pyrexia, is an elevation of body temperature above the normal daily variation. It is one of the most common reasons for consultation in primary care, particularly in children. In the UK, a temperature of 38°C or higher is generally considered a fever. It is a physiological response to a wide range of conditions, most commonly self-limiting viral infections such as upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs), gastroenteritis, or common childhood illnesses.

Patients typically present with a history of feeling hot, flushed, or unwell. They may also report sweating, chills, or rigors. Associated symptoms often point towards the underlying cause, for example, cough and coryza in a URTI, or vomiting and diarrhoea in gastroenteritis. While distressing, fever is usually a benign and helpful part of the body's defence mechanism against infection. In most cases, the fever resolves within 3 to 5 days without specific medical intervention.

The primary role of the clinician is to provide reassurance and advice on symptomatic relief, while systematically assessing the patient to exclude features of serious illness or bacterial infection that may require specific treatment or urgent referral.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

It is crucial to identify features suggesting a serious underlying cause for a fever. The presence of any red flag warrants urgent review, investigation, or immediate hospital admission. Specific attention should be paid to children under 5, the elderly, and the immunocompromised.

  • Signs of Sepsis or Shock: Any signs suggesting systemic circulatory compromise require emergency admission. These include new-onset confusion or altered mental state, cyanosis, hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg), marked tachycardia, or cold, mottled peripheries.
  • Non-blanching Rash: A petechial or purpuric rash that does not disappear with pressure (the 'glass test') is a medical emergency, highly suggestive of meningococcal septicaemia.
  • Signs of Meningeal Irritation: In any age group, features such as neck stiffness, photophobia, a bulging fontanelle in infants, or a severe, unrelenting headache should prompt urgent referral to rule out meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Severe Respiratory Distress: A high respiratory rate (>30 breaths/min in adults), grunting, nasal flaring, severe use of accessory muscles, or an oxygen saturation <92% on air suggests severe pneumonia or sepsis and requires immediate hospitalisation.
  • Focal Neurological Signs: New onset of focal weakness, seizures (especially focal or prolonged), or reduced consciousness level are red flags for central nervous system infection or sepsis.
  • Specific concerns in children (NICE 'Red' Traffic Light Signs): These include appearing ill to a healthcare professional, decreased activity, no response to social cues, inability to rouse, weak high-pitched cry, bile-stained vomiting, or a fever >38°C in an infant under 3 months.
  • Severe, Unexplained Pain: Severe, localised pain (e.g., abdominal or limb pain) out of keeping with the expected symptoms may indicate a deep-seated infection, such as osteomyelitis or an abscess, or sepsis.
  • Immunocompromised Patient: Any patient with significant immunosuppression (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, or uncontrolled HIV) with a fever should be considered high risk and have a very low threshold for urgent assessment and admission.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is essential to differentiate benign from serious causes of fever. The assessment should be tailored to the patient's age and clinical context.

History Taking

  • Fever Characteristics: Ascertain the duration, height of the fever if measured, and associated symptoms like rigors or sweating.
  • Localising Symptoms: Systematically ask about symptoms that may pinpoint the source of infection: cough, sore throat, earache, urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency), abdominal pain, vomiting, or diarrhoea.
  • Red Flag Screen: Directly ask about rash, headache, neck stiffness, breathlessness, and confusion. In infants, ask about feeding, wet nappies, and irritability.
  • Past Medical History: Enquire about pre-existing conditions, particularly immunosuppression, respiratory or cardiac disease, and diabetes. Check immunisation status, especially in children.
  • Social History: Ask about recent travel, contact with sick individuals, or exposure to specific infectious agents.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the patient's overall appearance. Are they alert and responsive or lethargic and unwell? Look for signs of distress, pain, or confusion.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature, respiratory rate, pulse, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation (SpO2). Compare these to age-appropriate normal ranges.
  • Systematic Examination: Perform a 'top-to-toe' examination. Look for a rash (and perform the glass test if present). Check for a bulging fontanelle in infants. Examine the throat, ears, and lymph nodes. Auscultate the heart and chest. Palpate the abdomen. Assess hydration status (e.g., skin turgor, capillary refill time).

Scoring Tools

In primary care, scoring tools help to standardise risk assessment:

  • NICE Traffic Light System (for children under 5): This tool categorises children into low (green), intermediate (amber), or high (red) risk of serious illness based on clinical signs, guiding management decisions.
  • NEWS2 (National Early Warning Score 2): Used for adults, this score aggregates physiological measurements (RR, SpO2, BP, pulse, consciousness, temperature) to quantify acute-illness severity and prompt an appropriate clinical response, including escalation for suspected sepsis.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for fever is vast, but the vast majority of cases seen in primary care are due to common, self-limiting infections. The clinical assessment aims to identify the likely source and rule out serious pathology.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI): The most common cause. Presents with coryza, cough, sore throat, and malaise. Examination is often unremarkable apart from a raised temperature.
  • Acute Otitis Media: Common in children, presenting with ear pain, irritability, and sometimes a perforated eardrum.
  • Tonsillitis/Pharyngitis: Usually viral but can be bacterial (Strep A). Presents with a sore throat, painful swallowing, and visible inflammation or exudate on the tonsils.
  • Gastroenteritis: Usually viral. Presents with vomiting and/or diarrhoea, and sometimes abdominal cramps.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI): Can present with fever alone, especially in infants and the elderly. Other symptoms include dysuria, frequency, and loin pain if the kidneys are involved (pyelonephritis).

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Sepsis: A life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Look for red flags and high NEWS2 or 'red' traffic light scores. The source may be any bacterial infection (e.g., pneumonia, UTI, cellulitis).
  • Meningitis/Meningococcal Disease: Infection of the meninges, often presenting with fever, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, and a non-blanching rash. It is a medical emergency.
  • Pneumonia: Infection of the lung parenchyma. Suggested by focal chest signs (crepitations), cough, breathlessness, and pleuritic pain.
  • Kawasaki Disease (in children): A vasculitis of unknown cause. Characterised by a persistent high fever (>5 days) plus features like a widespread rash, bilateral conjunctivitis, changes in the lips/mouth ('strawberry tongue'), and cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Pyelonephritis: Infection of the kidney, which can lead to sepsis. Presents with fever, loin pain, vomiting, and systemic upset.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is focused on identifying and treating serious infections, while providing advice for symptomatic relief for those with likely self-limiting viral illnesses. Antibiotics are only indicated for suspected bacterial infections.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain that fever is a normal response to infection and usually resolves in a few days. Advise that the goal is not to eliminate fever, but to improve the child's or adult's comfort.
  • Hydration: Advise encouraging regular fluid intake to prevent dehydration. For infants, this means continuing breast or bottle feeds.
  • Appropriate Dress: Advise against under- or over-dressing. Tepid sponging is not recommended as it can cause shivering, which may raise the core temperature.
  • Monitoring: Advise parents and carers on what to look out for and when to seek further help (see Safety Netting).

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

Advise that antipyretics should be used to relieve distress or discomfort, not solely to reduce body temperature.

  • Paracetamol: An analgesic and antipyretic. It is first-line for all age groups. [Rx info]
  • Ibuprofen: A non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) with analgesic and antipyretic effects. Can be used as an alternative to paracetamol. [Rx info]
  • Alternating Agents: NICE guidance for children specifically advises against the routine use of alternating paracetamol and ibuprofen. Consider one agent first, and switch to the other only if the patient remains distressed despite the first.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Antibiotics: These are not effective against viruses and should only be prescribed when a bacterial cause is suspected and deemed likely (e.g., pneumonia, UTI, tonsillitis with a high Centor/FeverPAIN score) or if the patient is at high risk of complications. The choice of antibiotic depends on the likely source of infection and local guidelines. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of fever in the UK is guided by several key documents, most notably NICE guideline NG143 (Fever in under 5s: assessment and initial management) and NICE guideline NG51 (Sepsis: recognition, diagnosis and early management).

NICE NG143 provides the 'traffic light' system for identifying the risk of serious illness in children under 5 and gives clear recommendations on management in primary care. It emphasises that antipyretics should be used to manage a child's distress, not just to lower their temperature. It advises against routinely giving paracetamol and ibuprofen alternately but suggests switching if the child's distress is not alleviated with the first agent.

NICE NG51 provides guidance for all age groups on recognising the signs and symptoms of sepsis and on the immediate actions to take. It advocates for the use of structured assessment tools like NEWS2 in adults to identify high-risk patients who require urgent hospital treatment, including pre-hospital antibiotics where appropriate.

Across all guidelines, there is a strong emphasis on antimicrobial stewardship. Antibiotics should not be prescribed for uncomplicated viral infections that cause fever. A back-up or delayed prescription for antibiotics may be considered for some patients, with clear instructions on when to use it.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is paramount in managing fever, particularly when reassuring anxious patients or parents and promoting safe self-management.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Nature of Fever: Clearly state that fever is a normal, and often helpful, response of the body to fight infection. Reassure them that a high temperature is not in itself usually harmful.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that it is worrying when a loved one, especially a child, has a fever.
  • Empower with Self-Help: Focus on positive actions. "The most important things are to make sure they are comfortable and getting plenty of fluids to drink." Explain how to check for signs of dehydration (e.g., dry mouth, sunken eyes, fewer wet nappies).
  • Antipyretic Use: Explain that medicines like paracetamol or ibuprofen should be used if the person is distressed or unwell with the fever, not just for the number on the thermometer.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: If a viral cause is likely, provide a clear explanation. "This fever is caused by a virus. Antibiotics only work on bacteria, so they won't help it get better faster and can cause side effects."

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Antibiotics: If prescribing, explain why they are needed (e.g., "I can hear some changes in the chest that suggest a bacterial infection"). Briefly explain the importance of completing the course and common side effects.
  • Delayed Prescriptions: If providing a back-up prescription, give explicit, written instructions. For example, "Only start these antibiotics if the fever lasts more than 5 days or if they become significantly more unwell in themselves."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step. It involves providing clear, specific advice on when and how to seek further medical help if the condition does not follow the expected course. This should be tailored to the patient's age and presenting complaint.

Clear verbal and written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient, parent, or carer to re-consult or seek urgent care if they notice any of the following:

  • The fever lasts for more than 5 days or returns after getting better.
  • The person seems to be getting more unwell, or you are more worried than you were before.
  • Development of a new rash, particularly one that does not fade with pressure (non-blanching).
  • Signs of dehydration, such as passing very little urine, a dry mouth, or drowsiness.
  • Development of specific 'red flag' symptoms like severe headache with light sensitivity, new confusion, difficulty breathing, or a seizure.
  • For children specifically: advise to seek help if they develop any 'amber' or 'red' signs from the NICE traffic light system, such as reduced activity, poor feeding, or a weak cry.
  • They have any other symptom that they are concerned about.

For patients assessed as intermediate risk ('amber') but managed in the community, a scheduled follow-up may be appropriate, either via telephone or a face-to-face review, depending on the clinical scenario.

9. Pathophysiology

Fever is a regulated elevation of the body's core temperature, controlled by the central nervous system. The thermoregulatory centre is located in the preoptic nucleus of the anterior hypothalamus.

The process is initiated by pyrogens, which can be exogenous or endogenous. Exogenous pyrogens are microbial products, most classically lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from the cell wall of gram-negative bacteria. When these are detected by immune cells like macrophages and monocytes, it triggers the release of endogenous pyrogens.

Endogenous pyrogens are inflammatory cytokines, primarily interleukin-1 (IL-1), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α). These cytokines travel in the bloodstream to the hypothalamus. Here, they stimulate endothelial cells to produce prostaglandin E2 (PGE2). PGE2 acts on glial cells in the hypothalamus, which release cyclic AMP (cAMP). This cascade ultimately 'resets' the hypothalamic thermoregulatory set-point to a higher temperature.

The hypothalamus then initiates physiological responses to raise the body's temperature to this new set-point. This includes peripheral vasoconstriction to reduce heat loss from the skin (causing a feeling of cold), and shivering (involuntary muscle contractions) to increase metabolic heat production. This continues until the core body temperature matches the new, higher set-point. The process reverses when pyrogen levels fall, often resulting in sweating to cool the body down.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is used, it is essential to be aware of contraindications, interactions, and specific safety guidance.

Paracetamol

  • Contraindications: Severe hepatic impairment. Use with caution in patients with mild-to-moderate liver disease, chronic alcohol dependency, or low body weight.
  • Risks: Overdose is the primary risk, leading to severe and potentially fatal hepatotoxicity. It is crucial to adhere to the correct dose and frequency for the patient's age and weight, and to account for paracetamol in any other combination products being taken.

NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen)

  • Contraindications: History of hypersensitivity to aspirin or other NSAIDs, active or previous peptic ulcer disease, severe heart failure, severe renal or hepatic failure. Avoid in the third trimester of pregnancy.
  • Cautions: Use with caution in patients with asthma (can induce bronchospasm), hypertension, renal impairment, and those taking certain medications like anticoagulants, methotrexate, or diuretics. There is a historical, debated concern about NSAID use in chickenpox potentially increasing the risk of severe skin reactions; paracetamol is preferred.
  • Interactions: Can reduce the efficacy of antihypertensives and diuretics. Increases the risk of bleeding with anticoagulants and SSRIs. Increases the risk of nephrotoxicity when used with ACE inhibitors or angiotensin-II receptor blockers.

Antibiotics

  • General Principles: The choice of antibiotic should be guided by the likely source of infection, local antimicrobial guidelines, and patient-specific factors like allergies and comorbidities. Be mindful of MHRA alerts regarding specific antibiotic classes (e.g., risks associated with fluoroquinolones).
11. Management in special populations

Children

Assessment should be guided by the NICE NG143 'traffic light' system. Fever in an infant under 3 months (especially >38°C) requires urgent assessment. Antipyretics (paracetamol, ibuprofen) should be used for distress, not just to lower temperature. Aspirin must not be used in children under 16 due to the risk of Reye's syndrome. Ensure parents are aware of correct dosing based on age and weight.

Older adults

Older adults may not mount a significant febrile response and can have a serious infection with a normal or even low temperature. They often present atypically, with confusion, falls, or functional decline (delirium). There should be a very low threshold for suspecting sepsis, using the NEWS2 score, and investigating for sources like UTI and pneumonia. Be mindful of polypharmacy and potential drug interactions, particularly the renal effects of NSAIDs.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Fever in pregnancy requires prompt assessment to rule out serious causes like pyelonephritis, listeriosis, or chorioamnionitis. Paracetamol is the first-line antipyretic and is considered safe throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding. NSAIDs like ibuprofen should be avoided, particularly in the first and third trimesters. The underlying cause of the fever must be identified and treated appropriately, considering medication safety in pregnancy.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with immunosuppression (due to disease or medication), severe COPD, heart failure, or diabetes are at high risk of severe infection and sepsis. A fever in these patients warrants a more thorough and urgent assessment. Management decisions must account for their underlying condition. For example, in a diabetic patient, infection can precipitate hyperglycaemic emergencies. For the immunocompromised, fever may be the only sign of a life-threatening opportunistic infection, and a low threshold for hospital admission is essential.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Febrile Toddler

  • Presentation: A 2-year-old boy is brought to the surgery by his mother. He has been hot and irritable for 24 hours with a temperature of 38.8°C. He has a runny nose and has been refusing some food but is drinking water.
  • Assessment: On examination, he is alert but miserable. His respiratory rate is 28/min and his chest is clear. His throat is red but there is no exudate. His eardrums are normal. He has no rash. He is well-hydrated. Based on the NICE traffic light system, he is categorised as 'amber' due to reduced activity but has no 'red' signs.
  • Diagnosis: Viral Upper Respiratory Tract Infection.
  • Management: The GP reassures the mother about the likely viral nature of the illness. They advise continuing to offer fluids and using weight-appropriate paracetamol to help with his distress. The GP provides clear written and verbal safety netting advice, explaining exactly which symptoms (e.g., becoming difficult to wake, developing a non-blanching rash, not passing urine) should prompt an urgent call back or attendance at A&E.

Case Study 2: The Confused Older Adult

  • Presentation: A practice nurse requests a GP review for an 82-year-old man during a routine diabetes clinic. He seems more muddled than his baseline (he has mild dementia). His daughter reports he has been 'off his food' for 2 days.
  • Assessment: His temperature is only 37.6°C. However, his vital signs are: RR 26/min, HR 110/min, BP 105/70 mmHg, SpO2 93% on air. He is disorientated to time and place (new change). His chest has some crackles at the right base. His NEWS2 score is 8.
  • Diagnosis & Action: This is a high-risk presentation. The combination of new confusion, raised respiratory rate, tachycardia, and borderline hypotension, despite a low-grade fever, is highly suspicious of sepsis, likely secondary to pneumonia. The GP immediately calls 999 for an emergency ambulance, stating a suspected sepsis case with a NEWS2 score of 8. A detailed handover is prepared for the ambulance crew.
