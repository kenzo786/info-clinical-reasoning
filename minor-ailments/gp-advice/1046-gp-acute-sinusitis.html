Acute Sinusitis in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute sinusitis (more accurately, rhinosinusitis) is a very common presentation in primary care, characterised by inflammation of the mucosal lining of the nose and paranasal sinuses. It is almost always preceded by a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI), like the common cold.

Patients typically present with symptoms of nasal blockage or congestion, and/or nasal discharge (which may be clear initially but can become purulent). This is often accompanied by facial pain or pressure, which may be worse on bending forward, and a reduction or loss of the sense of smell. The symptoms are generally self-limiting. While the initial viral URTI may improve within a week, the sinus symptoms can persist. It is important to advise patients that symptoms of acute sinusitis commonly last for two to three weeks before resolving.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of acute sinusitis are benign and self-limiting, it is crucial to identify features suggesting complications, which require urgent assessment and referral to secondary care (usually ENT or A&E).

  • Signs of orbital complications: Periorbital oedema, redness or cellulitis; a displaced eyeball (proptosis); double vision (diplopia); painful or restricted eye movements; or reduced visual acuity. These may indicate orbital cellulitis or abscess.
  • Signs of intracranial spread: Severe frontal headache that is out of proportion to the nasal symptoms; frontal swelling (Pott's puffy tumour); signs of meningitis such as neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered consciousness; or any new focal neurological signs. These are red flags for serious complications like meningitis, intracranial abscess, or cavernous sinus thrombosis.
  • Severe systemic illness: High fever (above 38°C) and other signs of sepsis, or if the patient appears systemically very unwell.
  • Unilateral signs: Persistent and strictly unilateral symptoms, especially with blood-stained discharge or facial swelling, can rarely indicate a tumour and warrant urgent investigation.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach helps to confirm the diagnosis, assess severity, and screen for red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Pattern: Clarify the two key components: 1) nasal blockage or congestion and/or nasal discharge (anterior or post-nasal drip), and 2) facial pain/pressure or loss of smell. Ask about duration and severity. A "double-sickening" pattern (feeling better then worse) can suggest a secondary bacterial infection.
  • Associated Symptoms: Enquire about fever, headache, cough, and dental pain (especially in the upper teeth, suggesting maxillary sinusitis).
  • Past Medical History: Ask about previous episodes of sinusitis, asthma, allergies (allergic rhinitis), and any conditions causing immunosuppression.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about severe headache, visual changes, eye swelling, and systemic symptoms.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of systemic illness. Look for any facial or periorbital swelling and erythema.
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature and other vital signs if the patient appears unwell.
  • Facial Examination: Gently palpate over the frontal and maxillary sinuses to check for tenderness.
  • Anterior Rhinoscopy (if available): May reveal inflamed, oedematous nasal mucosa and/or purulent discharge. The presence of nasal polyps may suggest an underlying chronic inflammatory cause.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

While the diagnosis is often straightforward, several other conditions can present with similar symptoms.

Common Differentials

  • Common Cold (Viral URTI): Symptoms are generally more widespread (sore throat, cough) and less focused on localised facial pain and pressure. Symptoms usually peak earlier and improve within a week.
  • Allergic Rhinitis: Often features prominent itching of the nose and eyes, sneezing, and clear, watery rhinorrhoea. It may be seasonal or perennial and is less likely to cause significant facial pain or purulent discharge.
  • Dental Abscess: Can cause localised facial pain, swelling, and fever, mimicking maxillary sinusitis. Pain is typically centred on a specific tooth, which may be tender to percussion.
  • Facial Pain Syndromes: Conditions like migraine or trigeminal neuralgia can cause facial pain but are not typically associated with nasal blockage or discharge.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Orbital Cellulitis: Presents with red flag eye signs and requires immediate hospital admission.
  • Intracranial Infection (e.g., Meningitis): Marked by severe headache, fever, neck stiffness, and altered mental state.
  • Nasal Tumour: Rare, but should be considered in cases of persistent unilateral symptoms, blood-stained discharge, or unexplained facial swelling.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is focused on symptom relief and avoiding unnecessary antibiotics, in line with antimicrobial stewardship principles. Most cases resolve without prescription treatment.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain that symptoms are normal, usually viral, and can last 2-3 weeks.
  • Analgesia: Advise simple painkillers like paracetamol or ibuprofen for facial pain and fever.
  • Nasal Saline: Suggest nasal saline irrigation (douches or sprays) which can help clear nasal passages and relieve congestion.
  • Rest and Hydration: Advise adequate fluid intake and rest as needed. There is little evidence for steam inhalation.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline): Can provide rapid relief from nasal obstruction but should not be used for more than 7 days due to the risk of rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa). [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • High-Dose Intranasal Corticosteroids (e.g., Mometasone, Fluticasone): NICE suggests considering a 14-day course if symptoms have persisted for more than 10 days. They work by reducing mucosal inflammation. [Rx info]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Phenoxymethylpenicillin, Doxycycline): Should not be offered for symptoms lasting less than 10 days unless the patient is systemically very unwell or at high risk of complications. A delayed/back-up prescription may be considered if symptoms are not improving or are worsening after 10 days. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by NICE guideline NG79 (Sinusitis (acute): antimicrobial prescribing) and the supporting CKS topic. The core principles are to support self-management and practise responsible antimicrobial stewardship.

The guidance is clear: do not offer an antibiotic prescription for acute sinusitis for patients with symptoms for 10 days or less, as the condition is likely viral and self-limiting. Evidence shows that antibiotics make little difference to the duration or severity of symptoms in most cases but do cause side effects and contribute to antibiotic resistance.

For patients with symptoms lasting 10 days or more and not improving, guidelines support considering a high-dose intranasal corticosteroid. An immediate or a back-up antibiotic prescription should only be considered if symptoms are severe, worsening significantly, or the patient is at high risk of complications. Referral is recommended for recurrent (3-4 episodes per year) or chronic sinusitis (lasting >12 weeks).

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to patient satisfaction and adherence to advice, especially when an expected antibiotic is not prescribed.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that it is normal for sinusitis to last 2-3 weeks and that it usually gets better on its own.
  • Validate and Reassure: Acknowledge that the facial pain and congestion are unpleasant, but reassure them that it is not usually a sign of a serious problem.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: Use a simple rationale, e.g., "This is caused by a virus, similar to a cold. Antibiotics only work against bacteria, so they won't help you get better any faster and might cause side effects."
  • Promote Positive Actions: Focus on what they can do, such as using painkillers regularly, trying saline nasal rinsing, and when to consider a decongestant spray.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Delayed Prescriptions: If giving a back-up prescription, provide explicit instructions on when to use it (e.g., "Only use this if your symptoms are significantly worse in 3 days, or haven't started to improve at all after 7 days").
  • Intranasal Steroids: Explain that these reduce swelling and take a few days to start working. Emphasise the need for correct technique and regular use.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a critical part of the consultation, ensuring patients know when to seek further help. Advice should be clear, specific, and ideally supported by a written leaflet.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical care (A&E or 999) if they develop any of the following red flag symptoms:

  • Swelling or redness around the eye.
  • Any change in vision, such as double vision or reduced sight.
  • A very severe headache, particularly if it's mainly at the front of the head.
  • Feeling confused, very drowsy, or sensitivity to light with a stiff neck.

Advise the patient to re-consult their GP if:

  • Symptoms are worsening rapidly or significantly.
  • Symptoms have not started to improve after 3 weeks.
  • They have frequent episodes of sinusitis.
9. Pathophysiology

Acute rhinosinusitis is an inflammatory condition of the paranasal sinuses. The process typically begins with a viral URTI. The viral infection leads to inflammation and oedema of the nasal mucosa, including the narrow drainage channels of the sinuses (the ostia).

This swelling obstructs the ostia, impairing the normal process of mucociliary clearance. Mucus becomes trapped within the sinus cavity, and the oxygen concentration within the sinus decreases. This stagnant, hypoxic environment is an ideal breeding ground for bacteria that are normally harmless commensals of the upper respiratory tract, such as *Streptococcus pneumoniae* and *Haemophilus influenzae*.

While the initial trigger is viral, a secondary bacterial infection occurs in a small percentage of cases (around 2%). The inflammation, mucosal oedema, and pressure from trapped secretions cause the characteristic symptoms of facial pain, pressure, and congestion. The production of purulent discharge is a result of the congregation of immune cells (neutrophils) fighting the infection.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, it is important to consider contraindications and specific safety points for each class of medication.

Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline)

  • Key Risk: The main issue is rhinitis medicamentosa (rebound congestion) if used for more than 7 consecutive days. Patients should be explicitly warned about this. Caution is advised in patients with hypertension or heart disease.

Intranasal Corticosteroids (e.g., Mometasone)

  • Safety: Generally very safe with minimal systemic absorption at standard doses. Local side effects like nasal irritation or dryness can occur. Patient counselling on correct administration technique is important to maximise efficacy and minimise local irritation.

Antibiotics (e.g., Phenoxymethylpenicillin, Doxycycline)

  • Contraindications: Penicillin allergy is an absolute contraindication for phenoxymethylpenicillin. Doxycycline is contraindicated in pregnancy, breastfeeding, and children under 12 years due to its effects on developing teeth and bones.
  • Interactions: Absorption of doxycycline can be significantly reduced by antacids, calcium, iron, and zinc supplements. Advise patients to take these at a different time of day.
11. Management in special populations

Children

In children, symptoms often include a persistent cough and irritability alongside nasal discharge. It can be difficult to distinguish from a simple viral URTI. Management is primarily supportive with simple analgesia. Topical decongestants are generally not recommended in children under 6. A higher index of suspicion for orbital complications is needed, and any periorbital swelling warrants urgent assessment.

Older adults

Older adults may present atypically, sometimes with confusion or delirium rather than classic symptoms. They are at higher risk of complications and may have comorbidities (e.g., cardiovascular disease) that contraindicate treatments like oral decongestants. Polypharmacy increases the risk of drug interactions, so a careful medication review is essential before prescribing.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care measures are first-line. Paracetamol and saline nasal sprays are safe. Some intranasal corticosteroids (e.g., budesonide, beclometasone) are considered safe to use. Decongestants should generally be avoided. If an antibiotic is essential, penicillins are preferred. Doxycycline is contraindicated during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are immunocompromised (e.g., on chemotherapy or high-dose steroids) are at higher risk of severe or fungal infections and warrant a lower threshold for investigation and antibiotic treatment. In patients with asthma, treating sinusitis effectively can help improve asthma control. In those with cystic fibrosis, sinusitis is often chronic and requires specialist input.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: Uncomplicated Acute Sinusitis

  • Presentation: A 34-year-old teacher presents with a 6-day history of a blocked nose, green nasal discharge, and pain over his cheeks. It followed a cold. He is otherwise well.
  • Assessment: He is afebrile. Vitals are normal. There is some tenderness over the maxillary sinuses but no swelling or erythema. No red flags are identified.
  • Diagnosis: Acute viral rhinosinusitis.
  • Management: The GP explains the likely viral cause and that antibiotics are not needed. They advise on regular paracetamol, trying a saline nasal rinse, and warns that symptoms may take 2-3 weeks to fully resolve. Clear safety netting advice is given about red flag symptoms.

Case Study 2: Persistent Symptoms & Delayed Prescription

  • Presentation: A 52-year-old woman consults on day 12 of her symptoms. She has ongoing purulent nasal discharge and significant facial pressure, which is not improving. She feels unwell but has no fever.
  • Assessment: Examination is unremarkable apart from inflamed nasal mucosa. There are no red flags. She is systemically well.
  • Diagnosis: Acute rhinosinusitis, with persistent symptoms making a bacterial component more likely.
  • Management: The GP recommends starting a high-dose intranasal corticosteroid spray for 14 days. A delayed prescription for doxycycline is issued with clear instructions to only start it if her symptoms have not begun to improve in another 3-4 days, or if she starts to feel systemically unwell.

Case Study 3: A Red Flag Presentation

  • Presentation: A parent calls about their 8-year-old child who has had a "bad cold" for a week. Today, the child has woken up with a swollen, red, and painful left eyelid and a temperature of 38.5°C.
  • Assessment: The history alone is highly suggestive of a serious complication.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP suspects orbital cellulitis secondary to sinusitis. They advise the parent to take the child immediately to the nearest hospital Emergency Department for urgent assessment and treatment, explaining the seriousness of the situation clearly but calmly.
