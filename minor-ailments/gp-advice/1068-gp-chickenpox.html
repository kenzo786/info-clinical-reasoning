Chickenpox in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Chickenpox (varicella) is a common, highly infectious childhood illness caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). It typically presents in primary care with a characteristic vesicular rash. The illness often starts with a prodrome of fever, malaise, and loss of appetite for 1-2 days before the rash appears.

The rash classically begins on the head, chest, or back before spreading to the rest of the body. It evolves in stages: starting as small red macules, progressing to papules, then fluid-filled vesicles (blisters), which then become pustular before crusting over. A key diagnostic feature is the presence of lesions in different stages of development simultaneously ('cropping'). The main symptom is intense itching (pruritus). Most healthy children recover fully within one to two weeks from onset.

The incubation period is typically 10 to 21 days after exposure. An individual is infectious from about 48 hours before the rash appears until all vesicles have crusted over, which usually takes about 5-7 days from the onset of the rash. The condition is generally benign in children but can be more severe in adults, infants, and immunocompromised individuals.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While typically self-limiting in children, it is crucial to identify features suggesting severe complications, which warrant urgent review or hospital admission.

  • Neurological symptoms: Signs such as new-onset drowsiness, confusion, seizures, ataxia (unsteadiness), or severe headache may indicate varicella encephalitis or meningitis. This is a medical emergency.
  • Respiratory symptoms: Significant cough, shortness of breath, or chest pain, particularly in adults, smokers, or immunocompromised individuals, may suggest varicella pneumonitis, which can be severe.
  • Severe skin signs: Widespread, painful, or haemorrhagic (bleeding into vesicles) rash. Spreading erythema, warmth, and tenderness around lesions suggests severe secondary bacterial infection, such as cellulitis or necrotising fasciitis.
  • Systemic signs of severe illness: High, persistent fever, rigors, or signs of dehydration (e.g., reduced urine output). These may indicate severe secondary infection or sepsis.
  • At-risk populations with chickenpox:
    • Immunocompromised patients: (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids). They are at risk of severe, disseminated disease and require urgent specialist assessment.
    • Pregnant people: Non-immune pregnant women who develop chickenpox are at risk of severe maternal illness and require urgent assessment. There is also a risk to the fetus (congenital varicella syndrome) or newborn.
    • Neonates (infants <28 days old): Can develop severe, life-threatening illness, particularly if the mother develops the rash between 5 days before and 2 days after birth.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach helps confirm the diagnosis and screen for complications.

History Taking

  • Rash Characteristics: Confirm the onset, progression (macule to vesicle to crust), and distribution of the rash. Ask about the presence of spots in different stages at the same time.
  • Associated Symptoms: Enquire about fever, degree of itch, and general wellbeing (malaise, appetite).
  • Exposure History: Ascertain any recent contact with a known case of chickenpox or shingles.
  • Past Medical History: Specifically ask about immunosuppression (disease or medication), history of asthma or significant lung disease, and for women, the possibility of pregnancy. Check immunisation history, although VZV vaccine is not part of the routine UK schedule.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about shortness of breath, chest pain, headache, drowsiness, confusion, or signs of secondary bacterial infection (e.g., a spot becoming very red, hot, swollen, and painful).

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess the patient's overall state. Are they alert and well, or lethargic, irritable, or dehydrated?
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature and assess for tachycardia or tachypnoea. Oxygen saturations should be checked if any respiratory symptoms are present.
  • Skin Examination: Inspect the entire skin surface, including scalp and mucous membranes. Note the distribution and character of the rash, looking for the classic 'crops' of lesions at different stages. Check for signs of secondary bacterial infection like spreading erythema or cellulitis.
  • Focused Examination: Perform a chest auscultation if there is a cough or breathlessness. A neurological assessment is required if there are concerns about headache, drowsiness or ataxia.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The "crops" of lesions in different stages are highly characteristic of chickenpox, but other diagnoses should be considered.

Common Differentials

  • Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease: Caused by a Coxsackie virus. Vesicles are typically located on the palms, soles, and in/around the mouth. Patients are usually less systemically unwell.
  • Impetigo: A bacterial skin infection (Staph/Strep). Presents as vesicles or pustules that rupture to form characteristic honey-coloured crusts. It is typically localised and lacks a widespread, centrally-spreading distribution.
  • Insect bites: Can be multiple and itchy, but usually lack the typical progression to vesicles and crusts. There is often a central punctum and no prodromal fever.
  • Scabies: Presents with an intensely itchy papular rash, often with visible burrows in classic sites (e.g., finger webs, wrists). Itch is typically worse at night. A vesicular component can occur but the overall picture is different.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Disseminated Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): Can cause a widespread vesicular eruption (eczema herpeticum if superimposed on eczema) and can be mistaken for chickenpox, especially in immunocompromised individuals. Requires urgent antiviral treatment.
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN): A severe drug reaction. May start with vesicles but rapidly progresses to widespread epidermal detachment and severe mucosal involvement. This is a medical emergency.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is primarily supportive, focusing on symptom relief and preventing complications. Antivirals are reserved for specific at-risk groups.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Hydration and Nutrition: Encourage adequate fluid intake. Offer soft, bland foods if the mouth is sore.
  • Itch Relief: Keep the patient cool as heat and sweat worsen itching. Pat skin dry instead of rubbing after bathing. Keep fingernails short and clean to minimise skin damage from scratching.
  • Infection Control: Advise the patient to stay off school or work until all vesicles have crusted over. They should avoid contact with pregnant people, newborns, and anyone who is immunocompromised.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol can be used to manage fever and pain. [Rx info]
  • Topical Preparations: Calamine lotion or other cooling gels can be applied to soothe the skin. Their clinical efficacy is debated but they are widely used. [Rx info]
  • Sedating Antihistamines: Oral chlorphenamine may be considered to relieve itching, particularly if it is disrupting sleep. Warn about sedative effects. [Rx info]
  • Withdrawn / Contraindicated Advice: Patients should be explicitly advised to AVOID non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) like ibuprofen, due to a link with an increased risk of severe necrotising soft-tissue infections.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir): Not recommended for otherwise healthy children. Should be considered for patients at increased risk of severe infection, such as adults and adolescents (>14 years), and those with significant comorbidities. Treatment is most effective if started within 24 hours of rash onset. [Rx info]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin): Only indicated for the treatment of suspected secondary bacterial skin infections, such as cellulitis or infected eczema. Not to be used prophylactically. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management is guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) for Chickenpox. The guidance emphasises that chickenpox is usually a self-limiting illness in children that does not require specific medical treatment.

Key NICE recommendations focus on providing supportive care advice. This includes managing fever with paracetamol and managing itch with cooling lotions and, if necessary, a sedating antihistamine like chlorphenamine. The guidance strongly advises against the use of ibuprofen due to the associated risk of severe skin and soft tissue reactions.

Antiviral treatment (e.g., oral aciclovir) is not recommended for otherwise healthy children. However, it should be considered for individuals at risk of a more severe course, including adults and adolescents, and should be initiated within 24 hours of rash onset for maximum benefit. Urgent admission and specialist advice are recommended for at-risk groups such as immunocompromised patients, pregnant women, and neonates who develop the infection.

The UK immunisation programme does not routinely include vaccination against varicella for all children. It is offered selectively to individuals who are susceptible (non-immune) and are in close contact with someone who is particularly vulnerable to chickenpox (e.g., immunocompromised patients).

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to reassuring parents and patients, ensuring appropriate self-care, and explaining the rationale for management decisions.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that chickenpox is a common viral illness that the body's immune system will clear on its own. Explain the timeline: a few days of feeling unwell, followed by a rash that will blister and then scab over within about a week.
  • Explain Infectivity: Advise that the person is infectious from two days before the rash appears until every spot has crusted over. Emphasise the importance of avoiding school/work and contact with high-risk individuals during this time.
  • Itch and Fever Management: Provide positive, actionable advice on managing symptoms (e.g., "Keep his nails short and clean to stop deep scratching," "You can use paracetamol for the fever").
  • The Ibuprofen Warning: Clearly and directly explain why ibuprofen should be avoided. For example: "It is very important not to give ibuprofen for chickenpox, as there is evidence it can cause serious bacterial skin infections in this situation. Stick to paracetamol if needed for fever."

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Antivirals: If prescribing aciclovir, explain why it is being used (e.g., "Because you are an adult, chickenpox can be more severe, and this medicine can help reduce the severity if started early"). Counsel on the importance of completing the course and staying well-hydrated.
  • Antibiotics: If prescribing for a secondary infection, explain exactly what it is for. For example: "This antibiotic is not for the chickenpox virus itself, but to treat the bacterial infection in this specific spot which has become red and painful."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients or their carers know when to seek further medical help. Advice should be specific and clear.

Advise the patient or their parent/carer to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection: If any of the spots become very painful or if the skin around them becomes red, hot, swollen, and the redness is spreading.
  • Signs of pneumonia: Developing a cough, difficulty breathing, or chest pain.
  • Signs of encephalitis: Becoming drowsy, confused, or difficult to wake. Developing a severe headache, vomiting, or becoming unsteady on their feet.
  • Dehydration: The person is not drinking, passing much less urine than normal, or has a dry mouth and seems unwell.
  • General worsening: If the person seems to be getting significantly more unwell in any way, or the fever is very high and not responding to paracetamol.
  • Uncertainty: If they have any other symptom that they are concerned about.

For most children, no routine follow-up is required. For adults on antiviral treatment or those with secondary infections being managed in the community, a review may be appropriate to ensure a good response to treatment.

9. Pathophysiology

Chickenpox is the clinical manifestation of a primary infection with the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a DNA virus from the human herpesvirus family. Transmission occurs primarily via inhalation of airborne respiratory droplets from an infected individual or, less commonly, by direct contact with the fluid from skin vesicles.

The virus initially replicates in the nasopharynx and regional lymph nodes. This is followed by a primary viraemia, where the virus spreads via the bloodstream to organs of the reticuloendothelial system, such as the liver and spleen, for further replication. A second, more significant viraemia then occurs approximately 10-21 days after initial infection. During this secondary viraemic phase, the virus disseminates throughout the body and seeds the skin.

Viral replication within capillary endothelial cells and the epidermis leads to cellular degeneration and the formation of characteristic vesicles. The fluid within these vesicles is rich in infectious virions. The host immune response, involving both cellular and humoral immunity, is crucial for controlling the infection and leads to the crusting of lesions and recovery. Following the primary infection, VZV establishes lifelong latency in the dorsal root ganglia of the sensory nervous system. Reactivation of this latent virus later in life causes herpes zoster (shingles).

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is used, awareness of contraindications, interactions, and specific safety guidance is essential.

Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir)

  • Efficacy: Most effective when started within 24 hours of rash onset. It can reduce symptom duration and severity but does not 'cure' the infection.
  • Dosing: Aciclovir requires dose adjustment in patients with renal impairment. It is crucial to ensure patients maintain good hydration to prevent crystalluria and renal damage.
  • Side Effects: Generally well tolerated but can cause gastrointestinal upset (nausea, diarrhoea) and headache.

NSAIDs (Ibuprofen)

  • MHRA/NICE Guidance: There is an association between the use of NSAIDs in chickenpox and an increased risk of severe necrotising soft tissue infections. For this reason, both NICE and the MHRA advise that paracetamol should be used in preference to NSAIDs for pain and fever in patients with chickenpox. This is a critical piece of prescribing advice.

Sedating Antihistamines (e.g., Chlorphenamine)

  • Side Effects: The primary side effect is drowsiness. Patients/carers must be warned about this, and adults should be advised not to drive or operate machinery if affected. Effects can be potentiated by alcohol or other CNS depressants.
  • Cautions: Use with caution in older adults due to increased risk of confusion, falls, and anticholinergic effects.
11. Management in special populations

Children

This is the most commonly affected group. The illness is usually mild and self-limiting. Management is supportive, focusing on symptom relief (paracetamol, cooling lotions, chlorphenamine for itch). The key message is the avoidance of ibuprofen. School or nursery exclusion is required until all spots have crusted over.

Older adults

Chickenpox is less common but more severe in adults. They have a higher risk of complications, particularly varicella pneumonitis (especially in smokers). There should be a low threshold for prescribing oral aciclovir within 24 hours of rash onset to reduce the severity and duration of the illness. Close safety netting for respiratory symptoms is essential.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

A non-immune pregnant woman exposed to or developing chickenpox requires urgent specialist (obstetric/virology) advice. There is a risk of severe maternal illness and complications for the fetus (Congenital Varicella Syndrome) or newborn (neonatal varicella). Management may involve varicella-zoster immunoglobulin (VZIG) post-exposure or treatment with aciclovir. Most common OTC remedies are safe, but paracetamol is the analgesic of choice.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are immunosuppressed (e.g., due to chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, HIV) are at high risk of severe, disseminated, and potentially fatal chickenpox. They require immediate referral for specialist assessment and will almost always be treated with antiviral therapy (often intravenous). Post-exposure prophylaxis with VZIG may also be indicated following a significant contact.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Uncomplicated Child

  • Presentation: A parent calls about their 4-year-old son who developed a fever yesterday and today has a widespread, itchy, spotty rash. The spots are on his chest and back and some are like little blisters.
  • Assessment: Based on the description of a prodromal fever and vesicular rash with 'cropping' (implied by the caller's description), a diagnosis of chickenpox is made. The child is drinking well and otherwise behaving normally.
  • Management: The GP reassures the parent that this is typical chickenpox. They advise on self-care (fluids, keeping cool) and recommend paracetamol for fever and calamine lotion for itch. They explicitly advise against using ibuprofen. Clear safety netting advice is given regarding signs of complications, and advice on school exclusion is provided.

Case Study 2: The Adult Patient

  • Presentation: A 28-year-old man presents via e-consultation with a photo of a vesicular rash on his torso, which appeared that morning. He feels feverish, exhausted, and has a headache. He has never had chickenpox before.
  • Assessment: The rash is characteristic of early chickenpox. Given his age, he is at higher risk of a severe illness. He is a non-smoker with no other medical history.
  • Management: The GP arranges a prescription for a 7-day course of oral aciclovir, stressing the importance of starting it immediately (as it is within 24 hours of onset) and staying well hydrated. He is given strict safety netting advice, particularly regarding chest pain or shortness of breath, and advised to self-isolate.

Case Study 3: Secondary Bacterial Infection

  • Presentation: A 7-year-old girl is brought to the surgery. She had chickenpox for 5 days and seemed to be recovering. However, over the past 24 hours, one spot on her shin has become very red, swollen, hot, and is increasingly painful. She now has a fever again.
  • Assessment: On examination, there are healing, crusted chickenpox lesions over her body. The lesion on her left shin shows a 4cm diameter area of erythema, warmth, and tenderness consistent with cellulitis.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP diagnoses chickenpox with a secondary bacterial skin infection. A prescription for oral flucloxacillin is issued. The parent is counselled on how to administer it and given clear instructions to seek urgent review if the redness spreads rapidly, or if she becomes more systemically unwell.
