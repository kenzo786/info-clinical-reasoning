Headache in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Headache is an extremely common reason for consultation in primary care. Most headaches are primary, meaning they are not caused by an underlying disease. The three most common types are tension-type headache, migraine, and cluster headache. A fourth important entity, highly prevalent in primary care, is medication-overuse headache.

Patients with tension-type headache typically describe a bilateral, pressing or tightening sensation of mild to moderate intensity, which is not aggravated by routine physical activity. Migraine classically presents as a unilateral, pulsating, moderate to severe headache aggravated by activity, often accompanied by nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia. An aura may precede the headache. Cluster headaches are severe, strictly unilateral attacks of pain around the eye or temple, associated with autonomic features on the same side (e.g., red eye, nasal congestion, eyelid swelling). The attacks are relatively short but occur in bouts.

The key role of the clinician is to differentiate between primary headache types, identify the small but important proportion of patients with serious secondary causes, and to provide advice on management, including the significant risk of medication-overuse headache.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

Identifying features of a serious secondary headache is a critical function in primary care. The presence of any red flag warrants further investigation, urgent review, or immediate emergency department referral.

  • Thunderclap headache: A sudden-onset, severe headache reaching maximum intensity within 5 minutes. This is a red flag for subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) and requires immediate emergency admission.
  • Neurological signs or symptoms: New onset of focal neurological deficit (e.g., limb weakness, speech disturbance), altered consciousness, confusion, or seizures suggests an intracranial lesion (e.g., stroke, tumour, bleed) or infection.
  • Features of meningitis or encephalitis: Headache accompanied by fever, neck stiffness, photophobia, and/or a non-blanching rash. This requires urgent assessment for central nervous system infection.
  • Features of Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): In a patient over 50, a new-onset or different type of headache, especially with temporal artery tenderness, jaw claudication, or visual disturbance (including transient visual loss). This requires urgent assessment and management to prevent permanent vision loss.
  • Features of raised intracranial pressure: Headache that is worse on waking, coughing, or straining. It may be associated with vomiting or visual changes (e.g., papilloedema on fundoscopy). This can indicate a space-occupying lesion.
  • Positional headache: A headache that significantly worsens on standing up and improves on lying down may suggest a CSF leak.
  • Trauma: A new headache following a significant head injury.
  • Immunocompromised or cancer patient: A new-onset headache in a patient with cancer (especially lung, breast, or melanoma) or immunosuppression raises suspicion of metastases or opportunistic infection.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is essential to differentiate headache types and screen for red flags.

History Taking

  • Headache Characteristics (SOCRATES): Site (unilateral/bilateral), Onset (sudden/gradual), Character (pulsating/tightening), Radiation, Associated symptoms (nausea, aura, autonomic features), Timing (duration, frequency, pattern), Exacerbating/relieving factors, Severity.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about thunderclap onset, fever, neck stiffness, neurological symptoms, visual changes, jaw claudication, and features of raised intracranial pressure.
  • Medication History: Crucially, ask about the type, frequency, and quantity of all acute headache treatments (both OTC and prescribed) to assess for medication overuse. Ask about prophylactic treatments.
  • Past Medical and Family History: Enquire about previous headache diagnoses, history of migraine, cancer, immunosuppression, or head trauma. A family history of migraine is common.
  • Social and Lifestyle Factors: Assess the impact on daily life. Enquire about sleep, stress, diet (caffeine intake), and hydration.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of systemic illness or distress.
  • Vital Signs: Measure blood pressure. A significantly raised BP can be a cause of headache (malignant hypertension). Fever may suggest infection.
  • Neurological Examination: Perform a focused neurological exam, including assessment of cranial nerves (especially fundoscopy to check for papilloedema), power, reflexes, and coordination.
  • Head and Neck Examination: Palpate the temporal arteries for tenderness or thickening (in over 50s). Assess for neck stiffness. Check the temporomandibular joint.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The vast majority of headaches seen in primary care are primary headaches. The assessment aims to confidently diagnose the primary headache type while excluding secondary causes.

Common Primary Headaches

  • Tension-Type Headache: Most common. Bilateral, non-pulsating, 'tight band' feeling. Not aggravated by activity. No nausea or vomiting.
  • Migraine: Can be with or without aura. Typically unilateral, pulsating, and severe, leading to avoidance of activity. Associated with nausea and/or photophobia and phonophobia.
  • Cluster Headache: Rare but severe. Strictly unilateral pain around the eye/temple, lasting 15-180 minutes, with ipsilateral autonomic features. Patients are often agitated during an attack.
  • Medication-Overuse Headache (MOH): A chronic daily or near-daily headache (≥15 days/month) in a patient with a pre-existing headache disorder, caused by regular overuse of acute headache medication for >3 months. The headache often improves after the overused medication is stopped.

Serious Secondary Causes to Exclude

  • Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH): Differentiated by its 'thunderclap' onset.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis: Differentiated by fever, rash, neck stiffness, and altered mental state.
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): Suspected in patients >50 with new headache, jaw claudication, and tender temporal arteries. An ESR/CRP is a key investigation.
  • Space-Occupying Lesion/Raised ICP: Suggested by progressive headache, neurological signs, seizures, or headache pattern that is worse on waking/straining.
  • Sinusitis: Often presents with facial pain, nasal blockage, and purulent discharge. The headache is typically over the frontal or maxillary areas.
5. Management options in primary care

Management depends on the headache type and aims to treat acute attacks and, if necessary, reduce their frequency with prophylaxis. Addressing medication overuse is a key priority.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Headache Diary: Encourage patients to keep a diary to track symptoms, frequency, and potential triggers (e.g., stress, lack of sleep, dehydration, certain foods).
  • Lifestyle Modification: Advise on maintaining regular sleep patterns, managing stress, staying hydrated, and eating regular meals.
  • Trigger Avoidance: If specific triggers are identified from the diary, advise on how to manage them.
  • Physical Therapies: For some patients with tension-type headache or cervicogenic headache, physiotherapy or acupuncture may be beneficial.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Simple Analgesia: Paracetamol or an NSAID like ibuprofen are first-line for acute tension-type headache and migraine. [Rx info]
  • Combination Analgesics: Products containing paracetamol or an NSAID with caffeine can be effective but carry a higher risk of causing medication-overuse headache.
  • Important Counselling: Advise patients to take acute medication as soon as an attack starts but to limit use to no more than 2 days per week (a maximum of 10-15 days per month depending on the drug) to prevent MOH.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Triptans (e.g., Sumatriptan): Serotonin (5-HT1B/1D) receptor agonists. First-line for acute migraine attacks not responding to simple analgesia. Not effective for tension-type headache. [Rx info]
  • Prophylactic Agents: Considered for patients whose headaches are frequent or significantly impacting their quality of life. The choice depends on headache type and patient comorbidities.
    • For migraine: Propranolol (a non-selective beta-blocker) or amitriptyline (a tricyclic antidepressant) are common first-line options. [Rx info]
    • For tension-type headache: Amitriptyline is considered for chronic tension-type headache. [Rx info]
  • Codeine/Opioids: Should be avoided for headache management due to high risk of dependence and medication-overuse headache, with limited efficacy.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The primary UK guideline is NICE guideline CG150: 'Headaches in over 12s: diagnosis and management' (2012, last updated 2021) and the associated NICE CKS topic.

The core principles of the NICE guideline are:

  • Accurate Diagnosis: Use clinical history as the primary tool to differentiate between tension-type, migraine, and cluster headaches, and to identify possible secondary causes. Neuroimaging is not recommended for patients with a typical primary headache presentation and no red flags.
  • Information and Support: Provide patients with information about their specific headache type, trigger factors, and options for management.
  • Acute Treatment: Offer combination therapy for acute migraine (e.g., an oral triptan with an NSAID or paracetamol). Treat at the onset of an attack.
  • Medication Overuse: Be vigilant for medication-overuse headache (MOH). Explain the risk to all patients taking acute treatments. For patients with established MOH, the primary management is abrupt withdrawal of the overused medication (with support).
  • Prophylaxis: Offer prophylactic treatment if acute attacks are frequent (e.g., >2 per month) or disabling. Topiramate and amitriptyline are recommended options based on efficacy and side-effect profile. Propranolol is also a key option. CGRP antagonists are available via specialist advice.
  • Referral: Refer to specialists if the diagnosis is uncertain, if a serious secondary cause is suspected, or if primary headaches are refractory to standard treatments.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to successful headache management, particularly for chronic conditions like migraine.

Counselling for Diagnosis and Self-Care

  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge the significant impact headaches can have on a person's life, work, and family.
  • Explain the Diagnosis: Clearly explain the type of headache (e.g., "This sounds like migraine, which is a neurological condition, not 'just a bad headache'"). Reassure them that while there is no cure, there are effective ways to manage it.
  • Introduce the Headache Diary: Frame it as a powerful tool to help both patient and clinician understand the headache pattern and identify triggers.
  • Empower with Lifestyle Advice: Focus on positive actions they can take around sleep, diet, and stress management.

Counselling on Medications

  • Acute Treatment Strategy: Advise patients to take their acute medication right at the start of an attack for best effect. Explain the "2 days a week" rule clearly to prevent medication overuse. For example, "To prevent these pills from causing more headaches, you shouldn't take them on more than ten days in any given month."
  • Prophylaxis Explained: If starting a preventative medicine, explain that it doesn't stop attacks completely but aims to reduce their frequency and severity. Counsel that it may take several weeks to see a benefit and that finding the right one can involve some trial and error.
  • Side Effects: Briefly counsel on common side effects of any prescribed medication (e.g., drowsiness with amitriptyline, tiredness with propranolol).
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is essential for any headache consultation. Patients need to know when to seek help if their symptoms change or worsen. This advice should be specific and clear.

Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • A sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache, unlike anything they have experienced before.
  • The headache becomes progressively worse over a period of days or weeks.
  • They develop a fever, rash, or stiff neck with the headache.
  • They experience any new weakness in their arms or legs, problems with speech or balance, or confusion.
  • They have a seizure or blackout.
  • The headache starts after a head injury.
  • In patients over 50, if they develop jaw pain on chewing or problems with their vision.
  • The headache changes significantly in character or stops responding to previously effective treatments.

For patients starting a new prophylactic medication, a planned follow-up in 4-6 weeks is appropriate to assess efficacy and tolerability. Patients being managed for medication-overuse headache require close support and follow-up during the withdrawal phase.

9. Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology varies between primary headache types.

Tension-Type Headache (TTH): The exact mechanism is not fully understood but is thought to involve both peripheral and central factors. Peripherally, increased pericranial muscle tension and tenderness (myofascial nociception) play a role, particularly in episodic TTH. In chronic TTH, central mechanisms are more prominent. This involves sensitisation of central pain pathways in the trigeminal nucleus and higher centres in the brainstem and thalamus, leading to a reduced threshold for pain perception.

Migraine: This is a complex neurovascular disorder. The current leading theory involves neuronal dysfunction rather than a primary vascular event. An external or internal trigger activates brainstem nuclei, leading to sensitisation of the trigeminovascular system. This results in the release of vasoactive neuropeptides, including Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide (CGRP), which cause painful inflammation and vasodilation of cranial blood vessels. The migraine aura is explained by cortical spreading depression—a slow wave of neuronal and glial depolarisation that spreads across the cerebral cortex, followed by a period of suppressed activity.

Cluster Headache: The pathophysiology is thought to involve the hypothalamus (explaining the circadian rhythm of attacks) and the trigemino-autonomic reflex. Activation of the posterior hypothalamus triggers the trigeminal system, causing the severe pain, and also the parasympathetic nervous system via the sphenopalatine ganglion, causing the characteristic autonomic symptoms (lacrimation, nasal congestion, etc.).

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing for headache, awareness of contraindications, key interactions, and safety alerts is vital.

NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen, Naproxen)

  • Contraindications: Active peptic ulceration or history of GI bleed. Use with caution in patients with asthma, renal impairment, heart failure, and uncontrolled hypertension. Avoid in the third trimester of pregnancy.
  • Interactions: Increased risk of GI bleeding with SSRIs and anticoagulants. Can reduce the effectiveness of antihypertensive medication.

Triptans (e.g., Sumatriptan)

  • Contraindications: Ischaemic heart disease, coronary vasospasm (including Prinzmetal's angina), peripheral vascular disease, previous stroke or TIA, uncontrolled hypertension.
  • Interactions: Risk of serotonin syndrome if used concurrently with SSRIs, SNRIs, or MAOIs, although the absolute risk is considered low. Should not be used within 24 hours of another triptan or an ergotamine-containing preparation.

Prophylactics

  • Propranolol: Contraindicated in asthma and patients with bradycardia or heart block. Use with caution in diabetes as it can mask hypoglycaemic symptoms.
  • Amitriptyline: Contraindicated following acute myocardial infarction and in arrhythmia. Use with caution in older adults (risk of anticholinergic effects, sedation, falls), and in patients with prostatic hypertrophy or epilepsy.
  • Sodium Valproate: No longer recommended for migraine prophylaxis in women of childbearing potential unless they are on the Pregnancy Prevention Programme, due to the high risk of birth defects and neurodevelopmental disorders (MHRA safety alert).
11. Management in special populations

Children

Migraine is common in children but may present differently, sometimes with cyclical vomiting or abdominal migraine as the main feature. Headaches can be shorter-lived. Tension-type headaches are also common. Red flags (e.g., behavioural change, deteriorating school performance, abnormal neurology) must be taken very seriously and have a lower threshold for investigation. Ibuprofen and paracetamol are first-line acute treatments. Triptans may be used from age 12 (or younger under specialist advice). Lifestyle advice and reassurance are key.

Older adults

Any new-onset headache in a person over 50 requires careful assessment to exclude a secondary cause. Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) is a primary concern and must be excluded. There should be a lower threshold for investigation (e.g., ESR/CRP) and referral. Prescribing requires caution due to polypharmacy and comorbidities. NSAIDs carry higher risks of GI and renal side effects. Amitriptyline increases the risk of falls and confusion.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Management focuses on non-pharmacological methods first. For acute treatment, paracetamol is considered safe throughout pregnancy. Ibuprofen should be avoided in the third trimester. Sumatriptan may be considered for severe migraine where benefits outweigh risks, but specialist advice is often sought. Prophylactic medications like amitriptyline and propranolol should generally be avoided unless advised by a specialist. Codeine-containing products should be avoided. The UK Teratology Information Service (UKTIS) provides detailed safety information.

People with significant comorbidities

Treatment choices must be tailored. For a patient with asthma, beta-blockers like propranolol are contraindicated for migraine prophylaxis. For a patient with heart disease, triptans and NSAIDs may be contraindicated. For patients with a history of depression, an SSRI/SNRI for their mood may interact with a triptan, and a sedating prophylactic like amitriptyline could be beneficial or problematic depending on the context. Always check for contraindications and interactions based on the patient's full medical history.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: Classic Migraine

  • Presentation: A 34-year-old female presents with a 10-year history of episodic headaches. She describes a pulsating, left-sided headache lasting about a day, making her want to lie down in a dark, quiet room. She often feels nauseous. The headaches occur 1-2 times a month.
  • Assessment: Her history is typical for migraine without aura. Examination, including BP and a brief neurological check, is normal. There are no red flags. She is using paracetamol with limited effect.
  • Management: The GP confirms the diagnosis of migraine. They advise on keeping a simple diary to look for triggers. They prescribe sumatriptan to be taken at the onset of a headache, alongside ibuprofen, and explain how to use it. They also provide clear advice on limiting acute medication use to a maximum of 10 days per month to avoid medication-overuse headache. Safety netting advice is given.

Case Study 2: Medication-Overuse Headache

  • Presentation: A 52-year-old male with a background of tension-type headaches presents with a "constant, daily muzzy headache" for the last 6 months. It's there when he wakes up. He finds himself taking co-codamol 30/500, which he buys over the counter, at least 4-5 times a day to "keep it at bay".
  • Assessment: The history is highly suggestive of medication-overuse headache (MOH) secondary to opiate overuse, on a background of chronic tension-type headache. Examination is unremarkable.
  • Management: The GP explains the concept of MOH, stating that the codeine is now likely the cause of the chronic daily headache. They agree on a plan for abrupt withdrawal of all co-codamol, warning the patient that their headache may worsen for 1-2 weeks before improving. They offer a prophylactic medication (amitriptyline) to start once the codeine is stopped, to manage the underlying primary headache. Close follow-up is arranged.

Case Study 3: Suspected Giant Cell Arteritis

  • Presentation: A 72-year-old female presents with a 2-week history of a new type of headache, located over her right temple. The area is tender to touch. For the last few days, her jaw has been aching when she eats. This morning, she had a 5-minute episode where the vision in her right eye 'greyed out'.
  • Assessment: This presentation has multiple red flags for Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): age >50, new-onset headache, temporal tenderness, jaw claudication, and amaurosis fugax (transient visual loss).
  • Diagnosis & Action: The GP suspects GCA and recognises it as a medical emergency. They contact the on-call medical or ophthalmology team at the local hospital to arrange for an immediate assessment. While awaiting transfer, they consider giving the first dose of high-dose oral prednisolone as per local guidelines. They do not wait for blood test results (ESR/CRP) before making the urgent referral.
