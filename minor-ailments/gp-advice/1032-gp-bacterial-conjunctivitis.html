Bacterial Conjunctivitis in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Bacterial conjunctivitis is a common reason for consultation in primary care, characterised by inflammation of the conjunctiva. It typically presents with acute onset of a red eye, which is often unilateral initially but frequently spreads to the other eye within a day or two. The hallmark symptom is a mucopurulent discharge, causing the eyelids to feel sticky or be stuck together, especially upon waking.

Patients usually complain of a gritty or burning sensation but should not have significant pain or photophobia. Vision is not typically affected, though it may be momentarily blurred by discharge. The condition is usually self-limiting, with most cases resolving within 5 to 7 days without any specific medical intervention. Common causative organisms include Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, and Haemophilus influenzae.

The primary role of the clinician is to differentiate this benign condition from more serious causes of a red eye, provide advice on self-care, and manage patient expectations regarding the natural course of the illness, while promoting antimicrobial stewardship.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases are benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying cause that requires urgent same-day assessment by an ophthalmologist or specialist optometrist.

  • Marked eye pain or photophobia: Suggests inflammation of deeper structures, such as in keratitis, scleritis, or anterior uveitis. Simple conjunctivitis causes irritation or grittiness, not true pain.
  • Significant reduction in visual acuity: Any vision loss that cannot be cleared by blinking away discharge is a major red flag for conditions like microbial keratitis, uveitis, or acute glaucoma.
  • Ciliary flush: A pattern of deep, violaceous redness concentrated in a ring around the cornea, indicating inflammation of the cornea or deeper structures, rather than the generalised injection of simple conjunctivitis.
  • Corneal issues: Any haziness, opacity, or a white spot on the cornea may indicate an ulcer or keratitis. Fluorescein staining in practice can reveal epithelial defects.
  • Pupil abnormalities: A distorted, irregular, fixed, or unreactive pupil is a sign of serious pathology such as anterior uveitis or acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Contact lens wear: A contact lens wearer with a painful red eye must be urgently referred to rule out microbial keratitis, a potentially sight-threatening condition.
  • Vesicular rash consistent with shingles: A rash in the distribution of the ophthalmic branch of the trigeminal nerve suggests Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus.
  • Severe purulent discharge in a neonate (within 28 days of birth): This is an ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate hospital admission to rule out sight-threatening gonococcal or chlamydial conjunctivitis.
  • Proptosis or painful/restricted eye movements: May suggest orbital cellulitis, a medical emergency.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is essential to distinguish bacterial conjunctivitis from more serious conditions.

History Taking

  • Symptom characteristics: Clarify the nature of the discomfort (gritty vs. true pain). Ask about discharge (sticky, purulent vs. watery), and itching (suggests allergy). Note the onset and whether it started in one or both eyes.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about severe pain, light sensitivity (photophobia), and any change in vision.
  • Contact Lens Use: This is a critical question. Establish the type of lenses and hygiene practices. Advise immediate cessation of wear.
  • Past Ocular History: Previous episodes, eye surgery, trauma, or underlying conditions like glaucoma or dry eye syndrome.
  • General Medical History: Recent upper respiratory tract infection (suggests viral cause), atopy (suggests allergic), or immunosuppression.

Clinical Examination

  • Visual Acuity: Measure for each eye using a Snellen chart. Vision should be normal in simple conjunctivitis. This is the most important clinical test to perform.
  • Inspection:
    • Observe the pattern of redness (generalised suggests conjunctivitis; ciliary flush suggests a serious cause).
    • Examine the discharge (purulent in bacterial; watery in viral/allergic).
    • Check the cornea for clarity and lustre. Look for any opacities.
    • Assess the pupils for size, shape, and reaction to light. They should be equal and reactive.
  • Palpation: Gently check for pre-auricular lymph nodes (common in viral conjunctivitis, rare in bacterial).
  • Eyelid Eversion: Consider if a foreign body is suspected, though not routine for a typical conjunctivitis presentation.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for a red eye is broad, but a focused history and examination can usually distinguish benign from serious causes.

Common Benign Causes

  • Viral Conjunctivitis: Often caused by adenovirus. Tends to cause a more watery discharge, may follow a recent cold, and is often associated with a palpable pre-auricular lymph node. It is highly contagious.
  • Allergic Conjunctivitis: Itching is the dominant symptom. Usually bilateral and associated with a watery discharge, chemosis (conjunctival swelling), and a personal or family history of atopy (asthma, eczema, hay fever).
  • Blepharitis: Inflammation of the eyelid margins, causing chronic irritation, grittiness, and crusting. The conjunctiva may be secondarily inflamed.
  • Dry Eye Syndrome: Causes intermittent redness and a gritty, burning sensation, often worse towards the end of the day.

Serious Differentials to Exclude (Red Flag Conditions)

  • Microbial Keratitis: Infection of the cornea. Presents with pain, photophobia, reduced vision, and often a visible corneal opacity. A key differential in contact lens wearers.
  • Anterior Uveitis (Iritis): Inflammation inside the eye. Characterised by significant pain, photophobia, ciliary flush, and a small or irregular pupil.
  • Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma: A rapid rise in intraocular pressure. Causes severe pain, blurred vision with halos, a semi-dilated pupil, and often systemic symptoms like headache and vomiting.
  • Scleritis: Severe, deep, boring eye pain which can disturb sleep. Associated with a bluish-red hue to the sclera. Often linked with systemic autoimmune disease.
  • Corneal Abrasion or Foreign Body: History of trauma or a distinct sensation of something in the eye. Pain is often relieved by topical anaesthetic.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is focused on confirming the absence of red flags, providing reassurance and self-care advice, and limiting unnecessary antibiotic use in line with antimicrobial stewardship principles.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Reassurance and Expectation Management: Explain that bacterial conjunctivitis is typically a mild, self-limiting infection that usually clears in about a week without treatment.
  • Hygiene Measures: Advise meticulous hand washing before and after touching the eyes. Recommend using separate towels and pillows to prevent transmission to others.
  • Lid hygiene: Advise gently cleaning away discharge from the eyelids using a cotton wool pad soaked in cooled boiled water. Use a fresh pad for each wipe and for each eye.
  • Contact Lenses: Instruct patients to stop wearing contact lenses immediately and not to restart until 48 hours after the infection has completely resolved. Their old lenses and case should be discarded.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Lubricating Eye Drops (e.g., Hypromellose): These can help to soothe the gritty feeling and wash away allergens or debris but do not treat the infection itself. [Rx info]
  • Chloramphenicol 0.5% eye drops / 1% ointment: Available as a Pharmacy (P) medicine from a pharmacist for acute bacterial conjunctivitis. Clinical judgement should still be applied regarding its necessity.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Antibiotics (e.g., Chloramphenicol, Fusidic acid):
    • Chloramphenicol: A broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotic. [Rx info]
    • Fusidic acid: A narrow-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotic, particularly effective against Staphylococci. Often favoured for its simpler dosing schedule. [Rx info]
    • Place in therapy: A delayed or back-up prescription is the preferred strategy for most patients. Consider immediate antibiotics only if the patient is systemically unwell, at high risk of complications, or significantly distressed by symptoms.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of acute infectious conjunctivitis in the UK is primarily guided by the NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS). The core principles are to exclude red flags, promote self-care, and adhere to antimicrobial stewardship.

NICE guidance highlights that the condition is frequently self-limiting. Evidence from systematic reviews shows that while topical antibiotics can increase the rate of clinical cure at days 2-5, around 65% of cases resolve with placebo within this timeframe anyway. The absolute benefit of antibiotics is therefore modest.

For this reason, NICE recommends a shared decision-making approach. For most non-severe cases, it is appropriate to advise self-care measures for 3 days before considering an antibiotic. A delayed (or 'back-up') prescription is a recommended strategy. This involves giving the patient a prescription but advising them to only use it if symptoms have not started to improve after 3 days, or if they worsen significantly.

Public Health England (now UKHSA) guidance states that it is not necessary to exclude children from school or nursery if they have conjunctivitis, although some settings may have their own policies. Good hygiene measures are paramount to prevent spread.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to managing patient expectations, ensuring safety, and reducing the pressure for inappropriate antibiotic prescribing.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that this is a very common, mild infection that the body is good at fighting on its own, and it should clear up within a week.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that the symptoms are uncomfortable but reassure them that it is not damaging their eye and their vision should remain normal.
  • Explain Hygiene Measures: Emphasise the importance of hand washing and not sharing towels to prevent spreading the infection to family members or their other eye. Demonstrate how to clean the eyelids.
  • Explain the Antibiotic Rationale: "Antibiotics are often not needed for this. The infection will almost always clear up by itself. Using drops won't make you better much faster and can cause side effects like stinging. We only use them if things aren't improving on their own."

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Delayed Prescriptions: Give explicit instructions: "Try cleaning the eye for the next 3 days. Only start these antibiotic drops if it's not starting to get better by then, or if it seems to be getting worse."
  • Contact Lens Wearers: This is a critical point. "It is absolutely vital that you do not wear your contact lenses while your eye is red. Wait until 48 hours after it is completely back to normal before you start wearing them again. You must throw away the lenses you were using and the storage case."
  • Administration: Briefly explain how to correctly administer eye drops (wash hands, gently pull down lower lid, apply one drop, avoid touching the bottle to the eye).
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step. It involves providing clear, specific advice on when and how to seek further medical help if the condition deviates from its expected benign course.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (from their GP, an optician, or A&E) if they develop any of the following 'red flag' symptoms:

  • The eye becomes significantly painful (rather than just gritty or irritated).
  • They become sensitive to bright light (photophobia).
  • Their vision becomes blurred or reduced (and is not cleared by blinking).
  • The pupil (the black part of the eye) looks an unusual shape or size.
  • The redness becomes very intense and localised around the edge of the coloured part of the eye.
  • The symptoms are not starting to improve after one week.
  • If they are a contact lens wearer, they should seek help immediately if they have a painful red eye.

For most patients managed as simple bacterial conjunctivitis, no routine follow-up is necessary. The onus is on the patient to re-consult if the condition worsens or fails to resolve as expected based on the safety netting advice provided.

9. Pathophysiology

Bacterial conjunctivitis is an inflammation of the conjunctiva, the transparent mucous membrane that lines the inner surface of the eyelids (the palpebral conjunctiva) and covers the anterior surface of the sclera (the bulbar conjunctiva). The condition is caused by direct contact with pathogenic bacteria.

The ocular surface has several defence mechanisms, including the flushing action of tears, which contain lysozyme and immunoglobulins. When these defences are overcome or breached (e.g., via contaminated hands, fomites), bacteria can adhere to the conjunctival epithelium and proliferate.

This colonisation triggers a type III hypersensitivity reaction and a local inflammatory response. Mast cells degranulate, and inflammatory mediators are released, causing vasodilation of the conjunctival blood vessels (leading to erythema or 'injection') and increased vascular permeability (leading to oedema/chemosis). This is followed by a chemotactic response, attracting neutrophils to the site of infection. The accumulation of neutrophils, dead epithelial cells, fibrin, and bacteria constitutes the characteristic mucopurulent discharge. In simple bacterial conjunctivitis, the inflammation is confined to the conjunctiva; the cornea and deeper ocular structures are not involved.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is prescribed, it is essential to be aware of contraindications and specific safety guidance.

Chloramphenicol

  • Mechanism: A broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotic that inhibits bacterial protein synthesis.
  • Contraindications: Should not be used in patients with a personal or family history of blood dyscrasias, including aplastic anaemia. Avoid in known hypersensitivity.
  • Safety: The risk of aplastic anaemia with topical use is idiosyncratic and extremely rare (estimated between 1 in 224,000 and 1 in 450,000 courses), but it is a serious consideration. Do not prescribe for longer than necessary.
  • Pregnancy/Breastfeeding: Use with caution, and only if potential benefit outweighs risk. Systemic absorption is possible.

Fusidic Acid

  • Mechanism: A narrow-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotic, primarily active against Gram-positive bacteria like Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to fusidic acid.
  • Considerations: Its narrow spectrum makes it less suitable if Gram-negative pathogens (e.g., Haemophilus) are suspected. The viscous nature of the gel formulation can cause transient blurring of vision upon application.

General Considerations

  • Contact Lenses: No eye drops or ointments should be used while wearing soft contact lenses, as preservatives can accumulate in the lens material, causing irritation.
  • Resistance: As with all antibiotics, unnecessary use contributes to antimicrobial resistance. The strategy of delayed prescribing is key to mitigating this.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Bacterial conjunctivitis is very common in children and highly contagious in nursery and school settings. Management principles are the same, with an emphasis on hygiene. Fusidic acid is often preferred due to its twice-daily dosing regimen, which aids adherence. Public Health England advises that exclusion from school is not usually necessary. A key red flag in this group is neonatal conjunctivitis (in infants <28 days old), which requires urgent same-day hospital assessment to exclude chlamydia or gonorrhoea.

Older adults

Consider the patient's ability to self-administer eye drops. They may have comorbidities like dry eye syndrome, which can present similarly. Be alert for atypical presentations of more serious conditions; for example, a patient with cognitive impairment may not be able to report pain or visual loss effectively. A lower threshold for seeking an optician's opinion may be wise if the diagnosis is uncertain.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care and hygiene measures are first-line. If an antibiotic is deemed necessary after discussion, either chloramphenicol or fusidic acid may be used for a short course where the benefit is thought to outweigh the potential risks, in line with current BNF advice. The patient should be counselled that systemic absorption is low but cannot be excluded.

Contact lens wearers

This group requires a different approach due to the high risk of sight-threatening microbial keratitis. They should be treated as having a red flag condition until proven otherwise. They must be advised to stop lens wear immediately and be urgently assessed by a practitioner who can perform a slit-lamp examination (usually a specialist optometrist or an ophthalmologist). Do not prescribe topical antibiotics in primary care for this group without a definitive diagnosis from a specialist.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Nursery Child

  • Presentation: A mother brings her 3-year-old son who woke up with his right eye "glued shut" with yellow pus. This morning, his left eye has started to look red and sticky. He has been otherwise well.
  • Assessment: The child is happy and afebrile. Vision appears grossly normal (he can identify small toys across the room). There is purulent discharge and conjunctival injection in both eyes. The corneas are clear and pupils are reactive. No red flags.
  • Diagnosis: Acute bacterial conjunctivitis.
  • Management: The GP reassures the mother that this is a common, self-limiting infection. They advise on regular, gentle cleaning with cooled boiled water and stress the importance of hand hygiene. They issue a delayed prescription for fusidic acid eye drops, advising the mother to only collect and use it if the eyes are not starting to improve after 3 days. Safety netting advice is provided.

Case Study 2: The Contact Lens Wearer

  • Presentation: A 24-year-old university student presents with a very painful, red left eye that started overnight. He is sensitive to the light in the consulting room. He wears monthly disposable contact lenses and admits to sometimes sleeping in them.
  • Assessment: Visual acuity is 6/9 in the right eye but reduced to 6/18 in the affected left eye. There is marked, generalised redness and some ciliary flush. The patient is in visible discomfort.
  • Diagnosis & Action: This presentation has multiple red flags (pain, photophobia, reduced vision, contact lens wear). This is a potential microbial keratitis until proven otherwise. The GP advises the patient to remove his lenses immediately. They do not prescribe any treatment but instead phone the local eye casualty unit, explain the case, and arrange for the patient to be seen urgently the same day for a slit-lamp examination.

Case Study 3: The Uncertain Case

  • Presentation: A 55-year-old woman presents with a red, watery right eye for two days. She describes it as "uncomfortable" and denies discharge, but reports some mild aching and sensitivity to sunlight.
  • Assessment: Visual acuity is equal in both eyes. The right eye shows moderate, generalised redness, but there appears to be a hint of ciliary injection. The cornea is clear, but the pupil on the affected side seems slightly smaller than the other.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Although some features fit with a simple viral conjunctivitis, the combination of ache, mild photophobia, and possible pupil asymmetry raises suspicion of anterior uveitis. The diagnosis is uncertain. The GP explains this to the patient and advises her to see a local optometrist with urgent access/glaucoma services the same day. The optometrist can perform a more detailed examination (including slit-lamp and pressure checks) to rule out serious pathology.
