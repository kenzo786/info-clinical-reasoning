Scabies in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Scabies is an intensely pruritic skin infestation caused by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis. The cardinal symptom is a severe, widespread itch that is classically worse at night, disrupting sleep. It is transmitted through direct, prolonged skin-to-skin contact, and despite common misconceptions, it is not related to poor hygiene.

Patients typically present with an intensely itchy rash composed of erythematous papules, vesicles, and excoriation marks. Pathognomonic but not always visible are the 'burrows' – thin, greyish, wavy lines on the skin where the female mite has tunnelled. Common sites include the interdigital web spaces, the flexor surfaces of the wrists, elbows, axillae, the waistline, and the genitals in males. In infants and the elderly, the presentation can be atypical, with involvement of the face, scalp, palms, and soles.

Following initial infestation, symptoms can take up to eight weeks to appear. However, in cases of re-infestation, symptoms can develop much more rapidly, often within 1-3 days. The condition is self-perpetuating unless treated, and because of the delay in symptom onset, all household members and close contacts should be treated simultaneously, regardless of whether they have symptoms.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While typically managed in primary care, certain features of a scabies presentation warrant caution, further action, or urgent referral to dermatology.

  • Crusted (Norwegian) Scabies: This is a hyper-infestation with millions of mites, presenting as widespread, crusted, or warty lesions. It is highly contagious and occurs in individuals who are elderly, immunosuppressed (e.g., due to HIV, chemotherapy), or have neurological conditions that impair scratching. It requires urgent specialist input for management, often with combination oral and topical therapy.
  • Severe Secondary Bacterial Infection: Intense scratching can lead to breaks in the skin, allowing for secondary bacterial infection. Signs such as spreading erythema, pustules, cellulitis, or systemic symptoms like fever and malaise require assessment and treatment with antibiotics. In severe cases, this can lead to sepsis or post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis.
  • Diagnostic Uncertainty: If the diagnosis is unclear, especially if the presentation is atypical or has failed to respond to correctly administered first-line treatment, referral to dermatology should be considered for confirmation (e.g., via skin scraping).
  • Outbreak in an Institutional Setting: An outbreak in a care home, nursing facility, or other communal living environment requires a coordinated public health response. The local Health Protection Team should be informed to assist with screening, contact tracing, and mass treatment protocols to control the spread.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A diagnosis of scabies is often made clinically based on a characteristic history and examination findings.

History Taking

  • Pruritus Characteristics: Ascertain the onset, duration, and nature of the itch. A key feature is the nocturnal exacerbation.
  • Contact History: Ask if any household members, sexual partners, or other close contacts have developed a similar itchy rash. This is a very strong diagnostic clue.
  • Distribution of Rash: Enquire about the specific locations of the rash and where it started.
  • Past Medical History: Note any history of immunosuppression, as this increases the risk of crusted scabies. A personal or family history of atopy may suggest eczema as a differential.
  • Previous Treatments: Ask about any prior treatments used (both prescribed and OTC) and crucially, how they were applied and whether contacts were also treated.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Look for evidence of widespread excoriation.
  • Skin Examination: Systematically examine the skin, paying close attention to classic sites: finger webs, wrists, elbows, axillae, belt line, buttocks, feet, and male genitalia. Look for burrows, erythematous papules, and vesicles. A magnifying lens may be helpful.
  • Dermoscopy: If available, dermoscopy can be a useful tool to visualise the mite at the end of a burrow, often appearing as a "delta-wing jet" sign. This can confirm the diagnosis non-invasively.
  • Burrow Ink Test: A simple diagnostic aid involves rubbing ink from a fountain pen over a suspected burrow and then wiping it off with an alcohol swab. Ink that has tracked into the burrow will remain, highlighting its path.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The intense pruritus of scabies means it can be confused with several other dermatological conditions.

Common Differentials

  • Eczema (Atopic, Discoid, or Pompholyx): Eczema can cause severe itch and a papular rash. However, the distribution may be more typical for eczema (e.g., flexural creases in atopic eczema), and a personal or family history of atopy may be present. The key differentiating feature is often the history of itchy contacts in scabies.
  • Insect Bites (e.g., from bedbugs or fleas): Bites often appear as papules or weals, but they are typically found on exposed skin areas, are not usually concentrated in classic scabies sites like finger webs, and burrows are absent.
  • Dermatitis Herpetiformis: An intensely itchy, blistering rash associated with coeliac disease. It typically affects the elbows, knees, and buttocks. While very itchy, the presence of symmetrical vesicles and association with gluten sensitivity helps distinguish it.
  • Urticaria: Characterised by weals (hives) that are transient, typically lasting less than 24 hours in one location. The morphology of the rash is different from scabies.
  • Folliculitis: Inflammation of hair follicles, presenting as small pustules or papules centred on a hair follicle. Itching can occur but is not usually as severe or widespread as in scabies.

Distinguishing Features: The presence of burrows is pathognomonic for scabies. A history of other household members or close contacts developing a similar severe itch is the most significant clue pointing towards scabies over other diagnoses.

5. Management options in primary care

Management requires meticulous treatment of the infested person and all contacts, combined with environmental measures. Patient education is key to success.

Non-Pharmacological / Decontamination Advice

  • Treat All Contacts Simultaneously: This is the most critical step to prevent re-infestation. All household members and any close skin-to-skin contacts (including sexual partners) from the last 8 weeks should be treated on the same day as the index case, even if asymptomatic.
  • Laundry Measures: On the first day of treatment, all bedding, towels, and clothing used in the last 72 hours should be washed in a hot cycle (at least 50°C) and ideally tumble-dried on a hot setting to kill the mites.
  • Sealing Items: Any items that cannot be washed should be sealed in a plastic bag for at least 72 hours, as the mite cannot survive for more than 2-3 days away from a human host.
  • Avoid Close Contact: Advise patients to avoid close skin-to-skin contact with others until the first application of treatment is complete.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) / Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

Topical insecticides are the mainstay of treatment. Two full applications, one week apart, are required.

  • Permethrin 5% dermal cream: This is the first-line treatment. It is a synthetic pyrethroid that acts on the mite's nerve cell membranes, causing paralysis and death. It is generally well-tolerated. [Rx info]
  • Malathion 0.5% aqueous liquid: This is an alternative if permethrin is unsuitable or has failed. It is an organophosphate insecticide that inhibits cholinesterase enzymes in the mites. [Rx info]
  • Ivermectin (oral): This is an anthelmintic medication that is effective against scabies. In the UK, it is unlicensed for routine scabies but is recommended by specialists for treatment-resistant cases, crusted scabies, or in outbreaks where topical application for all contacts is impractical. Its use is typically initiated by, or on the advice of, a dermatologist. [Rx info]
  • Crotamiton cream/lotion: Has some scabicidal properties but is less effective than permethrin. It is sometimes used for its antipruritic effect after successful treatment. [Rx info]
  • Topical Corticosteroids: A mild or moderate potency steroid can be used to manage the persistent itch (post-scabetic eczema) after the mites have been eradicated.
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of scabies in the UK is primarily guided by recommendations from the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) and the British Association for Sexual Health and HIV (BASHH).

Guidelines emphasise that a diagnosis can usually be made on clinical grounds. The cornerstone of management is the "whole-house" approach. First-line treatment recommended by NICE is permethrin 5% dermal cream. Malathion 0.5% liquid is the recommended second-line agent. The guidance is unequivocal that for treatment to be successful, two applications of the chosen topical scabicide must be applied one week apart.

Crucially, all household members and close sexual contacts must be treated at the same time as the index patient, irrespective of whether they have symptoms. This simultaneous treatment is essential to break the cycle of re-infestation. The guidelines also provide specific advice on environmental decontamination, such as the hot washing of linen and clothing used in the 72 hours prior to treatment.

For crusted scabies or cases that have not responded to two courses of correctly applied topical therapy, guidelines support specialist referral and consideration of oral ivermectin, often in combination with topical treatments. There is no role for antibiotics unless there is clear evidence of secondary bacterial infection.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is vital to ensure compliance and successful eradication of the infestation, and to address patient anxiety and stigma.

Counselling for Treatment and Prevention

  • Reassurance and Destigmatisation: It is crucial to reassure the patient that scabies is not caused by poor hygiene and can affect anyone.
  • Explanation of Treatment Rationale: Clearly explain the need to treat everyone in the household at the same time. Use an analogy like: "Even if your family members don't have the itch yet, they could be incubating the mites, and if we don't treat everyone, they will just pass it straight back to you."
  • Detailed Application Instructions: Provide clear, step-by-step instructions (ideally written) on how to apply the cream/lotion. Emphasise applying it to the entire body from the neck down, including between the fingers and toes, under the nails, and on the soles of the feet. For children and the elderly, include the face and scalp. Explain the required duration (e.g., 8-12 hours for permethrin) before washing it off. Remind them to reapply to hands if they are washed.
  • Explain the Second Application: Stress the importance of the second treatment one week later to kill any mites that have hatched from eggs since the first application.
  • Post-Treatment Itch: Manage expectations by explaining that the itch can persist for several weeks (2-4, sometimes up to 6) after the mites have been killed. This is a hypersensitivity reaction to mite debris and does not necessarily mean treatment has failed.
  • Environmental Measures: Clearly explain the laundry and bagging procedures for clothes, bedding, and towels.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Clear safety netting advice is essential to help patients distinguish between expected post-treatment symptoms and signs of treatment failure or complications.

Advise the patient and/or their family to re-consult if they experience any of the following:

  • Appearance of new burrows: If new burrows or typical scabies lesions appear after the second treatment has been completed, this suggests treatment failure or re-infestation.
  • Persistent itch beyond 4-6 weeks: While post-scabetic pruritus is common, if the itch is not gradually improving or is still severe after a month, a review is needed to exclude persistent infestation.
  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection: Instruct them to seek review if the skin becomes hot, red, swollen, painful, or starts to discharge pus or fluid, or if they develop a fever.
  • Symptoms in an institutional setting: If the patient is in a care home or other communal facility, any new cases should be reported to staff immediately to manage a potential outbreak.

A routine follow-up is not typically required for uncomplicated scabies if clear instructions have been given. However, a review should be offered if the diagnosis is uncertain or if the patient is considered at high risk of non-compliance or complications. For persistent symptoms, it is crucial to re-take the history, ensuring that the initial and second treatments were applied correctly by all contacts and that environmental measures were undertaken, before concluding treatment failure.

9. Pathophysiology

Scabies is an ectoparasitic infestation of the skin caused by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis, an obligate human parasite. The mite is microscopic, typically measuring 0.3-0.5 mm.

The life cycle begins after a fertilised female mite burrows into the stratum corneum of the epidermis. She tunnels through the skin, laying 2-3 eggs per day and depositing faecal pellets (scybala). The female remains in the burrow for her entire lifespan of 4-6 weeks. The eggs hatch into larvae within 3-4 days, which then moult through nymphal stages to become adult mites over a period of 10-14 days. The newly matured mites migrate to the skin surface to mate, and the cycle repeats.

The characteristic intense pruritus and inflammatory rash are not caused by the burrowing itself but are the result of a delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction to the mite's eggs, saliva, and faeces. In a primary infestation, this sensitisation process takes several weeks, which is why the onset of symptoms is delayed (typically 2-6 weeks). In a subsequent infestation, the immune system is already primed, and the inflammatory response occurs much more rapidly, usually within 24-48 hours. The nocturnal worsening of the itch is thought to be related to increased mite activity at warmer temperatures when the host is in bed.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing scabicides, it is important to be aware of their specific contraindications, side effects and application requirements.

Permethrin 5% dermal cream

  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to permethrin, other pyrethroids or pyrethrins, or any of the excipients. Note that some creams contain chrysanthemum extract.
  • Side Effects: The most common side effect is transient local skin irritation, such as stinging, burning, or itching, which can be difficult to distinguish from the symptoms of the infestation itself. It can also cause dry skin. Systemic reactions are very rare.
  • Application: Must be applied to the whole body (excluding the head in most adults) and left on for 8-12 hours before being washed off. Must be reapplied to hands if they are washed during the treatment period.

Malathion 0.5% aqueous liquid

  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to malathion or its excipients.
  • Side Effects: Can also cause local skin irritation. The liquid has a distinct odour and is flammable – patients should be advised to avoid naked flames and smoking during the application period.
  • Application: Must be applied to the whole body and left on for 24 hours. This longer application time compared to permethrin may affect compliance.

Ivermectin (Oral)

  • Considerations: In the UK, this is an unlicensed medication for scabies, meaning the prescriber takes on greater clinical responsibility. Its use is guided by specialists. It should be taken on an empty stomach with water.
  • Contraindications: Avoid in children weighing less than 15kg and in pregnancy/breastfeeding unless the benefit outweighs the risk (specialist advice needed). Use with caution in patients with impaired blood-brain barriers.
  • Interactions: It may potentiate the effect of GABA agonists (e.g., benzodiazepines, valproate).
11. Management in special populations

Children

Permethrin 5% cream is licensed for use in infants from 2 months of age. Malathion 0.5% is licensed from 6 months. For children, the treatment should be applied to the entire body, including the face, neck, scalp, and ears, avoiding the area around the eyes and mouth. It is often best to apply the treatment at night and put mittens on young children to prevent them from sucking the cream off their hands. Honey is not a treatment for scabies.

Older adults

Older adults, particularly those in residential care, are at higher risk of scabies outbreaks and may present atypically, sometimes with less prominent itch. They are also more susceptible to crusted scabies. Treatment with permethrin or malathion should include the face, neck, scalp, and ears. Careful consideration of comorbidities and polypharmacy is needed. There should be a low threshold for suspecting crusted scabies and seeking specialist advice.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Permethrin 5% cream is the first-line treatment and is considered safe to use during pregnancy and breastfeeding. Malathion may also be used. If breastfeeding, the treatment should be washed off the nipples before feeding the infant, and reapplied afterwards. Oral ivermectin is generally not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding and should only be used on specialist advice where benefits clearly outweigh risks.

People with significant comorbidities

Individuals who are immunocompromised (e.g., on high-dose steroids, chemotherapy, or with uncontrolled HIV) have a significantly increased risk of developing crusted scabies. This form is highly contagious and difficult to treat. There should be a very low threshold for urgent referral to a dermatologist for confirmation and management, which often requires combination therapy with both topical agents and oral ivermectin.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The University Student

  • Presentation: A 20-year-old university student presents with a 4-week history of a intensely itchy rash. The itch is severe at night, preventing sleep. He notes it is worst between his fingers and on his wrists. On questioning, he reveals his flatmate has also been complaining of being "itchy all over".
  • Assessment: Examination reveals multiple excoriated papules on his hands, wrists, and abdomen. A few linear burrows are visible on the sides of his fingers. The clinical picture is classic for scabies.
  • Management: The GP diagnoses scabies. They prescribe two tubes of permethrin 5% cream. The GP provides a patient information leaflet and meticulously explains the treatment process: apply neck-down for 12 hours, then wash off. Crucially, they stress that his flatmate and any other close contacts must be treated on the exact same days. He is advised to do a hot wash of all bedding and clothes. The second application for everyone is scheduled for one week later. He is warned the itch may persist for a few weeks after treatment.

Case Study 2: The Elderly Care Home Resident

  • Presentation: A practice nurse is called to a care home to see an 88-year-old resident with dementia who has become more agitated. Staff have noticed she is constantly scratching and has a widespread rash. She is on long-term oral steroids for polymyalgia rheumatica.
  • Assessment: On examination, the patient has widespread, scaly, crusted plaques covering her back, limbs, and hands. There are thick crusts under her fingernails. The itch seems present but not as frantic as might be expected. The presentation is highly suspicious of crusted scabies.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The nurse contacts the GP, who agrees with the suspected diagnosis of crusted scabies. Recognising this as a red flag situation, the GP immediately contacts the local dermatology department for an urgent review and advice. They also inform the care home manager and advise them to contact the local Health Protection Team to manage the outbreak, as other residents and staff are at very high risk. Management will likely involve specialist-led combination therapy with oral ivermectin and topical scabicides.

Case Study 3: The Post-Treatment Itch

  • Presentation: A 35-year-old woman returns for review. She, her partner, and two children were all diagnosed with scabies four weeks ago. She is adamant they all completed the two permethrin treatments correctly and followed all the laundry advice. However, she is still "incredibly itchy" and is worried the treatment didn't work.
  • Assessment: She has no new burrows or lesions suggestive of active infestation. Her skin is very dry and shows signs of scratching, consistent with eczematous changes. Her family members' itch has resolved.
  • Diagnosis: Post-scabetic pruritus (eczema).
  • Management: The GP reassures her that this is a very common reaction and does not mean she is still infested. They explain that it's an allergic-type reaction to the remnants of the mites in her skin. They prescribe a moderately potent topical corticosteroid to use for a week or two and recommend liberal use of a simple emollient to rehydrate the skin. Clear safety netting is given to return only if new burrows appear.
