Head Lice (Pediculosis Capitis) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Head lice (pediculosis capitis) is a common ectoparasitic infestation of the scalp, particularly prevalent among school-aged children (4-11 years). It is caused by the human head louse, Pediculus humanus capitis. Patients, or more commonly their parents, typically present with scalp itching (pruritus), though many infestations are asymptomatic. The itching is a result of a hypersensitivity reaction to louse saliva and may take several weeks to develop after an initial infestation.

Direct visualisation of a louse or nits (empty egg cases) on the hair shaft is a common trigger for consultation. Nits are small, oval, whitish-grey eggs glued to the hair shaft, often found behind the ears and at the nape of the neck. An active infestation is confirmed only by finding a live louse. Transmission is almost exclusively via direct head-to-head contact, making outbreaks common in schools and households. It is important to reassure patients and families that head lice infestation is not a sign of poor hygiene and does not transmit other diseases.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

Infestation with head lice is a benign condition and rarely requires urgent referral. However, certain features warrant clinical attention to manage complications or consider alternative diagnoses.

  • Severe secondary bacterial infection: Intense scratching can lead to excoriation and subsequent impetiginisation, characterised by weeping, crusting (golden crusts), pustules, and localised pain. In severe cases, this can lead to cellulitis or scalp abscesses requiring systemic antibiotics.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the diagnosis is unclear after a thorough examination and combing, or if symptoms (e.g., severe, persistent itching) are disproportionate to the findings, consider other dermatological conditions.
  • Treatment failure: Recurrent, confirmed infestations despite correct use of multiple first-line treatments may indicate incorrect application technique, re-infestation from an untreated contact, or, less commonly, true insecticide resistance. While not an emergency, it requires a structured reassessment of the management plan.
  • Severe distress or social impact: In rare cases, a chronic or severe infestation can cause significant psychological distress, sleep disturbance, or social exclusion, warranting more intensive support and management.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is key to confirming the diagnosis and guiding management.

History Taking

  • Presenting Complaint: Ascertain the primary symptom (e.g., itching, visible lice/nits). Ask about duration and severity.
  • Source and Contacts: Enquire about known outbreaks at school or nursery. Ask if other household members or close contacts have been checked for lice. This is critical for preventing re-infestation.
  • Previous Treatments: Detail any treatments used (OTC or prescribed). Crucially, ask about the exact application method, duration of contact time, and whether a second application was performed as required.
  • Past Medical History: Note any pre-existing scalp conditions, such as eczema or psoriasis, or known allergies.

Clinical Examination

  • Visual Inspection: Good lighting is essential. Part the hair and inspect the entire scalp, particularly the occiput and post-auricular areas, for live lice and nits. Nits within 6mm of the scalp are likely viable; those further down the shaft are usually hatched or dead.
  • Detection Combing: This is the gold standard for diagnosing an active infestation.
    • The hair can be wet or dry. Wetting the hair and applying conditioner can immobilise the lice, making them easier to find.
    • Use a purpose-made, fine-toothed plastic detection comb (not a standard grooming comb).
    • Comb the entire head of hair, section by section, from the roots to the ends.
    • After each stroke, wipe the comb on a piece of white tissue or kitchen roll and inspect for live lice.
    • The presence of at least one live louse confirms an active infestation requiring treatment.
  • Skin Examination: Check the scalp, neck, and behind the ears for signs of secondary bacterial infection such as excoriation, erythema, weeping, or crusting.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

While often straightforward, other conditions can mimic the appearance of nits or cause scalp itching.

Common Differentials

  • Dandruff (Seborrhoeic Dermatitis): Presents with white or yellowish flakes of skin on the scalp and in the hair. Unlike nits, these flakes are easily brushed or shaken off the hair shaft.
  • Hair Casts: These are cylindrical, white-ish tubes of keratin that encircle the hair shaft. They can be easily slid along the hair, whereas nits are fixed in place.
  • Product Residue: Remnants of hairspray, gel, or shampoo can sometimes be mistaken for nits but can be washed out.

Less Common Differentials to Consider

  • Scalp Eczema or Psoriasis: These conditions cause itching and flaking but are typically associated with characteristic erythematous plaques and scaling. A personal or family history is often present.
  • Scabies: While it can affect the scalp (especially in infants), scabies typically causes a more widespread, intensely itchy rash with characteristic burrows in web spaces, wrists, and the trunk.
  • Booklice (Psocids): Rarely, these insects can be found in hair but they do not bite or feed on blood. They are transient and can be washed out.
5. Management options in primary care

Management focuses on eradicating the lice, managing symptoms, and preventing re-infestation. Treatment should only be initiated if a live louse is found.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Detection Combing: This can be used as a treatment method in itself (often called "wet combing" or "bug busting"). It involves meticulous combing with a detection comb on conditioned, wet hair every 3-4 days for at least two weeks to remove lice as they hatch. It is labour-intensive but avoids insecticides.
  • Contact Screening: All household members and close contacts should be checked with a detection comb. Only those with a confirmed active infestation (live lice) should be treated, and all affected members should be treated simultaneously.
  • School/Nursery Policy: Children with head lice do not need to be kept off school. The school may be informed to advise other parents to check their children.
  • Environmental Measures: Fumigating rooms or washing clothing and bedding on a hot cycle is not necessary and has no impact on treatment efficacy.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

These are the mainstay of treatment. Two applications are required, 7 days apart, to kill lice that hatch from eggs that survived the first application.

  • Physical Insecticides (First-line): These act by physically coating and suffocating the lice and their eggs. They are highly effective and resistance is unlikely to develop.
    • Dimeticone 4% (silicone-based): Applied to dry hair and left for 8 hours or overnight. [Rx info]
    • Isopropyl myristate and cyclohexyl-siloxane solution: A physical insecticide that dissolves the louse's exoskeleton, leading to dehydration. [Rx info]
  • Chemical Insecticides (Second-line): Resistance is now widespread in the UK, making these less effective.
    • Malathion 0.5% (organophosphate): An aqueous liquid applied and left for 12 hours or overnight. Flammable. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Antibiotics: Oral or topical antibiotics (e.g., flucloxacillin, fusidic acid) are indicated for the treatment of secondary bacterial skin infections only. They do not treat the lice infestation itself. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of head lice in the UK is primarily guided by the NICE CKS (Clinical Knowledge Summaries) topic on Head Lice. These guidelines are based on systematic reviews of the available evidence.

The core principles are:

  • Diagnosis: An active infestation should only be diagnosed if a live head louse is found. Nits alone do not confirm an active infestation. Detection combing is the recommended diagnostic method.
  • Treatment Decision: Treatment is only recommended for individuals with a confirmed active infestation. Prophylactic treatment for contacts is not advised; they should be checked by detection combing and treated only if live lice are found.
  • First-Line Treatment: NICE recommends using either a physical insecticide (e.g., dimeticone 4% lotion) or the wet combing method. The choice depends on patient preference and suitability.
  • Second-Line Treatment: A chemical insecticide (malathion 0.5%) can be considered if the first-line options are ineffective or unsuitable. However, prescribers and patients should be aware of the high levels of resistance.
  • Application: Guidelines stress the critical importance of correct application, including using sufficient product to cover all hair, leaving it on for the recommended time, and repeating the application after seven days to ensure the entire louse life cycle is broken.
7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication can improve adherence, reduce anxiety, and prevent unnecessary treatments.

Counselling for Diagnosis and Self-Care

  • Reassurance: Emphasise that head lice are very common in children, are not a sign of poor hygiene, and do not carry other diseases. This can reduce stigma and guilt.
  • Explain the Diagnosis: Clearly explain the difference between live lice (active infestation requiring treatment) and nits (which can be empty cases from a previous infestation). Show the patient/parent a louse on the tissue if found.
  • Explain the Life Cycle: Briefly explain that eggs (nits) hatch after 7-10 days. This is why a second treatment application at day 7 is crucial to kill the newly hatched lice before they can lay more eggs.
  • Empower with Technique: Clearly explain or demonstrate the detection combing method. Provide advice on choosing a suitable product and the importance of following the manufacturer's instructions precisely (amount, contact time, repeat application).
  • Managing Contacts: Advise that all household members should be checked, but only those with live lice should be treated, and all at the same time to prevent "ping-pong" re-infestation.

Counselling on Treatment Failure

  • Explore Reasons: If treatment appears to have failed, calmly explore the most common reasons: not following instructions correctly, not completing the second application, or immediate re-infestation from an untreated close contact. Resistance is a less common cause of failure than these factors.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Clear safety netting advice is essential to ensure treatment success and manage complications.

Advise the patient or parent to seek further advice if they experience any of the following:

  • Live lice are still present after completing a full course of treatment: Check for correct application technique and re-infestation from contacts. If confirmed, advise trying a treatment from a different class (e.g., switching from a chemical to a physical insecticide) or using the wet combing method.
  • The scalp becomes sore, weepy, or crusted: This may indicate a secondary bacterial infection that could require assessment and treatment with antibiotics.
  • The itching is severe and persistent despite clearing the infestation: Consider other causes of scalp pruritus, such as a contact dermatitis reaction to the product used, or an alternative underlying skin condition.
  • The infestation repeatedly recurs: This warrants a discussion to meticulously review the treatment and contact-tracing strategy.

A formal follow-up is not usually required for uncomplicated head lice. However, it is good practice to advise re-checking the hair with a detection comb 2-3 days after the second application, and again a week later, to ensure the infestation has cleared.

9. Pathophysiology

Head lice infestation is caused by Pediculus humanus capitis, an obligate human ectoparasite. The louse is a wingless insect, typically 2-3 mm long, that lives exclusively on human scalp hair and feeds on blood. It cannot fly or jump; transmission occurs via direct head-to-head contact.

The life cycle is key to understanding treatment regimens. The female louse lays eggs (nits), gluing them firmly to the base of hair shafts. She lays 3-4 eggs per day and can live for up to 30 days. The nits are positioned close to the scalp to benefit from its warmth for incubation. They hatch into nymphs after 7-10 days. The nymph moults three times over about 9-12 days before becoming a sexually mature adult louse. The empty nit case remains attached to the growing hair shaft.

Itching (pruritus) is the main symptom and is caused by a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction to allergens in the louse's saliva, which is injected during feeding. This is why itching may not start until several weeks after a primary infestation has begun. In subsequent infestations, the sensitisation is already present, and itching can start much sooner. Scratching can lead to secondary bacterial infection with skin commensals like Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When advising on treatment, it is essential to be aware of formulation-specific properties and safety advice.

Physical Insecticides (e.g., Dimeticone, Isopropyl myristate)

  • Mechanism: They are not neurotoxins. They coat the lice and disrupt their ability to manage water, leading to death by suffocation or dehydration. This mode of action means resistance is not expected to develop.
  • Safety: They are considered very safe and are not systemically absorbed. Skin irritation is uncommon. They are suitable for most people, including those with asthma or sensitive skin. Some formulations may be flammable, so hair should be kept away from naked flames or heat sources while wet. They can make shower trays and baths very slippery.

Chemical Insecticides (e.g., Malathion)

  • Mechanism: Acts as a neurotoxin by irreversibly inhibiting the cholinesterase enzyme in lice, causing paralysis and death.
  • Safety & Risks: Resistance is widespread in the UK. Alcohol-based formulations are flammable and hair must be allowed to dry naturally away from any heat source. Can cause skin irritation. Systemic absorption is minimal with correct use but they should be used with more caution than physical insecticides in certain populations (e.g., pregnancy).

Wet Combing

  • Contraindications: None. It is safe for everyone, including infants and pregnant people.
  • Considerations: Its success is highly dependent on using the correct technique and on the persistence of the person performing it. It can be very time-consuming, especially for children with long, thick hair.
11. Management in special populations

Children

This is the most commonly affected group. Dimeticone 4% is licensed for use from 6 months of age. Malathion is also licensed from 6 months. Wet combing is a safe option for infants under 6 months. It is vital to ensure the child can tolerate the lengthy application times without distress and to keep preparations out of their eyes and mouth.

Older adults

Infestation is less common but can occur, particularly in residential care settings where close contact facilitates spread. Management is the same, but skin may be more fragile, so gentle combing is advised. Check for potential interactions if a secondary infection requires systemic antibiotics in a patient on multiple medications.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Wet combing is the preferred first-line method as it is completely free of chemicals. If medication is required, dimeticone 4% lotion is considered safe as it is not absorbed through the skin into the bloodstream. Chemical insecticides like malathion should generally be avoided unless the benefits are felt to outweigh the risks, and after discussion with the patient. Always consult the latest BNF or manufacturer's guidance.

People with significant comorbidities

In patients with severe scalp conditions like eczema or psoriasis, insecticide preparations can be irritant. A physical insecticide or wet combing may be better tolerated. There should be a low threshold for treating secondary bacterial infection in immunocompromised individuals.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Primary School Child

  • Presentation: A 7-year-old girl presents with her mother, who found "white things" in her hair. The child has been itching her scalp for a week. The school sent a letter home about a head lice outbreak.
  • Assessment: Wet detection combing is performed in the treatment room. Several live lice are found on the tissue after combing. The scalp shows mild redness from scratching but no crusting.
  • Diagnosis: Active head lice infestation.
  • Management: The clinician recommends an over-the-counter dimeticone 4% lotion. They counsel the mother on the need for two applications, 7 days apart, and the importance of covering all hair from root to tip. They advise checking all other household members with a detection comb and treating them only if live lice are found. Safety netting is provided regarding signs of secondary infection.

Case Study 2: Apparent Treatment Failure

  • Presentation: A 9-year-old boy returns with his father. He was treated for head lice two weeks ago with a malathion lotion. His father is adamant he still sees lice in his son's hair and thinks the product didn't work.
  • Assessment: A detailed history reveals the father only performed one application of the lotion. Detection combing confirms the presence of several live lice of various sizes, and nits close to the scalp.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The clinician explains the louse life cycle and why the second application is essential for eradicating newly hatched lice. They reassure the father that this is a common reason for apparent failure, rather than resistance. They advise a new course of treatment, suggesting a physical insecticide this time. They reinforce the need to treat on day 1 and day 8 and to check contacts.

Case Study 3: The Sore Scalp

  • Presentation: A mother phones for advice about her 5-year-old son. He had itchy head lice for a couple of weeks, and now his scalp is very sore and weepy behind his ears.
  • Assessment: The child is brought to the surgery. Examination reveals multiple excoriations and areas of golden-yellow crusting on the occiput and post-auricular scalp, consistent with secondary impetiginisation. Detection combing finds live lice.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Active head lice infestation complicated by secondary bacterial infection. The GP prescribes a course of oral flucloxacillin for the impetigo. They also advise the mother to treat the head lice infestation for the whole family, recommending the wet combing method initially to avoid applying chemical lotions to the already inflamed and broken skin. They provide clear advice on when to seek review if the infection does not improve.
