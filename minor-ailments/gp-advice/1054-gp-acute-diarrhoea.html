Acute Diarrhoea in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute diarrhoea is a very common reason for consultation in primary care. It is defined as the passage of three or more loose or liquid stools per day, for a duration of less than 14 days. In the UK, it is most often caused by a viral infection (gastroenteritis) and is typically self-limiting.

Patients usually present with a sudden onset of watery diarrhoea, which may be accompanied by nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and a mild fever. The most common infectious causes are viruses like norovirus and rotavirus. Bacterial causes, such as Campylobacter or Salmonella from contaminated food, are less frequent but can cause more severe symptoms. The condition usually resolves within 5 to 7 days without specific medical intervention.

The main clinical concern is dehydration, particularly in the very young and in older, frail adults. The primary role of the clinician is to assess hydration status, exclude features of serious underlying pathology, and provide advice on self-care, focusing on fluid replacement and hygiene measures to prevent transmission.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of acute diarrhoea are benign, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a more serious underlying cause or significant complications. The presence of red flags warrants further investigation, urgent review, or immediate hospital admission.

  • Signs of severe dehydration or shock: Such as tachycardia, hypotension, reduced consciousness, or passing no urine. This requires immediate fluid resuscitation.
  • Blood and/or mucus in the stool (dysentery): This suggests mucosal inflammation and may indicate a more aggressive bacterial infection (e.g., Shigella, Campylobacter) or a flare of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Severe, persistent abdominal pain or localised tenderness: This may indicate a surgical abdomen, toxic megacolon, or ischaemic colitis and requires urgent surgical assessment.
  • High fever (>38.5°C) or systemic signs of sepsis: Features like rigors, confusion, or severe malaise suggest bacteraemia and require urgent admission and investigation.
  • Recent hospitalisation or antibiotic use: A high index of suspicion for Clostridioides difficile infection is needed, which requires specific investigation and treatment.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Individuals with significant immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy or uncontrolled HIV) are at high risk of severe or opportunistic infections and require a lower threshold for investigation and admission.
  • Symptoms lasting more than 14 days: The diarrhoea is now classified as persistent, and requires investigation for causes such as Giardia, IBD, or colorectal disease.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to history and examination is essential to assess severity, identify the likely cause, and rule out red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Characteristics: Ascertain onset, duration, frequency, and nature of the diarrhoea (watery vs. bloody/mucoid). Ask about associated vomiting, abdominal pain, and fever.
  • Dehydration Assessment: Ask about thirst, urine output (volume and frequency), dizziness (especially on standing), and lethargy.
  • Epidemiological Clues: Enquire about recent foreign travel, contact with others with similar symptoms, consumption of high-risk foods (e.g., undercooked poultry), and any recent antibiotic use or hospital admission.
  • Past Medical History: Note pre-existing conditions such as IBD, diverticular disease, diabetes, or any cause of immunosuppression.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically ask about severe pain, blood in stool, weight loss, and inability to keep oral fluids down.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Assess for signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor) and general wellbeing (e.g., signs of sepsis like confusion, lethargy).
  • Vital Signs: Measure temperature, respiratory rate, pulse, and blood pressure (including postural BP if dehydration is suspected). Oxygen saturations are also important.
  • Abdominal Examination: Palpate for focal tenderness, guarding, rebound tenderness, or abdominal masses. Auscultate for bowel sounds.

Stool Culture

A stool sample for microscopy, culture, and sensitivities (MC&S) and C. difficile toxin testing is not routinely required. It should be considered if the patient has bloody diarrhoea, is systemically unwell, is immunocompromised, if symptoms are prolonged (>7 days), or if an outbreak is suspected.

4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential for acute diarrhoea is broad, but most cases are infectious gastroenteritis. The clinical assessment aims to distinguish this from more serious conditions.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Gastroenteritis: The most common cause. Often associated with vomiting and a community outbreak (e.g., norovirus). Stools are watery and non-bloody.
  • Bacterial Gastroenteritis: Often linked to food poisoning (e.g., Campylobacter, Salmonella) or travel. May cause more severe symptoms, fever, and sometimes bloody diarrhoea.
  • Traveller's Diarrhoea: Usually bacterial (e.g., ETEC). Occurs during or shortly after travel to a high-risk region.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Clostridioides difficile Infection: A key differential in those recently treated with antibiotics or hospitalised. Causes profuse, foul-smelling, watery diarrhoea.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): A first presentation of Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis can manifest as acute, often bloody, diarrhoea with abdominal pain.
  • Diverticulitis: Typically presents with left-sided abdominal pain and fever, but diarrhoea can be a feature.
  • Ischaemic Colitis: Consider in older adults with vascular disease who present with sudden abdominal pain followed by bloody diarrhoea.
  • Colorectal Cancer: Unlikely to present as acute diarrhoea, but should be considered if there is a persistent change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, or weight loss.
5. Management options in primary care

Management is primarily supportive, focusing on preventing and treating dehydration. Antibiotics are not indicated for most cases.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Fluid Replacement: This is the cornerstone of management. Advise drinking plenty of clear fluids (e.g., water, dilute squash).
  • Dietary Advice: Advise eating as normally as possible once appetite returns. Small, light meals are better tolerated. There is no evidence to support specific food avoidance or the BRAT diet.
  • Infection Control: Advise strict hand hygiene. Patients should not attend work or school until at least 48 hours after their last episode of diarrhoea or vomiting. They should also avoid preparing food for others.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS): Sachets (e.g., Dioralyte) dissolved in water provide a balanced solution of electrolytes and glucose to enhance water absorption. Strongly recommended for frail or elderly patients and young children. [Rx info]
  • Antimotility Agents (e.g., Loperamide): Acts as an opioid-receptor agonist in the gut wall to reduce peristalsis and increase fluid absorption. Can provide symptomatic relief in adults with watery diarrhoea and no red flags. It should be avoided if fever is high or if there is blood in the stool. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Antibiotics: Not recommended for most cases of acute gastroenteritis. Consider only in specific circumstances such as for systemically unwell patients with suspected sepsis, or for treating specific pathogens identified on stool culture in severe or persistent cases (e.g., Campylobacter, Giardia). [Rx info]
  • Antiemetics (e.g., Cyclizine, Metoclopramide): May be considered for short-term use if vomiting is severe and preventing oral rehydration, but use with caution. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

Management in the UK is guided by NICE CKS topics on Gastroenteritis and Diarrhoea, and Public Health England (now UKHSA) guidance on infectious intestinal diseases. For children, NICE Guideline NG183 (Diarrhoea and vomiting in under 5s) is the key resource.

The core principles are universal across all guidelines. The primary focus is the assessment of hydration status and the provision of adequate fluid and electrolyte replacement. Oral rehydration therapy is the mainstay of treatment, especially for vulnerable groups.

Guidelines are clear that investigations such as stool cultures are not needed for most patients. They should be reserved for cases with red flags, persistent symptoms, or in outbreak situations. Similarly, antibiotics should not be routinely prescribed for uncomplicated acute diarrhoea. This is because most cases are viral, and even in bacterial cases, antibiotics rarely provide significant benefit but contribute to side effects and antimicrobial resistance. Antimotility agents like loperamide may be used for symptom control in adults with mild-to-moderate watery diarrhoea but are contraindicated in bloody diarrhoea or suspected C. difficile infection.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is crucial for managing patient expectations and ensuring safe self-management.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Natural History: Clearly state that the illness is usually caused by a virus and is self-limiting, typically resolving within 5-7 days.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge that the symptoms are unpleasant but reassure the patient that it is a common illness that the body's immune system can usually clear on its own.
  • Explain Fluid Importance: Emphasise that the most important treatment is drinking plenty of fluids to avoid dehydration. Explain the benefits of oral rehydration sachets, especially if the person is frail.
  • Explain Antibiotic Ineffectiveness: Use a clear explanation. For example, "This is most likely a tummy bug caused by a virus, and antibiotics don't work on viruses. They won't speed up your recovery and can sometimes cause side effects like a rash or even make the diarrhoea worse."
  • Hygiene Advice: Provide explicit advice on hand washing and the 48-hour exclusion period from work/school to prevent spreading the infection to others.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Loperamide Use: If recommending loperamide, advise on the correct dosage and to stop using it once the diarrhoea settles. Crucially, advise them to stop and seek medical advice if they develop a high fever, severe abdominal pain, or see blood in their stool.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a vital part of the consultation. It involves giving the patient specific advice on when to re-consult or seek more urgent medical attention.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to seek further help if they experience any of the following:

  • Symptoms are not starting to improve after 5-7 days or are getting worse.
  • They are unable to keep any fluids down due to persistent vomiting.
  • They show signs of severe dehydration (e.g., feeling very dizzy when standing, confusion, passing very little or no urine).
  • They develop severe or worsening abdominal pain.
  • They pass any blood or mucus in their stool.
  • They develop a high fever and feel systemically very unwell.

Follow-up is not routinely required for uncomplicated cases. However, for more vulnerable patients (e.g., the very elderly, those with significant comorbidities) managed in the community, a follow-up phone call in 24-48 hours may be appropriate to check on their progress and hydration status.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute diarrhoea is fundamentally a disorder of intestinal water and electrolyte transport. It can be classified into several types based on the underlying mechanism.

Secretory Diarrhoea: This is caused by enterotoxins produced by bacteria (e.g., Vibrio cholerae, Enterotoxigenic E. coli). These toxins actively stimulate the secretion of chloride ions from intestinal crypt cells into the lumen. Sodium and water follow the osmotic gradient, resulting in large-volume watery diarrhoea that persists even when fasting.

Inflammatory (Exudative) Diarrhoea: This occurs when invasive pathogens (e.g., Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella) or inflammatory conditions like IBD damage the intestinal mucosa. The inflamed and ulcerated lining cannot absorb water and electrolytes effectively. Furthermore, the damaged mucosa leaks serum, mucus, and blood into the intestinal lumen, leading to frequent, smaller-volume stools that often contain blood and pus (dysentery).

Osmotic Diarrhoea: This happens when poorly absorbed solutes remain in the gut lumen, drawing water in by osmosis. A common cause is viral damage to the small bowel brush border, leading to temporary lactase deficiency. The unabsorbed lactose acts as an osmotic agent. This type of diarrhoea typically stops when the patient fasts.

Viral gastroenteritis often involves a combination of osmotic diarrhoea (due to mucosal damage and enzyme deficiencies) and a mild secretory component.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When medication is considered, it is essential to be aware of contraindications and specific safety guidance.

Loperamide

  • Contraindications: Should not be used in children under 12 (OTC), or in patients with acute dysentery (bloody diarrhoea and fever), acute ulcerative colitis, or suspected C. difficile infection. Its use risks precipitating ileus or toxic megacolon in these inflammatory conditions.
  • Risks: Main side effects are constipation, dizziness, and abdominal cramps. MHRA has highlighted reports of serious cardiac adverse reactions with high doses associated with misuse or abuse.

Antibiotics (e.g., Fluoroquinolones like Ciprofloxacin)

  • Place in therapy: Very limited role. May be used for severe traveller's diarrhoea but resistance is an issue.
  • MHRA Alerts: There are significant safety warnings regarding fluoroquinolones, including the risk of disabling and potentially permanent side effects involving tendons, muscles, joints, and the nervous system. They should only be used when other commonly recommended antibiotics are inappropriate. They are also associated with a small increased risk of aortic aneurysm and dissection.

Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)

  • Precautions: While generally very safe, they should be used with caution in patients with severe renal impairment or heart failure, where fluid and electrolyte balance must be carefully managed. The correct volume of water must be used for reconstitution to ensure the solution is isotonic.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Dehydration is the primary risk and can develop rapidly. Management is guided by NICE NG183. Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) is the cornerstone of treatment. Parents should be advised to offer fluids frequently. Antimotility drugs like loperamide are contraindicated. Advise parents on recognising signs of dehydration (e.g., reduced wet nappies, drowsiness, sunken eyes). Children should be kept off school or nursery for 48 hours after the last episode of symptoms.

Older adults

Are at higher risk of dehydration, complications, and C. difficile infection. They may present atypically (e.g., with confusion or a fall). There should be a lower threshold for clinical assessment, checking vital signs, and sending a stool sample if there are risk factors for C. difficile. Polypharmacy increases the risk of drug interactions and side effects. Be cautious with any medication. Oral rehydration salts should be strongly encouraged.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Rehydration is the first-line and most important treatment. Loperamide can be considered for short-term use if necessary, but self-care should be tried first. If antibiotics are required, choices must be carefully considered for safety in pregnancy and breastfeeding (e.g., azithromycin may be an option, fluoroquinolones should be avoided). Always consult the latest BNF guidance.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with IBD may be having a flare-up; antimotility agents are hazardous and should be avoided. Patients with diabetes need advice on 'sick day rules' for managing their glucose levels and medications. Those with cardiac or renal failure require careful management of fluid intake to avoid overload. Immunosuppressed patients are at high risk of severe infection and need urgent assessment and a lower threshold for investigation and treatment.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The University Student

  • Presentation: A 20-year-old student presents with a 24-hour history of profuse watery diarrhoea and vomiting. Several of his flatmates are unwell with similar symptoms. He feels thirsty and has mild abdominal cramps.
  • Assessment: He is alert. Temp: 37.5°C, pulse 88/min, BP 115/75 mmHg. Mucous membranes are slightly dry but skin turgor is normal. Abdomen is soft with mild diffuse tenderness.
  • Diagnosis: Acute viral gastroenteritis (likely norovirus outbreak). No red flags.
  • Management: The GP explains the likely viral cause and self-limiting nature. Advises strict fluid intake, using water and suggesting oral rehydration salts from the pharmacy. Provides advice on hygiene and to stay off university for 48 hours after symptoms cease. Clear safety netting advice is given regarding signs of worsening dehydration or new symptoms.

Case Study 2: The Older Adult Post-Antibiotics

  • Presentation: An 82-year-old man presents with a 4-day history of profuse, foul-smelling diarrhoea, 8-10 times a day. He finished a course of co-amoxiclav for a chest infection 10 days ago. He feels weak and his appetite is poor.
  • Assessment: He appears lethargic. Temp: 38.1°C, pulse: 105/min, BP: 100/60 mmHg. His mouth is dry and his abdomen is generally tender.
  • Diagnosis & Action: The history strongly suggests Clostridioides difficile infection. The patient is clinically dehydrated and systemically unwell. The GP arranges an urgent stool sample for C. difficile toxin (CDT) and MC&S. Due to his frailty and signs of significant illness, the GP arranges for same-day hospital admission for assessment and intravenous fluid replacement.
