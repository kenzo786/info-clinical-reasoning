Indigestion / Dyspepsia in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Dyspepsia is a common reason for consultation in primary care, describing a complex of upper gastrointestinal symptoms including epigastric pain or discomfort, heartburn, bloating, early satiety, belching, and nausea. It is often recurrent and can significantly impact quality of life. In many cases, no underlying organic pathology is found, leading to a diagnosis of functional (or non-ulcer) dyspepsia.

Patients typically present with symptoms linked to eating, often described as a 'gnawing' or 'burning' epigastric pain. Symptoms can be triggered or exacerbated by certain foods (e.g., fatty, spicy), alcohol, caffeine, or medications, particularly Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs). The clinical course is often chronic with intermittent episodes.

The primary role of the clinician is to differentiate common, manageable causes like functional dyspepsia or gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) from more serious underlying pathology. A key initial step is to screen for red flag symptoms and consider the role of factors like *Helicobacter pylori* infection and offending medications.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The presence of 'ALARM' features in a patient with dyspepsia should prompt consideration of an urgent direct access upper GI endoscopy (two-week wait referral in the UK) to exclude malignancy. It is crucial to identify these features promptly.

  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing is a cardinal red flag for oesophageal cancer.
  • Unintentional weight loss: An unexplained and significant loss of weight is a worrying constitutional symptom for upper GI malignancy.
  • Persistent vomiting: Particularly if it is recurrent and unexplained, as it may suggest gastric outlet obstruction.
  • Epigastric mass: A palpable mass on abdominal examination requires urgent investigation.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: A new or unexplained iron deficiency anaemia may be caused by chronic occult bleeding from a gastric or oesophageal tumour.
  • Haematemesis or melaena: Vomiting blood or passing black, tarry stools indicates upper GI bleeding and warrants emergency assessment and admission, as it could be due to a bleeding peptic ulcer or tumour.
  • New, unexplained, and persistent dyspepsia in a patient aged 55 or over: NICE guidance suggests a lower threshold for considering endoscopy in this age group due to the increased incidence of gastric cancer.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach is essential to guide diagnosis and management, focusing on symptom patterns, risk factors, and red flags.

History Taking

  • Symptom Analysis: Characterise the main symptom(s) – epigastric pain, heartburn, bloating. Clarify timing and relationship to meals, posture, and trigger foods. Ask about nocturnal symptoms.
  • Red Flag Screen: Systematically enquire about all ALARM features: dysphagia, weight loss, persistent vomiting, haematemesis, melaena.
  • Medication Review: Specifically ask about use of NSAIDs (including over-the-counter use), aspirin, bisphosphonates, corticosteroids, and calcium channel blockers, all of which can cause or worsen dyspepsia.
  • Past Medical and Social History: Enquire about previous *H. pylori* infection, peptic ulcer disease, or GORD. Document smoking status and alcohol consumption.

Clinical Examination

  • General Observation: Look for signs of pallor (anaemia) or cachexia (weight loss).
  • Abdominal Examination: Palpate for epigastric tenderness or, more importantly, a palpable epigastric mass.

Assessment Tools

  • *Helicobacter pylori* Testing: For patients with uninvestigated dyspepsia and no red flags, a non-invasive test should be offered. Options include a urea breath test or a stool antigen test. Serology is less useful as it cannot distinguish active from past infection.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The differential diagnosis for dyspepsia is broad. The clinical assessment aims to distinguish benign functional disease from organic pathology.

Common Causes

  • Functional Dyspepsia: The most common diagnosis. Characterised by chronic or recurrent dyspeptic symptoms in the absence of any identifiable structural disease. Diagnosis is one of exclusion.
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD): Heartburn is the predominant symptom, often with acid regurgitation. Symptoms are typically worse after large meals or lying down.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Often associated with *H. pylori* infection or NSAID use. Epigastric pain is the classic feature, which may be relieved by food or antacids (duodenal ulcer) or worsened by food (gastric ulcer).
  • Medication-Induced Dyspepsia: Caused by drugs like NSAIDs, aspirin, steroids, or bisphosphonates.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Oesophageal or Gastric Cancer: The key concern. More likely in older patients or those with ALARM symptoms (dysphagia, weight loss, anaemia).
  • Biliary Tract Disease: Conditions like biliary colic or cholecystitis can present with severe, colicky right upper quadrant or epigastric pain, often radiating to the back, related to fatty food intake.
  • Pancreatitis: Usually presents with a sudden onset of severe, constant epigastric pain radiating to the back, often with vomiting and systemic upset.
  • Cardiac Pain: An inferior myocardial infarction can present with epigastric pain and nausea. Consider this particularly in patients with cardiovascular risk factors, if symptoms are exertional, or associated with breathlessness or sweating.
5. Management options in primary care

Management follows a stepwise approach, starting with lifestyle advice and empirical treatment based on the likely underlying cause.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Lifestyle Modification: Advise weight management if overweight, smoking cessation, and reduction in alcohol consumption.
  • Dietary Advice: Encourage eating smaller, more frequent meals and avoiding known individual trigger foods (e.g., coffee, chocolate, fatty or spicy foods). Advise avoiding eating within 3 hours of bedtime.
  • Medication Review: Stop or review the need for any medications known to cause dyspepsia where clinically appropriate.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

  • Antacids and Alginates: Provide rapid, short-term relief. Antacids (e.g., magnesium trisilicate, aluminium hydroxide) neutralise stomach acid. Alginates (e.g., sodium alginate) form a protective raft that floats on top of the stomach contents, acting as a physical barrier to reflux. [Rx info]

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs): (e.g., Omeprazole, Lansoprazole). These potently suppress acid production by irreversibly blocking the H+/K+ ATPase pump in gastric parietal cells. They are first-line for persistent symptoms. The aim is to use the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible duration. [Rx info]
  • H2-Receptor Antagonists (H2RAs): (e.g., Famotidine). These reduce acid secretion by blocking histamine H2 receptors on parietal cells. They are less potent than PPIs and can be considered as a step-down therapy or for intermittent use. [Rx info]
  • *H. pylori* Eradication Therapy: If *H. pylori* is confirmed, a one-week course of treatment is offered. This typically consists of a PPI plus two antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin and clarithromycin, or metronidazole and clarithromycin). [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

The management of dyspepsia in the UK is primarily guided by NICE guideline NG184 (Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease and dyspepsia in adults). The guidance promotes a logical, evidence-based pathway that balances symptom control with appropriate investigation and stewardship of resources and medications.

Key NICE recommendations include a "test and treat" strategy for *H. pylori* in patients with uninvestigated dyspepsia who do not have ALARM symptoms. If *H. pylori* is negative or symptoms persist after eradication, a one-month trial of a full-dose PPI is recommended. Routine endoscopy is not recommended for uninvestigated dyspepsia unless ALARM symptoms are present.

The guidance strongly advocates for stepping down acid-suppression therapy once symptoms are controlled. This involves reducing the PPI dose or switching to 'as-needed' use to minimise the long-term risks associated with these medicines. The need for long-term PPIs should be reviewed annually. For patients taking NSAIDs, the guidance discusses strategies for gastroprotection in at-risk individuals.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Effective communication is key to successful management, helping patients understand their condition and engage with the treatment plan.

Counselling for Self-Care

  • Explain the Condition: Reassure the patient that dyspepsia is very common and usually not a sign of a serious problem, especially in the absence of red flags. Explain the likely cause (e.g., functional dyspepsia, GORD).
  • Empower with Lifestyle Advice: Frame lifestyle changes (diet, alcohol, smoking) as the most important and effective long-term strategy for managing their symptoms. Provide specific, achievable goals.
  • Explain the 'Test and Treat' Rationale: If testing for *H. pylori*, explain that it is a common bug that can cause irritation and ulcers, and that a course of antibiotics can often cure the problem.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • PPIs/H2RAs: Explain that these medicines work by reducing stomach acid, which allows the lining to heal and reduces pain. Counsel on the importance of taking PPIs 30-60 minutes before a meal for maximum effect.
  • Stepping Down Therapy: Discuss the plan to reduce the medication once symptoms are controlled. Explain this is to minimise potential long-term side effects and to find the lowest dose that keeps them comfortable. For example, "We will try this for a month to get things under control, and then we will look to either reduce the dose or for you to take it only when you need it."
  • Side Effects: Briefly mention common side effects and advise them to read the patient information leaflet. For *H. pylori* eradication, warn about the possibility of diarrhoea or a metallic taste.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a crucial final step to ensure patients know when and how to seek further help if their condition changes or fails to improve as expected.

Clear verbal and, ideally, written instructions should be provided. Advise the patient to re-consult or seek urgent care if they experience any of the following:

  • The development of any ALARM symptoms, particularly difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), unexplained weight loss, or persistent vomiting.
  • Vomiting blood or passing black, tarry stools (melaena) - this requires emergency assessment.
  • Severe, unremitting pain, especially if radiating to the back.
  • Symptoms that do not resolve after the agreed initial management plan (e.g., a one-month trial of a PPI).
  • The symptoms change significantly in character.

A planned follow-up appointment should be arranged to review the response to treatment, typically after 4 weeks of PPI therapy or after completion of *H. pylori* eradication. The purpose of this review is to assess symptom control and to create a long-term plan, which usually involves stepping down acid suppression therapy.

9. Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of dyspepsia is multifactorial. A balance normally exists in the upper GI tract between aggressive factors (gastric acid, pepsin) and protective mechanisms (the mucus-bicarbonate layer, prostaglandins, adequate blood flow). Dyspepsia arises when this balance is disrupted.

In Peptic Ulcer Disease, this disruption is key. *Helicobacter pylori*, a spiral-shaped bacterium, survives in the acidic stomach environment by producing urease. It causes chronic inflammation of the gastric mucosa (gastritis), which can lead to increased acid secretion and impaired mucosal defence, predisposing to ulcer formation. NSAIDs cause damage primarily by inhibiting cyclo-oxygenase (COX) enzymes, which reduces the production of prostaglandins that are vital for maintaining the protective mucosal barrier and blood flow.

In Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD), symptoms arise from the reflux of acidic stomach contents into the oesophagus, most commonly due to transient relaxations of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS).

Functional Dyspepsia is the most common form, but its pathophysiology is least understood. It is considered a disorder of gut-brain interaction. Proposed mechanisms include visceral hypersensitivity (where normal physiological stimuli are perceived as painful), delayed gastric emptying, and impaired gastric accommodation to a meal. Psychological factors and prior gastrointestinal infections can also play a role.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing, it is essential to consider potential risks, interactions, and long-term effects.

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

  • Contraindications: Hypersensitivity is the main contraindication. Use with caution in patients with or at risk of osteoporosis and in those at risk of C. difficile infection.
  • Interactions: PPIs can reduce the absorption of some drugs that require an acidic environment (e.g., ketoconazole, itraconazole). There is a well-described interaction with clopidogrel (omeprazole and esomeprazole are potent CYP2C19 inhibitors), potentially reducing its antiplatelet effect, though the clinical significance is debated.
  • MHRA Alerts & Long-term Risks: Be aware of MHRA advice regarding long-term use, which has been associated with a small increased risk of fractures, hypomagnesaemia, subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus, and microscopic colitis. Always aim to step down to the lowest effective dose.

H. pylori Eradication Therapy

  • Contraindications: Based on the individual antibiotic components (e.g., penicillin allergy for amoxicillin). Clarithromycin is contraindicated with certain statins (e.g., simvastatin).
  • Side Effects & Risks: Common side effects include diarrhoea, nausea, and taste disturbance. C. difficile infection is a risk. Advise patients to complete the full course to reduce the risk of treatment failure and antibiotic resistance.

Antacids & Alginates

  • Interactions: Can reduce the absorption of many other drugs, including antibiotics, levothyroxine, and bisphosphonates. Advise patients to leave a 2-hour gap between taking antacids and other medications.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Recurrent abdominal pain is common, but true dyspepsia is less so. Red flags (e.g., weight loss, GI bleeding, persistent vomiting) warrant urgent specialist referral. *H. pylori* test-and-treat strategies are not typically used in children in primary care. Management is often led by paediatricians.

Older adults

Older adults have a higher risk of serious pathology like gastric cancer and peptic ulcer complications (bleeding, perforation). There should be a lower threshold for considering endoscopy, especially for new-onset symptoms. They are also more susceptible to medication side effects (e.g., NSAID-induced ulcers) and long-term PPI complications. A thorough medication review is critical.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Dyspepsia and heartburn are extremely common in pregnancy due to hormonal effects on the lower oesophageal sphincter and pressure from the growing uterus. First-line management is lifestyle and dietary advice. Antacids and alginates are considered safe to use. If symptoms persist, a PPI such as omeprazole may be considered if the benefits outweigh the risks, after discussion with the patient. Most PPIs are considered safe in breastfeeding, but latest guidance should always be checked.

People with significant comorbidities

In patients with cardiovascular disease, atypical chest or epigastric pain should always raise suspicion of a cardiac cause. For those on long-term NSAIDs for conditions like rheumatoid arthritis, co-prescription of a PPI for gastroprotection is recommended if they are at high risk of GI complications. Drug interactions with PPIs should be carefully considered in patients on polypharmacy.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Young Professional with Functional Dyspepsia

  • Presentation: A 32-year-old marketing manager presents with a 6-month history of intermittent epigastric burning and bloating. It is worse with coffee and during stressful periods at work. There are no red flag symptoms.
  • Assessment: Examination is unremarkable. A stool antigen test for *H. pylori* is performed and comes back negative.
  • Diagnosis: Functional dyspepsia.
  • Management: The GP explains the diagnosis and reassures her. They provide lifestyle advice regarding reducing caffeine intake and managing stress. They prescribe a one-month course of omeprazole to be taken 30 minutes before breakfast. A follow-up is arranged to review symptoms and plan to step down to as-needed use. Clear safety netting advice is given.

Case Study 2: The Older Adult with ALARM Symptoms

  • Presentation: A 68-year-old male smoker presents with a 2-month history of new-onset indigestion. Over the last month, he feels food is "getting stuck" when he swallows and he has lost about 5kg in weight without trying.
  • Assessment: The presence of dysphagia and unintentional weight loss in a man over 55 are major red flags. Examination is otherwise normal.
  • Diagnosis & Action: Suspected oesophageal or gastric cancer. The GP explains the need for an urgent hospital test to look at his gullet and stomach. An urgent two-week wait direct access endoscopy is arranged and the patient is counselled on the reasons for the urgent referral.

Case Study 3: NSAID-Induced Dyspepsia

  • Presentation: A 62-year-old female with osteoarthritis has been taking naproxen for 3 months. She presents with new, persistent epigastric pain.
  • Assessment: Her symptoms are highly likely to be due to her NSAID. There are no red flags. Her abdomen is soft with mild epigastric tenderness.
  • Management: The GP discusses the link between the NSAID and her symptoms. They review her analgesia, but she feels naproxen is essential for her mobility. The GP co-prescribes lansoprazole for gastroprotection, explaining it will help protect her stomach lining from the NSAID. She is advised to take the lansoprazole regularly and given safety netting advice on signs of GI bleeding.
