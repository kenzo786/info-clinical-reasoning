Oral Thrush (Candidiasis) in Primary Care
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Oral thrush, or oral candidiasis, is a common fungal infection of the mouth, most frequently caused by an overgrowth of Candida albicans yeast. While this yeast is a normal commensal in the mouth for many people, it can become pathogenic when local or systemic factors disrupt the normal balance of oral microflora. In primary care, patients often present with symptoms that develop suddenly.

The classic presentation is of pseudomembranous candidiasis, which appears as creamy-white, slightly raised patches or plaques on the tongue, inner cheeks, palate, or pharynx. These patches, often described as looking like cottage cheese, can typically be wiped away, revealing a red, raw, and sometimes slightly bleeding mucosal surface underneath. Patients may complain of a loss of taste, an unpleasant taste in the mouth, or a "cottony" feeling. The condition can cause soreness and a burning sensation, which may lead to difficulties in eating and drinking.

Common triggers include recent courses of broad-spectrum antibiotics, use of inhaled or systemic corticosteroids, poorly fitting dentures, and suboptimal oral hygiene. Underlying medical conditions such as diabetes mellitus, nutritional deficiencies (iron, B12, folate), and immunosuppression (e.g., due to HIV or chemotherapy) are significant predisposing factors. While often straightforward to treat, recurrence is common if the underlying trigger is not addressed.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While most cases of oral thrush can be managed in primary care, certain features should prompt further investigation or urgent referral to exclude serious pathology or systemic infection.

  • Signs of systemic illness or oesophageal spread: Symptoms such as fever, difficulty or pain on swallowing (odynophagia), or the sensation of food getting stuck in the throat suggest the infection may have spread to the oesophagus (Candida oesophagitis). This is more common in severely immunocompromised individuals and warrants urgent assessment, potentially including endoscopy.
  • Suspected underlying immunosuppression: The new onset of oral thrush in an otherwise healthy adult with no obvious risk factors (e.g., recent antibiotics/steroid use) should raise suspicion of an undiagnosed systemic condition, most notably HIV infection or diabetes mellitus. Routine screening for these conditions is warranted.
  • Widespread or recurrent infection despite treatment: If oral thrush is extensive, fails to respond to initial topical antifungal treatment, or recurs quickly after treatment cessation, this indicates a possible underlying systemic cause, a resistant Candida species, or significant immunosuppression. Referral for specialist investigation and management may be required.
  • Associated weight loss or other systemic symptoms: Unexplained weight loss, persistent fatigue, or other constitutional symptoms in a patient with oral thrush could indicate an underlying malignancy or other serious systemic disease requiring investigation.
  • Suspicion of malignancy: If a white patch in the mouth is non-scrapable, firm, or associated with ulceration, induration, or bleeding, it must be distinguished from potentially malignant disorders like leukoplakia or squamous cell carcinoma. An urgent referral to an oral medicine or maxillofacial specialist is indicated in such cases.
3. Focused history and clinical assessment

A structured approach to the consultation is key to diagnosing oral thrush and identifying underlying predisposing factors.

History Taking

  • Presenting Complaint: Clarify the nature of the oral symptoms (e.g., white patches, soreness, altered taste, difficulty swallowing). Ask about onset and duration.
  • Medical History: Systematically enquire about risk factors: recent antibiotic or steroid (inhaled or oral) use, diabetes, immunosuppressive conditions (HIV, cancer), autoimmune diseases (Sjögren's syndrome), and nutritional deficiencies (anaemia). Ask about symptoms suggestive of undiagnosed diabetes (polydipsia, polyuria) or HIV.
  • Dental and Social History: Ask about denture use (fit, hygiene routine), smoking status, and alcohol intake. Poor oral hygiene is a significant contributor.
  • Medication Review: Review the patient's full medication list, paying close attention to broad-spectrum antibiotics, corticosteroids, and drugs that can cause a dry mouth (e.g., anticholinergics, diuretics).

Clinical Examination

  • Oral Examination: Inspect the entire oral mucosa systematically (tongue, buccal mucosa, palate, gingivae, pharynx). Note the presence, appearance, and distribution of any lesions. Gently attempt to wipe off a white patch with a gauze or tongue depressor to see if it leaves a raw, erythematous base, characteristic of pseudomembranous candidiasis. Check for angular cheilitis (sore, cracked corners of the mouth).
  • Denture Examination: If the patient wears dentures, ask them to remove them. Inspect the dentures for cleanliness and the underlying mucosa for signs of denture stomatitis (inflammation).
  • General Assessment: Assess for signs of systemic illness or nutritional deficiency (e.g., pallor, cachexia). There are no specific scoring tools for oral thrush, but the clinical picture guides the need for further investigation (e.g., blood tests for glucose, FBC, haematinics, HIV test).
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

The primary differential is for white lesions in the mouth. A key initial diagnostic step is determining if the white lesion can be scraped off.

Lesions that can typically be scraped off

  • Oral Thrush (Pseudomembranous Candidiasis): Presents as creamy-white plaques that reveal an erythematous base when wiped away. Often associated with soreness and risk factors.
  • Food debris / Materia Alba: A soft accumulation of bacteria, food debris, and epithelial cells that can be easily wiped away, revealing normal underlying mucosa. Often related to poor oral hygiene.
  • Slough from a chemical or thermal burn: A recent history of contact with hot food/liquid or a caustic substance (e.g., aspirin held against the mucosa) can cause a necrotic white surface that sloughs off.

Lesions that cannot be scraped off (Red Flag Differentials)

  • Leukoplakia: A persistent white patch that cannot be wiped off and is not attributable to any other diagnosis. It is a potentially malignant disorder, and biopsy is often required. Smoking is a major risk factor.
  • Oral Lichen Planus: An autoimmune condition that often presents as a network of fine white lines (Wickham's striae) on the buccal mucosa, but can also appear as plaques or erosive lesions. It is typically bilateral.
  • Oral Hairy Leukoplakia: A benign, corrugated or "hairy" white lesion, usually on the lateral borders of the tongue. It is caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) and is almost exclusively seen in individuals with significant immunosuppression, particularly HIV.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma: Can present as a white patch (or a red patch, or a mixed red/white ulcerated lesion). It is often indurated, may bleed, and is a crucial diagnosis not to miss.
  • Frictional Keratosis: A white patch caused by chronic mechanical irritation (e.g., from a sharp tooth or ill-fitting denture). It usually resolves on removal of the irritant.
5. Management options in primary care

Management involves treating the infection with antifungal medication and, crucially, identifying and addressing any predisposing factors to prevent recurrence.

Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care Advice

  • Oral Hygiene: Advise meticulous oral hygiene, including regular brushing of teeth (and tongue). For denture wearers, it is essential to remove dentures overnight, clean them thoroughly with a brush and paste, and soak them in a denture cleaning solution.
  • Dietary Advice: Limiting intake of sugary foods and drinks may be helpful, as yeast thrives on sugar.
  • Inhaler Technique: For patients using corticosteroid inhalers, advise them to rinse their mouth with water and spit out after each use, and to use a spacer device to reduce oral deposition of the steroid.
  • Smoking Cessation: Provide advice and support for smoking cessation.
  • Managing Dry Mouth: Advise on sipping water regularly and using saliva substitutes if a dry mouth is a contributing factor.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Options

There are no specific OTC antifungal medicines for oral thrush. Management focuses on hygiene measures. Antiseptic mouthwashes may disrupt the normal oral flora and are not generally recommended.

Prescription-Only Medicines (POMs)

  • Topical Antifungals: These are first-line for uncomplicated oral thrush. The choice depends on local guidance and patient factors.
    • Miconazole Oral Gel: An imidazole antifungal that is effective against Candida species. The gel should be held in the mouth for as long as possible before swallowing. [Rx info]
    • Nystatin Suspension: A polyene antifungal. The suspension should be swished around the mouth and held for as long as possible before swallowing. [Rx info]
  • Systemic Antifungals: Reserved for cases that are extensive, severe, have not responded to topical treatment, or in patients who are immunocompromised.
    • Fluconazole Capsules: A triazole antifungal that is taken orally and acts systemically. It is highly effective but has a greater potential for drug interactions and side effects than topical agents. [Rx info]
6. Evidence base and guidelines summary

UK management of oral candidiasis is guided by sources such as the British National Formulary (BNF), Clinical Knowledge Summaries (CKS), and guidance from professional bodies like the British Association for Oral and Maxillofacial Surgeons. There is no single, overarching NICE guideline specifically for oral thrush in adults in primary care, but principles are drawn from guidelines on denture care, HIV, and cancer-related complications.

The consensus approach is to use a topical antifungal as first-line treatment for uncomplicated cases. NICE CKS recommends a 7-day course of miconazole oral gel as the first choice for adults, with nystatin suspension as an alternative if miconazole is unsuitable. Treatment should be continued for 48 hours after symptoms have resolved to prevent relapse.

Systemic antifungals like oral fluconazole are recommended for patients with widespread infection, those who are unresponsive to topical therapy, or those who are severely immunocompromised. For recurrent thrush, the key principle is to investigate and manage underlying causes. Guidelines emphasise the importance of addressing predisposing factors such as optimising glycaemic control in diabetics, reviewing medications, improving oral and denture hygiene, and smoking cessation.

7. Patient communication and counselling

Clear communication is essential for effective treatment and preventing recurrence.

Counselling for Self-Care and Diagnosis

  • Explain the Cause: Reassure the patient that thrush is caused by an overgrowth of a yeast that normally lives harmlessly in the mouth and is not usually contagious. Explain that it has likely occurred because of a specific trigger (e.g., "the antibiotics you just took have upset the normal balance of bacteria in your mouth").
  • Address Underlying Factors: Clearly explain the importance of self-care measures. For denture wearers, provide explicit instructions on cleaning. For inhaler users, demonstrate correct technique and the "rinse and spit" method.
  • Validate Concerns: Acknowledge the discomfort and nuisance of the symptoms, but provide reassurance that it is usually easily treated.

Counselling for Prescribed Treatments

  • Instructions for Topical Agents: Emphasise the correct application. For miconazole gel or nystatin suspension, advise applying it after food and holding it in the mouth for as long as possible to maximise contact time with the mucosa before swallowing.
  • Duration of Treatment: Advise the patient to complete the full course of treatment, typically continuing for at least 48 hours after symptoms have cleared, to eradicate the infection completely.
  • Drug Interactions: When prescribing, particularly miconazole or fluconazole, check for and advise on significant potential interactions (e.g., with statins or warfarin).
  • Side Effects: Briefly counsel on common side effects, such as nausea or gastrointestinal upset, and when to seek advice.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is a critical component of the consultation to ensure patients know when to seek further help.

Advise the patient to re-consult their GP or seek further medical advice if they experience any of the following:

  • Symptoms do not improve: If the thrush does not start to improve within 7 days of starting treatment, or has not completely resolved by the end of the course.
  • Worsening symptoms: If the soreness or white patches spread or become more severe despite treatment.
  • Difficulty or pain swallowing: The development of odynophagia or dysphagia could indicate the infection has spread to the oesophagus and requires urgent assessment.
  • Recurrence of infection: If the thrush returns shortly after completing treatment, as this suggests an unaddressed underlying cause.
  • Development of new, concerning features: Such as non-scrapable white patches, lumps, or ulcers in the mouth.

For most uncomplicated cases, routine follow-up is not necessary if symptoms resolve as expected. However, if investigations for an underlying cause (e.g., blood tests for diabetes) have been initiated, a planned follow-up to discuss the results is essential.

9. Pathophysiology

Oral candidiasis results from the opportunistic proliferation of Candida species, predominantly Candida albicans. C. albicans is a dimorphic fungus, meaning it can exist in two forms: a budding yeast (blastospore) and a filamentous hyphal form.

In a healthy individual, Candida exists as a harmless commensal yeast, its growth kept in check by the host's immune system (particularly cell-mediated immunity via T-cells and phagocytes like neutrophils), competition from the resident bacterial microflora, and salivary antimicrobial proteins (e.g., lysozyme, lactoferrin). When this local ecosystem is disrupted, Candida can transform into its pathogenic hyphal form.

This transformation is key to its virulence. The hyphae are able to adhere to and invade the superficial layers of the oral epithelium, leading to inflammation and tissue damage. In pseudomembranous candidiasis (thrush), the characteristic white plaques are composed of tangled masses of fungal hyphae, desquamated epithelial cells, fibrin, bacteria, and inflammatory cells. The underlying erythema and bleeding upon removal of the plaque are due to this superficial mucosal invasion and the associated inflammatory response. Systemic and local risk factors (like immunosuppression or antibiotic use) lower the host's defences, creating a permissive environment for this transition from harmless coloniser to invasive pathogen.

10. Treatment-specific considerations

When prescribing antifungals, it is crucial to consider their specific profiles, contraindications, and interaction risks.

Miconazole Oral Gel

  • Interactions: Miconazole is a potent inhibitor of the cytochrome P450 enzyme CYP3A4 and a moderate inhibitor of CYP2C9. It can significantly increase the concentration of many drugs, most notably warfarin (risking dangerous INR elevation and bleeding) and certain statins (e.g., simvastatin, increasing risk of myopathy). It is contraindicated in patients taking warfarin. Always check for interactions before prescribing.
  • Contraindications: Besides use with interacting medicines, it is contraindicated in those with a known hypersensitivity and in patients with hepatic dysfunction.

Nystatin Suspension

  • Interactions: Nystatin is not absorbed from the GI tract, so it has no systemic effects or drug interactions. This makes it a safer choice in patients on complex medication regimens (e.g., warfarin).
  • Efficacy: It is generally considered slightly less effective than miconazole but remains a valuable topical option.

Fluconazole Capsules

  • Interactions: As a systemic azole, fluconazole is also a CYP enzyme inhibitor (CYP2C9, 2C19, and 3A4) and can interact with numerous drugs, including warfarin, phenytoin, and some sulphonylureas. The risk of interactions must be carefully assessed.
  • Contraindications: Avoid in pregnancy if possible. Use with caution in patients with hepatic or renal impairment. It can prolong the QT interval, so caution is needed with other QT-prolonging drugs.
  • Resistance: Overuse can contribute to the development of resistant Candida strains, such as C. glabrata or C. krusei, which are intrinsically less sensitive to fluconazole.
11. Management in special populations

Children

Oral thrush is very common in infants. Management is typically with topical nystatin suspension or miconazole oral gel (age-appropriate licensing must be checked; miconazole gel has a theoretical choking risk in very young infants). It's important to treat the mother's nipples if she is breastfeeding to prevent reinfection. Persistent or recurrent thrush in an older child warrants investigation for underlying issues like immune deficiency or unrecognised diabetes.

Older adults

Older adults are a high-risk group due to factors like denture wearing, polypharmacy causing dry mouth, and comorbidities. Management should focus heavily on optimising denture hygiene. Polypharmacy increases the risk of drug interactions, making topical nystatin a potentially safer first-line choice over miconazole in patients taking warfarin or statins. Atypical presentations, such as atrophic (red) rather than pseudomembranous candidiasis, may be more common under dentures.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Topical treatments (nystatin or miconazole) are considered safe for use during pregnancy and breastfeeding as systemic absorption is minimal. Oral fluconazole should be avoided, particularly high doses in the first trimester, due to a potential risk of teratogenicity. If a mother is breastfeeding, it may be necessary to treat her nipples with a topical antifungal cream to prevent cross-infection with the baby.

People with significant comorbidities

In patients who are significantly immunocompromised (e.g., undergoing chemotherapy, high-dose steroid therapy, or with advanced HIV), oral thrush can be more severe, extensive, and prone to relapse. There is a higher risk of oesophageal extension. These patients often require systemic antifungal treatment (e.g., oral fluconazole) from the outset and may need longer courses or prophylactic therapy. The threshold for seeking specialist advice is much lower. Drug interactions are a major consideration in this group due to complex medication regimens.

12. Illustrative clinical case studies

Case Study 1: The Inhaler User

  • Presentation: A 58-year-old woman with asthma presents with a sore mouth for the past two weeks. She has noticed white patches on her tongue.
  • Assessment: Her medical history confirms asthma, for which she uses a regular inhaled corticosteroid (ICS). She admits she doesn't always rinse her mouth after using it. Examination reveals white plaques on the dorsum of her tongue which, when gently scraped, leave a red base. The rest of the examination is normal.
  • Diagnosis: Oral candidiasis, secondary to ICS use.
  • Management: She is prescribed a course of miconazole oral gel. The GP counsels her on the cause and provides crucial advice on improving her inhaler technique: using a spacer and consistently rinsing her mouth with water and spitting after each dose. Safety netting is provided, advising her to return if it doesn't clear.

Case Study 2: The Denture Wearer on Warfarin

  • Presentation: An 82-year-old man complains of soreness at the corners of his mouth and under his top denture.
  • Assessment: He is on warfarin for atrial fibrillation. Examination reveals angular cheilitis and marked erythema on the palate conforming to the shape of his upper denture (denture stomatitis). He mentions he wears his dentures day and night. His INR is stable.
  • Diagnosis: Chronic atrophic candidiasis (denture stomatitis) with associated angular cheilitis.
  • Management: Given his warfarin use, miconazole is contraindicated. The GP prescribes nystatin suspension to be swished in the mouth and advises applying a small amount to the fitting surface of the denture before insertion. He is strongly advised to start removing his dentures overnight and to clean them meticulously. Safety netting includes returning if the soreness persists or if he develops white plaques. A dental review to check the denture fit is also recommended.
