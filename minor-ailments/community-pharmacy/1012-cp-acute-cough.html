Acute Cough in Community Pharmacy
Context: Acute Cough in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute cough is one of the most common reasons for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It is defined as a cough lasting for less than three weeks. In most cases, it is a symptom of an acute, self-limiting viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).

Patients typically present with a sudden onset of a dry or productive cough, often accompanied by other URTI symptoms such as sore throat, nasal congestion, sneezing, and malaise. A low-grade fever may be present. The cough itself, while other symptoms may resolve within a week, can persist. It is important to advise patients that a post-viral cough can commonly last for two to three weeks.

Common triggers are viral infections like the common cold, influenza, or COVID-19. The condition is usually benign and resolves without specific medical intervention. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations regarding the cough's natural duration.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Systemic signs of severe illness: Such as rigors, significant fever (>38°C), or marked breathlessness. This may indicate severe pneumonia or sepsis.
  • Chest pain: Pleuritic pain may suggest pneumonia, pulmonary embolism (PE), or pneumothorax. A dull, persistent, or central crushing pain requires urgent cardiac assessment.
  • Haemoptysis: Coughing up blood, especially if more than a minimal streak, is a red flag for conditions including PE, tuberculosis, bronchiectasis, or malignancy.
  • Marked shortness of breath: A respiratory rate >30 breaths/min, inability to complete sentences, or use of accessory muscles suggests significant respiratory compromise. Consider pneumonia, PE, or acute exacerbation of asthma/COPD.
  • Signs of sepsis: Confusion (new onset), cyanosis, hypotension, or tachycardia. These indicate a systemic inflammatory response to infection and require emergency referral.
  • Suspected foreign body: A history of sudden onset of choking or coughing, particularly in children or vulnerable adults, requires urgent assessment.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Individuals with significant immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, or uncontrolled HIV) are at high risk of severe infections and require a low threshold for referral.
  • Persistent unilateral wheeze: This could indicate an inhaled foreign body or an endobronchial lesion.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the identity and age of the patient. Many OTC cough products have specific age restrictions. This question also establishes if the patient is present or if it's a third-party consultation, which requires extra care.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the cough (dry, chesty, productive) and identify any associated symptoms (fever, sore throat, aches) or red flags (chest pain, haemoptysis, breathlessness). This helps tailor the product recommendation.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To differentiate an acute cough (<3 weeks) from a chronic cough (>8 weeks) which requires mandatory GP referral. A persistent cough of 3-4 weeks may also warrant a referral if it's not improving.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments or self-care measures the patient has already tried. This avoids recommending a product that has already failed and helps understand the progression of the illness.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for potential drug interactions with OTC products (e.g., SSRIs and dextromethorphan) and to identify any prescribed medicines that could be causing the cough (e.g., ACE inhibitors like ramipril). It also helps identify comorbidities (e.g., asthma, COPD, diabetes).
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage, daignosis and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting viral infections.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI): The most frequent cause. Characterised by coryzal symptoms, malaise, and a cough.
  • Acute Bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchi, almost always viral. Presents with a prominent cough, which may produce clear or coloured sputum.
  • Post-viral Cough: A persistent cough following an initial URTI, due to bronchial hyper-responsiveness.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Pneumonia: Suspect if cough is accompanied by fever, pleuritic pain, and signs of systemic illness.
  • Acute Exacerbation of Asthma/COPD: Suspect in patients with a known history who report increased breathlessness and wheeze.
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE): A key differential if there is pleuritic pain, haemoptysis, or sudden breathlessness.
  • Heart Failure: Consider if the cough is accompanied by breathlessness when lying down or swollen ankles.
  • Lung Cancer: Suspect in cases of persistent cough (>3 weeks) with haemoptysis or unexplained weight loss, especially in smokers.
  • Whooping Cough (Pertussis): Consider in cases of a prolonged, severe, paroxysmal cough.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief. Evidence for the effectiveness of many products is limited, and non-pharmacological advice remains paramount.

First-Line Advice (All Coughs)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise adequate rest and hydration. A hot drink with lemon and honey can be soothing (honey is unsuitable for infants under 1 year).
  • Demulcents (e.g., Simple Linctus, Glycerin):
    • Mechanism: Coat the pharynx, providing a soothing effect on the throat.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL. Available for adults and children (check product-specific age licensing).
    • Counselling: Advise that they do not suppress the cough reflex but can reduce irritation. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options / Limited Evidence

  • Expectorants (e.g., Guaifenesin):
    • Mechanism: Claim to increase the volume and reduce the viscosity of sputum, making a 'chesty' cough more productive.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Age restrictions apply (typically not for under 6s).
    • Counselling: Advise maintaining good hydration to aid the effect. Evidence for clinical efficacy is limited. [Rx info]
  • Suppressants (e.g., Dextromethorphan):
    • Mechanism: A weak opioid analogue that acts on the cough centre in the brainstem to suppress a dry, tickly, non-productive cough.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Not for use in children under 6 years.
    • Counselling: For dry coughs only. Can cause drowsiness. Check for interactions (e.g., SSRIs). [Rx info]

Withdrawn and Reclassified Medicines

  • Pholcodine: EXPLICITLY WITHDRAWN. All products were recalled in March 2023. Advise patients to not use any old products and to inform their anaesthetist if they have used it in the past 12 months before any surgery.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for acute cough, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Doxycycline):
    Context: These are not effective for viral coughs and are only prescribed by a GP if bacterial pneumonia is suspected or the patient is at high risk of complications. Pharmacists play a key role in explaining this to patients requesting antibiotics, upholding antimicrobial stewardship. Under a service like Pharmacy First, pharmacists may prescribe antibiotics for other conditions (e.g. sinusitis) but not for uncomplicated acute cough. [Rx advice]
  • Codeine Linctus:
    Context: Reclassified to a POM in 2024 due to risks of dependence and overdose. A GP may prescribe it for a short duration for a severe, troublesome dry cough where other measures have failed. Pharmacists should not recommend codeine-containing products available for other indications (e.g., co-codamol) for a cough. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral might be needed and what treatments a doctor might consider, preventing the perception that the pharmacy is a barrier to "stronger" medicine.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for acute cough.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the natural, self-limiting course of a viral cough. A key phrase is: "It's normal for a cough to last for up to three or even four weeks after a cold, so don't be alarmed if it lingers."
  • Justify No Antibiotics: When a patient requests antibiotics, provide a clear, confident explanation. "This cough is caused by a virus, and antibiotics only work against bacteria. They won't help you get better any faster but could cause side effects."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Recommend rest, plenty of fluids, and soothing hot drinks with honey and lemon (for over 1s).
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain what the recommended product does (e.g., "This won't stop the cough, but it will soothe the irritation in your throat") and highlight key warnings (e.g., "This can make you feel drowsy, so be careful if you need to drive").
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to make sure I've explained that clearly, can you tell me when you should go and see your GP?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help. This ensures that a patient with a self-limiting illness knows when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The cough has not improved at all within 3-4 weeks.
  • They start to feel much more unwell.
  • They have a pre-existing condition like asthma or COPD and feel their symptoms are worsening more than usual.
  • They develop new symptoms, such as a rash or persistent headache.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Coughing up blood.
  • Significant shortness of breath or difficulty breathing.
  • Sharp, stabbing chest pain.
  • New and unexplained confusion.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

The cough reflex is a vital protective mechanism for the respiratory system, clearing the airways of foreign material, microbes, and excess secretions. It is a complex reflex arc involving sensory receptors, afferent pathways, a central cough centre, and efferent pathways.

Sensory receptors (nociceptors and mechanoreceptors) are located throughout the respiratory tract, from the larynx down to the terminal bronchioles. In an acute viral URTI, the primary trigger is inflammation. Viral pathogens cause epithelial damage and inflammatory cell infiltration. This releases inflammatory mediators such as bradykinin, prostaglandins, and tachykinins.

These mediators sensitise the afferent nerve endings of the vagus nerve (cranial nerve X). This leads to an exaggerated response to stimuli like cold air or inhaled particles—a phenomenon known as bronchial hyper-responsiveness. The afferent signals travel via the vagus nerve to the cough centre in the medulla oblongata of the brainstem. The cough centre processes these signals and initiates the efferent response. This involves coordinated signals to the diaphragm, abdominal muscles, and larynx, resulting in the characteristic explosive expiratory manoeuvre of a cough. The persistence of cough after the initial infection has cleared (post-viral cough) is thought to be due to this ongoing airway inflammation and neuronal hypersensitivity.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and interactions.

Dextromethorphan

  • Interactions: Risk of serotonin syndrome when combined with serotonergic drugs like SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, sertraline), SNRIs, and MAOIs. Always check the patient's medication history for these.
  • Side Effects: Can cause drowsiness and dizziness. A "Do not drive or operate machinery" warning is appropriate.
  • Misuse Potential: Be aware of the potential for misuse (as a recreational hallucinogen at high doses) and be vigilant for requests that seem inappropriate.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Hidden Ingredients: Many "all-in-one" products contain paracetamol or ibuprofen. It is essential to counsel patients not to take additional separate doses of these analgesics to avoid accidental overdose.
  • Decongestants: Products containing decongestants (e.g., pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine) are contraindicated in patients with hypertension, heart disease, and in those taking MAOIs.

Regulatory Updates

  • Pholcodine: WITHDRAWN. Do not supply or recommend. Advise patients of the reason (anaphylaxis risk with general anaesthesia).
  • Codeine: RECLASSIFIED. Linctus is now POM. Do not recommend OTC products containing codeine (e.g., for pain) to treat a cough.
11. Use in special populations

Children

OTC cough and cold medicines containing active ingredients are not for use in children under 6. For children 6-12, they should be used with caution and for no more than 5 days. The first-line recommendation for all children is a simple demulcent such as glycerin or a warm drink of lemon and honey (honey only for children >1 year). Always check product-specific licensing.

Older adults

Be cautious with products that can cause drowsiness or confusion, such as dextromethorphan, due to the increased risk of falls. Check for polypharmacy and potential interactions. A referral to the GP should be considered sooner in this group if symptoms are not resolving.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with rest, fluids, and steam inhalation is first-line. Simple linctus or honey/lemon are safe. Most other OTC cough products should be avoided unless specifically advised by a doctor. Dextromethorphan and guaifenesin are not recommended. Always check the latest BNF/specialist resources. The primary role is to advise self-care and refer to the GP if symptoms are severe.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with asthma or COPD, avoid suppressants like dextromethorphan as the cough may be productive and necessary for airway clearance. For patients with hypertension or heart disease, avoid any products containing decongestants. For patients with diabetes, recommend sugar-free formulations where possible.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Viral Cough & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I need something for this really annoying dry, tickly cough."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. To get you the right thing, can I ask a few quick questions? First, who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me. I'm 35."
  • Pharmacist: "And what other symptoms do you have?"
  • Patient: "Just a bit of a runny nose. The cough started about three days ago after a cold."
  • Pharmacist: "Have you tried anything for it yet?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and do you take any regular prescribed medication?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing at all."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a straightforward viral cough. A simple linctus would be a good choice to soothe the tickle. Alternatively, a product with dextromethorphan would help to suppress the cough reflex. It can make some people drowsy, so be careful with driving. The most important thing is to rest and drink plenty of fluids. A cough like this can last for a couple of weeks, so don't worry if it doesn't vanish overnight. If it's not better in 3 weeks, or you start feeling very unwell or breathless, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something for my dad's cough. It's really bad."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can try to help. How old is your dad?"
  • Patient: "He's 72."
  • Pharmacist: "And what are his symptoms exactly?"
  • Patient: "He's had the cough for a few days, but today he seems a bit confused, and he said it hurts in his chest when he breathes in."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. The confusion and the chest pain are signs that he needs to be assessed by a doctor urgently. An over-the-counter medicine isn't appropriate here. You need to contact his GP surgery straight away and tell them his symptoms. If you can't get an appointment today, you should call 111 for advice. Does that make sense?"
