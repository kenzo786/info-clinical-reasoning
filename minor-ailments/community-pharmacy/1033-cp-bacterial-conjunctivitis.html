Bacterial Conjunctivitis in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Bacterial conjunctivitis is a common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, characterised by inflammation of the conjunctiva. It typically presents with acute onset of a red eye, which is often unilateral initially but frequently spreads to the other eye within a day or two. The hallmark symptom is a mucopurulent discharge, causing the eyelids to feel sticky or be stuck together, especially upon waking.

Patients usually complain of a gritty or burning sensation but should not have significant pain or photophobia. Vision is not typically affected, though it may be momentarily blurred by discharge. The condition is usually self-limiting, with most cases resolving within 5 to 7 days without any specific medical intervention.

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care and hygiene, recommend appropriate OTC treatments where indicated, and manage patient expectations regarding the natural duration of the illness.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP, specialist optometrist, or A&E.

  • Marked eye pain or photophobia: Suggests inflammation of deeper structures, such as in keratitis, scleritis, or anterior uveitis. Simple conjunctivitis causes irritation or grittiness, not true pain.
  • Significant reduction in visual acuity: Any vision loss that cannot be cleared by blinking away discharge is a major red flag for conditions like microbial keratitis, uveitis, or acute glaucoma.
  • Ciliary flush: A pattern of deep, violaceous redness concentrated in a ring around the cornea, indicating inflammation of the cornea or deeper structures. This requires careful observation.
  • Corneal issues: Any haziness, opacity, or a white spot on the cornea may indicate an ulcer or keratitis.
  • Pupil abnormalities: A distorted, irregular, fixed, or unreactive pupil is a sign of serious pathology such as anterior uveitis or acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Contact lens wear: A contact lens wearer with a painful red eye must be urgently referred to rule out microbial keratitis, a potentially sight-threatening condition. This is a critical red flag in a pharmacy setting.
  • Vesicular rash consistent with shingles: A rash in the distribution of the ophthalmic nerve (forehead, side of nose) suggests Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus.
  • Severe purulent discharge in a neonate (within 28 days of birth): This is an ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate A&E referral.
  • Proptosis or painful/restricted eye movements: May suggest orbital cellulitis, a medical emergency.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the age of the patient, as OTC treatments for conjunctivitis (e.g., chloramphenicol) have specific age restrictions (typically for use in children aged 2 and over). This also establishes if the patient is present or if it's a third-party consultation.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the condition and screen for red flags. Key questions include: "Is the eye painful or just gritty?", "Are you sensitive to light?", "Is your vision affected?", "What is the discharge like - sticky and yellow, or watery?", "Is it itchy?".
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish if the condition is acute. Symptoms that are not improving or have been present for more than 5-7 days may warrant a referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out if the patient has tried any self-care measures or other products. If they have used an OTC product correctly for 2 days with no improvement, they must be referred to their GP.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for other eye conditions (e.g., glaucoma, dry eye) or systemic conditions. It is also important to ask specifically about contact lens wear, as this is a major red flag requiring referral.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting infections.

Common Benign Causes

  • Viral Conjunctivitis: Often causes a more watery discharge, may follow a recent cold, and is often associated with a palpable pre-auricular lymph node.
  • Allergic Conjunctivitis: Itching is the dominant symptom. Usually bilateral and associated with a watery discharge and a history of atopy.
  • Blepharitis: Inflammation of the eyelid margins, causing chronic irritation, grittiness, and crusting.
  • Dry Eye Syndrome: Causes intermittent redness and a gritty, burning sensation.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Microbial Keratitis: Infection of the cornea. Presents with pain, photophobia, and reduced vision. A key differential in contact lens wearers.
  • Anterior Uveitis (Iritis): Inflammation inside the eye. Characterised by significant pain, photophobia, and ciliary flush.
  • Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma: A rapid rise in intraocular pressure. Causes severe pain, blurred vision with halos, and a semi-dilated pupil.
  • Scleritis: Severe, deep, boring eye pain. Associated with a bluish-red hue to the sclera.
  • Corneal Abrasion or Foreign Body: History of trauma or a distinct sensation of something in the eye.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is to manage symptoms and, where appropriate, treat the bacterial infection. Non-pharmacological advice is paramount for all patients.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise on good hand hygiene and using separate towels. Counsel on cleaning the eyelids with a dedicated pad of cotton wool soaked in cooled boiled water for each eye to remove discharge.
  • Lubricating Eye Drops (e.g., Hypromellose):
    • Mechanism: Soothe the eye and relieve feelings of grittiness and irritation.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL. Suitable for most patients.
    • Counselling: Can be used as required for symptomatic relief. [Rx info]

Pharmacy (P) Medicines

  • Chloramphenicol 0.5% Eye Drops / 1% Eye Ointment:
    • Mechanism: A broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotic that stops bacterial growth.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Licensed for acute bacterial conjunctivitis in adults and children aged 2 years and over.
    • Counselling: Advise on the correct dosage schedule and the full 5-day course, even if symptoms improve. Warn that drops may sting temporarily. Ointment can cause temporary blurring of vision. Stress contact lens advice. [Rx info]
  • Propamidine Isetionate 0.1% Eye Drops:
    • Mechanism: An aromatic diamidine with antiseptic properties.
    • Licensing: P medicine for minor eye infections.
    • Counselling: Can be an alternative if chloramphenicol is unsuitable. Less specific than an antibiotic. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not prescribe these for conjunctivitis (unless qualified as an Independent Prescriber), awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after a referral.

  • Fusidic Acid Eye Drops:
    Context: This is a common POM antibiotic a GP might prescribe, often favoured for its twice-daily dosing, which can be easier for children. Knowing this helps explain why a GP visit might be beneficial if adherence is a concern. [Rx advice]
  • Quinolone Eye Drops (e.g., Ofloxacin):
    Context: These are broader-spectrum antibiotics that a GP or ophthalmologist might use for more persistent infections or after microbiological investigation. This knowledge is purely for context. [Rx advice]

This awareness helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral is appropriate and what treatments a doctor might consider, preventing the perception that the pharmacy is a barrier to "stronger" medicine. It also contextualises the products available OTC versus those requiring a prescription.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for conjunctivitis.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the self-limiting nature of the condition. A key phrase is: "This type of infection usually clears up on its own in about a week. The drops can help speed this up, but hygiene is the most important thing."
  • Justify Referral: If red flags are present, provide a clear, confident reason for referral. "Because you wear contact lenses, it's much safer for you to see an optometrist straight away to rule out anything more serious. We can't sell you anything over the counter for this."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame hygiene as an active treatment. Give clear instructions on hand washing, not sharing towels, and cleaning the eyelids.
  • Product Counselling (for Chloramphenicol): Explain the dose, the need to complete the 5-day course, and how to administer drops correctly. Crucially, advise: "You must not wear contact lenses while using these drops, and for 48 hours after you finish the course."
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to make sure I've been clear, what symptoms would mean you need to see a doctor urgently?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from a GP or optometrist. This ensures that a patient treating a presumed self-limiting illness knows when to act.

If a product is supplied, advise the patient to see their GP if:

  • The symptoms have not started to improve after 2 days of using the antibiotic treatment.
  • The condition has not completely resolved after completing the 5-day course of treatment.
  • They develop any new symptoms.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (from a GP, specialist optometrist, or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms during the course of the illness, such as:

  • Significant eye pain.
  • Sensitivity to light (photophobia).
  • Worsening redness.
  • Any change or blurring of their vision.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns about their treatment.

9. Pathophysiology

Bacterial conjunctivitis is an inflammation of the conjunctiva, the transparent mucous membrane that lines the inner surface of the eyelids (the palpebral conjunctiva) and covers the anterior surface of the sclera (the bulbar conjunctiva). The condition is caused by direct contact with pathogenic bacteria.

The ocular surface has several defence mechanisms, including the flushing action of tears, which contain lysozyme and immunoglobulins. When these defences are overcome or breached (e.g., via contaminated hands, fomites), bacteria can adhere to the conjunctival epithelium and proliferate.

This colonisation triggers a type III hypersensitivity reaction and a local inflammatory response. Mast cells degranulate, and inflammatory mediators are released, causing vasodilation of the conjunctival blood vessels (leading to erythema or 'injection') and increased vascular permeability (leading to oedema/chemosis). This is followed by a chemotactic response, attracting neutrophils to the site of infection. The accumulation of neutrophils, dead epithelial cells, fibrin, and bacteria constitutes the characteristic mucopurulent discharge. In simple bacterial conjunctivitis, the inflammation is confined to the conjunctiva; the cornea and deeper ocular structures are not involved.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC eye products requires awareness of their specific risks and correct use.

Chloramphenicol

  • Contraindications: A personal or family history of blood dyscrasias like aplastic anaemia is an absolute contraindication for the pharmacist to supply. Also contraindicated in known hypersensitivity.
  • Storage: Some brands of chloramphenicol drops require refrigeration before opening. Always check the product packaging and counsel the patient accordingly. Once opened, they are typically stored at room temperature and discarded after 28 days. The ointment does not require refrigeration.
  • Course Length: It is licensed for a 5-day course. If symptoms persist, referral is necessary.

Contact Lenses

  • Universal Precaution: This is the single most important safety consideration. Advise ALL patients with a red eye to cease lens wear. Soft lenses can absorb preservatives from eye drops, causing irritation, and can harbour bacteria, dramatically increasing the risk of sight-threatening keratitis.
  • Disposal: Advise patients to discard the pair of lenses they were wearing when the infection started, along with their lens case, to prevent re-infection.

Eye Drop Instillation

  • Technique: Many patients use eye drops incorrectly. Counsel them to wash hands, pull down the lower eyelid to form a pocket, instil one drop, and then close the eye. They should not touch the dropper tip to the eye or any surface.
11. Use in special populations

Children

OTC chloramphenicol is licensed for use in children aged 2 years and over. For any child under 2, referral to the GP is mandatory. Neonatal conjunctivitis (<28 days old) is a medical emergency requiring immediate A&E referral. Self-care with lid hygiene is appropriate for all ages.

Older adults

Be mindful of physical limitations. Check if the patient will be able to administer the drops themselves or if they have a carer who can help. dexterity issues or poor vision can make administration difficult and ineffective. Dry eye syndrome is also more common in this population and can be a confounding factor.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

The standard pharmacy approach is to avoid the supply of any medicated eye drops and refer the patient to their GP for assessment. While the risk from topical chloramphenicol is low, a referral is the safest course of action to allow the prescriber to make a risk/benefit assessment. Advise on self-care and hygiene measures in the interim.

Contact lens wearers

This group should not be sold any OTC treatment for a red eye. This is not a population for treatment but a population for urgent referral to a specialist optometrist or ophthalmologist due to the high risk of microbial keratitis.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Straightforward Request and OTC Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I think I need some drops for conjunctivitis. My eye is red and was stuck together this morning."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Can I just ask a few questions to make sure we get the right thing? Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 42."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Is the eye painful or just gritty?"
  • Patient: "No, not painful, just feels like there's something in it. It's not blurry or sensitive to light."
  • Pharmacist: "And do you wear contact lenses or take any other medication?"
  • Patient: "No contacts, and no other medicines."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a simple bacterial conjunctivitis. I can supply you with chloramphenicol antibiotic eye drops. You'll need to use them for 5 days, even if it gets better sooner. It's really important to wash your hands often and use a separate towel. If it's not starting to improve in two days, or if your vision changes or it becomes painful, you must see a GP."

Scenario 2: Red Flag Referral (Contact Lens Wearer)

  • Patient: "Can I get some of those antibiotic drops? My eye is really red."
  • Pharmacist: "I can certainly help with that. Who are they for?"
  • Patient: "Me, I'm 21."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. What are your symptoms? Is it painful?"
  • Patient: "Yes, it's quite sore, and the light seems really bright."
  • Pharmacist: "The next question is very important: do you wear contact lenses?"
  • Patient: "Yes, I do."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that information. Because you wear contact lenses and the eye is painful and sensitive to light, it is not safe to sell you anything over the counter. These are signs of a possible infection on the surface of your eye which needs to be seen urgently. You should take your lenses out immediately and go to a local optometrist (optician) or the eye casualty department today. Do not wear your lenses again until you have been told it is safe to do so."
