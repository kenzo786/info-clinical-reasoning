Shingles (Herpes Zoster) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Shingles (herpes zoster) is the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). It is a condition that commonly presents in a community pharmacy, often with patients seeking advice for a painful new rash.

The condition typically begins with a prodrome of pain, burning, tingling, or itching in a specific, unilateral dermatomal distribution. This neuropathic pain can precede the rash by several days and may be accompanied by systemic symptoms like headache, photophobia, and general malaise. A few days after the onset of pain, a characteristic vesicular rash erupts in the same dermatome, starting as red patches that quickly develop into clusters of fluid-filled blisters. These vesicles then crust over within 7-10 days.

The rash is classically unilateral and does not cross the body's midline. The entire episode usually lasts 2-4 weeks. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to correctly identify the condition, differentiate it from other skin complaints, screen for red flags that require urgent medical referral, and provide advice on symptomatic relief and self-care. Early identification is crucial as prescription antiviral treatment is most effective when started promptly.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious or complicated case of shingles. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Ophthalmic Involvement (Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus): Any shingles rash affecting the forehead, scalp, or around the eye (V1 dermatome). Hutchinson's sign (vesicles on the tip, side, or root of the nose) is a critical sign indicating a high risk of sight-threatening eye complications. This requires an immediate, same-day referral to an ophthalmologist via A&E or an eye casualty unit.
  • Ear Involvement (Ramsay Hunt Syndrome): A rash in or around the ear canal, especially if associated with ipsilateral facial drooping or weakness, hearing loss, or vertigo. This requires urgent referral to a GP for assessment and specialist referral.
  • Motor Weakness: Any weakness in the muscles of the affected area (e.g., a weak grip if the rash is on the arm) requires GP referral.
  • Signs of CNS Involvement: New onset of severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, confusion, or drowsiness suggests meningitis or encephalitis and requires emergency hospital admission.
  • Disseminated Zoster: A widespread rash with more than 20 vesicles outside the single dermatome area. This indicates the infection is not well-contained and requires urgent medical assessment, especially in the immunocompromised.
  • Severely Immunocompromised Patient: Patients on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, biologics, or with uncontrolled HIV are at high risk of severe disease and must be referred urgently to their GP or specialist team.
  • Uncontrolled Pain: Pain that is severe and not managed by OTC analgesics requires GP assessment for prescription treatment.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. This is crucial as shingles is most common in older adults who are at higher risk of complications and are priority candidates for antiviral treatment. Establishes if it is a third-party consultation.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the diagnosis of shingles. Ask about the nature of the pain (burning, tingling) and the appearance of the rash (one-sided, blistering). Crucially, ask about the exact location to rule out red flags like eye or ear involvement.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To determine eligibility for prescription antiviral treatment. Antivirals are most effective if started within 72 hours of the rash appearing. This question establishes the urgency of the GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what analgesia or self-care measures the patient has tried. This helps tailor advice on further pain relief and avoids duplicating treatment.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify anyone who is immunocompromised (e.g., taking steroids, methotrexate, biologics) as they are a red flag group requiring urgent referral. Also checks for contraindications to simple analgesics like NSAIDs.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary, especially if the presentation is atypical.

Common Differentials to Consider

  • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): Can present with clustered vesicles. However, HSV infections are typically recurrent in a non-dermatomal site. 'Zosteriform simplex' can mimic shingles and may need a viral swab by a GP to confirm.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Can cause a blistering rash but distribution relates to contact with an irritant, not a dermatome. Itching is often more prominent than the neuropathic pain of shingles.
  • Impetigo: A bacterial infection with vesicles and "honey-coloured" crusts. Usually not confined to a dermatome and is less painful. Can be a secondary infection of shingles lesions.
  • Insect Bites: Typically causes itchy papules or vesicles in a random, not dermatomal, pattern.

Pre-Eruptive Pain Differentials (Before Rash Appears)

  • Before the rash, the pain of shingles can be mistaken for other conditions like sciatica, pleurisy, or cardiac pain, depending on the location. Pharmacy teams should be aware of this possibility if a patient describes a new, unexplained unilateral pain.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief of pain and discomfort. Referral for assessment for prescription antivirals is the most important intervention for eligible patients.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the core advice.
    • Rash Care: Advise keeping the rash clean and dry. Cool compresses can soothe the area.
    • Clothing: Recommend loose-fitting clothing made from natural fibres.
    • Covering: Suggest covering the rash with a non-adherent dressing (like a sterile paraffin gauze dressing). This protects the rash from irritation and prevents transmission of the virus.
  • Analgesia:
    • Mechanism: Provide pain relief.
    • Products: Paracetamol and/or ibuprofen (if suitable for the patient).
    • Counselling: Advise on correct dosages and ensure the patient is not taking other paracetamol-containing products. Check for contraindications to NSAIDs, including a history of peptic ulceration, asthma, renal impairment, or heart failure. Use NSAIDs with caution in older adults. Recommend taking with or after food.

Second-Line Options / Soothing Preparations

  • Topical Preparations (e.g., Calamine lotion):
    • Mechanism: Provides a soothing and cooling effect on the skin as the lotion evaporates.
    • Licensing: GSL.
    • Counselling: Can be applied to intact or crusted skin to relieve itching. Avoid applying to open blisters to prevent irritation. Allow the lotion to dry before covering the area.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for shingles, awareness is vital for explaining the rationale for an urgent GP referral and managing patient expectations.

  • Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir, Valaciclovir, Famciclovir):
    Context: These medicines inhibit viral replication, helping to speed healing and reduce the risk of post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN). They are most effective when started within 72 hours of rash onset; treatment may still be considered after this if new vesicles are still forming. A prompt GP referral is therefore essential for eligible patients, particularly those over 50, immunocompromised individuals, or those with non-truncal shingles.
  • Neuropathic Analgesia (e.g., Amitriptyline, Gabapentin):
    Context: For severe acute pain, or for established PHN (pain persisting beyond 3 months after rash onset), a GP may prescribe neuropathic analgesics. OTC analgesics are often insufficient for neuropathic pain. Explaining this to the patient helps justify the need for GP assessment rather than self-management alone.

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team to confidently explain why a referral is the most important clinical action, rather than simply selling an OTC product.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for a patient presenting with suspected shingles.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Diagnosis & Urgency: Clearly and simply explain what shingles is ("a reactivation of the chickenpox virus affecting one nerve"). Crucially, explain the need for a prompt GP assessment: "It's very important you contact your GP today. There is a prescription medicine that works best if it's started within three days of the rash appearing, and it can help you get better faster and reduce the risk of long-term pain."
  • Explain Infectivity: Give clear advice on preventing transmission. A key phrase is: "You can't pass shingles to someone else, but you can give chickenpox to anyone who hasn't had it before. Please avoid contact with pregnant women, young babies, and people with a weak immune system until all your blisters have dried up and scabbed over."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Recommend keeping the rash clean and covered with a non-stick dressing, wearing loose clothing, and using cool compresses. Advise on appropriate OTC painkillers.
  • The Risk of PHN: Briefly explain post-herpetic neuralgia to older patients to reinforce the importance of seeing the GP for assessment for antiviral treatment. "For some people, the nerve pain can last for a long time after the rash goes. Seeing the doctor promptly can help reduce that risk."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help after the initial consultation.

After advising referral or providing self-care advice, give specific instructions. Advise the patient to consult their GP or seek further help if:

  • The pain is severe and not controlled with simple painkillers.
  • The rash is not healing or shows signs of becoming infected (e.g., increasing redness, warmth, or weeping pus).
  • They simply feel they are getting worse rather than better.
  • The pain continues for more than a few months after the rash has healed (as this may be post-herpetic neuralgia).

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (usually A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Any rash on the forehead, nose or near the eye, combined with any eye symptoms (pain, redness, blurred vision).
  • Weakness in the face or limbs.
  • A severe headache, neck stiffness, or new confusion.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns about managing the symptoms.

9. Pathophysiology

Shingles is caused by the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a double-stranded DNA virus from the human herpesvirus family. The primary infection with VZV causes chickenpox (varicella), usually in childhood. After the resolution of the primary infection, the virus is not cleared from the body but becomes latent, ascending from skin lesions via sensory nerves to establish a dormant infection in the dorsal root ganglia, cranial nerve ganglia, or autonomic ganglia.

The virus remains latent for decades, suppressed by a robust cell-mediated immune response. Reactivation occurs when this specific VZV-specific T-cell immunity wanes, a process associated with increasing age, immunosuppressive therapy, or certain illnesses (e.g., HIV, malignancy). Upon reactivation, the virus replicates within the ganglion, causing intense inflammation, neuronal necrosis, and neuralgia.

The replicated virus then travels anterograde down the sensory nerve to the area of skin supplied by that ganglion (the dermatome). Viral replication in the epidermal cells leads to the characteristic vesicular rash. The associated acute neuritic pain is a direct result of the inflammatory process within the ganglion and peripheral nerve. If this nerve damage is severe and healing is incomplete, it can lead to the chronic, often debilitating, pain state known as post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN).

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of patient-specific risks.

Simple Analgesics

  • Paracetamol: Check if the patient is taking any other paracetamol-containing products ('all-in-one' cold and flu remedies) to avoid inadvertent overdose.
  • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen): Check for contraindications such as a history of peptic ulceration, renal impairment, heart failure, or hypersensitivity (including NSAID-exacerbated asthma). Use with caution in older adults.

Dressings

  • Adhesive Dressings: Advise against using dressings with strong adhesives directly over the blisters, as removal can damage the fragile skin. Recommend non-adherent or low-adherent dressings.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Shingles is uncommon and usually mild in healthy children. Antiviral treatment is not routinely required. Management focuses on simple analgesia and rash care. However, any child presenting with shingles should be referred to their GP for a definitive diagnosis and management plan.

Older adults

This group has the highest risk of complications, especially PHN. An urgent, same-day referral to the GP for assessment for antivirals is essential for anyone over 50. Be cautious when recommending OTC analgesics, particularly NSAIDs, due to increased risk of side effects.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

This is a mandatory referral. Any pregnant woman with a rash needs to be assessed by a doctor to confirm the diagnosis and establish a management plan. They must not be managed with OTC products alone. Shingles itself is not a high risk for the foetus, but the diagnosis needs to be certain. Advise them to contact their GP or midwife urgently.

People with significant comorbidities

Anyone who is known to be immunocompromised (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, biologics for autoimmune conditions) must be referred urgently for same-day medical assessment, as they are at high risk of severe or disseminated disease.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Urgent GP Referral for Antivirals

  • Patient: "Hello, I think I have shingles. I'm 68. The rash came up yesterday on my side and it's really burning."
  • Pharmacist (after WWHAM questioning confirms symptoms and no red flags): "Thank you for that information. You've done the right thing coming in today. This does sound like shingles, and because you're over 50 and the rash just started, it's very important you speak to your GP today. There's a prescription tablet that can help the rash heal quicker and lower the chance of having pain long-term, but it works best if you start within three days. Please call your surgery as soon as you can. For the pain now, you can take paracetamol, and covering the rash with a non-stick dressing might make it more comfortable."

Scenario 2: Emergency Referral for Ophthalmic Shingles

  • Patient: "I need some cream for these spots on my forehead. They've just appeared and they're quite sore."
  • Pharmacist: "Let me have a look. Can I ask, do you have any spots on the tip or side of your nose?"
  • Patient: "Yes, just this one here on the side of my nose."
  • Pharmacist (identifying Hutchinson's sign): "Thank you for showing me. Because of where the rash is, and especially the spot on your nose, there is a risk it could affect your eye. You need to have this checked immediately at the hospital's eye casualty department. Please go there now. Do not wait for a GP appointment. This is to protect your vision."

Scenario 3: Self-care advice post-GP visit

  • Patient: "Hi, I've just picked up my prescription for valaciclovir for shingles. The doctor said to keep the rash clean, what's the best way to do that?"
  • Pharmacist: "Yes, of course. The main thing is to keep the area clean with water and pat it dry gently with a clean towel. Don't scrub it. To stop it rubbing on your clothes and to prevent spreading the virus, it's a good idea to cover it with a non-adherent dressing - something like a paraffin gauze dressing, which we have here. It won't stick to the blisters. Just change it daily. Remember to take the tablets for the full week, even if you start to feel better."
