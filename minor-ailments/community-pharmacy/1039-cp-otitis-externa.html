Otitis Externa in Community Pharmacy
Context: Otitis Externa in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Otitis externa, or inflammation of the external ear canal, is a common condition for which people seek advice in a community pharmacy. Patients typically complain of a rapid onset of ear pain (otalgia), itching, a feeling of fullness or blockage in the ear, and sometimes a degree of hearing loss. The pain can be severe and is characteristically worsened by touching the tragus or pulling on the pinna.

A scanty discharge (otorrhoea) may be present, which can be thin and serous or thicker and purulent. It is often triggered by factors that disrupt the protective environment of the ear canal, such as swimming, excessive cleaning with cotton buds, or underlying skin conditions like eczema. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate the condition from other causes of earache, identify red flags requiring urgent referral, provide advice on self-care and analgesia, and refer to a GP for definitive diagnosis and treatment.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Suspicion of malignant (necrotising) otitis externa: This is an invasive infection of the temporal bone, most often seen in elderly, diabetic, or immunocompromised patients. Key features are severe, deep, unrelenting pain that is often worse at night. This is a medical emergency.
  • Cranial nerve palsy: The development of facial weakness (facial nerve palsy) in the context of an ear infection is a major red flag for malignant otitis externa.
  • Spreading cellulitis: Redness, swelling, and tenderness that extends beyond the ear canal to involve the pinna, face, or neck. This requires assessment for systemic antibiotics.
  • Complete canal obstruction: If the ear canal is so swollen that it is completely occluded, topical treatments cannot be administered effectively and the patient needs specialist assessment.
  • Vertigo: The presence of true spinning dizziness could indicate labyrinthitis or other inner ear pathology, warranting a medical assessment.
  • Sudden onset sensorineural hearing loss: This is not a feature of otitis externa and requires urgent medical assessment.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. This helps identify high-risk groups (e.g., elderly, very young) who may need a more cautious approach or immediate referral.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the earache. Key diagnostic questions include: "Does it hurt when you press on the cartilage at the front of the ear, or pull on the earlobe?". A "yes" strongly suggests otitis externa. Also ask about itching, discharge, and hearing loss to build a clinical picture and screen for red flags (facial weakness, severe pain, spreading redness).
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish the timeline. Otitis externa is typically acute (developing over 1-2 days). Symptoms that fail to resolve after a week of GP-prescribed treatment, or chronic symptoms, require medical review.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what has been tried. Has the patient been using cotton buds (a common cause)? Have they tried any other ear drops (e.g., olive oil, which is inappropriate for an infection)? This informs advice on what to stop doing and helps understand symptom progression.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify any significant comorbidities from their medication history. The most important conditions to identify are diabetes or any cause of immunosuppression (e.g., high-dose steroids, chemotherapy), as these patients are at high risk of severe infection and need urgent referral.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. The key task in the pharmacy is to determine if the symptoms are consistent with otitis externa or suggest another diagnosis.

Common Differentials

  • Acute Otitis Media with Perforation: The pain often improves once the eardrum perforates and discharges. Tragal tenderness is less common.
  • Furunculosis: A very painful, localised boil in the outer ear canal, rather than generalised inflammation.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Itching is often the main symptom, caused by allergens in earrings, hearing aids, or cosmetics.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Malignant (Necrotising) Otitis Externa: Suspect in diabetic/immunocompromised patients with severe, deep pain that is worse at night. This is an emergency.
  • Mastoiditis: Presents with pain and swelling over the mastoid bone behind the ear. A complication of otitis media.
  • Ramsay Hunt Syndrome: Severe pain associated with facial weakness and a vesicular rash in or around the ear.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

For established otitis externa, there are no curative OTC options and a GP referral for prescription drops is necessary. The pharmacy role is focused on analgesia, self-care advice, and offering preventative products where appropriate.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Simple Analgesia: Recommend regular paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain relief while awaiting a GP appointment. This is the most important intervention the pharmacy can provide for symptomatic relief.
  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: Advise the patient to keep the ear completely dry (use a petroleum jelly-coated cotton ball in the outer ear when showering) and to avoid inserting anything into the ear canal.

Products for Prevention or Very Mild, Early Symptoms

  • Acetic Acid 2% Ear Drops/Spray:
    • Mechanism: Helps restore the natural acidic environment of the ear canal, making it less hospitable to bacteria. It has antibacterial and antifungal properties.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL. Can be used for the treatment of mild, superficial infections of the external auditory canal. [Rx info]
    • Counselling: Best used for prevention in susceptible individuals (e.g., after swimming) or at the very first sign of itching. May cause stinging. If symptoms of pain and inflammation are already established, a referral is more appropriate.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for explaining to patients why a referral is necessary and what to expect.

  • Topical Antibiotic +/- Corticosteroid drops/sprays:
    Context: This is the mainstay of treatment for moderate to severe otitis externa. A GP will prescribe a product containing an antibiotic (like ciprofloxacin or gentamicin) to treat the infection and a corticosteroid (like hydrocortisone or dexamethasone) to reduce pain and inflammation quickly. Some clinical pathways may allow supply via a community pharmacist under a Patient Group Direction (PGD). [Rx advice]
  • Oral Antibiotics:
    Context: These are rarely used and are only prescribed by a GP if the infection has spread beyond the ear canal (cellulitis) or the patient is systemically unwell. Explaining this helps manage patient expectations. [Rx advice]

This knowledge allows the pharmacy team to confidently explain that a GP assessment is required to access the most effective treatments for an established infection.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication is the cornerstone of managing otitis externa presentations in the pharmacy, focusing on accurate triage, self-care, and managing expectations.

Key Counselling Points

  • Justify the Referral: When referral is necessary, explain why. For example: "Based on your symptoms, it sounds like an infection in the ear canal. The most effective treatments are antibiotic ear drops, which need to be prescribed by a GP after they've had a look inside your ear."
  • Emphasise Analgesia: Advise that pain is the most distressing symptom and that regular paracetamol or ibuprofen is the best way to manage it while awaiting assessment.
  • Instruct on Self-Care: Clearly explain the importance of keeping the ear dry. "It's crucial to keep water out of the ear to allow it to heal. When you shower, pop a cotton wool ball covered in Vaseline into the outer bowl of your ear."
  • Prevention Advice: For swimmers or recurrent sufferers, counsel on preventative measures. "After swimming, you can use a spray like EarCalm to help dry the ear and keep the skin healthy."
  • Correct Drop Administration: If a patient has been prescribed drops, check they know how to use them: lie on the opposite side for a few minutes after instilling to allow the drops to penetrate fully.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help, particularly after recommending self-care or referral to a GP.

If referring to a GP for routine assessment, advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (e.g., call NHS 111 or attend A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms while waiting, such as:

  • Severe, worsening pain, especially at night.
  • Weakness in the muscles of their face.
  • Feeling very dizzy or unwell.
  • Redness spreading onto the skin of the face.

If a product has been sold for very mild symptoms, or self-care has been advised, instruct the patient to contact their GP if:

  • The symptoms do not start to improve within 2-3 days.
  • The pain becomes more severe.
  • They develop a discharge from the ear.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

The external auditory canal has several innate defence mechanisms. It is lined with skin that produces cerumen (earwax), which is hydrophobic, acidic (pH 4-5), and contains lysozymes, creating a hostile environment for bacterial and fungal growth. The canal's anatomy, with its migrating epithelium, also facilitates natural cleaning.

Otitis externa occurs when these defences are breached. Common mechanisms include:

  • Moisture: Water from swimming or showering can raise the pH of the canal, creating a more favourable environment for bacterial growth (particularly Pseudomonas aeruginosa). This macerates the skin, breaking down the barrier.
  • Trauma: The use of cotton buds, hearing aids, or fingernails can cause micro-abrasions in the delicate canal skin, providing a portal of entry for pathogens.
  • Underlying Dermatoses: Conditions like eczema or psoriasis can cause inflammation and skin breakdown, predisposing to secondary infection.

Once the barrier is compromised, bacteria—most commonly Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus—colonise the skin. This triggers an acute inflammatory response, leading to vasodilation, oedema (swelling), and pain.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe pharmacy practice requires awareness of risks associated with both OTC products and the general condition.

Acetic Acid Preparations

  • Counselling: Advise patients that a stinging or burning sensation upon application is common but usually transient. It should not be used if the eardrum is known to be perforated.

Analgesics

  • Standard Cautions: Ensure standard checks are completed for the safe supply of paracetamol and ibuprofen, including checking for asthma, gastrointestinal issues, and interacting medicines.

General Cautions for Counselling

  • Ototoxicity: It is useful for pharmacists to be aware that certain prescription antibiotic drops (aminoglycosides like gentamicin) are ototoxic and should not be used if the eardrum is perforated. This knowledge reinforces why a proper GP assessment to visualise the eardrum is so important before treatment, and why patients should not use old, prescribed drops from a household medicine cabinet.
  • Ineffective Remedies: Advise against using remedies like olive oil drops once an infection is suspected, as the moisture can worsen the bacterial environment.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Earache in children requires careful questioning to rule out other causes like acute otitis media or a foreign body in the ear. Otitis externa is less common than middle ear infections in young children. Due to the difficulty in assessment, a direct referral to the GP is the safest course of action.

Older adults

This group requires a high index of suspicion and a low threshold for urgent referral. If an older person, particularly with diabetes, complains of severe ear pain, necrotising otitis externa must be considered, and they should be advised to contact their GP immediately.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with paracetamol is safe for pain relief. However, for a definitive diagnosis and treatment of suspected otitis externa, a GP referral is required. Acetic acid drops are generally considered safe, but medical assessment is still recommended to confirm the diagnosis.

People with significant comorbidities

Any patient with diabetes or a condition causing immunosuppression (or taking related medicines) who presents with symptoms of otitis externa should be referred to their GP on the same day for assessment due to the significantly higher risk of severe, invasive infection.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Referral for Established Infection

  • Patient: "Hi, I've got a really painful ear. It's agony if I touch it here." (Points to tragus).
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear that. To advise you properly, who is this for and what are the symptoms?"
  • Patient: "It's for me. The pain started yesterday, and it feels blocked and itchy inside. I've been swimming a bit recently."
  • Pharmacist: "And have you taken any medication for it?"
  • Patient: "Just some paracetamol, but it's not really touching it."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you. The fact that it hurts when you touch the outside strongly suggests an infection of the ear canal, what we call otitis externa. There isn't anything we can sell you over the counter to cure that, as you'll need antibiotic ear drops. You should make an appointment to see your GP today. In the meantime, keep taking paracetamol and ibuprofen regularly to manage the pain and be very careful to keep the ear dry."

Scenario 2: Urgent Referral for Red Flag

  • Customer: "I need some advice for my dad. He's 75 and has diabetes, and he's been up all night with terrible ear pain."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, thank you for coming in. Severe ear pain in someone with diabetes can be serious. What other symptoms does he have? Is he generally well?"
  • Customer: "He just says the pain is deep inside his head on that side. He feels okay otherwise."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Given your dad's diabetes and the severe pain he is in, it is very important he gets medical attention today. He needs to be checked for a serious type of infection that this group is at risk of. Please contact his GP surgery as soon as you leave here and tell them a pharmacist has advised an urgent, same-day assessment due to suspected severe ear infection in a diabetic patient. If you can't get through, call 111."
