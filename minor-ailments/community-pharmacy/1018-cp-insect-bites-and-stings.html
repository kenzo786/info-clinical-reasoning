Insect Bites and Stings in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Insect bites and stings are a very common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, especially during spring and summer. Most reactions are mild and localised. In the UK, common culprits include mosquitoes, midges, fleas, bedbugs, horseflies, wasps, and bees. Patients typically present with a skin reaction characterised by an itchy papule or weal, surrounded by an area of erythema. The bite or sting may be painful, particularly from wasps, bees, or horseflies.

The onset of the reaction is usually rapid, occurring within minutes of the event. The majority of simple, localised reactions are self-limiting and resolve within a few hours to several days. The key role for the pharmacy team is to differentiate these benign reactions from more severe local reactions, secondary bacterial infections, and rare but life-threatening systemic allergic reactions such as anaphylaxis, through effective questioning and triage.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of Anaphylaxis: This is a medical emergency requiring an immediate 999 call. Features include sudden onset of wheeze or stridor, respiratory distress, angioedema (swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat), widespread urticaria, hypotension, tachycardia, or collapse.
  • Severe or Spreading Cellulitis: Signs of secondary bacterial infection such as rapidly expanding erythema, severe warmth, bulla formation, ascending lymphangitis (red tracks on the skin), or significant systemic upset (fever, rigors, confusion). This requires urgent referral to a GP.
  • Bites in a High-Risk Location: Any sting inside the mouth or throat, or significant swelling around the eyes, can compromise the airway or vision and warrants urgent assessment.
  • Systemic Symptoms with a Tick Bite: While an erythema migrans rash itself requires a prompt GP appointment, the presence of fever, neck stiffness, or severe headache following a known tick bite would warrant an urgent GP referral.
  • Unusually Severe and Extensive Local Reaction: A large local reaction (swelling >10 cm in diameter) that is extremely painful or blistered requires GP assessment.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. Many OTC products have specific age restrictions. It also clarifies if the consultation is for a third party, which requires additional caution.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the reaction (e.g., itchy, painful, swollen). Crucially, this step is used to screen for red flag symptoms like breathlessness, facial swelling, or widespread rash.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish the timeline. A reaction that occurred hours ago is managed differently from one that is worsening after several days. Symptoms not improving or worsening after 2-3 days may suggest a secondary infection and require referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what self-care measures or treatments the patient has already tried. This avoids product duplication and helps to understand symptom progression.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for underlying medical conditions (e.g., diabetes, immunosuppression), allergies (including previous reactions to stings), and potential drug interactions with OTC products.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are simple local reactions.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Simple Localised Reaction: A small, itchy, red swelling at the bite site. Suitable for self-management.
  • Large Local Reaction: An extensive area of swelling (>10 cm), peaking at 24-48 hours. This is an allergic response, not an infection, but may warrant GP referral if severe.
  • Papular Urticaria: Crops of very itchy papules, common in children, representing a hypersensitivity reaction.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Cellulitis: A key differential for a worsening bite. Suspect if there is spreading, warm, tender erythema, especially with fever. This requires GP referral for antibiotics.
  • Anaphylaxis: A medical emergency. Distinguished by rapid onset of airway, breathing, or circulation problems.
  • Lyme Disease: Must be considered if a tick bite is reported or suspected. A slowly expanding 'bulls-eye' rash warrants routine GP referral.
  • Urticaria / Angioedema (other causes): Consider other triggers if there is no clear history of a bite.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief. Non-pharmacological advice is paramount and should always be provided.

First-Line Advice (All Bites/Stings)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This is the foundation of management. Advise washing the area, applying a cold compress, and elevating the limb to reduce swelling. For bee stings, advise scraping the stinger out, not squeezing it. Strongly advise against scratching.

Symptomatic Relief Options

  • Oral Antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine, Chlorphenamine):
    • Mechanism: Block H1 histamine receptors to reduce itching and weal formation.
    • Licensing: Available as GSL and P medicines. Check product-specific age licensing (e.g., Cetirizine/Loratadine for 2+/6+, Chlorphenamine for 1+).
    • Counselling: Advise that older agents like chlorphenamine cause drowsiness. [Rx info]
  • Mild Topical Corticosteroid (e.g., Hydrocortisone 1% cream):
    • Mechanism: A topical anti-inflammatory to reduce local redness, swelling, and itch.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Licensed for adults and children over 10 years for allergic contact dermatitis and insect bite reactions.
    • Counselling: Apply thinly once or twice daily. Do not use on the face, anogenital region, or on broken or infected skin. [Rx info]
  • Antipruritics (e.g., Crotamiton):
    • Mechanism: Offers symptomatic relief from itching.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Can be used in adults and children over 3 years.
    • Counselling: Apply 2-3 times a day. Can be used as an alternative or adjunct to antihistamines. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While most pharmacy staff do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for giving appropriate advice and managing patient expectations after referral to a GP.

  • Potent Topical Steroids (e.g., Betamethasone):
    Context: A GP may prescribe a stronger steroid cream for a severe local reaction that is not infected. [Rx advice]
  • Oral Steroids (e.g., Prednisolone):
    Context: A short course may be prescribed by a GP for a very severe large local reaction to quickly reduce inflammation. [Rx advice]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin):
    Context: These are only prescribed by a GP if there are clear signs of a secondary bacterial infection like cellulitis. They are not effective for the bite reaction itself. This is a key antimicrobial stewardship message. Under certain national services, pharmacists may be able to supply antibiotics for infected bites. [Rx advice]
  • Adrenaline Auto-Injectors:
    Context: Following a systemic reaction, a patient will be referred to a specialist who may recommend they carry adrenaline auto-injectors (e.g., EpiPen, Jext). [Rx advice]
7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for insect bites and stings.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that swelling, redness and itch are a normal reaction. Differentiate a large local reaction (allergic) from an infection, explaining it can look dramatic but will resolve over a week.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as the primary treatment. Emphasise cleaning the area, using a cold compress, and elevating the limb as active and effective measures.
  • Justify Product Choice: Explain what the recommended OTC product does (e.g., "This antihistamine tablet will help reduce the itchiness and swelling from the inside out") and highlight key practical advice (e.g., "This cream is for the itch, but you must not put it on any broken skin").
  • Prevention Advice: Proactively offer advice on preventing future bites, such as using an insect repellent containing DEET and wearing long, loose clothing in high-risk environments.
  • Check Understanding: Reinforce safety netting by checking the patient knows what to do if things get worse, e.g., "Just to be sure, what signs would make you call your GP?"
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from their GP or emergency services. This must be done for every consultation.

Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The swelling, redness or pain gets much worse after 24-48 hours.
  • The local symptoms do not start to improve after 3-4 days.
  • They start to feel unwell with a fever or muscle aches.
  • A 'bulls-eye' rash appears around the bite in the days or weeks afterwards (a potential sign of Lyme disease).

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (A&E or 999) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Difficulty breathing or wheezing.
  • Swelling in the face, mouth, or throat.
  • Feeling dizzy, faint, or confused.
  • A widespread, raised, itchy rash (hives) spreading across the body.
9. Pathophysiology

The skin's reaction to an insect bite or sting is an inflammatory response to proteins in the insect's saliva or venom. This response can be non-allergic or allergic.

In a simple local reaction, pharmacologically active substances in the venom or saliva directly trigger mast cells in the skin to degranulate. This releases inflammatory mediators, primarily histamine, which causes local vasodilation (erythema), increased vascular permeability (the weal or oedema), and stimulation of sensory nerve endings (itch and pain).

Allergic reactions are mediated by the immune system. A large local reaction is an IgE-mediated type I late-phase hypersensitivity reaction. In a previously sensitised individual, insect antigens cross-link IgE antibodies on mast cells, causing immediate histamine release (the initial weal and flare) followed by a later influx of eosinophils and other inflammatory cells. This cellular infiltrate is responsible for the more extensive, indurated, and prolonged swelling that peaks at 24-48 hours. Anaphylaxis is a systemic manifestation of this same type I hypersensitivity.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and patient factors.

Oral Antihistamines

  • Sedation: First-generation antihistamines like chlorphenamine cause drowsiness. A "Do not drive or operate machinery" warning is mandatory. Less sedating agents like loratadine or cetirizine are preferred for most adults.

Topical Hydrocortisone 1%

  • Contraindications: Do not sell for use on the face, eyes, or anogenital areas. It is crucial to confirm the skin is not broken or infected, as steroids can worsen infection. Not licensed for use in children under 10.

Insect Repellents (for prevention)

  • DEET: This is the most effective repellent. Advise customers that a concentration of 50% provides the best balance of efficacy and duration. For children or pregnant women, lower concentrations (20-30%) can be recommended, and it should be applied sparingly.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Self-care and simple measures are first-line. Oral antihistamines (e.g., chlorphenamine, cetirizine syrup) can be sold, but product-specific age licensing must be strictly followed. Hydrocortisone cream is not licensed for children under 10. Papular urticaria is common; reassure parents that it is a hypersensitivity reaction and will settle.

Older adults

Avoid recommending sedating antihistamines due to the increased risk of confusion, falls, and anticholinergic side effects. Be aware that this group may be more susceptible to secondary infection and have a lower threshold for GP referral.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Non-pharmacological advice is first-line. If medication is needed, oral loratadine or cetirizine are generally considered the antihistamines of choice. Topical hydrocortisone can also be used. The role of the pharmacy is to provide this initial advice and refer to the GP if symptoms are severe or concerning.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with diabetes or those who are immunosuppressed, maintain a high index of suspicion for secondary infection and have a low threshold for GP referral. Patients with a history of atopy may have more exaggerated reactions.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Bite & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I was bitten by something in the garden yesterday and it's incredibly itchy. Can I get something for it?"
  • Pharmacy Staff: "Certainly. Let me ask a few questions to help. Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 45."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "Okay. What are the symptoms other than the itch? Any pain or swelling of your face or lips?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing like that. It's just a single red lump on my arm, about the size of a 10p coin. It's just very, very itchy."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "And have you tried anything or taken any other medicines?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet, and I just take a tablet for my blood pressure."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a simple bite reaction. An antihistamine tablet like loratadine will work well to reduce the itch. You could also apply a small amount of hydrocortisone cream directly to the bite. It's important not to scratch it. If it gets a lot more red and starts to feel hot and painful, or isn't better in a few days, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Signs of Infection

  • Patient: "My daughter has a horsefly bite on her leg from two days ago and it's getting really bad."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help with that. Who is it for and how old is she?"
  • Patient: "It's for my daughter, she's 8."
  • Pharmacist: "Can you describe what it looks like now?"
  • Patient: "Well, it was just a bite, but now the whole area is bright red, it's really swollen and she says it's hot to touch. She's been feeling a bit shivery today as well."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you. Based on what you're describing – the spreading redness, heat, and your daughter feeling shivery – I'm concerned she might be developing a skin infection called cellulitis. An over-the-counter product isn't suitable. She needs to be seen by a GP today to see if she needs antibiotics. Please call your surgery as soon as you can and explain the symptoms to them."
