Vaginal Thrush in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Vaginal thrush, or vulvovaginal candidiasis, is a very common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It is caused by an overgrowth of yeast, most commonly Candida albicans, which is a normal commensal of the vaginal flora. It is not considered a sexually transmitted infection (STI), although sexual activity can sometimes trigger it.

Patients typically present with vulval itching (pruritus) as the predominant symptom. This is often accompanied by vulval soreness or irritation. A characteristic, non-offensive, thick, white, 'cottage cheese-like' vaginal discharge is common, but the discharge can also be watery or absent. Other symptoms can include superficial dyspareunia (pain during intercourse) and external dysuria (stinging on passing urine due to inflamed skin).

The condition is usually uncomplicated and responds well to short-course antifungal treatment. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology or contraindications to sale through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest an alternative diagnosis, a need for prescription treatment, or a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP.

  • Recurrent episodes: Four or more episodes in 12 months, or two episodes in 6 months. This requires a GP assessment for a potential underlying cause and a structured management plan.
  • Treatment failure: Persistent symptoms despite completing a full course of an appropriate OTC therapy. This requires GP assessment.
  • Signs of systemic illness or deep-seated infection: Fever, chills, lower abdominal pain, or deep dyspareunia. These are not features of uncomplicated thrush and require urgent referral.
  • Associated STI symptoms: Features such as a purulent or malodourous discharge, abnormal bleeding, or pelvic pain warrant referral for STI screening.
  • Vulval skin changes: Features like ulceration, blisters, significant skin thinning (atrophy), or white plaques (leukoplakia) are red flags for other vulval dermatoses and require referral.
  • Age outside licensed range: Patients under 16 or over 60 are not eligible for OTC treatment and must be referred to their GP.
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding: While topical treatment can be used, any suspected first episode or need for treatment during pregnancy or breastfeeding warrants GP or midwife confirmation before a pharmacy sale. Oral treatments are contraindicated.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this, especially when considering the supply of P medicines like oral fluconazole.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's identity and age (must be 16-60 for OTC supply). Establishes if it is a third-party request, which requires extra caution as the patient cannot be directly assessed.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm symptoms are consistent with uncomplicated thrush (itch, white non-smelly discharge). Key questions about odour (fishy smell suggests BV), pain (suggests PID/STI), or skin changes (suggests dermatoses) help to rule out conditions requiring referral.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand the pattern. Is this a first episode? Have they had it before? Crucially, have they had more than two episodes in the last 6 months (a key referral criterion)?
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out if they have used any treatment for this episode and if it has failed (a referral criterion). It also establishes if they have successfully used a treatment before, which increases diagnostic confidence.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for medical conditions (e.g., diabetes, immunosuppression) and medicines that may predispose them to thrush or interact with treatment (e.g., warfarin or statins with oral fluconazole). This question also covers pregnancy and breastfeeding status, which are key contraindications for oral treatment.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for the pharmacy team to conduct an effective consultation, ensure the presenting symptoms align with uncomplicated thrush, and identify when a referral is necessary.

Common Differentials (Requiring Referral or Different Advice)

  • Bacterial Vaginosis (BV): Often presents with a thin, grey/white, malodourous discharge with a characteristic 'fishy' smell. Itch is less prominent than in thrush. Requires referral as antifungal treatment is ineffective.
  • Trichomonas Vaginalis: A sexually transmitted infection. Typically causes a profuse, yellow-green, frothy discharge and vulvovaginal soreness. Mandatory GP/GUM clinic referral.
  • Allergic or Irritant Contact Dermatitis: Can cause vulval erythema, itch, and soreness due to a reaction to products like soaps or detergents. Discharge is not a primary feature. Requires advice on irritant avoidance.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Urgent Referral

  • Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs): Chlamydia or gonorrhoea can cause vaginal discharge and pelvic pain. Herpes simplex virus causes painful blisters or ulcers.
  • Lichen Sclerosus: An inflammatory skin condition causing intense itch and soreness. Examination reveals pale, thin, or crinkled skin. Requires specialist diagnosis and treatment.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Presents with lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, and potentially fever. Requires urgent medical assessment.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is to resolve the symptoms of an uncomplicated thrush infection. Advice on self-care should accompany any product recommendation.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise avoiding potential irritants like perfumed soaps and vaginal douching. Recommend using an emollient wash substitute and wearing loose-fitting cotton underwear.

OTC Treatment Options

  • Topical Imidazoles (e.g., Clotrimazole):
    • Mechanism: Inhibit fungal ergosterol synthesis, which disrupts the cell membrane, leading to fungal cell death.
    • Licensing: Available as GSL and P. Available as internal pessaries, internal creams, and external creams. Combination packs are often preferred to treat both internal and external symptoms.
    • Counselling: Advise inserting the pessary/internal cream at night. Warn that topical creams and pessaries can damage latex condoms and diaphragms. [Rx info]
  • Oral Azoles (Fluconazole):
    • Mechanism: Same mechanism as topical imidazoles but delivered systemically.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Supplied as a single 150mg capsule for uncomplicated thrush. Strict inclusion/exclusion criteria apply (age 16-60, not pregnant/breastfeeding, not recurrent).
    • Counselling: A convenient single-dose option. A full WWHAM check is mandatory to rule out interactions (e.g., with statins, warfarin) and contraindications. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not prescribe these (unless qualified as an independent prescriber), awareness is vital for explaining to patients why a GP referral is necessary and managing their expectations.

  • Longer Treatment Courses:
    Context: A GP will manage recurrent (≥4 episodes/year) or severe thrush. This often involves a longer initial 'induction' course of treatment (e.g., several oral doses or a week of pessaries), followed by a 6-month 'maintenance' regimen (e.g., one capsule or pessary per week/month) to prevent recurrence. [Rx advice]
  • Alternative Antifungals:
    Context: If a vaginal swab shows treatment failure or a resistant species (like C. glabrata), a GP may prescribe other antifungals, such as oral Itraconazole or topical Nystatin, based on culture results. This is beyond the scope of OTC management. [Rx advice]
  • Topical Steroid Combination:
    Context: In cases of severe vulval inflammation and itching, a GP may prescribe a combination product containing an antifungal and a mild topical steroid (e.g., hydrocortisone) for rapid symptom relief. [Rx advice]
7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for patients seeking advice for vaginal thrush.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Condition: Reassure the patient that thrush is very common, not an STI, and is caused by an overgrowth of a normal yeast.
  • Justify Referral If Needed: If referral criteria are met (e.g., recurrent episodes, age), explain clearly and calmly why they need to see a GP. For example, "Because you've had this a few times recently, it's important your GP investigates to see if there's an underlying reason and to set up a proper treatment plan."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Recommend avoiding irritants, using emollients as a soap substitute, and wearing loose cotton underwear to help prevent future episodes.
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain how to use the product correctly (e.g., "Insert the pessary at night before you go to sleep"). Crucially, provide key warnings: "This cream can damage condoms, so you'll need to use a different form of contraception," or for fluconazole, "This is a one-off dose to take now."
  • Manage Expectations: Advise that symptoms should start to improve within a couple of days but may take up to a week to resolve completely.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from the pharmacy or their GP. This ensures a person knows when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • Their symptoms do not start to improve within 7 days of starting treatment, or do not fully resolve within 14 days.
  • Their symptoms return within two months of finishing treatment.
  • They develop any new symptoms that are red flags, such as lower abdominal pain, fever, or a bad-smelling discharge.
  • The treatment itself causes significant local irritation or a rash.

Advise the patient to return to the pharmacy if they have any further questions. For a patient who has been referred, it is good practice to explain what they should say to the GP (e.g., "Tell the GP that the pharmacist referred you because you have had thrush four times this year").

9. Pathophysiology

Vulvovaginal candidiasis results from an overgrowth of Candida species, which are part of the normal commensal fungal flora of the vagina in up to 20% of asymptomatic women. The most common causative organism is Candida albicans (80-90% of cases). Other species, such as C. glabrata, are less common but may be associated with recurrent or resistant infections.

Symptomatic infection (candidiasis) is not a true infection from an external source, but rather a disturbance of the vaginal ecosystem that allows the yeast to proliferate from its commensal state to a pathogenic, invasive form. This involves the transformation of yeast cells into hyphae, which can invade the vaginal epithelial cells.

This overgrowth triggers a local inflammatory response from the host. The release of inflammatory mediators and cytokines in the vaginal and vulval tissues leads to the classic symptoms of erythema, oedema, and intense pruritus. The thick, white discharge is composed of exfoliated epithelial cells, inflammatory cells, and clumps of yeast hyphae.

Several factors can disrupt the delicate balance of the vaginal microbiome and predispose an individual to Candida overgrowth. These include hormonal factors (high oestrogen levels in pregnancy), metabolic factors (poorly controlled diabetes mellitus), and suppression of normal bacterial flora by broad-spectrum antibiotics, or general immunosuppression.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines for thrush requires awareness of product-specific risks, contraindications, and interactions which must be checked during the pharmacy consultation.

Topical Imidazoles (Clotrimazole etc.)

  • Contraception Interaction: The oil-based excipients in pessaries and creams can weaken the latex in condoms and diaphragms. This is a critical counselling point as it can lead to contraceptive failure. Advise use of alternative methods during and for at least 5 days after treatment.
  • Application: Advise patients to insert pessaries or internal creams high into the vagina at bedtime to maximise contact time and minimise leakage.

Oral Fluconazole (P Medicine)

  • Drug Interactions: Fluconazole inhibits CYP enzymes and has significant interactions. It is essential to check for interacting medicines during the WWHAM consultation. Key interactions include increasing levels of warfarin (risk of bleeding), some statins (e.g., simvastatin - risk of myopathy), and sulfonylureas (risk of hypo). Always check the BNF.
  • Contraindications: The OTC license has strict contraindications. It must not be sold to patients who are pregnant or breastfeeding, are under 16 or over 60, or have a history of liver problems. It cannot be sold if the patient is taking certain interacting medicines.
  • Recurrence: OTC fluconazole is only licensed for infrequent episodes. If a patient has had more than two episodes in the last 6 months, they must be referred.
11. Use in special populations

Children

OTC thrush treatments are not licensed for use in anyone under the age of 16. Any child presenting with symptoms of vulvovaginitis must be referred to their GP for investigation, as other causes like threadworms or irritant dermatitis are more common.

Older adults

OTC thrush treatments are not licensed for use in anyone over the age of 60. This is because symptoms of itching and soreness are much more likely to be due to other conditions like atrophic vaginitis or a vulval dermatosis, which require a medical diagnosis. All patients over 60 must be referred to their GP.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Oral fluconazole is strictly contraindicated and must not be supplied. While topical clotrimazole is considered safe, pharmacy policy often dictates that a diagnosis should first be confirmed by a GP or midwife for the first episode during pregnancy before any pharmacy supply. For subsequent episodes, supply may be appropriate depending on local policy and patient history. Always advise the patient to mention it to their midwife.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes can be treated OTC for an uncomplicated episode, but should be asked about their glycaemic control. Poor control is a risk factor for recurrence, and frequent episodes warrant a GP review of their diabetes management. Patients who are immunocompromised should be referred to their GP as they may require longer or different treatment courses.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Straightforward OTC Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, can I get something for thrush?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. To make sure we get you the right thing, can I ask who it's for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 28."
  • Pharmacist: "And what symptoms are you getting?"
  • Patient: "It's just the usual, really itchy and a bit of white discharge. No smell or anything."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Have you had it before?"
  • Patient: "Yes, a couple of years ago. I used the tablet and it worked fine."
  • Pharmacist: "And are you taking any other medication or have any medical conditions? Are you pregnant or breastfeeding?"
  • Patient: "No to all of that."
  • Recommendation: "Great, thank you. Based on that, the single fluconazole tablet would be a suitable option again. Take the capsule now with some water. It can take a day or two for the symptoms to start clearing up. If it's not better in a week, or if it comes back within two months, you should see your GP. Does that sound okay?"

Scenario 2: Mandatory Referral due to Recurrence

  • Patient: "Hi, I need another one of those thrush tablets please."
  • Pharmacist: "No problem. Can I just run through a few quick questions? I see you bought one about two months ago. Is this a new episode?"
  • Patient: "Yes, it's come back again. This is the third time since Christmas."
  • Pharmacist: "Thanks for letting me know. Because it has come back a few times in a short period, the pharmacy guidelines say that I can't sell you the tablet this time. It's really important that you make an appointment with your GP."
  • Patient: "Oh, why is that?"
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Having thrush this frequently is called 'recurrent thrush', and it means your GP needs to check there isn't an underlying reason for it. They can also give you a different treatment plan to try and stop it from coming back so often. An over-the-counter tablet is only for occasional episodes. I'd recommend you call your surgery today and tell them what we've discussed."
