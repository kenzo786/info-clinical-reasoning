Headache in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Headache is an extremely common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy. Most headaches are primary, meaning they are not caused by an underlying disease. The three most common types are tension-type headache, migraine, and cluster headache. A fourth important entity, highly prevalent in community pharmacy, is medication-overuse headache.

Patients with tension-type headache typically describe a bilateral, pressing or tightening sensation of mild to moderate intensity, which is not aggravated by routine physical activity. Migraine classically presents as a unilateral, pulsating, moderate to severe headache aggravated by activity, often accompanied by nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia. An aura may precede the headache. Cluster headaches are severe, strictly unilateral attacks of pain around the eye or temple, associated with autonomic features on the same side (e.g., red eye, nasal congestion).

The key role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate between primary headache types through effective questioning, identify the small but important proportion of patients with serious secondary causes requiring referral, provide advice on self-care, and recommend appropriate OTC treatments, including counselling on the risk of medication-overuse headache.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of any red flag warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Thunderclap headache: A sudden-onset, severe headache reaching maximum intensity within 5 minutes. This is a red flag for subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) and requires immediate emergency admission.
  • Neurological signs or symptoms: New onset of focal neurological deficit (e.g., limb weakness, speech disturbance), altered consciousness, confusion, or seizures suggests an intracranial lesion (e.g., stroke, tumour, bleed) or infection.
  • Features of meningitis or encephalitis: Headache accompanied by fever, neck stiffness, photophobia, and/or a non-blanching rash. This requires urgent assessment for central nervous system infection.
  • Features of Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): In a patient over 50, a new-onset or different type of headache, especially with temporal artery tenderness, jaw claudication, or visual disturbance (including transient visual loss). This requires urgent referral to a GP.
  • Features of raised intracranial pressure: Headache that is worse on waking, coughing, or straining. It may be associated with vomiting or visual changes. This can indicate a space-occupying lesion.
  • Positional headache: A headache that significantly worsens on standing up and improves on lying down may suggest a CSF leak.
  • Trauma: A new headache following a significant head injury.
  • Immunocompromised or cancer patient: A new-onset headache in a patient with cancer or immunosuppression raises suspicion of metastases or opportunistic infection.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. Many analgesics have specific age restrictions, and this identifies if a third-party consultation is occurring, which requires extra care, especially in assessing red flags.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the headache's characteristics (e.g., pulsating, tight band) and screen for red flags (thunderclap onset, neurological signs, fever, neck stiffness). This helps differentiate migraine from tension-type headache and determines if referral is needed.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To determine if the headache is acute or chronic. A new, persistent, or worsening headache requires more caution. Frequency of headaches and analgesic use helps identify potential medication-overuse headache.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments the patient has tried and their effectiveness. This avoids duplicate recommendations and is key to diagnosing medication-overuse headache.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for prescribed medicines that can cause headache and to identify comorbidities (e.g., asthma, where NSAIDs need caution; hypertension, where some combination products are contraindicated). It also allows a check for drug interactions.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are primary headaches.

Common Primary Headaches

  • Tension-Type Headache: Most common. Bilateral, non-pulsating, 'tight band' feeling. Not aggravated by activity.
  • Migraine: Can be with or without aura. Typically unilateral, pulsating, and severe. Associated with nausea and/or photophobia and phonophobia.
  • Cluster Headache: Rare but severe. Strictly unilateral pain around the eye/temple with autonomic features. Requires referral.
  • Medication-Overuse Headache (MOH): A chronic daily headache (≥15 days/month) from regular overuse of acute analgesia. A key diagnosis to consider in pharmacy.

Serious Secondary Causes to Consider for Referral

  • Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH): Differentiated by its 'thunderclap' onset.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis: Differentiated by fever, rash, neck stiffness, and altered mental state.
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): Suspected in patients >50 with new headache, jaw claudication, and tender temporal arteries.
  • Space-Occupying Lesion/Raised ICP: Suggested by progressive headache, neurological signs, or headache worse on waking/straining.
  • Sinusitis: Often presents with facial pain, nasal blockage, and purulent discharge.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management focuses on symptomatic relief and providing robust self-care advice, with a strong emphasis on preventing medication-overuse headache.

First-Line Advice (All Headaches)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise on identifying and managing triggers with a headache diary, ensuring adequate hydration, and maintaining regular sleep. For an acute attack, resting in a quiet, dark room can help.

OTC Analgesia

  • Simple Analgesics (e.g., Paracetamol, Ibuprofen):
    • Mechanism: Paracetamol's mechanism is complex; Ibuprofen is an NSAID that inhibits prostaglandin synthesis.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available for adults and children (check product-specific age licensing for ibuprofen).
    • Counselling: First-line for acute tension-type headache and migraine. Advise limiting use to a maximum of 15 days per month for paracetamol and 10 days for ibuprofen to prevent MOH. [Rx info]
  • Combination Analgesics (e.g., containing caffeine):
    • Mechanism: Caffeine acts as an analgesic adjuvant.
    • Licensing: P medicine.
    • Counselling: Can be effective but carry a higher risk of causing medication-overuse headache and should be limited to a maximum of 10 days of use per month. Avoid other sources of caffeine. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for headache, awareness is vital for counselling patients, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Triptans (e.g., Sumatriptan):
    Context: These are 5-HT1B/1D agonists used for the acute treatment of migraine attacks only. Some are available as P medicines following a detailed pharmacist consultation. Knowledge of contraindications (e.g., ischaemic heart disease) is critical for safe supply. A GP may prescribe them if OTC options fail. [Rx advice]
  • Prophylactic Agents (e.g., Propranolol, Amitriptyline):
    Context: These medicines are prescribed by a GP to reduce the frequency and severity of migraine or chronic tension-type headaches. Pharmacists play a role in counselling on side effects (e.g., drowsiness with amitriptyline) and explaining that they need to be taken every day, not just during an attack. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral might be needed for preventative treatment or for headaches that are not well-managed with OTC options.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for headache.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations & Explain MOH: Clearly explain the risk of medication-overuse headache. A key phrase is: "It's important not to take these painkillers for more than two or three days a week on a regular basis, as using them too often can actually cause your headaches to become more frequent."
  • Validate and Reassure: Acknowledge the debilitating nature of conditions like migraine. Reassure patients that while most headaches are not serious, their symptoms are real and manageable.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Actively recommend keeping a headache diary to identify triggers like stress, certain foods, or poor sleep. Frame this as a primary management strategy.
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain the correct way to use acute treatments – i.e., at the first sign of an attack. Always check for hidden paracetamol or ibuprofen in branded multi-ingredient products to prevent accidental overdose.
  • Justify Referral: If a referral is necessary, explain why clearly and calmly. For example, "Because this headache is new for you and you're over 50, it's important that a GP assesses you to rule out anything serious."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help. This ensures that a patient with a self-limiting headache knows when to act if their condition changes or does not follow the expected course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • Their headache pattern changes significantly or becomes more frequent.
  • The headache is not responding to OTC treatments, and they are using them more than 10-15 days per month.
  • The headache is interfering with their daily life, and they may benefit from a preventative (prophylactic) medicine.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • A sudden, explosive headache that reaches peak intensity in minutes ('thunderclap' headache).
  • Headache accompanied by a high fever, stiff neck, or a rash.
  • New neurological symptoms like confusion, drowsiness, weakness on one side of the body, or problems with vision or speech.
  • Headache following a recent head injury.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology varies between primary headache types.

Tension-Type Headache (TTH): The exact mechanism is not fully understood but is thought to involve both peripheral and central factors. Peripherally, increased pericranial muscle tension and tenderness (myofascial nociception) play a role, particularly in episodic TTH. In chronic TTH, central mechanisms are more prominent. This involves sensitisation of central pain pathways in the trigeminal nucleus and higher centres in the brainstem and thalamus, leading to a reduced threshold for pain perception.

Migraine: This is a complex neurovascular disorder. The current leading theory involves neuronal dysfunction rather than a primary vascular event. An external or internal trigger activates brainstem nuclei, leading to sensitisation of the trigeminovascular system. This results in the release of vasoactive neuropeptides, including Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide (CGRP), which cause painful inflammation and vasodilation of cranial blood vessels. The migraine aura is explained by cortical spreading depression—a slow wave of neuronal and glial depolarisation that spreads across the cerebral cortex, followed by a period of suppressed activity.

Cluster Headache: The pathophysiology is thought to involve the hypothalamus (explaining the circadian rhythm of attacks) and the trigemino-autonomic reflex. Activation of the posterior hypothalamus triggers the trigeminal system, causing the severe pain, and also the parasympathetic nervous system via the sphenopalatine ganglion, causing the characteristic autonomic symptoms (lacrimation, nasal congestion, etc.).

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and interactions.

NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen)

  • Cautions: Must be used with caution in patients with a history of asthma, as they can induce bronchospasm. Caution is also required in patients with hypertension, renal impairment, or a history of GI issues. Always confirm the patient has no history of peptic ulcers.
  • Interactions: Increased risk of GI bleeding with anticoagulants and SSRIs.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Hidden Ingredients: Many "all-in-one" cold/flu or headache remedies contain paracetamol or ibuprofen. It is essential to counsel patients not to take additional separate doses of these analgesics to avoid accidental overdose.
  • Decongestants: Products containing decongestants (e.g., pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine) are contraindicated in patients with hypertension and heart disease.

Codeine-Containing Analgesics

  • Misuse and MOH: While available OTC for pain, these products (e.g., co-codamol) have a very high risk of causing medication-overuse headache and dependence. They should not be recommended for headache. Be vigilant for requests that may indicate misuse or established dependence.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Paracetamol is the first-line choice. Ibuprofen can also be used (check product-specific age licensing). Any severe, persistent, or unusual headache in a child warrants a referral to the GP. Migraine can present with abdominal pain in children. Reassure parents but provide clear safety netting advice.

Older adults

Have a very low threshold for referral. Any new headache in a patient over 50 requires careful questioning to rule out GCA. NSAIDs carry a much higher risk of GI and renal side effects in this group and should be recommended with caution and for short-term use only. Check for polypharmacy and potential drug interactions.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with rest and hydration is first-line. Paracetamol is the analgesic of choice at any stage of pregnancy. NSAIDs such as ibuprofen should be avoided, especially in the third trimester. Patients should be referred to their GP if paracetamol is not effective, as most other OTC and prescribed options are not recommended.

People with significant comorbidities

Tailor recommendations carefully. For patients with asthma, check if they have used NSAIDs safely before; otherwise, recommend paracetamol. For patients with hypertension, avoid products containing NSAIDs or decongestants. For patients with diabetes, recommend sugar-free formulations where possible.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Migraine & OTC Triptan Supply

  • Patient: "Hi, I get these awful migraines. My usual painkillers don't work. I saw an ad for a migraine tablet you can get here."
  • Pharmacist (after WWHAM questioning confirms a clear history of stable migraine without red flags): "Okay, thank you for that. Based on what you've told me, your headaches do sound like typical migraines. There is a specific migraine treatment called sumatriptan we can supply. Have you ever used it before?"
  • Patient: "No, never."
  • Recommendation: "This medicine works differently from regular painkillers. It's not for prevention; you take one tablet as soon as the headache part of the migraine starts. It's important that you are not at risk of heart problems, so I have to ask: do you have high blood pressure, high cholesterol, or any heart conditions?" (Continues with mandatory P-medicine checklist for triptans). "If it works, great. If not, or if you get them often, it would be worth talking to your GP about daily preventative treatments. Remember the safety netting advice we discussed."

Scenario 2: Referral for Suspected Medication-Overuse Headache

  • Patient: "Can I have another box of co-codamol please?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Can I just check who this is for?"
  • Patient: "Me. I get these headaches almost every day now."
  • Pharmacist (after WWHAM): "How many of these tablets would you say you take each day, and how long has this been going on?"
  • Patient: "Probably 6 or 8 a day. It's been like this for months. The headache is there when I wake up."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for being open with me. It sounds like you might have developed something called a medication-overuse headache. This can happen when you take codeine-based painkillers regularly. Paradoxically, the medicine starts to cause the headache. I can't sell you these today, as it would make the problem worse. The best thing to do is to see your GP. They can support you in stopping the co-codamol and can prescribe a different type of medicine to prevent the headaches from starting. It's tough, but it's the only way to break the cycle."

Scenario 3: Urgent Referral for Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something for my mum's headache. She's 75."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. What are her symptoms? Is this a usual headache for her?"
  • Patient: "No, it's new, started last week. It's on one side of her head. She also says her jaw hurts when she chews her food, and the side of her head is really sore to touch."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you. Based on what you've said—her age, the new headache, the jaw pain, and the tenderness—this needs to be looked at by a doctor today. It could be a condition called Giant Cell Arteritis, which needs urgent treatment to protect her eyesight. Please call her GP surgery as soon as you leave and tell the receptionist exactly what you told me. If they can't see her this morning, you should call 111. An over-the-counter medicine isn't suitable here."
