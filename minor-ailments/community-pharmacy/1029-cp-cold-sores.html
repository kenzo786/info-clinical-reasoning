Cold Sores (Herpes Labialis) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Cold sores are a common condition for which people seek advice in a community pharmacy. They are caused by the reactivation of the Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV), most commonly HSV-1. Patients typically present with a characteristic sequence of symptoms. It often begins with a prodrome of tingling, itching, or burning at a specific site on or around the lips, lasting several hours to a day. This is followed by the eruption of small, fluid-filled vesicles.

These vesicles may coalesce, then burst and weep, forming a painful ulcer which subsequently crusts over. The entire episode is self-limiting and typically resolves without scarring within 7 to 10 days. The condition is recurrent, with the virus lying dormant in the trigeminal ganglion between outbreaks. Common triggers for reactivation include ultraviolet (UV) light exposure, fatigue, stress, febrile illness, trauma to the area, or immunosuppression.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious complication. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Ocular Involvement (Herpetic Keratitis): Any symptoms affecting the eye, such as eye pain, grittiness, redness, photophobia, blurred vision, or vesicles on the eyelid or tip of the nose (Hutchinson's sign). This is an ophthalmological emergency requiring same-day referral.
  • Eczema Herpeticum: A widespread, painful, vesicular rash that is often 'punched-out' and crusted, superimposed on areas of pre-existing eczema. The patient may be systemically unwell with fever. This is a dermatological emergency requiring urgent referral.
  • Severe or Disseminated Infection: Multiple lesions spreading beyond the perioral area, or severe, non-healing ulcers, particularly in an immunocompromised patient. This requires urgent referral to a GP.
  • Neurological Symptoms: The presence of severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, fever, or new focal neurological signs or confusion is a red flag for herpes simplex encephalitis. This is a rare but life-threatening complication requiring immediate emergency hospital admission.
  • Neonatal Herpes: Any suspected herpes lesion in a newborn (up to 28 days old) is a medical emergency requiring immediate referral for hospital admission.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. OTC treatments for cold sores have age restrictions. It also establishes if the consultation is for a third party, which requires additional caution.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the symptoms match a typical cold sore (e.g., prodromal tingle, vesicles on the lip). Crucially, this step is used to screen for red flags, such as eye involvement, widespread lesions, or systemic illness.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To confirm the acute, cyclical nature. A lesion that fails to heal within two weeks is not a typical cold sore and requires mandatory GP referral to exclude other causes like skin cancer.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments the patient has already tried. This avoids product duplication and helps understand what has or hasn't worked for them in the past.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify any prescribed medicines that indicate significant comorbidities, particularly immunosuppressants (e.g., steroids, methotrexate, chemotherapy), which would necessitate a referral to the GP due to the risk of severe infection.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are uncomplicated herpes labialis.

Common Differentials

  • Impetigo: A bacterial infection often seen in children, characterised by golden, honey-coloured crusts. It is not necessarily confined to the lip border and lacks a prodrome.
  • Aphthous Ulcers: These occur *inside* the mouth on the soft tissues and are not preceded by blisters.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC): A persistent, non-healing ulcer or crusted lesion on the lip requires an urgent GP referral, especially in older patients with a history of sun exposure.
  • Eczema Herpeticum: A key differential in patients with atopic eczema who present with a sudden, widespread, painful vesicular rash. This requires immediate referral.
  • Syphilitic Chancre: Can present as a single, typically painless, firm ulcer on the lip and warrants referral.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief and modestly shortening the duration of the episode. Non-pharmacological advice is paramount.

First-Line Advice & Non-Medicated Options

  • Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise on rest, hydration, and simple analgesia if painful. Emphasise hygiene measures. Advise use of a high-factor lip balm in sunlight to prevent recurrence.
  • Hydrocolloid Patches:
    • Mechanism: Non-medicated patches that form a protective barrier over the lesion. They create a moist healing environment, reduce crusting, and can help conceal the sore.
    • Licensing: General Sales List (GSL).
    • Counselling: Can be applied at any stage of the outbreak to protect the lesion and aid healing. [Rx info]

Second-Line (Active Treatment)

  • Topical Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir 5% Cream):
    • Mechanism: Inhibits viral DNA polymerase, thereby stopping viral replication.
    • Licensing: Pharmacy (P) medicine. Check product-specific age licensing.
    • Counselling: Crucially, advise that this is only effective if started at the very first sign of the prodrome (the 'tingle stage'). It may shorten the episode by about half a day. Advise on application frequency and hand washing before and after use. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for cold sores, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir, Valaciclovir):
    Context: These are prescribed by a GP or specialist for specific situations, such as a severe primary infection, for patients who are immunocompromised, or to manage very frequent and distressing recurrences. They are not used for routine, uncomplicated cold sores. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team to confidently explain to a patient why an OTC product is appropriate or why a GP referral is needed to access different treatments, managing expectations and reinforcing the pharmacy's clinical role.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and tailored advice are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for cold sores.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that cold sores are a common, self-limiting viral issue. Reassure the patient that the lesion will heal by itself, usually within 7-10 days.
  • Explain Transmission Risk: This is a key public health role. Clearly advise that the virus is contagious. Counsel on the importance of frequent hand washing, and avoiding kissing, sharing cups or towels, and performing oral sex during an outbreak to prevent spreading the virus. This is especially important to protect newborns and immunocompromised people.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Recommend applying sunblock lip balm to prevent flare-ups, and using simple analgesia like paracetamol for pain.
  • OTC Product Counselling: If supplying topical aciclovir, stress that "it works best if you apply it right at the start, when you feel the tingle". For patches, explain they "help protect the sore and stop a crust from forming".
  • Justify a Referral: If referral is necessary, explain why in simple terms. For example, "Because this hasn't healed in over two weeks, it's important to get a doctor to have a proper look at it to make sure it's nothing else."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a person when they should seek further help from another healthcare professional. This ensures they know when to act if their condition worsens or does not follow the expected course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the person to consult their GP if:

  • The cold sore does not show signs of healing within 7-10 days or has not completely resolved within 2 weeks.
  • They are experiencing very frequent or severe outbreaks.
  • They feel systemically unwell with a fever or swollen glands.
  • They think the sore has become infected with bacteria (e.g., it is weeping pus or surrounded by red, spreading skin).

Advise the person to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Any pain, redness, or changes in vision in the eye.
  • A severe, explosive rash, especially if they also have eczema.
  • A severe headache, drowsiness, or confusion.
9. Pathophysiology

Cold sores are the result of reactivation of the Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV). The vast majority of oral-facial lesions are caused by HSV-1.

The process begins with a primary infection, which often occurs in childhood and may be subclinical or present as acute herpetic gingivostomatitis. Following this initial infection, the virus is not cleared from the body. Instead, it ascends along a sensory nerve axon to the corresponding dorsal root ganglion, where it establishes a lifelong state of latency. For herpes labialis, this is typically the trigeminal ganglion.

During latency, the viral genome persists within the neuron, but replication is suppressed. Various stimuli can trigger viral reactivation. These include local factors (UV radiation, trauma) and systemic factors (illness, psychological stress, hormonal changes). Upon reactivation, the virus replicates and travels back down the sensory nerve axon to the epithelial cells of the skin or mucosa supplied by that nerve. Here, it causes a productive, lytic infection, destroying host cells and leading to the characteristic inflammatory response and formation of vesicles. This cycle of latency and reactivation is the hallmark of HSV infection.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines for cold sores requires a focus on appropriate use and counselling.

Topical Aciclovir 5% Cream

  • Efficacy is Time-Critical: The main counselling point is that its effectiveness is highly dependent on early application during the prodromal (tingle) stage. Its benefit is minimal once vesicles have formed.
  • Side Effects: Generally very well tolerated. Can occasionally cause local stinging or burning on application.
  • Application: Advise users to apply a thin layer to the affected area, and to wash hands before and after to prevent autoinoculation or transmission.

Hydrocolloid Patches

  • Non-Medicated: It is important to explain that these patches contain no antiviral medicine. Their purpose is to act as a barrier, protect the lesion, and promote a moist healing environment which can reduce scabbing.
  • Cosmesis and Discretion: They can be a good recommendation for patients who are particularly self-conscious about the appearance of a cold sore, as they can be covered with makeup.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Primary herpetic gingivostomatitis (widespread sores in the mouth, fever, drooling) requires an urgent GP referral. For simple recurrent cold sores, self-care is first line. Check product-specific licensing for topical antivirals; many are suitable for children but confirming age is essential.

Older adults

Have a high index of suspicion and a low threshold for referral for any lip lesion that is persistent or non-healing, due to the increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

First-line management is self-care and non-pharmacological advice. Topical aciclovir cream is considered safe for use as systemic absorption is negligible. Hydrocolloid patches are also safe. Other treatments should be avoided. Emphasise hygiene to prevent transmission to the baby.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are immunocompromised (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids) should always be referred to their GP for assessment of a cold sore, as they are at risk of severe or disseminated infection. OTC sale is not appropriate. For patients with atopic eczema, be vigilant for signs of eczema herpeticum (widespread, painful rash) and refer urgently if suspected.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Standard Request and Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, can I get some Zovirax? I've got that tell-tale tingle on my lip."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "Of course. I'll just get the pharmacist. They need to ask you a couple of quick questions."
  • Pharmacist: "Hi there. You're wanting the cold sore cream? Is it for you?"
  • Patient: "Yes, for me. I'm 32. I get them a couple of times a year. No other symptoms and I don't take any regular medicines."
  • Pharmacist: "And you've had it before and know how to use it?"
  • Patient: "Yes, just put it on as soon as you feel the tingle."
  • Recommendation: "Perfect. You're doing exactly the right thing by starting it early. Remember to apply it five times a day for about five days, and wash your hands after so it doesn't spread. If the sore does come through, remember it's contagious, so avoid kissing anyone until it's healed up completely. If it's not better within 10 days or so, pop back and see us or your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral Due to a Red Flag

  • Patient: "Hello, my daughter has a terrible cold sore, it's all over her face. She has bad eczema too."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help. Can you tell me more? Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for my daughter, she's 8. She had one little sore on her lip yesterday but now she has loads of little blisters all over her cheeks where her eczema is bad. She's got a bit of a temperature and is miserable."
  • Pharmacist (recognising a red flag): "Thank you for that information. Based on what you've described, this sounds like it could be something more serious than a simple cold sore, especially with her eczema. It's something we call eczema herpeticum."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "An over-the-counter cream won't be strong enough, and she needs to be seen by a doctor straight away. You should contact your GP surgery immediately and tell them exactly what you've told me. If you can't get an urgent appointment, you should take her to A&E. Does that make sense?"
