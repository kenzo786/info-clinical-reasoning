Threadworms (Enterobiasis) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Threadworm infection is the most common worm infection in the UK and a very frequent reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy. The classic presentation is intense perianal itching (pruritus ani), which is characteristically worse at night. This can lead to sleep disturbance, making children irritable and tired.

While often asymptomatic, customers may report seeing small, white, thread-like worms on the surface of stools or around the anus of their child at night. In girls, irritation can extend to the vulval area. Transmission is faecal-oral via microscopic eggs, which are easily spread within households through contaminated hands, surfaces, towels, and bedding. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to confirm the likely diagnosis through effective questioning, recommend the correct OTC treatment for the whole family, and provide robust advice on hygiene measures to prevent re-infection.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious or alternative diagnosis. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP.

  • Severe or persistent abdominal pain: While mild tummy ache can occur, severe or persistent pain is not typical and requires a medical assessment to rule out other causes like appendicitis.
  • Blood in the stool: This is never caused by threadworms and always requires a GP referral to investigate causes such as an anal fissure or other bowel pathology.
  • Altered bowel habit or significant weight loss: Persistent diarrhoea or unexplained weight loss points away from a simple threadworm infection and needs medical investigation.
  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection: If scratching has caused the skin to become very red, swollen, painful or weepy, this suggests a bacterial infection (cellulitis) that needs assessment for antibiotic treatment.
  • Symptoms persisting despite correct treatment: If symptoms are unchanged after completing a full two-dose course of medication alongside strict hygiene measures, the person should see their GP to re-evaluate the diagnosis.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the age of the patient. Mebendazole has a pharmacy (P) license for adults and children over 2 years old. Patients under 2 require GP referral. This also establishes if it is a third-party request.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the diagnosis is likely threadworms (classic nocturnal itch, visible worms) and to actively screen for red flag symptoms (e.g., severe pain, blood in stool) that mandate referral.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand the timeline. While not critical for diagnosis, a very long history of untreated or recurrent symptoms may warrant a conversation about compliance with hygiene measures.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out if any treatments have been tried. If a previous course of mebendazole failed, it is crucial to explore adherence to the two-dose regimen and the 6-week hygiene plan before re-supplying.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for contraindications and comorbidities. The key check is for pregnancy. Although drug interactions with mebendazole are rare, it is good practice to ask.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis for perianal itch is essential for effective triage and identifying when referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy with classic symptoms will be threadworms.

Common Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Perianal Dermatitis/Eczema: The skin may appear red, dry, or cracked. Itching is not usually confined to night-time.
  • Haemorrhoids: A common cause of itching in adults, who may also describe pain or bleeding.
  • Anal Fissure: Typically causes a sharp pain during bowel movements, with some bright red blood on the toilet paper.
  • Skin Conditions: Persistent itching could be a sign of psoriasis or lichen sclerosus affecting the genital skin.
  • Fungal Infections (Candidiasis): Can cause an itchy red rash, particularly in skin folds.

The key distinguishing feature for threadworms is the classic nocturnal timing of the itch and/or visual confirmation of the worms.

5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management requires both medication and stringent hygiene. Counselling on the importance of hygiene is as important as the sale of a product. The whole household must be treated at the same time.

First-Line Advice (Essential for All)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be explained as an active part of the treatment, required for 6 weeks to break the lifecycle. Advise strict hand washing, keeping nails short, wearing close-fitting underwear at night, and a daily morning shower to remove eggs. Bedding, towels and night clothes should be hot-washed (60°C).

Primary OTC Treatment

  • Mebendazole:
    • Mechanism: An anthelmintic that works by preventing worms from absorbing glucose, causing their death over a few days.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Supplied as tablets or liquid. Licensed for adults and children aged 2 years and over.
    • Counselling: A single dose is taken, then a second dose MUST be taken two weeks later to treat re-infection from any hatching eggs. All household members must be treated at the same time. Can be chewed, swallowed, or crushed. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While pharmacy teams do not prescribe these medicines, awareness is vital for explaining referrals and managing patient expectations.

  • Mebendazole for children under 2:
    Context: The use of mebendazole in children between 6 months and 2 years is off-label. If treatment is deemed necessary for a child in this age group, they must be referred to their GP for a prescription and specialist advice. Pharmacy teams should not supply it OTC for this age group.

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team to confidently explain why a GP appointment is necessary for very young children or for pregnant women, framing the pharmacy as a gateway to appropriate care rather than a barrier.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for threadworms.

Key Counselling Points

  • Normalise and Reassure: Explain that threadworms are extremely common, especially in families with children, and are not a sign of being dirty. This reduces embarrassment.
  • Explain the 'Why': Give a simple explanation of the life cycle to reinforce the need for the hygiene rules and the second dose. For example, "The first dose kills the worms, but not their eggs. The second dose in two weeks kills the new worms that have hatched."
  • Provide a Hygiene Checklist: Empower the patient with a clear, actionable list of hygiene measures. Emphasise these must continue for 6 weeks to break the cycle completely.
  • Treat the Household: Clearly explain that everyone in the home must be treated on the same day, as people can be infected without having any symptoms.
  • Set Expectations: Let them know that itching may continue for a few days after the first dose while the dead worms are cleared from the body. Reinforce that the second dose is not optional.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from their GP. This ensures they know when to act if the condition does not resolve as expected.

Advise the patient or parent to consult their GP if:

  • Symptoms of itching do not resolve after the second dose of medicine (and the hygiene measures have been followed).
  • Any red flag symptoms develop, such as severe abdominal pain or blood in the stool.
  • The skin around the anus becomes very red and sore, suggesting a skin infection from scratching.
  • They are seeking treatment for a child under 2 years old, or for someone who is pregnant.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or are unsure about the advice.

9. Pathophysiology

The causative organism for threadworm infection is Enterobius vermicularis, an intestinal nematode (roundworm). Infection begins with the ingestion of its microscopic eggs. The eggs are resilient and can survive for up to two weeks in the environment, for example in dust, on clothing, or on toys.

Once ingested, the eggs travel to the small intestine (duodenum) where they hatch into larvae. The larvae continue to migrate down the gastrointestinal tract, maturing into adult worms in the caecum and large intestine. The adult worms are small and white, with males measuring 2-5mm and females 8-13mm.

Approximately one month after ingestion, the gravid female worm, full of eggs, migrates down the colon and out of the anus. This migration typically occurs at night. The female worm lays thousands of microscopic eggs on the perianal skin. A sticky, gelatinous substance secreted with the eggs, and the movement of the worm itself, causes the characteristic intense itching (pruritus ani). The subsequent scratching contaminates the fingertips with eggs, which are then transferred to the mouth (auto-infection) or to surfaces, where they can infect others (cross-infection). This completes the life cycle.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC mebendazole requires awareness of product-specific risks.

Mebendazole

  • Pregnancy: Must not be supplied OTC to a patient who is pregnant. It is contraindicated in the first trimester. They must be referred to their GP for advice. Hygiene measures are first-line.
  • Breastfeeding: While considered compatible, it is good practice to refer to the GP to allow a shared decision to be made. The primary recommendation should be hygiene measures.
  • Age: Licensed for P supply for ages 2 and over. All children under 2 must be referred to their GP.
  • Interactions: The patient medication record should be checked if possible. Avoid combination with metronidazole due to a rare risk of severe skin reactions (Stevens-Johnson syndrome). Other interactions are uncommon due to low systemic absorption.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Mebendazole is licensed as a P medicine for children aged 2 years and older. For any child under 2, a GP referral is mandatory for consideration of off-label treatment. The first-line advice from the pharmacy for any age is always the hygiene method.

Older adults

Treatment is the same as for other adults. However, be mindful that persistent itch in this group has a broader differential diagnosis, and a lower threshold for referral to the GP is appropriate if symptoms are atypical or do not resolve.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Referral to the GP is mandatory for anyone who is pregnant. Mebendazole is contraindicated in the first trimester. In the second or third trimesters, or during breastfeeding, a doctor must weigh the risks and benefits. The pharmacy recommendation is hygiene measures only.

People with significant comorbidities

Mebendazole is generally well-tolerated with low systemic absorption, so it is safe for most patients with comorbidities such as diabetes, asthma, or heart disease. Caution is required in those with severe liver impairment, and a check for drug interactions (especially metronidazole) is important.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Standard Request & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I think my son has worms. Can I get some Ovex?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Can I just ask a few questions to make sure it's the right thing? Who is it for and how old are they?"
  • Patient: "It's for my son, he's 5. But I think we all need it."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. And what symptoms has he had?"
  • Patient: "He's been really itchy at night for the last few days, and really grumpy."
  • Pharmacist: "Have you tried anything yet or is this the first time?"
  • Patient: "First time. I've never had to buy it before."
  • Pharmacist: "And is anyone in the house pregnant or taking any regular medication?"
  • Patient: "No, all fine."
  • Recommendation: "That sounds exactly like threadworms. You're right, the whole family needs to be treated. You should all take one tablet today, and then a second one in two weeks' time. The most important part is hygiene to stop you catching them again. I'll pop a leaflet in the bag that explains it, but it involves things like hot washing bedding and strict hand hygiene for six weeks. Does that sound okay?"

Scenario 2: Referral due to Pregnancy

  • Patient: "Hi, can I get treatment for threadworms for my family?"
  • Pharmacist: "Certainly. Can I just check who it's for?"
  • Patient: "For me, my husband, and our 4-year-old daughter."
  • Pharmacist: "No problem. Are you taking any other medicines or do you have any other medical conditions?"
  • Patient: "No, but I am pregnant. I'm about 10 weeks."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. Because you're in the early stages of pregnancy, the medication isn't recommended. The safest approach is to focus on the strict hygiene methods, as this can break the cycle on its own. Your husband and daughter can take the treatment. I can't supply the medicine for you today, you will need to speak to your GP for advice. In the meantime, I can go through the hygiene measures with you now, as you should start those straight away."
