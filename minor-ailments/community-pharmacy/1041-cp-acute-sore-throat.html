Acute Sore Throat in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute sore throat is an extremely common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, characterised by pharyngeal pain that is often worse on swallowing. It is most frequently a symptom of acute pharyngitis or tonsillitis. In over 90% of cases in adults, the cause is viral and self-limiting.

Patients typically present with a rapid onset of throat pain, which may be associated with pyrexia, headache, and malaise. On inspection, there is typically pharyngeal and/or tonsillar erythema. Tonsillar swelling or exudates may be present. The condition is usually benign, with most symptoms resolving within one week, regardless of the underlying cause (viral or bacterial).

Common triggers are viral infections, such as rhinovirus, adenovirus, and coronavirus. The most common bacterial cause is Group A beta-haemolytic streptococcus (GABHS). The pharmacy team's role is to identify features suggestive of a more serious diagnosis through effective questioning, recommend appropriate self-care and OTC treatments, and manage expectations about the natural duration of symptoms.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Airway compromise: Any signs of stridor (a high-pitched whistling sound on breathing), respiratory distress, drooling, or a muffled 'hot potato' voice suggest potential airway obstruction. This requires immediate emergency referral.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy): Characterised by severe unilateral (one-sided) throat pain, trismus (difficulty opening the mouth), deviation of the uvula away from the affected side, and a visible swelling of the soft palate. This requires urgent referral.
  • Systemic sepsis: Features such as marked fever (>38°C), rigors, tachycardia, hypotension, or new-onset confusion indicate a systemic response to infection that warrants urgent hospital evaluation.
  • Inability to swallow fluids: If a patient, particularly a child or frail adult, is unable to swallow their own saliva or maintain hydration, they require urgent medical assessment.
  • Suspected Scarlet Fever: A sore throat accompanied by a characteristic fine, sandpapery rash on the torso, a flushed face, and a 'strawberry tongue'. This requires urgent GP assessment and is a notifiable disease.
  • Immunocompromised patient: A sore throat in a patient with significant immunosuppression (e.g., on chemotherapy, certain DMARDs like methotrexate, or anti-rejection drugs) requires an urgent referral to exclude serious complications like neutropenia.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. Many OTC sore throat products, particularly medicated lozenges, have specific age restrictions (e.g., not for under 6s or 12s). This also establishes if the patient is present.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the sore throat and screen for red flags. Key questions include "Is the pain on one side or all over?", "Any difficulty breathing or swallowing?", "Have you noticed a rash?".
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish the timeline. A sore throat that is not improving after 1 week or worsens significantly requires a GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments the patient has already tried, particularly analgesics like paracetamol or ibuprofen. This avoids duplication and helps gauge symptom severity.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for comorbidities and interactions. Key considerations are immunosuppressant drugs (methotrexate, carbimazole), which make a sore throat a red flag, and checking for contraindications to NSAID-containing lozenges (e.g., flurbiprofen).
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting to the pharmacy are simple viral infections.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis: The most frequent cause. Often accompanied by other URTI symptoms like cough and coryza.
  • Bacterial Tonsillitis (GABHS): Clinically difficult to distinguish from viral causes without a formal scoring tool, but may present with more marked tonsillar exudate and fever without a cough.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Infectious Mononucleosis (Glandular Fever): Suspect in adolescents or young adults with a severe sore throat, profound fatigue, and significant neck swelling.
  • Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy): A key differential to exclude if there is unilateral pain and trismus.
  • Scarlet Fever: Suspect if a sore throat is accompanied by the characteristic sandpaper rash.
  • Epiglottitis: A rare but critical differential if there are signs of airway distress like drooling or stridor.
  • Agranulocytosis: A rare but serious cause in patients taking certain medicines (e.g., carbimazole), presenting with a severe sore throat and fever.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief. Non-pharmacological advice and simple analgesia are first-line, with other options available for targeted relief.

First-Line Advice (All Sore Throats)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise adequate rest, hydration, and soothing remedies like a hot drink with lemon and honey (honey is unsuitable for infants under 1 year).
  • Systemic Analgesia (e.g., Paracetamol, Ibuprofen):
    • Mechanism: Provide systemic pain relief and reduce fever. Ibuprofen also has an anti-inflammatory effect.
    • Licensing: GSL. Available for adults and children (check product-specific age licensing).
    • Counselling: This is the most effective way to relieve pain from a sore throat. Advise regular dosing. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options (For Local Relief)

  • Local Anaesthetic Products (e.g., Benzocaine, Lidocaine):
    • Mechanism: Act directly on nerve endings in the throat mucosa to provide a numbing effect and temporary pain relief.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available as lozenges and sprays. Age restrictions apply (typically not for under 6s or 12s).
    • Counselling: Advise that this will cause numbness. Be careful with hot food/drink soon after use. [Rx info]
  • Anti-inflammatory Products (e.g., Flurbiprofen, Benzydamine):
    • Mechanism: Topical NSAIDs that reduce inflammation locally in the throat.
    • Licensing: P medicines. Age restrictions apply (typically 12+).
    • Counselling: Must perform a full check for NSAID contraindications (asthma, stomach, kidney, heart problems). [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for sore throat, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Phenoxymethylpenicillin):
    Context: GPs use scoring tools (like FeverPAIN) to decide on prescribing. They are only indicated for suspected bacterial cases (e.g., high score or scarlet fever) to modestly reduce symptom duration and prevent rare complications. This knowledge is key for upholding antimicrobial stewardship when patients request antibiotics. Under the Pharmacy First service in England, pharmacists can supply antibiotics for sinusitis or ear infections, but not for uncomplicated sore throats. [Rx advice]
  • Corticosteroids (e.g., Dexamethasone):
    Context: A GP or hospital doctor may consider a single, one-off dose of an oral steroid for adults with a very severe, painful, swollen throat to help them swallow. This is an off-label use and is not routine. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral may be appropriate and what treatments a doctor might consider, preventing the perception that the pharmacy is simply a barrier to treatment.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for acute sore throat.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the natural course. A key phrase is: "It's normal for a sore throat to last for about a week. Antibiotics don't usually speed this up as it's nearly always caused by a virus."
  • Justify No Antibiotics: When a patient requests antibiotics, provide a clear, confident explanation. "This sore throat is caused by a virus, and antibiotics only work against bacteria. They won't help you get better any faster but could cause side effects like an upset stomach."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as the primary treatment. Recommend regular paracetamol or ibuprofen, rest, plenty of fluids, and soothing hot drinks.
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain what the recommended product does and its limitations. For example, "These lozenges have a local anaesthetic to numb the pain in your throat for a while, but you should still take paracetamol or ibuprofen for more effective, longer-lasting pain relief."
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to make sure I've explained everything clearly, what symptoms would make you go and see a doctor?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help. This ensures that a patient with a self-limiting illness knows when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The sore throat is not starting to improve after one week.
  • They are a patient who gets recurrent sore throats.
  • They have a weakened immune system and are feeling unwell.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (call GP or 111 immediately, or go to A&E if severe) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Difficulty breathing.
  • Difficulty swallowing their own saliva or opening their mouth.
  • Severe pain that is much worse on one side of the throat.
  • A high temperature that doesn't come down with analgesia, or feeling systemically very unwell.
  • A new skin rash.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute pharyngitis is the inflammation of the pharynx, the part of the throat behind the mouth and nasal cavity. It is a direct result of an inflammatory response to an invading pathogen, most commonly a virus (e.g., rhinovirus, adenovirus) or less commonly a bacterium (e.g., Streptococcus pyogenes).

The pathogen adheres to and invades the epithelial cells of the pharyngeal mucosa. This triggers the innate immune system, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and mediators like prostaglandins and bradykinin. This cascade results in vasodilation and increased permeability of local blood vessels, leading to the classic signs of inflammation: erythema (redness), oedema (swelling), and heat.

The oedema and the direct stimulation of afferent nerve fibres (nociceptors) by inflammatory mediators cause the sensation of pain (odynophagia). In bacterial tonsillitis, a significant influx of neutrophils can lead to the formation of pus, which appears as a visible tonsillar exudate. Systemic symptoms like fever and malaise are caused by the systemic circulation of cytokines like Interleukin-1 (IL-1), IL-6, and TNF-alpha.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and cautions.

Anti-inflammatory Products (Flurbiprofen, Benzydamine)

  • Contraindications: As these are topical NSAIDs, they must not be sold to patients with a history of hypersensitivity to aspirin or other NSAIDs, active peptic ulcer disease, or severe heart, kidney or liver failure. Use with caution in asthma.
  • Counselling: Always ask about other NSAID use (e.g., ibuprofen tablets) to avoid excessive dosage.

Local Anaesthetic Products (Benzocaine)

  • Side Effects: The numbing effect can temporarily interfere with the swallowing reflex. Advise caution when eating or drinking hot items shortly after use.
  • Rare Risks: Very rarely, benzocaine can cause methaemoglobinaemia, a serious blood disorder. This is more of a risk in children, which is why age limits are strict.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Hidden Ingredients: Many "all-in-one" cold and flu remedies contain paracetamol or ibuprofen. It is essential to counsel patients not to take additional separate doses of these analgesics to avoid accidental overdose.
  • Decongestants: Products also containing decongestants (e.g., pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine) are contraindicated in patients with hypertension, heart disease, and in those taking MAOIs.
11. Use in special populations

Children

OTC medicated lozenges and sprays are generally not licensed for children under 6, with many restricted to age 12+. Always check the specific product's license. The first-line recommendation for all children is an age-appropriate dose of paracetamol or ibuprofen, alongside simple soothing measures like a warm drink of lemon and honey (honey should only be given to children over 1 year old).

Older adults

Be cautious with NSAID-containing products (e.g., flurbiprofen) due to the increased risk of gastrointestinal and renal side effects. A thorough check for interacting medications and comorbidities is essential. Have a lower threshold for referral to the GP in this group.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with rest, fluids, steam inhalation and gargling with salt water is first-line. Paracetamol is the analgesic of choice. Most medicated lozenges and sprays should be avoided as there is insufficient safety data. Simple, non-medicated lozenges are acceptable. Refer to the GP if symptoms are severe or require treatment beyond simple analgesia.

People with significant comorbidities

  • Immunosuppression: A sore throat is a red flag. Refer urgently to their GP or specialist team.
  • Asthma: Avoid NSAID-containing products (e.g., flurbiprofen) in patients with a history of NSAID-induced bronchospasm.
  • Diabetes: Recommend sugar-free formulations of lozenges and linctuses where possible.
12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Viral Sore Throat & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, can I get something for this awful sore throat? It's really painful to swallow."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. To make sure I get you the safest and best thing, who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 40."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. How long has it been troubling you, and is the pain all over or just on one side?"
  • Patient: "Started two days ago, and it's all over. I have a bit of a cough and cold too."
  • Pharmacist: "Have you tried anything yet?"
  • Patient: "Just some paracetamol last night, which helped a bit."
  • Pharmacist: "And do you take any regular medication or have any other medical conditions?"
  • Patient: "No, I'm generally fit and well."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a typical viral infection. The best thing you can do is continue with regular paracetamol or ibuprofen for the pain. To help with the local pain, you could try these lozenges which contain a local anaesthetic to numb your throat. It's normal for this to last about a week, so keep up with fluids and rest. If it's not getting any better by then, or you start to feel very unwell, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something strong for my throat. It's agony on this one side."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear that. Can you tell me more? How long has it been like this?"
  • Patient: "About three days, but it's got really bad today. I can barely open my mouth."
  • Pharmacist (identifying red flags): "Okay, thank you for telling me that. When the pain is severe on one side and you're finding it difficult to open your mouth, it can be a sign of something more serious than a simple sore throat, like an abscess. An over-the-counter medicine isn't suitable, and you need to be assessed by a doctor today."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "I would advise you to contact your GP surgery right now and tell them you have severe unilateral throat pain and difficulty opening your mouth. If you cannot get an urgent appointment, you should go to A&E. Does that make sense?"
