Indigestion / Dyspepsia in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Dyspepsia is a common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, describing a complex of upper gastrointestinal symptoms including epigastric pain or discomfort, heartburn, bloating, early satiety, belching, and nausea. It is often recurrent and can significantly impact quality of life.

Patients typically present with symptoms linked to eating, often described as a 'gnawing' or 'burning' epigastric pain. Symptoms can be triggered or exacerbated by certain foods (e.g., fatty, spicy), alcohol, caffeine, or medications, particularly Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs).

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate common, manageable causes from more serious underlying pathology through effective questioning. This involves providing advice on self-care, recommending appropriate OTC treatments, and understanding when an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services is necessary.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any 'ALARM' features that suggest a more serious underlying cause for dyspepsia. The presence of any red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing is a cardinal red flag for oesophageal cancer.
  • Unintentional weight loss: An unexplained and significant loss of weight is a worrying constitutional symptom for upper GI malignancy.
  • Persistent vomiting: Particularly if it is recurrent and unexplained, as it may suggest gastric outlet obstruction.
  • Epigastric mass: A palpable mass on abdominal examination requires urgent investigation.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Symptoms like unusual tiredness and pallor in the context of dyspepsia may indicate chronic bleeding and warrant referral.
  • Haematemesis or melaena: Vomiting blood or passing black, tarry stools indicates upper GI bleeding and requires emergency referral.
  • New, unexplained, and persistent dyspepsia in a patient aged 55 or over: This requires GP referral for assessment, which may include consideration for endoscopy.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the age and identity of the patient. Many OTC dyspepsia products have age restrictions (e.g., PPIs are licensed for over 18s). This also establishes if the patient is present.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the symptoms (e.g., burning pain vs. acid reflux) and to screen for the critical ALARM red flags (e.g., "Have you had any difficulty swallowing or any unexplained weight loss?").
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To differentiate between recent onset and chronic symptoms. A persistent problem, or new symptoms in someone over 55, requires a GP referral. Short-term symptoms are more appropriate for OTC care.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments the patient has tried (e.g., other OTCs, lifestyle changes). This avoids duplication and helps gauge symptom severity.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: Essential to check for causative medicines (e.g., NSAIDs, bisphosphonates) and potential comorbidities (e.g., heart disease, where a cardiac cause of pain must be considered). It also helps to avoid drug interactions.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting are benign and can be managed with self-care and OTC products.

Common Causes Suitable for Pharmacy Support

  • Functional Dyspepsia: Recurrent symptoms with no underlying structural cause.
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD): Where heartburn is the main symptom. Suitable for short-term OTC management.
  • Medication-Induced Dyspepsia: Often caused by NSAIDs purchased over the counter.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Oesophageal or Gastric Cancer: The key concern, especially in older patients or those with ALARM symptoms.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Suspect with persistent, localised epigastric pain. While some cases are linked to *H. pylori* (requiring a GP), NSAID-induced ulcers are also common.
  • Biliary Tract Disease: Suspect if pain is severe, colicky, and related to fatty foods.
  • Cardiac Pain: Must be considered if symptoms are atypical, exertional, or if the patient has cardiac risk factors. This requires emergency referral.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is symptom control and enabling self-management of uncomplicated dyspepsia. Lifestyle advice is a critical first step.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise on avoiding trigger foods, eating smaller meals, weight management if appropriate, and smoking cessation.

Treatments for Rapid Symptom Relief

  • Antacids and Alginates:
    • Mechanism: Antacids (e.g., magnesium carbonate) neutralise stomach acid for fast relief. Alginates (e.g., sodium alginate) form a viscous gel (raft) that floats on the stomach contents, preventing reflux into the oesophagus.
    • Licensing: Mostly GSL. Suitable for most adults and older children (check product-specific licenses).
    • Counselling: Excellent for immediate, short-term relief. Advise that liquid formulations work quickest. Must be taken at a different time from other medications (leave a 2-hour gap). [Rx info]

Treatments for Preventing Symptoms (Acid Suppression)

  • Pharmacy (P) Medicine PPIs (e.g., Pantoprazole, Esomeprazole):
    • Mechanism: Potently reduce the amount of acid the stomach produces. Their effect is delayed but lasts longer than antacids.
    • Licensing: P medicine, for adults over 18 for the short-term treatment of reflux symptoms. Supply is restricted to a limited duration (usually 2 weeks).
    • Counselling: For prevention, not instant relief. Take 30-60 mins before food. Crucially, the patient must be referred to their GP if symptoms persist after the treatment course, if they need it frequently, or if any ALARM symptoms are present. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Full-Dose Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) / H2-Receptor Antagonists (H2RAs):
    Context: A GP will prescribe these, often at higher doses and for longer durations than available OTC, for persistent symptoms or conditions like severe oesophagitis or peptic ulcer disease. Pharmacists should support adherence and counsel on long-term use when dispensing. [Rx advice]
  • *H. pylori* Eradication Therapy:
    Context: If a GP confirms *H. pylori* infection (via a breath or stool test), they will prescribe a one-week "triple therapy" course, consisting of a PPI and two antibiotics. Pharmacists play a key role in counselling on the importance of completing the full course and managing potential side effects. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral is the correct course of action for symptoms that are persistent or accompanied by red flags, rather than continued OTC use.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for dyspepsia.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Reassure the patient that indigestion is very common and often managed effectively with lifestyle changes and short-term treatment.
  • Justify Product Choice: Clearly explain the difference between products. For example, "This Gaviscon is great for quick relief now, as it creates a protective barrier. This pantoprazole tablet works differently by reducing acid production, so it's better for preventing the symptoms from coming on in the first place."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Actively recommend and explain the rationale behind lifestyle advice (e.g., "Eating smaller meals can help prevent the stomach from becoming too full and pushing acid upwards.").
  • Interaction Counselling: Always remind patients to leave a two-hour gap between antacids and other medicines, especially for crucial drugs like thyroxine or antibiotics.
  • Check Understanding of Referral: When selling a P medicine like an OTC PPI, confirm the patient understands when to see a doctor. "So, just to check, you'll use this for two weeks, but if it's not better by then, you'll make an appointment with your GP?"
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from the GP after an OTC consultation.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • Symptoms do not improve after the recommended duration of OTC treatment (e.g., 14 days for an OTC PPI).
  • Symptoms recur quickly after stopping treatment.
  • They find they are using OTC remedies frequently and for a long time.
  • They have any concerns or the symptoms change.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Difficulty swallowing.
  • Vomiting blood or what looks like coffee grounds.
  • Passing black, tarry stools.
  • Significant, unexplained weight loss.
  • Severe pain.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of dyspepsia is multifactorial. A balance normally exists in the upper GI tract between aggressive factors (gastric acid, pepsin) and protective mechanisms (the mucus-bicarbonate layer, prostaglandins, adequate blood flow). Dyspepsia arises when this balance is disrupted.

In Peptic Ulcer Disease, this disruption is key. *Helicobacter pylori*, a spiral-shaped bacterium, survives in the acidic stomach environment by producing urease. It causes chronic inflammation of the gastric mucosa (gastritis), which can lead to increased acid secretion and impaired mucosal defence, predisposing to ulcer formation. NSAIDs cause damage primarily by inhibiting cyclo-oxygenase (COX) enzymes, which reduces the production of prostaglandins that are vital for maintaining the protective mucosal barrier and blood flow.

In Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD), symptoms arise from the reflux of acidic stomach contents into the oesophagus, most commonly due to transient relaxations of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS).

Functional Dyspepsia is the most common form, but its pathophysiology is least understood. It is considered a disorder of gut-brain interaction. Proposed mechanisms include visceral hypersensitivity (where normal physiological stimuli are perceived as painful), delayed gastric emptying, and impaired gastric accommodation to a meal. Psychological factors and prior gastrointestinal infections can also play a role.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and patient factors.

Antacids and Alginates

  • Interactions: The key counselling point is separating the dose by at least two hours from other medications, especially levothyroxine, bisphosphonates, and certain antibiotics (e.g., quinolones, tetracyclines), as they can impair absorption.
  • Sodium Content: Many preparations have a high sodium content. Be mindful of this when recommending to patients with hypertension, heart failure, or renal impairment. Highlight low-sodium versions where available.

OTC Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

  • Masking of Serious Conditions: The biggest risk is that inappropriate or prolonged use can mask the symptoms of underlying gastric cancer, delaying diagnosis. This is why adherence to the licensed indication (short-term use for reflux) and strict safety netting is vital.
  • Referral Triggers: A sale should always be accompanied by clear advice to see the GP if symptoms persist, worsen, or recur quickly. A request for a repeat supply should trigger further questioning and likely referral.

NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen)

  • Causative Factor: When selling an NSAID, it is good practice to ask if the person suffers from indigestion and to advise them to take it with or after food to minimise irritation.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Persistent dyspeptic symptoms in children are uncommon and warrant referral to the GP for investigation. Simple antacids may be licensed for children over a certain age (e.g., 12), but repeated use should be discouraged without a medical diagnosis.

Older adults

Maintain a high index of suspicion and a low threshold for referral. New-onset dyspepsia in anyone over 55 is a mandatory referral to the GP. Be aware of polypharmacy and potential for NSAID-induced symptoms.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care and simple antacids or alginates are first-line and generally considered safe. If these are ineffective, the patient should be referred to their GP to discuss other options like a PPI. Avoid making recommendations for other products without checking specialist resources.

People with significant comorbidities

  • Cardiac Disease: Always consider that epigastric pain could be cardiac in origin. A new, unexplained pain in someone with cardiac risk factors requires an immediate referral.
  • Renal Impairment / Hypertension: Advise caution with high-sodium antacids.
  • Osteoporosis: While mainly a concern with long-term prescription use, be aware that PPIs are associated with a fracture risk. This reinforces that OTC PPIs are for short-term use only.
12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Reflux & OTC PPI Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I keep getting awful heartburn after my dinner. Can I get something for it?"
  • Pharmacist (WWHAM): "Certainly. Is it for you? (Yes). What are the symptoms exactly? (A burning feeling rising in my chest). How long has this been happening? (A few weeks). Have you tried anything? (Just some Gaviscon, it helps for a bit). And do you take any other medicines? (No)." The pharmacist also confirms no ALARM symptoms.
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like reflux. The Gaviscon is good for quick relief, but as it keeps happening, you could try a tablet that reduces the acid in your stomach. This is pantoprazole. You take one tablet each morning for two weeks. It's important that if it's not better after that, or if it comes back straight away, you see your GP. Does that sound okay?"

Scenario 2: Referral of an Older Patient

  • Patient: "I'd like some indigestion tablets for myself, please."
  • Pharmacist (WWHAM): "Okay. Can I ask your age? (I'm 62). And what are your symptoms? (Just a nagging pain at the top of my tummy). How long has that been going on? (About a month now, I've never had it before). Have you tried anything? (No). Are you on any other medication? (Just my blood pressure pills)."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that information. Because these symptoms are new for you and you're over 55, the national guidance recommends that you should speak to your GP about it rather than trying something from the pharmacy first. They will be able to make sure it's nothing that needs further investigation. I'd recommend you make an appointment with your surgery this week."
