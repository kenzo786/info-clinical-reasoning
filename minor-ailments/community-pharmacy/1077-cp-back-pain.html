Acute Low Back Pain in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Low back pain is an extremely common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It is typically defined as pain in the lumbosacral region, between the bottom of the ribs and the top of the legs. The vast majority of cases are 'non-specific' or 'mechanical' back pain, meaning there is no identifiable serious underlying pathology and the pain originates from the structures of the back, such as muscles, ligaments, and joints.

Patients usually present with a sudden or gradual onset of pain, which may be localised or radiate into the buttocks or thigh. The pain is often described as an ache or stiffness, and is typically worsened by movement or certain postures. An episode of acute, non-specific low back pain usually resolves within a few days to weeks. Sciatica, where pain radiates down the leg below the knee, may take longer to resolve. The pharmacy team's role is to exclude serious causes through targeted questioning, provide self-management advice, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations about recovery.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags requires an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: Bilateral sciatica, severe or progressive bilateral neurological deficit, difficulty urinating or urinary retention, loss of anal sphincter tone or faecal incontinence, and saddle anaesthesia (numbness around the buttocks, perineum, and inner thighs). This is a medical emergency requiring immediate A&E referral.
  • Spinal Malignancy/Metastasis: A personal history of cancer, unexplained weight loss, pain that is worse at night or when lying down, and being generally unwell.
  • Spinal Infection (e.g., discitis, osteomyelitis): Fever, chills, recent bacterial infection, history of intravenous drug use, or being immunocompromised. The pain is often severe and constant.
  • Vertebral Fracture: History of significant trauma, or minor trauma in a patient with osteoporosis or on long-term steroids. There may be a structural deformity or severe localised bony tenderness.
  • Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA): A pulsatile abdominal mass, severe pain that may radiate to the back, especially in older male smokers with a history of vascular disease. This requires emergency assessment.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. Many OTC analgesics have age restrictions. It also establishes if the patient is pregnant or breastfeeding, where treatment options are very limited.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the pain (e.g., "Where exactly is the pain?", "What does it feel like - ache, sharp, shooting?"). Crucially, to screen for red flags by asking about radiation below the knee, numbness, weakness, and any bladder or bowel problems.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To differentiate an acute episode from chronic pain. Back pain lasting more than 6 weeks with no improvement may require GP assessment.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what self-care measures or treatments the patient has tried. This avoids duplicating therapy and helps assess the severity of the condition. Have they already seen a GP or physiotherapist?
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for contraindications and interactions with OTC analgesics (e.g., NSAIDs with anticoagulants, other NSAIDs, or certain antihypertensives). To identify any relevant comorbidities (e.g., asthma, stomach problems, kidney disease, heart failure).
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Non-specific Low Back Pain: The most common diagnosis. Pain is localised to the back and is mechanical in nature.
  • Sciatica (Lumbar Radiculopathy): Unilateral leg pain, often radiating below the knee, which is typically worse than the back pain. May be associated with altered sensation.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: Suggested by bilateral leg symptoms and sphincter disturbance.
  • Malignancy/Infection/Fracture: Indicated by specific red flags in the history and systemic signs.
  • Inflammatory Arthritis (e.g., Ankylosing Spondylitis): Consider in younger adults with pain and stiffness that is worse in the morning and improves with exercise.
  • Referred Pain: Pain originating from other organs, such as pancreatitis or kidney stones. The history will usually be inconsistent with mechanical pain.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management focuses on promoting activity and providing symptomatic relief. Non-pharmacological advice is the cornerstone of treatment.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This is the most important recommendation. Advise patients to remain active and continue normal activities as much as possible. Discourage bed rest. Simple exercises, walking, and stretching are beneficial.
  • Heat/Cold Therapy: A hot water bottle or an ice pack wrapped in a towel can provide short-term pain relief.

Pharmacological Options

  • Topical NSAIDs / Rubefacients (e.g., Ibuprofen gel, heat rubs):
    • Mechanism: Topical NSAIDs deliver the drug locally to reduce inflammation with lower systemic absorption. Rubefacients cause local irritation and warmth, providing a counter-irritant effect to distract from the underlying pain.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL or P. Age restrictions apply.
    • Counselling: A good first-line pharmacological option due to lower systemic risk. Advise to apply a thin layer to the affected area. Do not use on broken skin. [Rx info]
  • Oral NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen):
    • Mechanism: Reduces pain and inflammation systemically.
    • Licensing: GSL or P (depending on strength and pack size). Not for use in children under 12 for back pain without medical advice.
    • Counselling: Should be taken at the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible time. Advise taking with or after food. A thorough WWHAM check for contraindications (stomach, kidney, heart problems, asthma) is essential. [Rx info]

Note: NICE guidance does not recommend paracetamol alone for managing low back pain, and combination products containing codeine should not be recommended for back pain in a pharmacy setting.

6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for back pain, awareness is vital for managing patient expectations and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Oral NSAIDs (e.g., Naproxen):
    Context: A GP may prescribe a stronger NSAID like naproxen, often with a stomach-protecting medicine (PPI), if OTC options are insufficient. Pharmacists should check for interactions and counsel on side effects upon dispensing. [Rx advice]
  • Weak Opioids (e.g., Codeine):
    Context: A GP may consider a short course of a weak opioid, usually in combination with paracetamol, for severe acute pain only when an NSAID is contraindicated or has failed. This is not a long-term solution. Pharmacists should be aware of the risk of dependence and counsel on side effects like constipation and drowsiness. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team explain why a GP referral is necessary for treatments that are not available over the counter, reinforcing a professional and consistent healthcare message.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for low back pain.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that most back pain is not serious and will get better. A key phrase is: "It's normal for a simple back strain to be painful for a week or two, but staying gently active is the best thing you can do for it."
  • Justify Referral or No Sale: If a referral is needed, explain why clearly and calmly (e.g., "The symptoms you've described need to be checked out by a doctor to make sure there's nothing more serious going on"). If a product is not suitable, explain the reason (e.g., "Because you have asthma, an anti-inflammatory tablet might not be safe for you").
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as the primary treatment. Emphasise that "motion is lotion" and that hurt does not equal harm. Encourage a gradual return to normal activities.
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain how to use the product safely. For oral NSAIDs, highlight taking them with food and for a limited time. For topical products, explain how to apply them and to wash hands after use.
  • Explain Why Imaging is Not Needed: If a patient asks about scans, explain that they are not usually helpful for simple back pain and don't change the treatment, which is to keep moving.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient on when and how to seek further help. This ensures they know when to act if their condition worsens or does not follow the expected course.

Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The pain is not improving after 1-2 weeks of self-care and OTC treatment.
  • The pain is worsening significantly.
  • They develop new tingling, numbness, or weakness in their leg(s).

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (via their GP, NHS 111, or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, especially:

  • Numbness around the buttocks or genital region.
  • Any change in bladder or bowel function (either inability to go or loss of control).
  • Progressive weakness in the legs.
  • The pain started after a major accident or trauma.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or their symptoms change.

9. Pathophysiology

The lumbar spine is a complex structure of vertebrae, intervertebral discs, facet joints, ligaments, and muscles. Non-specific low back pain arises from strain or sprain of these structures. It is considered a 'nociceptive' pain source, where local tissue injury or inflammation activates pain receptors.

In most acute cases, a precise pathoanatomical diagnosis is not possible or necessary. The pain is thought to be driven by a combination of minor tears in muscles or ligaments, inflammation of facet joints, or irritation of the outer layers of the intervertebral disc. This leads to protective muscle spasm, which itself can be painful and limit movement.

Sciatica is a neuropathic pain state. It occurs when a lumbar or sacral nerve root is compressed or chemically irritated, most commonly by a prolapsed intervertebral disc. The compression and associated inflammation of the nerve root leads to pain, numbness, and tingling felt in the specific dermatome supplied by that nerve.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires vigilance for contraindications and risks.

Oral NSAIDs (Ibuprofen)

  • Contraindications: A history of hypersensitivity to NSAIDs, active or previous GI ulceration/bleeding. Must be checked carefully before every sale.
  • Cautions: Use with caution in patients with asthma (can provoke bronchospasm), renal impairment, hypertension, and heart failure. Patients over 65 are at higher risk of side effects.
  • Interactions: Risk of GI bleeding is increased when used with anticoagulants, SSRIs, and corticosteroids. Can reduce the effect of many antihypertensives. Avoid selling to a patient already taking a prescribed NSAID (e.g., naproxen, diclofenac).

Topical Products

  • Cautions: While systemic absorption is low, caution is still advised in patients with hypersensitivity to NSAIDs, asthma, or renal problems. Should not be applied to broken or infected skin.

Products Containing Codeine

  • Recommendation: While available OTC in combination with paracetamol or ibuprofen for other types of pain, these should not be recommended for simple back pain as per NICE guidance. If a patient specifically requests them for back pain, it is an opportunity to educate them about more appropriate first-line treatments like keeping active and using topical or oral NSAIDs if suitable.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Back pain is a red flag in children and they must be referred to their GP for assessment. Do not recommend OTC treatment without a doctor's advice.

Older adults

Be extremely cautious with oral NSAIDs due to the increased risk of GI, renal, and cardiovascular side effects. A topical product is a much safer first-line pharmacological option. Have a lower threshold for referral to the GP as red flag conditions are more common.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Refer to GP for assessment. Non-pharmacological advice (exercise, posture) is key. Oral NSAIDs are contraindicated in the third trimester of pregnancy and should be avoided throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding. Topical NSAIDs should also be avoided unless advised by a doctor. Paracetamol (if appropriate for the patient) is the safest oral analgesic but has limited benefit in back pain.

People with significant comorbidities

A thorough WWHAM check is vital. Oral NSAIDs are contraindicated or require caution in many long-term conditions.

  • Asthma: Avoid oral NSAIDs if the patient has a history of NSAID-induced bronchospasm.
  • Stomach/GI problems: Avoid oral NSAIDs in patients with a history of ulcers.
  • Kidney/Heart/Liver disease: Avoid oral NSAIDs.
A topical product or referral may be the only safe options.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Mechanical Back Pain & Successful Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I've got a really sore lower back. Can you recommend something?"
  • Pharmacist: "I can certainly try. First, who is it for?" (W)
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 40."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. What are the symptoms like? Is the pain going down your leg at all?" (W)
  • Patient: "No, it's just a dull ache right across my lower back. It started yesterday after I was gardening."
  • Pharmacist: "And have you tried anything yet?" (A)
  • Patient: "Just a hot water bottle, which helped a bit."
  • Pharmacist: "Do you take any other medicines or have any other health problems, like asthma or stomach issues?" (M)
  • Patient: "No, I'm generally fit and well."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a simple mechanical back strain. The best thing is to keep gently moving. For the pain, a topical ibuprofen gel is a good, safe option to rub on the area. Alternatively, you could take ibuprofen tablets with food for a few days. If it's not starting to feel better in a week, or you develop any leg pain or numbness, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Sciatica with a Red Flag & Urgent Referral

  • Patient: "Can I have some strong painkillers? My back is killing me."
  • Pharmacist: "Let's see what the best thing to do is. Who are they for?" (W)
  • Patient: "Me. I'm 60."
  • Pharmacist: "Can you tell me more about the pain?" (W)
  • Patient: "It's in my back but it's shooting down both of my legs. And the weirdest thing is, the area where I'd sit on a saddle feels a bit numb."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. The pain in both legs and especially the numbness you've described are red flags that need to be checked out immediately. Selling you something over the counter would not be safe. You need to go to A&E straight away to be assessed today. Please don't delay."
