Allergic Conjunctivitis in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Allergic conjunctivitis is a very common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, particularly during spring and summer. It is a Type I hypersensitivity reaction of the conjunctiva to an allergen. Patients typically present with bilateral, acute onset of intense itching, which is the hallmark symptom. This is often accompanied by watery discharge, conjunctival redness (hyperaemia), and mild to moderate swelling of the conjunctiva (chemosis) and eyelids (oedema).

The condition is frequently associated with a personal or family history of atopy, such as allergic rhinitis (hay fever), asthma, or eczema. Symptoms can be seasonal, commonly triggered by pollen, or perennial, triggered by indoor allergens like house dust mites or animal dander. Vision is not typically affected, though it may be momentarily blurry due to excessive tearing. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate the condition from more serious causes of red eye, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause, requiring referral to a GP or, more appropriately, an optometrist or A&E/Eye Casualty. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral.

  • Significant eye pain: Pain, as opposed to itching or grittiness, is not a typical feature. Severe pain could indicate conditions like keratitis, scleritis, anterior uveitis, or acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Photophobia (light sensitivity): Marked sensitivity to light is a strong indicator of corneal or intraocular inflammation (e.g., keratitis or anterior uveitis).
  • Marked reduction in visual acuity: Vision that is persistently blurred and does not clear with blinking is a significant red flag.
  • Corneal involvement: Any visible opacity, haziness, or ulcer on the cornea is an emergency. An optometrist can use fluorescein dye to check for this.
  • Unilateral red eye: Simple allergic conjunctivitis is almost always bilateral. A persistent, unilateral red eye should raise suspicion for infective keratitis, foreign body, or anterior uveitis.
  • Contact lens wearers: Any contact lens wearer with a red, painful eye must be urgently referred to an optometrist or eye casualty to rule out microbial keratitis, a sight-threatening emergency.
  • History of trauma or recent eye surgery: A red eye in this context requires specialist evaluation to exclude complications.
  • Abnormal pupil: A fixed, dilated, or irregular pupil points towards acute glaucoma or anterior uveitis.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age, as many OTC eye drops have specific age restrictions. It also establishes if the patient is present, as third-party consultations require extra caution.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To establish the core nature of the condition. Key questions: "Is it itchy?", "Are your eyes watery or sticky?", "Is it one eye or both?". Screen for red flags: "Is there any pain?", "Is your vision affected?", "Does light bother your eyes?".
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand the timeline. Is it acute? Does it happen at the same time every year (seasonal)? Or is it present all year round (perennial)? Symptoms that fail to improve with treatment warrant referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what self-care or OTC products the patient has already tried. This avoids duplicating treatment and helps gauge severity if first-line options have failed.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for comorbidities and relevant history. Crucial questions include: "Do you wear contact lenses?", "Do you have any other medical conditions, especially glaucoma?", and "Are you taking any other medicines?".
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting allergic or infective conjunctivitis.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Conjunctivitis: Highly contagious, watery discharge, gritty feeling. Often associated with a cold and tender lymph nodes in front of the ear. May start in one eye and spread to the other.
  • Bacterial Conjunctivitis: Sticky, pus-like discharge, causing eyelids to be stuck together. Itching is not prominent. Also contagious.
  • Dry Eye Syndrome: Causes redness and a burning or gritty sensation, often worsening through the day. Less likely to be itchy.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Anterior Uveitis / Iritis: Presents with a painful, photophobic red eye, often with blurred vision and a small or irregular pupil.
  • Infective Keratitis: A key differential in contact lens wearers. Marked by severe pain, photophobia, and reduced vision. A sight-threatening emergency.
  • Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma: Presents with a severely painful red eye, blurred vision (with haloes), headache, and nausea. The pupil is often fixed and mid-dilated. An ophthalmic emergency.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief. Non-pharmacological advice is paramount and should accompany any product recommendation.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise allergen avoidance (e.g., wraparound sunglasses, keeping windows closed). Cold compresses can soothe itching. Advise against eye rubbing.
  • Lubricants / Artificial Tears:
    • Mechanism: Help to dilute and flush allergens from the eye surface. Can be kept in the fridge for extra soothing effect.
    • Licensing: GSL. Suitable for all ages and special populations. Preservative-free options are best for frequent use or contact lens wearers.
    • Counselling: A safe, first-line choice for mild symptoms. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options (Pharmacological)

  • Topical Mast Cell Stabilisers (e.g., Sodium Cromoglicate):
    • Mechanism: Prevents histamine release from mast cells. Effective for prevention, not immediate relief.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Licensed for adults and children.
    • Counselling: Advise that it can take several days to work and must be used regularly throughout the exposure period. [Rx info]
  • Topical Dual-Action Agents (e.g., Olopatadine, Ketotifen):
    • Mechanism: Combine a potent antihistamine (for rapid relief of itching) with a mast cell stabilising effect (for longer-term control).
    • Licensing: P medicines. Age restrictions apply (typically from 3 years).
    • Counselling: Excellent choice for rapid relief of moderate-to-severe symptoms. Advise on contact lens precautions. [Rx info]
  • Oral Antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine):
    • Mechanism: Block H1 receptors systemically.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available as tablets and liquids with varying age licenses.
    • Counselling: Particularly useful when eye symptoms are accompanied by significant hay fever (sneezing, runny nose). Non-sedating options are preferred. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for managing patient expectations and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Topical Corticosteroids (e.g., Dexamethasone, Prednisolone drops):
    Context: These are highly effective for severe inflammation but are strictly reserved for specialist initiation (ophthalmologist). Pharmacists should understand and be able to explain the rationale for referral. The risks of inappropriate use are severe: they can worsen infections (especially herpes keratitis), increase intraocular pressure (risking glaucoma), and cause cataracts with long-term use. [Rx advice]
  • Other POMs:
    Context: Occasionally, more potent topical mast cell stabilisers (e.g., Lodoxamide) or other agents may be prescribed by a specialist for severe or complex cases like vernal keratoconjunctivitis. This knowledge helps pharmacists understand why a patient may be using a non-standard treatment upon returning from an ophthalmology appointment. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a referral is necessary for severe symptoms and what treatments a specialist might consider, reinforcing the pharmacy's role in safe triage.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for allergic conjunctivitis.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Condition: Clearly state this is an allergy ("like hay fever for the eyes"), not an infection, so it is not contagious.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame allergen avoidance as the most important step. Provide practical tips like checking the pollen forecast and wearing sunglasses. Strongly advise against rubbing the eyes.
  • Justify Product Choice: Explain the difference between preventative drops (sodium cromoglicate) and rapid-relief drops (olopatadine/ketotifen). For oral antihistamines, explain their role for combined eye and nose symptoms.
  • Eye Drop Technique: Many patients use eye drops incorrectly. Counsel on washing hands, pulling down the lower lid to form a pocket, instilling one drop, and pressing on the tear duct to minimise systemic absorption.
  • Contact Lens Management: This is a critical counselling point. Advise patients to stop wearing soft contact lenses during a flare-up. Explain that preservatives in multi-dose bottles can damage lenses. If drops are essential, preservative-free single use vials are the best option. Lenses should only be reinserted after symptoms have fully resolved.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from a GP or optometrist. This ensures they know when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP or an optometrist if:

  • The symptoms do not start to improve after 2-3 days of treatment.
  • The condition is not fully resolved within one to two weeks.
  • They need to use the treatment continuously for long periods.
  • They develop any new symptoms.

Advise the patient to seek urgent assessment (optometrist or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Significant pain in the eye.
  • Blurred vision or decreased sharpness of sight.
  • Sensitivity to light (photophobia).
  • Symptoms are only in one eye.
  • They wear contact lenses and have a painful red eye.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions.

9. Pathophysiology

Allergic conjunctivitis is a classic example of a Type I (immediate) hypersensitivity reaction. The process is initiated by the exposure of the conjunctival mucosa to a specific allergen (e.g., a pollen grain) in a predisposed (atopic) individual.

On first exposure, the allergen is processed by antigen-presenting cells, leading to the production of allergen-specific Immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies. This is the sensitisation phase. These IgE antibodies then bind to the surface of mast cells, which are abundant in the conjunctiva.

Upon subsequent exposure, the allergen cross-links the IgE antibodies on the surface of these sensitised mast cells. This triggers mast cell degranulation, causing the explosive release of inflammatory mediators, most notably histamine. Histamine binds to H1 receptors on conjunctival nerve endings, causing intense itching, and on blood vessels, causing vasodilation (redness) and increased vascular permeability (swelling). This immediate reaction is then often followed by a late-phase reaction hours later, perpetuating the inflammatory response.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks, particularly for eye preparations.

Preservatives (Benzalkonium Chloride)

  • Risk: This is the most common preservative in multi-dose eye drops. It can bind to soft contact lenses, causing irritation and corneal toxicity with prolonged use.
  • Counselling: Essential to advise soft contact lens wearers to remove lenses before instilling drops and wait at least 15 minutes before reinsertion. For frequent users, recommend preservative-free single-dose units.

Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline, Naphazoline)

  • Risk: These are found in some older "red eye relief" products. Prolonged use (>7 days) can cause rebound hyperaemia, where the redness worsens when the drop is stopped.
  • Cautions: Should be avoided in patients with a diagnosis of angle-closure glaucoma.

Oral Antihistamines

  • Risk: First-generation products (e.g., chlorphenamine) cause significant drowsiness. Even some second-generation products (e.g., cetirizine) can cause drowsiness in a minority of users.
  • Counselling: A "Do not drive or operate machinery" warning is appropriate until the patient knows how the medicine affects them.
11. Use in special populations

Children

First-line recommendation is non-pharmacological advice and simple lubricants. For medication, always check the product's specific age licence. Sodium cromoglicate is suitable for most ages. Dual-action agents like olopatadine and ketotifen are licensed for children from age 3. A referral is needed if the diagnosis is uncertain or for very young children.

Older adults

Be aware of co-existing dry eye syndrome, which can complicate the picture. When recommending oral antihistamines, always suggest a non-sedating option to minimise the risk of confusion and falls. Check for a history of glaucoma; if present, avoid products with decongestants.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with rest, allergen avoidance, and cold compresses is first-line. Simple lubricant eye drops are also safe. If medication is essential, topical sodium cromoglicate drops are the preferred choice as systemic absorption is negligible. Most other OTC products, including oral antihistamines and dual-action drops, should be avoided unless recommended by a GP. A referral to the GP is the safest option if self-care is insufficient.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with glaucoma, establish the type if known. Avoid vasoconstrictors (decongestants) in those with angle-closure glaucoma. For contact lens wearers, the key comorbidity is the lens itself; referral to an optometrist is the safest course of action for any painful red eye in this group.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Straightforward Seasonal Allergy & P Medicine Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, my hay fever is driving me mad. My eyes are so itchy and watery."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear that. To make sure I get you the best thing, can I ask a few questions? Is it for you?"
  • Patient: "Yes, it's for me, I'm 28."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Are both eyes affected? And apart from the itch and watering, any pain or blurring of your vision?"
  • Patient: "Both eyes, yes. No pain, but a bit blurry from all the watering. My nose is running too."
  • Pharmacist: "And how long has this been going on? Have you tried anything yet?"
  • Patient: "Started a few days ago, it's the same every May. I haven't taken anything yet this year."
  • Pharmacist: "Do you wear contact lenses or have any other medical conditions?"
  • Patient: "No lenses, and no other conditions."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like classic seasonal allergic conjunctivitis. A really good option is a dual-action eye drop like olopatadine, which is a Pharmacy medicine. It's an antihistamine to give fast relief from the itch, and it also works longer-term. Since your nose is affected too, you could also take an oral antihistamine tablet like cetirizine. Remember to keep using the drops daily while the pollen count is high. If things don't improve in a few days, or you get any pain or vision changes, you should see an optometrist."

Scenario 2: Referral Due to a Red Flag

  • Patient: "Can I get some eye drops for my daughter? Her eye is really red."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. How old is she?"
  • Patient: "She's 17."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. And what are her symptoms? You said her eye is red - is it just one eye or both?"
  • Patient: "It's just the right one, that's what's worrying me. It came on yesterday."
  • Pharmacist: "Is it painful or just itchy? And how is her vision?"
  • Patient: "She says it's really painful, and she's been keeping the curtains closed because the light hurts her eyes."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and the final, most important question: does she wear contact lenses?"
  • Patient: "Yes, she wears monthlies."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you. Based on what you've told me, especially that it's a painful, light-sensitive red eye in just one eye, and that she's a contact lens wearer, it's very important she doesn't use any over-the-counter drops. This is a 'red flag' situation, and she needs to be seen urgently by an eye specialist. She must take her lens out immediately. The best place to go is your local optometrist, as they have the equipment to examine her properly. If you can't get an emergency appointment with them, you should go to A&E or an Eye Casualty department today."
