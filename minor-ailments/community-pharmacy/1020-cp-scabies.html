Scabies in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Scabies is a common reason for people to seek advice in community pharmacy. It is an intensely pruritic skin infestation caused by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis. The cardinal symptom is a severe, widespread itch that is classically worse at night and can significantly disrupt sleep. It is transmitted through direct, prolonged skin-to-skin contact and is not related to poor hygiene.

Patients typically present with an intensely itchy rash of papules and excoriation marks. The key diagnostic sign, though not always seen, is the 'burrow' – a thin, greyish, wavy line on the skin. Common sites include the interdigital web spaces, the flexor surfaces of the wrists, elbows, axillae, the waistline, and male genitals. In infants and the elderly, the presentation can be atypical, affecting the face, scalp, palms, and soles.

Following initial infestation, symptoms can take up to eight weeks to appear. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to identify scabies, recommend appropriate OTC treatment, provide robust counselling on the correct application and environmental measures, and identify any red flags that necessitate referral to a GP.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious condition or complication, which would make an OTC sale inappropriate. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Suspected Crusted (Norwegian) Scabies: Widespread, crusted, scaly or warty lesions, which may not be very itchy. This is a hyper-infestation seen in the elderly or immunosuppressed and requires urgent specialist input. This is a "refer immediately" situation.
  • Signs of Severe Secondary Bacterial Infection: Intense scratching can lead to infection. Look for signs of cellulitis (spreading redness, heat, pain), pustules, weeping, or if the person feels unwell with a fever. This requires GP assessment for antibiotics.
  • Diagnostic Uncertainty: If the presentation is atypical, the rash is widespread without a clear history, or if it could be another serious skin condition, a definitive diagnosis from a GP is required.
  • Treatment Failure: If a person reports that two courses of a scabicide, applied correctly to all contacts, have failed to resolve the issue, they must be referred to their GP.
  • Outbreak in an Institutional Setting: If a patient mentions multiple cases in a care home or similar setting, this requires referral and a public health approach. Advise the patient/carer to inform the facility manager immediately.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age, as OTC scabies treatments have specific age licences. Establishes if it's a third-party request, requiring careful questioning about symptoms and the need to treat the entire household.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To build a clinical picture. Key questions include: "How would you describe the itch?", "Is it worse at night?", "Where on the body is the rash?", "Have you noticed any little wavy lines on your skin?". Screen for red flags like broken, weeping, or very red skin.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand the timeline. A crucial follow-up question is: "Has anyone else in your house, or any close contacts, developed an itch?". A 'yes' answer is highly suggestive of scabies.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out if any treatments have been tried. If yes, explore exactly how they were used: "Did you apply it everywhere from the neck down?", "Did everyone in the house get treated at the same time?". This helps identify incorrect use versus true treatment failure.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for conditions or medicines that cause immunosuppression (e.g., oral steroids, chemotherapy), which increases the risk of crusted scabies and warrants immediate referral. Also identifies other skin conditions or allergies.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral to a GP for a definitive diagnosis is necessary.

Common Differentials to Consider

  • Eczema (Atopic, Pompholyx): Can cause intense itch and a papular rash, but the history of itchy contacts is usually absent. A personal or family history of atopy is a strong pointer towards eczema.
  • Insect Bites (e.g., bedbugs): Typically appear as papules or weals on exposed skin areas. Burrows are absent, and the rash distribution is not usually in the classic scabies sites (e.g., finger webs).
  • Dermatitis Herpetiformis: An intensely itchy, blistering rash often on elbows, knees, and buttocks. It is associated with coeliac disease and is symmetrical.
  • Folliculitis: Presents as small pustules centred on hair follicles, which can be itchy but lacks the characteristic burrows and contact history of scabies.

Key Distinguishing Features for Pharmacy: The two most important clues pointing to scabies over other conditions are 1) itch that is severe and worse at night, and 2) a history of other household members or close contacts also developing an itch.

5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management requires meticulous application of treatment to the infested person and all contacts, combined with environmental measures. Patient education is the key to success.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Decontamination: This is as important as the medicine. Advise that all household/close contacts must be treated on the same day. On that day, wash all bedding, towels, and recently worn clothes at >50°C. Items that cannot be washed should be sealed in a plastic bag for 72 hours.

P Medicine Options

  • Permethrin 5% dermal cream:
    • Mechanism: A synthetic pyrethroid insecticide that paralyses and kills the mites. It is the first-line OTC treatment.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Licensed for adults and children aged 2 months and over.
    • Counselling: Must be applied to the whole body from the neck down and left on for 8-12 hours. A second application is essential one week later. [Rx info]
  • Malathion 0.5% aqueous liquid:
    • Mechanism: An organophosphate insecticide that acts as a second-line option if permethrin is unsuitable.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age licenses vary by product, but generally for adults and children aged 6 months and over.
    • Counselling: Must be applied to the whole body and left on for a full 24 hours. Has a strong smell and is flammable. A second application one week later is essential. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not prescribe these for scabies (unless they are an independent prescriber), awareness is vital for counselling patients after a GP referral and managing expectations.

  • Ivermectin (oral tablets):
    Context: This is an unlicensed POM for scabies, typically initiated by dermatologists for crusted scabies, widespread outbreaks in institutions, or cases that have failed to respond to correctly applied topical treatments. Pharmacists may see prescriptions from specialist clinics. [Rx advice]
  • Topical Corticosteroids:
    Context: A mild or moderate potency steroid (e.g., hydrocortisone, Eumovate) may be prescribed by a GP to manage the persistent itch (post-scabetic eczema) *after* a course of scabicide is complete. Pharmacists should not sell hydrocortisone for undiagnosed itchy rashes that could be active scabies. [Rx advice]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin):
    Context: Prescribed by a GP only if there is a clear secondary bacterial infection of the skin. They do not treat the scabies mites themselves. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral is necessary and what to expect, reinforcing the care pathway.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for scabies to ensure eradication and prevent re-infestation.

Key Counselling Points

  • Destigmatise and Reassure: Start by saying "This is very common, it can affect anyone and it's nothing to do with being dirty." This immediately puts the patient at ease.
  • Justify 'Whole Household' Treatment: Explain clearly why asymptomatic contacts must be treated. "The mites can take weeks to cause an itch, so your family members could have it without knowing. If we don't treat everyone at once, you'll just keep passing it back and forth."
  • Provide Meticulous Application Instructions: Show them the amount of cream needed (e.g., one 30g tube for an adult). Advise applying from the neck down to the soles of the feet, paying special attention to finger/toe webs, under nails, and skin folds. Remind them to reapply to hands if washed.
  • Manage the Post-Treatment Itch: This is critical. Warn them: "It is completely normal to still be itchy for up to four weeks after the treatment has worked. This is your body reacting to the leftover mite debris. It doesn't mean you are still infested."
  • Reinforce the Two-Dose Schedule: Emphasise that the second application in 7 days is not optional and is needed to kill any mites hatched from eggs.
  • Check Understanding: After explaining, confirm comprehension: "Just to check I've covered it all, can you remind me of the two most important things to do on treatment day?" (Answer: Treat everyone, wash the bedding).
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from a GP. This ensures they know the difference between normal post-treatment symptoms and signs of a problem.

Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • They see any new burrows appearing on the skin after the second treatment is complete.
  • The itch is not showing any signs of gradual improvement 4 weeks after treatment, or is getting worse.
  • The skin becomes very red, hot, swollen, or starts to ooze – these are signs of a bacterial infection.
  • They have followed all instructions for two full treatment courses and are still experiencing symptoms (this is now treatment failure).

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice if they have been referred for a red flag condition like suspected crusted scabies.

Finally, let them know they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they are unsure about any aspect of the treatment or have further questions.

9. Pathophysiology

Scabies is an ectoparasitic infestation of the skin caused by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis, an obligate human parasite. The mite is microscopic, typically measuring 0.3-0.5 mm.

The life cycle begins after a fertilised female mite burrows into the stratum corneum of the epidermis. She tunnels through the skin, laying 2-3 eggs per day and depositing faecal pellets (scybala). The female remains in the burrow for her entire lifespan of 4-6 weeks. The eggs hatch into larvae within 3-4 days, which then moult through nymphal stages to become adult mites over a period of 10-14 days. The newly matured mites migrate to the skin surface to mate, and the cycle repeats.

The characteristic intense pruritus and inflammatory rash are not caused by the burrowing itself but are the result of a delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction to the mite's eggs, saliva, and faeces. In a primary infestation, this sensitisation process takes several weeks, which is why the onset of symptoms is delayed (typically 2-6 weeks). In a subsequent infestation, the immune system is already primed, and the inflammatory response occurs much more rapidly, usually within 24-48 hours.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and counselling points.

Permethrin and Malathion

  • Skin Irritation: Both products can cause transient stinging or burning on application. It's helpful to warn patients this can happen but usually subsides.
  • Correct Application Volume: Advise that a 30g tube of cream is typically sufficient for one adult application. Under-dosing is a common cause of treatment failure.
  • Flammability (Malathion): Products containing malathion are alcohol-based. Give a clear warning to avoid smoking or naked flames until the product has completely dried.
  • Allergies: Check for hypersensitivity to the active ingredients or excipients. Permethrin is a pyrethroid, so check for chrysanthemum allergy.

Inappropriate Product Requests

  • Hydrocortisone Cream: Be vigilant for requests for hydrocortisone 1% cream for an undiagnosed widespread itch. While useful for post-scabetic eczema, selling it for active scabies will only mask the inflammation while allowing the infestation to worsen. Always probe the reason for the request.
  • Antihistamines: Oral antihistamines have a very limited role in managing the itch of active scabies, as it is not primarily histamine-mediated. They may be useful for their sedating properties at night but should not be recommended as a primary treatment.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Permethrin 5% cream (P) is licensed for infants from 2 months. Malathion 0.5% (P) is licensed from 6 months. For all children, application must include the face, neck, scalp and ears, taking care to avoid the eyes and mouth. Putting mittens on babies can prevent them from sucking the product off their hands.

Older adults

This group has a higher risk of atypical presentations and crusted scabies, which requires immediate referral. If OTC treatment is appropriate, application must include the face, neck, scalp, and ears. Be aware of physical frailty that may make correct application difficult, and stress the need for a carer to assist.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Permethrin 5% cream is the first-line OTC choice and is considered safe. Malathion can also be supplied. Advise breastfeeding mothers to wash the cream or lotion off the nipple area before feeding and reapply it afterwards. Refer to a GP if there is any uncertainty.

People with significant comorbidities

Anyone who is immunosuppressed (e.g., taking high-dose oral steroids, on chemotherapy) should be referred immediately to their GP without a sale, due to the high risk of crusted scabies. Do not attempt to manage these patients with OTC products.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Scabies & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Can I get something for a really bad itch? It's driving me mad at night."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 28. It started a few weeks ago, mainly between my fingers and on my wrists. My girlfriend is starting to get itchy too."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, the fact it's worse at night and your girlfriend has it too strongly suggests it could be scabies. It's very common. Have you tried anything?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet. I don't take any other medication."
  • Recommendation: "The best thing is a cream called Permethrin. You'll need enough for you and your girlfriend, and anyone else living with you. You all need to treat on the same day. You apply it from the neck down, leave it on for 8-12 hours, then wash it off. You must repeat this in 7 days. It's also vital to wash all your bedding and towels on a hot wash on the first day. Be aware, you might still feel itchy for a few weeks afterwards, which is normal. If you see any new skin marks after treatment, you'll need to see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Customer: "My mum's carer asked me to pick up some skin cream. She said my mum has scabies and the skin on her hands is getting very thick and crusty."
  • Pharmacist: "Thank you for letting me know. Who is the medicine for?"
  • Customer: "My mum. She's 85 and lives in a care home. She has bad arthritis and takes steroid tablets."
  • Pharmacist (Triage): "Okay, thank you. Based on what you've said – her age, the fact she takes steroid tablets, and the 'thick, crusty' skin – I can't sell you anything for her. This sounds like a more serious type of scabies that needs a doctor's attention straight away. An over-the-counter cream won't be strong enough. You need to ensure her GP sees her today. The care home also needs to be aware as this type is highly contagious. Does that make sense?"
