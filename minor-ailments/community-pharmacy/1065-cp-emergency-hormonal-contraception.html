Emergency Hormonal Contraception (EHC) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

A request for Emergency Hormonal Contraception (EHC) is a common consultation in the community pharmacy. It is not a presentation with symptoms, but rather a request to prevent an unplanned pregnancy following an episode of unprotected sexual intercourse (UPSI) or a contraceptive failure (e.g., a split condom or missed oral contraceptive pills).

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to establish the time since the event, assess the individual's medical eligibility for the available oral methods using a structured consultation, and supply the most effective treatment possible within the pharmacy setting. The main oral options available are Levonorgestrel (LNG) and Ulipristal Acetate (UPA).

The pharmacist must also recognise when referral for a copper intrauterine device (Cu-IUD) is the most appropriate and effective option. The consultation is an important opportunity to discuss ongoing contraceptive needs, assess for sexually transmitted infections (STIs), and address any safeguarding concerns.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause, a safeguarding concern, or where an oral method is unsuitable. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP, sexual health clinic, or emergency services (A&E).

  • Suspected Sexual Assault or Coercion: If there is any concern that the intercourse was non-consensual or the person is at risk of harm. This requires sensitive handling, adherence to local safeguarding protocols, and referral to specialist services.
  • Safeguarding issues in a young person (under 16): While a young person can consent to treatment under Fraser guidelines, concerns about child sexual exploitation or abuse require adherence to child protection procedures. A person under 13 is unable to consent, requiring immediate safeguarding referral.
  • Symptoms of a possible existing pregnancy: Such as an overdue period or other classic signs. EHC is not effective if the patient is already pregnant. A pregnancy test may be needed.
  • Symptoms suggesting ectopic pregnancy or PID: Significant, acute lower abdominal pain, particularly if unilateral, or associated with fever or discharge, requires urgent medical assessment.
  • Patient outside the licensed window for oral EHC: If a patient presents more than 120 hours after UPSI, they require urgent referral to a specialist service for a Cu-IUD.
  • Patient taking a potent enzyme-inducing medicine: Where the efficacy of oral EHC is severely compromised, referral for a Cu-IUD is the clinically appropriate action.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for supplying EHC as a Pharmacy (P) medicine.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's identity and age. This establishes if the patient is present for the consultation and allows for a Fraser competency assessment if they are under 16. It is vital EHC is supplied directly to the person who will use it.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the reason for the EHC request (e.g., condom failure, missed pill) and to screen for red flag symptoms like abdominal pain. This question also covers the menstrual history (LMP date, cycle length) to assess the risk of pregnancy within the cycle.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: For EHC, this question is adapted to "How long ago did the unprotected intercourse happen?". This is the most critical question to determine which options are available (LNG within 72 hours, UPA within 120 hours).
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To check if any other EHC has been taken in the current menstrual cycle or if advice has already been sought from a GP or clinic. This prevents duplicate or contradictory treatment.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To conduct a critical check for prescribed or OTC medicines, particularly liver enzyme-inducers (e.g., certain epilepsy drugs, St John's Wort) that significantly reduce EHC efficacy. It also screens for medical conditions that may be contraindications (e.g., severe asthma for UPA).
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. The main "diagnosis" is the need for emergency contraception, but other conditions must be considered.

Primary Consideration

  • Risk of Unplanned Pregnancy: The main reason for consultation. The assessment focuses on quantifying this risk based on cycle timing and providing the most effective intervention.

Conditions to Consider for Referral

  • Existing Pregnancy: If the patient's period is overdue or they report symptoms of pregnancy, this must be considered. A pregnancy test may be appropriate.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy: A rare but serious possibility. Suspect and refer urgently if the patient presents with unilateral lower abdominal pain.
  • Sexually Transmitted Infection (STI): Any UPSI carries a risk of STIs. The consultation is an opportunity to discuss this risk and signpost the patient to local STI testing services.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Consider referral if the patient presents with lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding, and/or vaginal or cervical discharge.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The choice of oral EHC is guided by effectiveness and the time elapsed since UPSI. The most effective method overall, the Cu-IUD, requires referral.

First-Line Advice (Referral for Most Effective Method)

  • Copper Intrauterine Device (Cu-IUD): The pharmacist should explain that this is the most effective method of emergency contraception (>99% effective) and that it requires referral to a GP or sexual health clinic for fitting. It is the most effective option available, and referral should be strongly recommended for patients taking enzyme-inducing medicines or where the efficacy of oral methods may be reduced.

Pharmacy (P) Medicine Oral Options

  • Ulipristal Acetate 30mg (UPA):
    • Mechanism: A selective progesterone receptor modulator that works by delaying or inhibiting ovulation.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Licensed for use up to 120 hours (5 days) after UPSI.
    • Counselling: Explain this is the most effective oral option. Advise a 5-day wait before starting or restarting hormonal contraception; barrier methods should be used in the interim. Advise taking a pregnancy test if the next period is more than 7 days late, or in 3 weeks, whichever is sooner. Common side effects include nausea, headache, and changes to the next period.
  • Levonorgestrel 1.5mg (LNG):
    • Mechanism: A synthetic progestogen that primarily works by delaying or inhibiting ovulation.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Licensed for use up to 72 hours (3 days) after UPSI.
    • Counselling: Less effective than UPA. Hormonal contraception can be started immediately; advise using barrier methods until the next period. Common side effects include nausea and changes to the next period. Advise seeking advice if vomiting occurs within 3 hours of taking the dose.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While UPA and LNG are supplied as P medicines in the pharmacy, awareness of the wider context of emergency contraception provision is essential for providing comprehensive advice and facilitating patient referrals.

  • Copper Intrauterine Device (Cu-IUD):
    Context: This is the most effective method overall. It is inserted by a trained healthcare professional at a GP practice or sexual health clinic. A key pharmacist role is identifying when this is the best option and making a timely, confident referral — particularly for patients on enzyme-inducing medicines, those who are outside the window for oral EHC, or where the efficacy of oral options may be reduced.
  • Oral EHC via Prescription or PGD:
    Context: Patients may also obtain UPA or LNG via a prescription from their GP, or under a Patient Group Direction (PGD) from sexual health clinics or commissioned community pharmacies. Awareness of these pathways is helpful when a patient mentions prior consultations or asks about NHS provision.

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain the different ways to access emergency contraception and justify why a referral to another service for the Cu-IUD might be the most clinically appropriate recommendation.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacist can provide during an EHC consultation.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain how oral EHC works (by delaying ovulation) and that it is not an abortion pill. State that it is not 100% effective and discuss the need for a pregnancy test.
  • Justify Recommendation: Confidently explain why a specific product is recommended (e.g., "I'm recommending this one as it is the most effective oral option and you are within the timeframe to take it"). If referring for a Cu-IUD, explain why ("This is the most effective method by far, and it isn't affected by your other medication").
  • Empower with Actionable Advice: Focus on next steps. Provide clear instructions on when to start or restart regular contraception, and the need for barrier methods in the interim. Discuss long-term contraception options.
  • Product-Specific Counselling: Explain what to do in case of vomiting (seek advice for a repeat dose if within 3 hours). Briefly cover common side effects like nausea, headache, and potential changes to their next period.
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "To make sure I've been clear, can you tell me when you need to do a pregnancy test?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from a doctor. This ensures they know how to act if treatment fails or a more serious condition is present.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • Their next period is more than 7 days late.
  • Their next period is unusually light or short.
  • They have any other reason to suspect they might be pregnant.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, particularly:

  • Sudden or severe pain in their lower abdomen. This is crucial as it could be a sign of an ectopic pregnancy.

Finally, advise them to take a pregnancy test if their period is late, or in 3 weeks’ time, whichever is sooner. They can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Emergency contraception acts within the reproductive cycle, specifically before fertilisation occurs. Both oral options work by interfering with ovulation; neither is effective once ovulation has already taken place, and neither is an abortifacient.

Their primary mechanism is the inhibition or delay of ovulation.

  • Levonorgestrel (LNG): As a progestogen, LNG administered in a high dose before the LH surge begins can prevent or postpone the surge, thereby stopping the follicle from rupturing and releasing an egg. If the LH surge has already started, LNG is largely ineffective.
  • Ulipristal Acetate (UPA): As a selective progesterone receptor modulator (SPRM), UPA also acts to delay ovulation. Its key advantage is that it remains effective even when the LH surge has already begun, providing a longer window of efficacy than LNG. It binds to progesterone receptors, blocking the normal hormonal cascade that leads to ovulation.

Neither LNG nor UPA is effective once ovulation has occurred. They do not prevent implantation of a fertilised egg and are not abortifacients. The Copper IUD (Cu-IUD) has a different mechanism. The copper ions released into the uterus create an inflammatory environment that is toxic to both sperm and eggs, thus preventing fertilisation. It may also have a post-fertilisation effect by preventing implantation, although its primary action is pre-fertilisation.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of EHC requires awareness of product-specific risks, interactions, and contraindications.

Ulipristal Acetate (UPA)

  • Interactions: Should not be used with drugs that increase gastric pH (e.g. PPIs). Its effect is reduced by enzyme-inducers. Do not use at the same time as progestogen-containing contraception; a 5-day wait is required before starting a hormonal method.
  • Contraindications: Do not supply to patients with severe asthma treated with oral glucocorticoids.

Levonorgestrel (LNG)

  • Interactions: Efficacy is significantly reduced by liver enzyme-inducing drugs (e.g., carbamazepine, phenytoin, rifampicin, St John's Wort). In these patients, referral for a Cu-IUD is the only safe and effective option.

General Cautions

  • Higher BMI: For individuals with a higher body weight, the efficacy of oral EHC may be reduced. Referral for a Cu-IUD should be strongly advised as the most effective option. Refer to current FSRH guidance and local pathways when advising on this, as the evidence base for specific thresholds has evolved.
  • Frequent Requests: Be vigilant for frequent requests for EHC from the same individual. This should trigger a sensitive conversation about the need for a reliable, long-term method of contraception and a potential referral.
11. Use in special populations

Children (Under 16 years)

A private consultation in a private room is mandatory. Competence must be assessed using the Fraser Guidelines and this assessment must be clearly documented. The pharmacist must be confident the person understands the advice, risks, and benefits. Safeguarding is a primary concern; local child protection procedures must be followed if any abuse or exploitation is suspected.

Older adults

This is not applicable, as EHC is for pre-menopausal individuals at risk of pregnancy.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

EHC is contraindicated if a person is already pregnant. If breastfeeding, Levonorgestrel (LNG) is the preferred oral option and can be taken without any interruption to feeding. After taking Ulipristal Acetate (UPA), breastfeeding should be avoided for one week; milk should be expressed and discarded during this time.

People with significant comorbidities

The most significant issue is medication that induces liver enzymes, which dramatically reduces the efficacy of oral EHC; these patients should be referred for a Cu-IUD. Patients with severe asthma treated with oral steroids should not be supplied with UPA. In cases of known severe liver disease, refer to a GP.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Straightforward Supply of UPA

  • Patient: "Hi, can I get the morning after pill please?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. To make sure I give you the best advice, I need to ask you a few questions in the consultation room. Is that okay?" (In the room) "So, first, who is the EHC for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me. I'm 26."
  • Pharmacist: "Thanks. And when did you have unprotected sex?"
  • Patient: "About 30 hours ago, on Saturday night."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and do you take any regular medicines or have any medical conditions?"
  • Patient: "No, none at all."
  • Recommendation: "Thank you. Based on what you've told me, the best oral option is a medicine called Ulipristal Acetate, as it's the most effective. It works by delaying the release of an egg. It's important you take a pregnancy test in 3 weeks, or if your next period is more than a week late. If you get any severe tummy pain, you must see a doctor straight away. Does that all make sense?"

Scenario 2: Referral due to Interacting Medication

  • Patient: "I need emergency contraception, I missed a few pills."
  • Pharmacist: (After moving to consultation room and initial questions) "And the last question, do you take any regular medication?"
  • Patient: "Yes, I take carbamazepine for my epilepsy."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that, it's very important. Unfortunately, your epilepsy medicine stops the emergency contraceptive pills from working properly, so I cannot supply them to you as they would not be effective. The best and most effective method for you is the copper coil, or IUD, as it's not affected by your medication at all. You need to get this fitted within 5 days, so I'm going to call the local sexual health clinic for you now to arrange an urgent appointment."
