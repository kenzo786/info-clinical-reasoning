Acute Diarrhoea in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute diarrhoea is a very common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It is defined as the passage of three or more loose or liquid stools per day, for a duration of less than 14 days. In the UK, it is most often caused by a viral infection (gastroenteritis) and is typically self-limiting.

Patients usually present with a sudden onset of watery diarrhoea, which may be accompanied by nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and a mild fever. The most common infectious causes are viruses like norovirus and rotavirus. The condition usually resolves within 5 to 7 days without specific medical intervention.

The main clinical concern is dehydration, particularly in the very young and in older, frail adults. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations regarding the diarrhoea's natural duration.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of severe dehydration or shock: Such as tachycardia, hypotension, reduced consciousness, or passing no urine. This requires immediate medical assessment.
  • Blood and/or mucus in the stool (dysentery): This suggests mucosal inflammation and may indicate a more aggressive bacterial infection or a flare of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Severe, persistent abdominal pain or localised tenderness: This may indicate a surgical abdomen, toxic megacolon, or ischaemic colitis and requires urgent medical assessment.
  • High fever (>38.5°C) or systemic signs of sepsis: Features like rigors, confusion, or severe malaise suggest a severe infection and require urgent referral.
  • Recent hospitalisation or antibiotic use: A high index of suspicion for Clostridioides difficile infection is needed, which requires GP assessment.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Individuals with significant immunosuppression (e.g., due to chemotherapy or uncontrolled HIV) are at high risk of severe infections and require a low threshold for referral.
  • Symptoms lasting more than 14 days: This is persistent diarrhoea and requires GP investigation.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. This is critical for assessing risk (very young and elderly are at higher risk of dehydration) and for ensuring any recommended product is licensed for their age group. It also establishes if the patient is present.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the diarrhoea (frequency, consistency) and check for red flags like blood or mucus. Associated symptoms like vomiting, fever, or severe pain help determine severity and point towards referral.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To confirm the diarrhoea is acute (less than 14 days). If symptoms have lasted longer, a mandatory GP referral is required for investigation.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments or self-care measures the patient has already tried (e.g., loperamide). This avoids duplication and helps gauge the severity of the illness if prior treatment has failed.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify any prescribed medicines that could be causing the diarrhoea (e.g., antibiotics, metformin, laxatives) or identify comorbidities (e.g., IBD, diabetes, heart failure, immunosuppression) that may affect product choice or necessitate a referral.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting viral infections.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Gastroenteritis: The most frequent cause. Often associated with vomiting and a community outbreak (e.g., norovirus).
  • Bacterial Gastroenteritis: Often linked to food poisoning or travel. May cause more severe symptoms.
  • Traveller's Diarrhoea: Usually bacterial. Occurs during or shortly after travel to a high-risk region.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Clostridioides difficile Infection: Suspect in those with recent antibiotic use or hospital admission.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): A first presentation can manifest as acute, often bloody, diarrhoea.
  • Diverticulitis: Typically presents with left-sided abdominal pain and fever, but diarrhoea can occur.
  • Ischaemic Colitis: Consider in older adults with vascular disease who present with sudden pain and bloody diarrhoea.
  • Colorectal Cancer: Suspect if there is a persistent change in bowel habit, not acute diarrhoea, but keep in mind as part of the wider differential for bowel symptoms.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief and prevention of dehydration. Non-pharmacological advice should always be provided.

First-Line Advice (All Diarrhoea)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise adequate fluid intake, eating as tolerated, and strict hygiene.
  • Oral Rehydration Salts (e.g., Dioralyte, Electrolade):
    • Mechanism: Provide a balanced solution of electrolytes (sodium, potassium) and glucose to enhance water absorption from the gut, effectively replacing losses.
    • Licensing: GSL. Suitable for all ages. Especially important for children and older adults.
    • Counselling: Explain that this is the most important treatment to prevent dehydration. Advise on correct reconstitution with water. [Rx info]

Second-Line Option (for Symptom Control in Adults)

  • Antimotility Agents (e.g., Loperamide):
    • Mechanism: An opioid-receptor agonist that acts directly on the gut wall to slow down bowel movements, allowing more time for water and electrolytes to be absorbed.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Restricted for use in adults and children over 12 years.
    • Counselling: For watery diarrhoea only. Advise patients to stop taking it as soon as the diarrhoea resolves. Crucially, advise them not to use it if they have a high fever or blood/mucus in their stool. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for diarrhoea, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Ciprofloxacin, Azithromycin):
    Context: These are not effective for viral diarrhoea and are only prescribed by a GP in specific situations, such as for severe symptoms following foreign travel where a bacterial cause is likely, or if a specific pathogen is identified from a stool sample in a systemically unwell patient. Pharmacists play a key role in explaining this to patients, upholding antimicrobial stewardship. [Rx advice]
  • Antiemetics (e.g., Metoclopramide, Cyclizine):
    Context: A GP may prescribe these if vomiting is the dominant symptom and is preventing the patient from keeping down oral rehydration fluids. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral might be needed and what treatments a doctor might consider, preventing the perception that the pharmacy is a barrier to "stronger" medicine.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for acute diarrhoea.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the natural course of the illness. A key phrase is: "It's normal for a tummy bug like this to last about 5 to 7 days. Your body is very good at clearing it on its own."
  • Justify No Antibiotics: When a patient requests antibiotics, provide a clear explanation. "This is most likely a virus, and antibiotics only work against bacteria. They won't help you get better any faster and can sometimes cause side effects."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame rehydration as the active treatment. "The most important thing you can do is keep your fluid levels up. These oral rehydration sachets are the best thing for that as they replace the essential salts and sugars you've lost."
  • Infection Control: Give clear, practical advice on preventing spread: "Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water, and stay off work or school until you've been clear of any symptoms for 48 hours."
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've explained everything, can you tell me the main signs that mean you should see a doctor?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help. This ensures that a patient with a self-limiting illness knows when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The diarrhoea has not started to improve within 7 days.
  • They are unable to manage their fluid intake because of persistent vomiting.
  • They are a person with significant comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, IBD) and feel their condition is being affected.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Passing blood or mucus in their stool.
  • Severe or worsening abdominal pain.
  • Signs of severe dehydration (e.g., feeling faint or dizzy, confusion, passing no urine).
  • A high fever and feeling very unwell.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute diarrhoea is fundamentally a disorder of intestinal water and electrolyte transport. It can be classified into several types based on the underlying mechanism.

Secretory Diarrhoea: This is caused by enterotoxins produced by bacteria (e.g., Vibrio cholerae, Enterotoxigenic E. coli). These toxins actively stimulate the secretion of chloride ions from intestinal crypt cells into the lumen. Sodium and water follow the osmotic gradient, resulting in large-volume watery diarrhoea that persists even when fasting.

Inflammatory (Exudative) Diarrhoea: This occurs when invasive pathogens (e.g., Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella) or inflammatory conditions like IBD damage the intestinal mucosa. The inflamed and ulcerated lining cannot absorb water and electrolytes effectively. Furthermore, the damaged mucosa leaks serum, mucus, and blood into the intestinal lumen, leading to frequent, smaller-volume stools that often contain blood and pus (dysentery).

Osmotic Diarrhoea: This happens when poorly absorbed solutes remain in the gut lumen, drawing water in by osmosis. A common cause is viral damage to the small bowel brush border, leading to temporary lactase deficiency. The unabsorbed lactose acts as an osmotic agent. This type of diarrhoea typically stops when the patient fasts.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and contraindications.

Loperamide

  • Contraindications: Do not supply for patients with bloody diarrhoea, high fever, or a history of inflammatory bowel disease like ulcerative colitis. This is because slowing the gut can worsen the infection or precipitate toxic megacolon.
  • Side Effects: Can cause constipation, dizziness, and abdominal cramps.
  • Misuse Potential: Be aware of the MHRA warning regarding serious cardiac side effects associated with taking doses higher than recommended. Be vigilant for inappropriate or frequent requests.

Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)

  • Cautions: While very safe, they should be recommended with caution in patients with severe heart failure or renal failure who are on a strict fluid and sodium diet. A referral to the GP may be more appropriate for these patients.
  • Counselling: It is essential to counsel the patient to mix the sachet with the correct amount of fresh drinking water. Making the solution too concentrated can worsen diarrhoea.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Dehydration is the primary risk. Oral rehydration therapy is the first-line and essential treatment. Loperamide is not licensed for children under 12. Diarrhoea in an infant under 1 year, especially with signs of dehydration (e.g., fewer wet nappies, sunken fontanelle), warrants an urgent GP referral.

Older adults

This group is at high risk of dehydration and complications. Have a lower threshold for referral to the GP. Loperamide should be used with caution as it may cause constipation or mask worsening symptoms. Always check for other medications and comorbidities.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with fluids and ORS is first-line. Loperamide is generally considered acceptable for short-term use, but the patient should be advised to try self-care first. If symptoms are severe or persistent, referral to the GP is the safest option.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with IBD, never recommend loperamide and refer them to their GP or specialist team. For patients with diabetes, recommend sugar-free ORS formulations if available and remind them to monitor their blood glucose closely. For those with severe renal or heart conditions, refer to the GP for advice on fluid management.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Viral Diarrhoea & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I've had a really bad tummy bug for a day or so. Can you give me something to stop it?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course, I can help with that. First, is it for yourself?" (W)
  • Patient: "Yes, for me. I'm 32."
  • Pharmacist: "What are your symptoms exactly? Is it just watery diarrhoea?" (W)
  • Patient: "Yes, just watery. No blood or anything. I feel a bit queasy but haven't actually been sick."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and how long has this been going on?" (H)
  • Patient: "It started yesterday."
  • Pharmacist: "Have you tried anything yet, and are you on any other medication?" (A, M)
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet, and I don't take any other medicines."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a typical tummy bug. The most important thing is to prevent dehydration, so I'd strongly recommend these rehydration sachets. To help with the symptoms, you can also take loperamide capsules. You take two now, and then one after each loose stool, but no more than six in 24 hours. Stop taking them as soon as you're back to normal. If it's not getting better in a couple of days, or you see any blood or get a high fever, you must see a doctor."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Customer: "I need some loperamide for my mum. She's had diarrhoea for two days."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help with that. How old is your mum?" (W)
  • Customer: "She's 75."
  • Pharmacist: "Can you tell me a bit more about her symptoms? Is there any blood in the stool?" (W)
  • Customer: "Yes, actually, she said there was a bit of blood this morning, and she's feeling quite feverish."
  • Pharmacist: "And does she take any other medication?" (M)
  • Customer: "Yes, she just finished some antibiotics for a water infection last week."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that information. Because your mum has blood in her stool, a fever, and has recently taken antibiotics, loperamide is not safe for her to take. These symptoms are 'red flags', which means she needs to be assessed by a doctor to find the cause. Please contact her GP surgery straight away and tell them her exact symptoms. An over-the-counter medicine isn't appropriate here."
