Contact Dermatitis in Community Pharmacy
Context: Contact Dermatitis in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Contact dermatitis is a common inflammatory skin condition that frequently presents in a community pharmacy. It is a localised eczematous reaction resulting from direct contact with a particular substance. There are two main types: irritant contact dermatitis (ICD) and allergic contact dermatitis (ACD). ICD is the most common form, caused by damage to the skin's barrier by substances like soaps, detergents, solvents, or frequent contact with water. ACD is a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction (Type IV) that occurs when a sensitised individual is re-exposed to an allergen, such as nickel, fragrances, or preservatives.

Patients typically present with symptoms of redness, itching, and a burning or stinging sensation in the affected area. The skin may become vesicular (blistered), dry, scaly, or cracked. In lighter skin, the rash is typically red, while in darker skin tones, it may appear as dark brown, purple, or grey patches. The distribution of the rash is a key clue, often corresponding directly to the area of exposure (e.g., on the hands from cleaning, under a watch strap). ICD symptoms can appear quickly, while ACD usually develops 24-72 hours after exposure. The condition is not contagious but can be very distressing for the patient.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of severe secondary bacterial infection: Such as rapidly spreading erythema, significant pain, purulent discharge, systemic fever, or malaise. This may indicate cellulitis or impetigo requiring urgent assessment and likely systemic antibiotics.
  • Eczema herpeticum: A widespread, painful, vesicular rash, often with "punched-out" erosions and systemic illness. This is a dermatological emergency requiring immediate referral.
  • Severe or extensive reaction: Dermatitis covering a large surface area of the body, causing significant blistering, or associated with facial or eyelid swelling. This requires urgent medical assessment.
  • Failure to respond to appropriate OTC therapy: If dermatitis is moderate and does not improve after 1-2 weeks of correct use of emollients and a mild topical corticosteroid, GP referral is necessary.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the rash does not fit a clear pattern of contact dermatitis and other conditions like psoriasis or a fungal infection seem possible, referral to a GP is appropriate.
  • Severe occupational dermatitis: Cases causing significant distress or preventing a person from working should be referred to their GP, who may involve an occupational health specialist.
  • Immediate systemic reaction: If a patient reports immediate swelling, hives, or breathlessness after contact with a substance (e.g., latex), this suggests anaphylaxis and requires emergency (999) referral.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age, as OTC hydrocortisone has a minimum age limit (typically 10 years old). This also establishes if the patient is present, allowing for direct observation of the rash.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature and location of the rash. Key questions include "Where on the body is the rash?" and "What does it look and feel like (e.g., itchy, red, dry, broken skin)?". This helps differentiate from other conditions and checks if the site is suitable for OTC treatment (e.g., not on the face or genitals). Also used to screen for red flags like spreading pain or fever.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To determine if the condition is acute. If the rash has been present for more than 1-2 weeks despite self-care, a GP referral is warranted.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what the patient has already tried. Have they used emollients? Have they tried an OTC steroid cream? If a treatment has already failed, recommending it again is inappropriate. This also helps identify the suspected trigger ("Have you used any new soaps or creams?").
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for any medications that might affect the skin and to identify comorbidities. Importantly, asking about allergies is key to ensure the patient is not allergic to any ingredients in the recommended product.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. The location and history are key to distinguishing these conditions in a pharmacy setting.

Common Differentials to Consider

  • Atopic Eczema: Often has a history starting in childhood and affects flexural sites like the back of the knees or inside of the elbows. A personal or family history of atopy is common.
  • Seborrhoeic Dermatitis: Affects areas like the scalp, eyebrows, and sides of the nose with greasy, yellowish scale. Not typically suited for OTC corticosteroid treatment.
  • Psoriasis: Presents as well-defined, red plaques with silvery scale, often on elbows and knees. Requires GP assessment.
  • Tinea (Fungal Infection): Often has a distinctive ring-like shape with a raised, scaling edge. Requires an antifungal, not a steroid alone which can worsen the condition.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Cellulitis: Presents as a spreading, hot, painful red area, often with the patient feeling unwell. Requires urgent GP referral.
  • Scabies: Characterised by intense itch (especially at night) and may affect other family members. Look for burrows on wrists and between fingers. Requires GP referral for diagnosis and treatment.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management focuses on advising avoidance of the trigger, restoring the skin barrier with emollients, and, where appropriate, reducing inflammation with a mild steroid.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Irritant/Allergen Avoidance and Emollients:
    • Mechanism: The core advice is to identify and avoid the cause. Emollients (creams and ointments) and soap substitutes repair the skin's natural barrier, increase hydration, and reduce water loss.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL. Available for all ages.
    • Counselling: Advise liberal and frequent application of a leave-on emollient and replacing all soaps and shower gels with a soap substitute or emollient wash. Warn that paraffin-based emollients are a fire risk when soaked into fabrics. [Rx info]

Second-Line for Inflamed Skin (Where Appropriate)

  • Mild Topical Corticosteroids (Hydrocortisone 1%):
    • Mechanism: Reduces the inflammatory response in the skin, thereby relieving redness, swelling, and itch.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Restricted for use in adults and children over 10 years, for a maximum of 7 days.
    • Counselling: For application to small areas of inflamed skin only. Not for use on the face, anogenital region, broken or infected skin. Advise applying a thin layer once or twice daily. Emollients should be applied first, with a 30-minute gap before steroid application. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not initiate these medicines, awareness is vital for counselling patients being referred to the GP and managing their expectations of what might be prescribed.

  • Potent Topical Corticosteroids (e.g., Betamethasone, Clobetasone):
    Context: A GP may prescribe a stronger steroid if the dermatitis is severe or on a thick-skinned area like the hands and has not responded to mild steroids. Pharmacists can reinforce the GP's instructions on short-term use. [Rx advice]
  • Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (e.g., Tacrolimus):
    Context: These are specialist or GP-initiated treatments for sensitive sites like the face or eyelids where steroids are best avoided. A pharmacist's role is to counsel on correct application if a prescription is dispensed. [Rx advice]
  • Systemic treatments (e.g., oral steroids, antibiotics):
    Context: Reserved for very severe acute cases or for treating secondary infections. Understanding this helps pharmacists explain why a referral is necessary for a patient who is systemically unwell or has widespread dermatitis. [Rx advice]
7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Empowering the patient with high-quality advice is the most important pharmacy intervention.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Cause and Solution: Use simple terms: "Your skin's protective barrier has been damaged by [e.g., water and soap]. The most important thing is to protect it. Using this emollient cream as your moisturiser and soap will help it repair."
  • Role of Emollients: Frame this as the primary treatment, not just a cosmetic moisturiser. "You can't overuse this. Apply it throughout the day, especially after washing your hands."
  • Role of Hydrocortisone: Be specific about its use. "This cream is just for the red, itchy patches to calm the inflammation down. Use it very thinly just once or twice a day, and for no more than a week." Address steroid fears: "It's a very mild steroid and is very safe when used correctly like this."
  • The '30-Minute Rule': "Always apply the emollient (moisturiser) first. Wait about 30 minutes for it to sink in properly before you apply the hydrocortisone cream on top."
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've explained it well, which cream are you going to use all over, and which one is just for the red bits?" can confirm understanding.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient on what to do if their condition does not improve or worsens. This must be done at every consultation.

Provide clear, specific advice. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The rash has not started to improve after using the treatment correctly for 7 days.
  • The dermatitis clears up but then comes back straight away after stopping treatment.
  • The rash is spreading or getting larger.
  • They are unsure of the cause and it keeps recurring.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice from their GP if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • The skin becomes very painful, hot, and red, or starts to weep pus.
  • They develop a fever and feel generally unwell.
  • The rash becomes blistered and painful.

Finally, always invite the patient to speak to the pharmacist again if they have any questions or are worried about their skin.

9. Pathophysiology

Contact dermatitis is broadly divided into two distinct pathophysiological processes: irritant and allergic.

Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD): This is a non-immunological, inflammatory reaction resulting from direct cytotoxic damage to keratinocytes. Irritants disrupt the skin's lipid barrier, increase transepidermal water loss, and damage cell membranes. This leads to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (like IL-1 and TNF-α) from skin cells, causing inflammation, erythema, and impaired barrier function. The reaction is dose-dependent; strong irritants (e.g., acids) cause an acute reaction, while weaker, cumulative irritants (e.g., water, detergents) cause chronic damage. It does not require prior sensitisation and can affect anyone.

Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD): This is a classic Type IV (delayed) T-cell mediated hypersensitivity reaction. It occurs in two phases. In the sensitisation phase, a low-molecular-weight chemical (hapten) penetrates the skin and binds to an endogenous protein, forming a hapten-protein complex. This complex is recognised as foreign by Langerhans cells (antigen-presenting cells), which process it and migrate to regional lymph nodes. Here, they present the antigen to naive T-cells, leading to their activation and proliferation, creating a population of memory T-cells. This phase is asymptomatic.

The elicitation phase occurs upon re-exposure to the same allergen. The allergen is processed by Langerhans cells, which present it to the sensitised memory T-cells in the skin. These T-cells are rapidly activated and release pro-inflammatory cytokines (like interferon-gamma and IL-17), which recruit other inflammatory cells. This results in the clinical signs of dermatitis, typically appearing 24-72 hours after re-exposure.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and licensing restrictions.

Emollients

  • Fire Risk: Products containing paraffin (in any concentration) can build up on fabrics (clothing, bedding, dressings). Advise patients that while the emollient itself isn't flammable, the soaked fabric can catch fire easily. They should not smoke or go near naked flames while using these products.
  • Aqueous Cream: Should be recommended as a soap substitute only. It contains a detergent (sodium lauryl sulfate) which can irritate the skin if used as a leave-on emollient, potentially worsening the dermatitis.
  • Pump Dispensers: For hygiene and ease of use, recommend a product with a pump dispenser, especially for large tubs.

Hydrocortisone 1% Cream/Ointment (P)

  • Licensing Restrictions: For sale to adults and children over 10 only. For a maximum of 7 days' use.
  • Contraindications: Must not be sold for use on the face, anogenital areas, or on broken or infected skin (e.g., tinea, impetigo, cold sores). If a patient has infected-looking skin, they must be referred to the GP.
  • Cautions: Should not be used under occlusive dressings, as this increases absorption and the risk of side effects.
11. Use in special populations

Children

First-line treatment is always avoidance of the trigger and liberal use of emollients/soap substitutes. Hydrocortisone 1% is licensed for use from age 10, but should be used with caution and for the shortest duration necessary. For children under 10, GP referral is required if emollients alone are not sufficient. Nappy rash is a form of irritant dermatitis and should be managed with barrier creams and frequent nappy changes.

Older adults

Their skin is often thinner and more fragile, so they are more susceptible to irritation and skin thinning from steroids. Emphasise gentle handling of the skin and the importance of emollients. Hydrocortisone should be used cautiously for no more than 7 days.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Emollients and soap substitutes are the first-line and safest option. Mild topical hydrocortisone, when used on small areas for a limited time (max 7 days), is considered safe as systemic absorption is minimal. Advise them to discuss with a GP or midwife if the condition is severe or requires prolonged treatment.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with diabetes, good skin care is crucial to prevent cracks and fissures that could lead to infection. Emphasise emollient use. For patients with known severe atopic eczema or psoriasis, it is best to advise they stick to their prescribed treatments and refer them to their GP if they are having a flare.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Irritant Dermatitis & Product Sale

  • Patient: "My hands are so dry and sore. Can you recommend a good hand cream?"
  • Pharmacy Staff: "I can certainly help. To make sure I give you the best advice, can I ask who it's for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 40. My hands are just really cracked and itchy on the back."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "How long has it been like this?"
  • Patient: "A few weeks now. I'm a cleaner, so my hands are in water a lot."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "And have you tried anything so far? Are you on any other medication?"
  • Patient: "Just a normal moisturiser but it's not helping. No other medicines."
  • Recommendation (by Pharmacist): "It sounds like you have irritant dermatitis from your work. The soap and water are stripping the natural oils from your skin. Firstly, the most important thing is to use an emollient cream instead of soap to wash your hands, and to use a really good moisturising emollient frequently through the day. Because it's red and itchy, I'd also recommend a 1% hydrocortisone cream. Use it just on the red bits twice a day, but only for a week. Always put the main moisturiser on first, wait 20-30 mins, then apply the hydrocortisone. If it's not better in a week, you must see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Infected Rash

  • Patient: "Can I get some of that eczema cream for my son? He's 12."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, let's see if we can help. Where is his rash?"
  • Patient: "It's on his leg. It was a dry patch but now it looks a bit of a mess."
  • Pharmacist: "When you say a mess, what does it look like now?"
  • Patient: "Well, it's weeping and has some yellow crusty bits on it. He says it's quite sore."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that description. The yellow crusts and weeping sound like the rash has become infected. A hydrocortisone cream on its own would make an infection worse. This needs to be seen by a doctor, as your son likely needs an antibiotic cream. I can't sell you anything for it today; you need to make an appointment with your GP as soon as you can."
