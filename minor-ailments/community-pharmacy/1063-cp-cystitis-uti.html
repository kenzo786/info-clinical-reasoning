Cystitis (UTI in women) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Cystitis, or lower urinary tract infection (UTI), is an extremely common reason for women to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It refers to inflammation of the bladder, most often caused by a bacterial infection. Patients typically present with a rapid onset of a cluster of urinary symptoms.

The classic triad of symptoms includes: dysuria (burning, stinging pain on passing urine), urinary frequency (needing to urinate more often than usual), and urinary urgency (a sudden, compelling need to urinate). Patients may also report suprapubic discomfort, cloudy or strong-smelling urine, and visible haematuria (blood in the urine). Systemic symptoms like fever are usually absent in uncomplicated cystitis.

The role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate uncomplicated cystitis from conditions requiring referral, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments for symptomatic relief, and explain when a GP consultation is necessary for prescription-only antibiotics.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious or complex condition. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of pyelonephritis (upper UTI): Loin or flank pain, fever (>38°C), chills, nausea, and vomiting. This suggests the infection has ascended to the kidneys and requires urgent medical assessment.
  • Signs of sepsis: New-onset confusion (delirium), very high heart rate, dizziness, or feeling systemically very unwell. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission.
  • Pregnancy: Any suspected UTI in a pregnant woman requires an immediate referral to her GP or midwife for assessment and prescription treatment to prevent complications.
  • Symptoms not improving or worsening: If symptoms have not improved after a few days of self-care, or if a previous course of antibiotics has been ineffective, a GP appointment is needed.
  • Recurrent UTIs: Women experiencing two UTIs in the last 6 months, or three in the last 12 months, should be referred to their GP for further investigation.
  • Male patients, children, or catheterised patients: These groups fall outside the scope of simple cystitis management in the pharmacy and should be referred to a GP.
  • Visible haematuria in women aged 60+ requires a GP referral to exclude urinary tract malignancy, even if symptoms of infection are present.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique using the WWHAM framework is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy before recommending any product or advice.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's identity, age, and sex. OTC treatments for UTI have specific age restrictions and this guidance applies to women only. Critically, this question identifies if the person is pregnant, which is a red flag for immediate referral.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the classic symptoms of cystitis (dysuria, frequency, urgency). It is vital to screen for red flags (fever, loin pain) and to ask about vaginal symptoms (discharge, itch) to rule out other causes like thrush or BV.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish if this is an acute onset, typical of cystitis. It also helps identify recurrent or persistent issues that require a GP referral. This question is key for services like Pharmacy First, which have specific criteria for symptom duration.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what self-care measures or treatments the patient has already tried. This avoids product duplication and informs the pharmacist if first-line advice has already failed, which may lower the threshold for referral.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for potential drug interactions with OTC products (e.g., sodium/potassium citrate with certain blood pressure medicines) and to identify comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, renal impairment) that may affect product choice or warrant referral. It also identifies any medication allergies.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy with classic symptoms will be cystitis.

Common Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Urethritis: Often caused by an STI (e.g., Chlamydia). Suspect if the main symptom is pain on urination without the urgency or frequency of cystitis, especially in a younger person with a new sexual partner. This requires a GP or sexual health clinic referral.
  • Vaginitis (e.g., Candidiasis, Bacterial Vaginosis): Can cause external dysuria. The presence of vaginal discharge, odour, or itch strongly suggests a vaginal cause rather than a UTI. This can often be managed in the pharmacy but is a different condition.
  • Overactive Bladder (OAB): Presents with chronic urgency and frequency but typically without the acute onset, pain, or other signs of infection. This requires a GP diagnosis.
  • Interstitial Cystitis / Bladder Pain Syndrome: A chronic condition involving bladder pain and urinary symptoms. A history of long-standing, fluctuating symptoms rather than an acute episode points away from simple cystitis and needs GP assessment.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

OTC options aim to provide symptomatic relief while the body's immune system fights the infection, or while waiting for a GP appointment. Definitive treatment requires antibiotics.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise simple analgesia like paracetamol or ibuprofen to manage pain and discomfort. Advise maintaining adequate hydration.

Symptomatic Relief Options

  • Urine Alkalising Agents (e.g., Potassium Citrate, Sodium Citrate):
    • Mechanism: These agents work by making the urine less acidic. This is thought to help relieve the burning and stinging sensation (dysuria) associated with cystitis.
    • Licensing: Typically supplied as a Pharmacy (P) medicine. Not licensed for use in men or children. Product-specific age restrictions apply (e.g., for women 16 to 65 years).
    • Counselling: Advise that this is for short-term use (e.g., 48 hours) and only relieves symptoms; it does not treat the infection. Patients should be advised to see their GP if symptoms don't improve. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

Awareness of POMs is vital for explaining to patients why a referral is necessary and for counselling under advanced services like NHS Pharmacy First.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Nitrofurantoin, Trimethoprim):
    Context: These are the definitive treatment for bacterial cystitis and require a prescription. The pharmacist's role is to explain that while OTC products help with symptoms, an antibiotic is needed to clear the bacteria. This manages expectations and justifies the referral. [Rx advice]
  • NHS Pharmacy First service:
    Context: Under this clinical pathway, accredited community pharmacists can supply prescription-only antibiotics (e.g., nitrofurantoin) directly to eligible women for uncomplicated UTIs, following a strict clinical protocol. This provides an alternative to a GP appointment for specific patient groups. A referral is still required for those who do not meet the inclusion criteria. [Rx advice]

This knowledge allows the pharmacy team to provide a complete patient journey, either via referral or, where available, through an in-house prescribing service, ensuring timely access to appropriate care.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for women presenting with symptoms of cystitis.

Key Counselling Points

  • Validate and Reassure: Acknowledge how uncomfortable and disruptive the symptoms are, but reassure the patient that uncomplicated cystitis is very common and treatable.
  • Explain Treatment Options: Clearly differentiate between OTC options for symptomatic relief (analgesia, citrate sachets) and antibiotics (from a GP or Pharmacy First service) which treat the underlying infection.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active step. Recommend taking paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain. Discuss preventative advice such as wiping front to back and voiding the bladder after intercourse.
  • OTC Product Counselling: When supplying a product like potassium citrate, explain: "This will help to ease the stinging feeling when you pass urine, but it won't kill the bacteria. You should only use it for two days, and if you're not feeling better by then, you must see your GP."
  • Justify Referral: If referral is needed, explain why clearly and calmly. For example: "Because you are pregnant, it is very important that you see your GP today to get the right antibiotic to protect both you and your baby."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help from a GP or urgent care. This must be done for every consultation.

Advise the woman to consult her GP if:

  • Her symptoms have not started to improve within 48 hours of starting OTC treatment or self-care.
  • Her symptoms worsen at any point.
  • The symptoms return shortly after finishing a course of antibiotics.

Advise the woman to seek urgent medical advice (e.g., GP, NHS 111, or A&E) if she develops any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • A high temperature or fever.
  • Pain in her back or sides (loin pain).
  • Nausea or vomiting.
  • New onset confusion or drowsiness.

Finally, advise her that she can always speak to the pharmacist again if she has any further questions or concerns about her symptoms or treatment.

9. Pathophysiology

Cystitis is predominantly caused by an ascending infection. Uropathogenic bacteria, most commonly Escherichia coli (UPEC) originating from the host's own gut flora, colonise the periurethral area. Due to the short length of the female urethra and its proximity to the anus, these bacteria can ascend into the bladder.

Once in the bladder, bacteria must overcome host defence mechanisms, including the flushing action of micturition and the antimicrobial properties of the urothelial surface. Pathogenic bacteria like E. coli possess virulence factors, such as fimbriae or pili, which allow them to adhere to and invade the urothelial cells lining the bladder wall. This adherence is a critical step, preventing them from being washed out with urine.

The bacterial invasion triggers a potent inflammatory response from the host's innate immune system. The urothelium releases cytokines and chemokines, which recruit neutrophils to the site of infection. This inflammatory cascade leads to the characteristic symptoms of cystitis: vasodilatation and sensory nerve activation cause pain (dysuria) and a feeling of bladder fullness, while inflammation of the trigone leads to irritation and the sensation of urgency and frequency. The presence of bacteria and inflammatory cells results in pyuria, causing the urine to appear cloudy.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines for cystitis requires awareness of product-specific risks and interactions.

Potassium and Sodium Citrate Sachets

  • Renal Impairment: These products are contraindicated in patients with moderate or severe kidney problems due to the risk of electrolyte disturbances such as hyperkalaemia.
  • Hypertension & Heart Disease: Products containing high levels of sodium should be used with caution, or avoided, in patients with high blood pressure or heart failure. Potassium-containing products also require caution.
  • Interactions: There is a risk of interaction with medications that affect potassium levels, such as ACE inhibitors (e.g., ramipril), ARBs (e.g., candesartan), and potassium-sparing diuretics. Always check the patient's medication history.

Analgesia

  • Paracetamol: Ensure the patient is aware of the maximum daily dose and check if they are taking any other paracetamol-containing products to avoid accidental overdose.
  • Ibuprofen: Check for contraindications such as history of stomach ulcers, asthma, renal impairment, or interactions with other medicines (e.g., warfarin, certain antihypertensives).
11. Use in special populations

Children & Men

OTC products for cystitis are not licensed for these groups. Any man or child presenting with UTI symptoms must be referred to a GP, as it may indicate an underlying structural issue and is considered a 'complicated' UTI.

Older adults

Be aware that older women may present with atypical symptoms like new-onset confusion (delirium) rather than classic urinary symptoms. Have a high index of suspicion for sepsis and a low threshold for urgent referral. Asymptomatic bacteriuria is common and should not be treated. Be cautious with OTC products in those with renal impairment or polypharmacy.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

This is a red flag. A suspected UTI in pregnancy requires immediate referral to a GP or midwife for assessment and treatment with a pregnancy-safe antibiotic. Do not sell OTC citrate products; provide advice and refer.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes are more prone to UTIs and may have worse outcomes; they should be encouraged to see their GP. Patients with renal impairment should not be sold citrate-based products. Those with an indwelling catheter should be referred to their GP or district nurse, as management is different.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple UTI & Advice (leading to referral)

  • Patient: "Hi, I've got cystitis again, it's so painful when I wee. Can I get some of those sachets?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Let me just ask a few quick questions to make sure they're right for you. Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 28."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and apart from the stinging, do you have other symptoms, like needing to go all the time?"
  • Patient: "Yes, constantly! It started yesterday. No fever or back pain though."
  • Pharmacist: "And are you taking any other medicines or do you have any other health conditions?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a typical uncomplicated UTI. The sachets can help with the stinging, and you can take paracetamol for the pain. However, these won't clear up the infection itself. For that, you'll need a short course of antibiotics from your GP. You should try to get an appointment today. The sachets can help make you more comfortable in the meantime. Remember to drink plenty of water and see the doctor if the symptoms aren't better in two days, or if you feel worse."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something for a water infection."
  • Pharmacist: "I can help with that. Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me. I'm 32."
  • Pharmacist: "Can you tell me your symptoms?"
  • Patient: "The usual, stinging and needing the loo. But I've started to get a really bad pain in my back today and I feel a bit shivery."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. The back pain and feeling shivery are signs that the infection might have spread to your kidneys. An over-the-counter medicine isn't suitable for that, and you need to be assessed by a doctor urgently. You should contact your GP surgery straight away and tell them your symptoms, including the back pain and feverish feeling. If you can't get an emergency appointment, you should call 111 for advice."
