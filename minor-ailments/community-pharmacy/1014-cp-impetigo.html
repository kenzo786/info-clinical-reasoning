Impetigo in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Impetigo is a common and highly contagious superficial bacterial skin infection, frequently the reason for a person seeking advice in a community pharmacy. It is primarily caused by Staphylococcus aureus and sometimes Streptococcus pyogenes. The infection typically occurs on areas of pre-existing skin damage, such as a cut, insect bite, or in skin affected by eczema or scabies.

There are two main clinical forms:

  • Non-bullous impetigo: This is the most common form (around 70% of cases). It starts as red macules or papules that quickly evolve into vesicles or pustules. These rupture to release a serous fluid that dries to form characteristic thick, adherent, "golden" or "honey-coloured" crusts. Lesions are often found around the nose and mouth.
  • Bullous impetigo: This form is caused exclusively by certain strains of S. aureus that produce exfoliative toxins. It presents with flaccid, fluid-filled blisters (bullae). The bullae rupture easily, leaving a moist, red base with a thin, varnish-like crust. The trunk and limbs are common sites.

The condition is usually not systemically serious and may be itchy but is not typically painful. Without treatment, it often resolves within two to three weeks, but treatment is advised to speed up recovery and reduce contagion. The pharmacy team's role is to identify cases suitable for OTC management, provide advice, and refer where necessary.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of systemic illness: Fever, malaise, rigors, or the patient appearing visibly unwell indicates the infection may be more than skin-deep and requires urgent medical assessment.
  • Spreading cellulitis: If the skin around the lesions becomes red, hot, swollen, and tender, this suggests cellulitis, a deeper skin infection requiring immediate GP referral for systemic antibiotics.
  • Widespread or rapidly progressing bullous impetigo: This is a significant red flag, especially in an infant, as it can indicate Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS), a medical emergency.
  • Deep, painful, or "punched-out" ulcers: This may suggest ecthyma, a deeper form of impetigo requiring a GP assessment.
  • Facial infection with periorbital swelling: Any infection near the eye with swelling requires urgent referral to rule out periorbital cellulitis.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Individuals with significant immunosuppression are at higher risk of severe infection and should be referred to their GP.
  • No improvement or worsening: If the condition does not improve after a course of appropriate OTC treatment, or worsens at any point, referral to the GP is necessary.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. OTC treatments for impetigo have specific age licensing. This also establishes if the consultation is for a third party, requiring more careful questioning.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To determine the nature of the skin lesions. Ask about appearance (crusts vs blisters), location, and spread. This helps differentiate between non-bullous and bullous impetigo and screen for red flags like spreading redness or systemic illness.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish the timeline. Impetigo is an acute condition. If the skin problem has been present for several weeks without resolution, or is frequently recurring, a GP referral is more appropriate to confirm the diagnosis and consider underlying causes like staphylococcal carriage.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To discover what self-care measures or treatments have been tried. If a patient has already used an appropriate OTC treatment correctly without success, this would trigger a referral to the GP.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify comorbidities and relevant medical history. A history of atopic eczema, for example, makes secondary infection more likely. It also identifies immunocompromised patients or those on medications that may affect treatment choices who should be referred.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary, as many skin conditions can mimic impetigo.

Common Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Herpes Simplex (Cold Sores): Typically presents as a tight cluster of vesicles around the lips, often with a prodromal tingling sensation. Referral is needed if the diagnosis is uncertain or if it is widespread.
  • Infected Eczema: Presents as crusting and weeping superimposed on chronically itchy, inflamed skin, often in typical flexural locations. The patient will need prescription treatment for both the infection and the underlying eczema from a GP.
  • Tinea (Ringworm): Fungal infections may have a crusted appearance but typically feature a raised, scaly border with central clearing. If suspected, referral is needed for confirmation and appropriate antifungal treatment.
  • Shingles (Herpes Zoster): Presents as a painful, vesicular rash in a distinct unilateral dermatomal pattern. This requires urgent referral for antiviral treatment.
  • Insect Bites: Can become infected and mimic impetigo, but there is usually a clear history of a preceding bite and intense itch. Widespread infection requires referral.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

OTC management is only appropriate for localised, non-bullous impetigo in patients with no red flags. Hygiene and self-care advice is the cornerstone of any recommendation.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This is the most important intervention. Advise on strict hygiene: regular hand washing, keeping nails short, and not sharing towels or flannels. Gently remove crusts with warm water before applying treatment.

First-Line OTC Treatment (Localised, Non-Bullous Impetigo)

  • Hydrogen Peroxide 1% cream:
    • Mechanism: A topical antiseptic that works by releasing oxygen when in contact with the skin, killing bacteria. It is the NICE-recommended first-line treatment.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Licensed for adults and children over 1 year of age.
    • Counselling: Explain that it directly treats the infection. Advise to apply a small amount to the affected areas 2-3 times a day for 5 days. A mild, short-lived stinging sensation can occur. [Rx info]

Treatments to Avoid

  • Other antiseptics: Over-the-counter antiseptic washes or creams (e.g., chlorhexidine, povidone-iodine) are not recommended by NICE for treating impetigo.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While most community pharmacists do not prescribe these medicines, awareness is vital for managing patient expectations after a referral and explaining why a GP visit is necessary.

  • Topical Antibiotics (e.g., Fusidic acid, Mupirocin):
    Context: These are prescribed by a GP if the first-line antiseptic cream is unsuitable or has failed. Routine use of fusidic acid is discouraged due to high bacterial resistance. Mupirocin is often reserved for confirmed MRSA cases. [Rx advice]
  • Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin, Clarithromycin):
    Context: A GP will prescribe these for widespread non-bullous impetigo, any case of bullous impetigo, or when the patient is systemically unwell or has spreading cellulitis. Flucloxacillin is first-line; clarithromycin is for those with a penicillin allergy. [Rx advice]

Knowledge of these POMs allows the pharmacy team to confidently explain the treatment pathway, reinforcing their clinical judgement and the importance of the referral.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and practical advice are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for impetigo.

Key Counselling Points for OTC Management

  • Explain the Condition: Describe impetigo as a common, contagious skin infection that is treatable but needs careful management to prevent spreading it to others or other parts of the body.
  • Justify Product Choice: "The national guidelines recommend this hydrogen peroxide cream as the best first choice for a small patch of impetigo. It's an antiseptic that treats the bacterial infection directly."
  • Demonstrate Application: Advise washing hands before and after, gently soaking off crusts with a clean cloth and warm water, and then applying a thin layer of cream to the affected skin.
  • Hygiene is Crucial: Emphasise using separate towels, flannels and bedding, and washing them on a hot cycle. Encourage frequent hand washing and keeping fingernails short.
  • School/Nursery Exclusion: Clearly advise that children should stay off school or nursery until the lesions are all crusted and dry, or for 48 hours after starting prescribed antibiotic treatment.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient on what to do if their condition does not improve as expected or worsens. This must be done for every patient, whether a product is sold or a referral is made.

Following the sale of an OTC product, advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The impetigo does not start to improve within the 5 days of treatment.
  • The lesions continue to spread or new ones appear after 48 hours of starting treatment.
  • The skin becomes very itchy or irritated by the treatment.
  • The infection clears but then comes back quickly.

Advise the patient to seek urgent GP advice or contact NHS 111 if they or their child develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • The skin around the sores becomes increasingly red, hot, swollen, and painful.
  • They begin to feel unwell with a fever, chills, or muscle aches.
  • The lesions change into large, fragile blisters.

Conclude by inviting them to speak to the pharmacist again if they have any more questions.

9. Pathophysiology

Impetigo is a superficial infection of the epidermis, the outermost layer of the skin. The infection is established when bacteria, primarily Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes, breach the skin's protective barrier. This breach can be a minor cut, an insect bite, or a pre-existing skin condition like atopic dermatitis.

In non-bullous impetigo, the bacteria colonise the stratum corneum. The host's immune system responds, leading to inflammation and the formation of vesicles or pustules filled with neutrophils, bacteria, and serous fluid. When these fragile pustules rupture, the exudate dries on the skin surface, mixing with skin debris and bacteria to form the characteristic thick, honey-coloured crusts.

Bullous impetigo is caused by specific strains of S. aureus that produce exfoliative toxins (ETA and ETB). These toxins are proteases that target and cleave a specific protein called desmoglein 1. Desmoglein 1 is a key component of desmosomes, which are structures that hold epidermal cells (keratinocytes) together. By breaking down desmoglein 1, the toxins cause a loss of adhesion between keratinocytes in the superficial epidermis, resulting in the formation of large, flaccid bullae.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and limitations.

Hydrogen Peroxide 1% Cream

  • Application: Should only be applied to the affected areas. Avoid contact with the eyes. If contact occurs, rinse immediately with water.
  • Side Effects: A mild stinging sensation is a common and expected side effect that usually subsides shortly after application. It can also cause skin erythema.
  • Bleaching effect: Hydrogen peroxide can bleach fabrics and hair, so care should be taken during application.
  • Duration of use: Treatment should be limited to 5 days. If there is no improvement, the patient should be referred to their GP.

General Counselling

  • Avoidance of off-label recommendations: Do not recommend other antiseptic products, wound care products, or topical antibiotics available for other indications (e.g. acne creams) for the treatment of impetigo. Stick to evidence-based recommendations from NICE.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Impetigo is very common in children. Hydrogen peroxide 1% cream is licensed for children from 1 year of age. For infants under 1 year, a GP referral is mandatory. Emphasise hygiene measures and school/nursery exclusion rules to parents/carers. Bullous impetigo in any child requires immediate GP referral.

Older adults

While OTC treatment can be appropriate for localised cases, maintain a lower threshold for referral to a GP, as older adults may have comorbidities or more fragile skin, increasing the risk of complications like cellulitis.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

There is no specific data on the use of topical hydrogen peroxide in pregnancy or breastfeeding, but systemic absorption is expected to be minimal, and it is generally considered low risk. However, many pharmacy teams will adopt a cautious approach and refer to the GP for definitive advice and management to ensure safety.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with chronic skin conditions like severe atopic eczema or psoriasis, or those who are immunocompromised, should be referred to their GP for management. Attempting OTC treatment in these groups is inappropriate as they require a more comprehensive assessment and prescription-strength treatment.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Case & OTC Sale

  • Parent: "Hi, my daughter has some weeping spots on her chin. The nursery said it might be impetigo."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help with that. First, who is it for and how old is she?"
  • Parent: "It's for my daughter, Lily. She's 4 years old."
  • Pharmacist: "Can you describe what the spots look like?"
  • Parent: "They're crusty and a bit yellow. There are just two of them. She's had them for a couple of days."
  • Pharmacist: "Has she tried anything, and is she on any other medication?"
  • Parent: "No to both. She is otherwise perfectly well."
  • Recommendation: "Thank you. It does sound like simple impetigo. As it's just a small area, the best thing to try is a cream called hydrogen peroxide. It's an antiseptic you apply 2-3 times a day for 5 days. Remember to wash your hands before and after, and don't share towels with her. She'll need to stay off nursery for 48 hours after you start treatment. If it's not getting better in a few days or it starts to spread, you must take her to the GP."

Scenario 2: Referral Due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something for this rash on my leg."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "Me. I'm 45."
  • Pharmacist: "What are the symptoms?"
  • Patient: "I had a few blisters which burst, and now the whole area around it is getting really red, hot, and it's quite sore to touch."
  • Pharmacist: "How long has the redness been spreading?"
  • Patient: "It's been getting worse over the last day. I feel a bit shivery too."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that information. Because the redness is spreading and you're feeling shivery, it suggests the infection might be going deeper into the skin. This is called cellulitis. An over-the-counter cream won't be strong enough. You need to see a GP today to get the right antibiotics. Please call your surgery as soon as you leave."
