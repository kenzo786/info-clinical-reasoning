Oral Thrush (Candidiasis) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Oral thrush, or oral candidiasis, is a common fungal infection that frequently presents in the community pharmacy. It is most often caused by an overgrowth of Candida albicans, a yeast that is a normal part of the oral microflora. The infection occurs when local or systemic factors disrupt this normal balance.

Patients typically describe a sudden onset of symptoms. The classic presentation features creamy-white, slightly raised patches on the tongue, inner cheeks, or roof of the mouth. These patches can often be wiped away, revealing a red, raw surface that may be sore or bleed slightly. Associated symptoms include an unpleasant or altered taste, a "cotton-like" feeling in the mouth, and soreness that can make eating and drinking uncomfortable.

Common triggers include recent antibiotic courses, use of inhaled corticosteroids, poorly fitting dentures, and suboptimal oral hygiene. The role of the pharmacy team is to correctly identify the likely cause, screen for red flags that require medical referral, provide advice on crucial self-care measures, and explain why a GP appointment is necessary to obtain prescription-only treatment.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of systemic illness or oesophageal spread: The patient reporting a fever, pain on swallowing (odynophagia), or a feeling of food getting stuck requires an urgent GP referral. This may indicate the infection has spread to the oesophagus (Candida oesophagitis) and is a medical emergency in the immunocompromised.
  • Suspected underlying immunosuppression: New-onset oral thrush in a healthy adult with no clear risk factors (like recent antibiotics) should trigger a routine GP referral for investigation into possible undiagnosed diabetes or HIV.
  • Widespread or recurrent infection: If thrush is extensive or returns quickly after treatment, this necessitates a GP referral to investigate for an underlying cause or resistant infection.
  • Associated weight loss or other systemic symptoms: The presence of unexplained weight loss or persistent fatigue alongside oral thrush requires a GP referral to rule out serious systemic disease.
  • Suspicion of malignancy: If a white patch in the mouth cannot be scraped off, is firm, or is associated with an ulcer, it must be considered a potential malignancy (e.g., leukoplakia, squamous cell carcinoma). This requires an urgent referral to a GP or dentist.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. This helps to assess risk factors (e.g., very young or elderly) and establish whether a third-party consultation is taking place, requiring extra caution.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the specific nature of the problem. Ask the patient to describe the appearance of the patches. Crucially, ask "Can you wipe the white patches off?". Also ask about soreness, taste changes, or pain on swallowing to screen for red flags.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To determine the duration. A recent onset is typical of thrush, whereas a lesion present for many weeks or months that isn't changing is a red flag for other pathology and needs referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out if the patient has tried anything, such as mouthwashes which can sometimes disrupt oral flora, or if they have previously been diagnosed and treated by a GP. Recurrent infection requires a referral.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify potential causes and risk factors. Ask specifically about recent antibiotics and steroid inhalers. Reviewing medication history can also identify immunosuppressant drugs or conditions like diabetes that increase risk.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a GP or dental referral is necessary. The key question is whether the white patch can be scraped off.

Differential for scrapable white patches

  • Oral Thrush: Creamy-white plaques that leave a red, raw base when wiped. This is the most common cause seen in pharmacy.
  • Food debris / Poor oral hygiene: Can be easily wiped away to reveal normal, healthy mucosa underneath.

Serious Differentials (Non-Scrapable Patches) for Referral

  • Leukoplakia: A persistent white patch that cannot be wiped off. It is a potentially malignant disorder requiring urgent referral.
  • Oral Lichen Planus: An autoimmune condition often appearing as a network of fine white lines, typically on the inner cheeks. Requires diagnosis by a specialist.
  • Oral Hairy Leukoplakia: A "hairy" looking white patch on the sides of the tongue, strongly associated with immunosuppression (e.g., HIV) and requiring medical investigation.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma: Can present as a persistent white or red patch, often with ulceration or hardness. This is a critical diagnosis to consider for urgent referral.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

There are no licensed OTC medicines available to treat oral thrush directly. Management in the pharmacy centres on providing comprehensive self-care advice and explaining the need for a referral to a GP for prescription treatment.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Referral for Treatment: Explain clearly that treatment for oral thrush requires a prescription from a GP. The primary role of the pharmacy is to advise on measures to support recovery and prevent recurrence.
  • Oral Hygiene: This is the most important intervention. Advise on meticulous hygiene, including regular brushing of teeth and gentle brushing of the tongue.
  • Denture Hygiene: This is critical for denture wearers. Advise them to:
    • Remove dentures overnight.
    • Clean dentures thoroughly with a non-abrasive paste and brush.
    • Soak dentures daily in a dedicated denture cleaning solution.
    • Clean gums and palate after removing dentures.
  • Inhaler Technique (for Asthma/COPD patients): Advise patients using a corticosteroid inhaler to rinse their mouth with water and spit it out immediately after each use. Using a spacer device is also highly recommended to reduce oral steroid deposition.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While pharmacists do not prescribe these medicines without an independent prescribing qualification, awareness is essential for counselling patients, managing their expectations after referral, and for ensuring safety when dispensing the subsequent prescription.

  • Topical Antifungals (e.g., Miconazole Oral Gel, Nystatin Suspension):
    Context: These are the first-line treatments a GP will prescribe for uncomplicated thrush. Miconazole gel is often preferred but has significant drug interactions (e.g., warfarin, statins) that pharmacists must check for upon dispensing. Nystatin is not absorbed systemically and is a safer choice where interactions are a concern. [Rx advice]
  • Systemic Antifungals (e.g., Fluconazole Capsules):
    Context: A GP will reserve these for severe cases, patients who are immunocompromised, or when topical treatment has failed. These also have potential for drug interactions and side effects that the pharmacy team should be aware of. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team explain why a GP visit is necessary and to provide appropriate counselling when the patient returns with a prescription.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication is the most important intervention for managing oral thrush in the pharmacy.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Diagnosis and Need for Referral: "Based on what you've told me, it sounds very likely you have oral thrush. This is a common fungal infection, but the treatment for it is only available on prescription, so you will need to see your GP to get the right medicine."
  • Justify No OTC Product Sale: "There are no over-the-counter antifungal medicines licensed for this, and using things like antiseptic mouthwash can sometimes make it worse. The most effective approach is a prescribed medicine alongside the self-care measures I'll explain now."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame hygiene measures as an active part of the treatment. For inhaler users, say: "Rinsing your mouth after using your inhaler is one of the most important things you can do to stop this from coming back." For denture wearers, be specific about the cleaning routine.
  • Manage Expectations: Reassure the patient that the condition is common and usually clears up quickly with the right treatment from the GP.
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've been clear, what's the most important step for you to take next?" can reinforce the need for a GP appointment.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting involves providing clear advice on when to seek further help after the pharmacy consultation. It is a critical part of the referral process.

After advising the patient to see their GP for treatment, provide them with the following safety netting points. Advise them to re-consult their GP if:

  • The symptoms do not improve after completing a full course of prescribed treatment.
  • The thrush comes back again after treatment has finished.
  • They develop any new or worrying symptoms.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice from their GP if they develop any red flag symptoms while waiting for their appointment, such as:

  • Pain or difficulty when swallowing.
  • A fever or feeling systemically unwell.
  • The white patches seem to be spreading rapidly.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or if the GP recommends a treatment they wish to discuss.

9. Pathophysiology

Oral candidiasis results from the opportunistic proliferation of Candida species, predominantly Candida albicans. C. albicans is a dimorphic fungus, meaning it can exist in two forms: a budding yeast (blastospore) and a filamentous hyphal form.

In a healthy individual, Candida exists as a harmless commensal yeast, its growth kept in check by the host's immune system (particularly cell-mediated immunity via T-cells and phagocytes like neutrophils), competition from the resident bacterial microflora, and salivary antimicrobial proteins (e.g., lysozyme, lactoferrin). When this local ecosystem is disrupted, Candida can transform into its pathogenic hyphal form.

This transformation is key to its virulence. The hyphae are able to adhere to and invade the superficial layers of the oral epithelium, leading to inflammation and tissue damage. In pseudomembranous candidiasis (thrush), the characteristic white plaques are composed of tangled masses of fungal hyphae, desquamated epithelial cells, fibrin, bacteria, and inflammatory cells. The underlying erythema and bleeding upon removal of the plaque are due to this superficial mucosal invasion and the associated inflammatory response. Systemic and local risk factors (like immunosuppression or antibiotic use) lower the host's defences, creating a permissive environment for this transition from harmless coloniser to invasive pathogen.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Awareness of risks associated with the likely prescribed POMs is essential for the pharmacy team during the dispensing process and for patient counselling.

Miconazole Oral Gel

  • Interactions: This is a critical safety issue. Miconazole inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP2C9. Before dispensing, it is vital to check if the patient is taking warfarin (contraindicated - risk of severe bleeding) or certain statins like simvastatin (risk of myopathy). This check is a key professional responsibility.
  • Counselling at Dispensing: Advise patient to apply after meals and hold in the mouth as long as possible.

Nystatin Suspension

  • Safety Profile: Nystatin is not absorbed from the GI tract and has no systemic drug interactions. It is a much safer option for patients on warfarin or other interacting medicines, a fact worth noting when liaising with prescribers if necessary.
  • Counselling at Dispensing: Advise patient to swish the liquid around the entire mouth for as long as possible before swallowing.

Fluconazole Capsules

  • Interactions: Also has significant drug interactions via CYP enzyme inhibition. A thorough check of the patient's medication record is required before dispensing.
  • Counselling at Dispensing: Counsel on common side effects like nausea and advise that it is usually a short course. Reinforce the importance of completing the course as prescribed.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Oral thrush is common in infants. Pharmacy advice should focus on supporting the parent and referring to the GP for a prescription (nystatin or miconazole, depending on age). If the mother is breastfeeding, she should be advised to see her GP as she may also need treatment for her nipples to prevent reinfection.

Older adults

This group is at high risk, especially denture wearers. The pharmacy consultation must include detailed questions about denture hygiene. When a prescription is presented for an elderly patient on multiple medicines, a clinical check for interactions (especially with miconazole) is paramount.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care advice is key. Advise the patient to see their GP, explaining that topical treatments like nystatin or miconazole are safe to be prescribed during pregnancy. Oral fluconazole is generally avoided. If breastfeeding, advise that they should mention this to the GP as they may need treatment for their nipples as well.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients who are immunocompromised (e.g., mention being on chemotherapy), the pharmacy team must refer urgently to a GP. Explain that thrush can be more serious in their situation and requires prompt medical assessment. When dispensing prescriptions for this group, be aware they may be on higher doses or longer courses of antifungals.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Referral for a Denture Wearer

  • Patient: "Hello, I think I have thrush in my mouth. My daughter said I should come here."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help with that. Can you tell me what your symptoms are?" (W)
  • Patient: "It's just really red and sore under my top denture. It's been like that for a few weeks." (W, H)
  • Pharmacist: "I see. Do you take your dentures out at night?" (Part of A)
  • Patient: "No, I've always kept them in." (A)
  • Pharmacist: "And are you on any regular medicines?" (M)
  • Patient: "Just my water tablet and a blood pressure pill." (M)
  • Recommendation: "Thank you. It sounds like the soreness is caused by an infection often linked to wearing dentures, a type of thrush. To clear it up, you'll need an antifungal medicine from your GP. What's really important is that you start taking your dentures out every night to let your gums rest, and give them a thorough clean and soak. I can show you the best cleaning tablets. Once you have the prescription from your GP, we can dispense it for you."

Scenario 2: Urgent Referral for a Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something for this white patch on my tongue. It's not going away."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Who is it for?" (W)
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 65." (W)
  • Pharmacist: "Can you describe it for me? Is it sore?" (W)
  • Patient: "Not really sore, just there. I tried to brush it off, but it doesn't move." (W)
  • Pharmacist: "And how long has it been there?" (H)
  • Patient: "A good couple of months now." (H)
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and do you smoke?" (Part of M - social history)
  • Patient: "Yes, 20 a day for 40 years." (M)
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that information. Because the patch is not wiping off and has been there for a while, it's very important that you get it checked out by your GP or a dentist. It's probably nothing to worry about, but any persistent patch like that needs a proper examination. Please make an appointment as soon as you can. An over-the-counter product isn't suitable here."
