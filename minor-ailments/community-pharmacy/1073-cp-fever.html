Fever in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Fever, or pyrexia, is an elevation of body temperature above the normal daily variation. It is one of the most common reasons for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy, particularly for children. In the UK, a temperature of 38°C or higher is generally considered a fever. It is a physiological response to a wide range of conditions, most commonly self-limiting viral infections such as upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs) or gastroenteritis.

Patients typically present with a history of feeling hot, flushed, or unwell. They may also report sweating, chills, or rigors. Associated symptoms often point towards the underlying cause, for example, a cough in a URTI, or vomiting and diarrhoea in gastroenteritis. While distressing for patients and parents, fever is usually a benign and helpful part of the body's defence mechanism. In most cases, it resolves within 3 to 5 days without specific medical intervention.

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments for symptomatic relief, and manage patient expectations regarding the fever's natural duration and course.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause for a fever. The presence of any red flag warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of Sepsis or Shock: Any signs suggesting systemic circulatory compromise require emergency referral. These include new-onset confusion or altered mental state, cyanosis, very pale or mottled skin, and a weak, rapid pulse.
  • Non-blanching Rash: A petechial or purpuric rash that does not disappear with pressure (the 'glass test') is a medical emergency, highly suggestive of meningococcal septicaemia.
  • Signs of Meningeal Irritation: Features such as neck stiffness, severe headache, dislike of bright lights (photophobia), or a bulging fontanelle in infants should prompt an emergency referral to rule out meningitis.
  • Severe Respiratory Distress: A very high respiratory rate, grunting sounds, nasal flaring, or an inability to talk in full sentences suggests severe pneumonia or sepsis and requires immediate hospitalisation.
  • Fever in an infant under 3 months: A temperature of 38°C or higher in an infant less than 3 months old is a red flag requiring immediate medical assessment.
  • Focal Neurological Signs: New onset of focal weakness, seizures (especially if they are focal or prolonged), or being difficult to wake are red flags for central nervous system infection.
  • Severe, Unexplained Pain or Agitation: Severe pain or a weak, high-pitched, and continuous cry in a child can indicate a serious underlying problem.
  • Immunocompromised Patient: Any patient with significant immunosuppression (e.g., on chemotherapy or high-dose steroids) presenting with a fever requires an urgent GP assessment.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. This is critical for fever, as a high fever in an infant under 3 months is a red flag. Age also determines which OTC products are licensed for use.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the fever (how high, any chills/rigors) and identify any associated red flags (non-blanching rash, neck stiffness, confusion, difficulty breathing). Localising symptoms (e.g., cough, ear pain, dysuria) can suggest the cause.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: A fever lasting more than 5 days, or one that improves and then returns, requires a GP referral to rule out a secondary bacterial infection or other serious cause.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments have been tried (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) and if they were effective at relieving distress. This helps gauge severity and avoids duplicating treatment.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for comorbidities (e.g., asthma, peptic ulcer disease, heart failure) which may contraindicate certain OTCs like ibuprofen. It also helps identify immunocompromised patients who require urgent referral.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral from the pharmacy is necessary. Most cases presenting are self-limiting viral infections.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Viral Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI): The most common cause, presenting with coryza, cough, and sore throat.
  • Acute Otitis Media: Common in children, presenting with ear pain and irritability.
  • Tonsillitis/Pharyngitis: Usually viral, presenting with a sore throat and painful swallowing.
  • Gastroenteritis: Usually viral, with associated vomiting and/or diarrhoea.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI): Can present with fever alone, especially in infants and the elderly.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Sepsis: Life-threatening response to infection. Suspect if red flags are present (e.g., confusion, mottled skin, very high heart rate).
  • Meningitis/Meningococcal Disease: Look for fever, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, and a non-blanching rash.
  • Pneumonia: Suspect if fever is accompanied by breathlessness, productive cough, and pleuritic chest pain.
  • Kawasaki Disease (in children): Suspect with a persistent high fever (>5 days) plus other signs like a rash, red eyes, and red, cracked lips ('strawberry tongue').
  • Pyelonephritis: Kidney infection. Suspect with fever, loin pain, and systemic upset.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is to relieve the distress associated with fever, not simply to lower the temperature. Non-pharmacological advice is paramount.

First-Line Advice (All Fevers)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise offering regular fluids to prevent dehydration. Dress the person in light, comfortable clothing and avoid overheating the room. Tepid sponging is not recommended.

Antipyretic Medicines

Advise that these should be used to improve comfort and relieve distress, not just to normalise the temperature. Do not advise routine alternating use of paracetamol and ibuprofen.

  • Paracetamol:
    • Mechanism: An analgesic and antipyretic that acts centrally. It is the first-choice agent.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available as oral suspension for infants from 2 months (weighing >4kg and not premature).
    • Counselling: Ensure correct dose for age/weight. Counsel on risk of overdose if taking other paracetamol-containing products. [Rx info]
  • Ibuprofen:
    • Mechanism: A non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) with antipyretic and analgesic effects, acting via inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available as oral suspension for infants from 3 months (weighing >5kg).
    • Counselling: May be considered if distress persists despite paracetamol. Check for contraindications (e.g., asthma, chickenpox, stomach issues). [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for fever, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Antibiotics:
    Context: These are not effective for the viral infections that cause most fevers. They are only prescribed by a GP or hospital doctor if a specific bacterial infection is suspected (e.g., pneumonia, pyelonephritis, sepsis). Pharmacists play a key role in upholding antimicrobial stewardship by explaining this to patients requesting antibiotics for simple fevers. Some advanced services may allow pharmacists to prescribe for specific infections (e.g., UTI), but not for fever itself. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral is necessary for assessment and potential prescription, rather than simply being a barrier to treatment.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for fever.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that fever is a normal response to infection and a sign the immune system is working. State that it typically lasts for 3-5 days.
  • Justify Antipyretic Use: A key phrase is: "The goal of giving paracetamol or ibuprofen is to make them more comfortable and relieve any distress, not just to bring the temperature down to normal."
  • Explain No Antibiotics: When a patient requests antibiotics, provide a clear, confident explanation. "Most fevers, especially with cold symptoms, are caused by viruses. Antibiotics only work against bacteria, so they won't help and can cause unwanted side effects."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Recommend encouraging small, frequent sips of fluid. Explain how to spot dehydration (e.g., few or no wet nappies for a baby).
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've explained that clearly, what sort of things should you look out for that would mean you need to see a doctor?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient or carer when they should seek further help. This ensures that a person with a self-limiting illness knows when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The fever continues for 5 days or more.
  • The patient is getting more unwell, or you are becoming more worried.
  • The fever went away for more than 24 hours and then returned.
  • There are signs of dehydration that are not improving with increased fluid intake.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • A rash that does not fade when a glass is pressed against it.
  • Difficulty breathing.
  • New confusion, drowsiness, or being difficult to wake.
  • A stiff neck or a dislike of bright lights.
  • A seizure or fit for the first time.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Fever is a regulated elevation of the body's core temperature, controlled by the hypothalamus in the brain. It is initiated by substances called pyrogens. Exogenous pyrogens, such as components of bacteria or viruses, stimulate immune cells to release endogenous pyrogens.

These endogenous pyrogens are inflammatory cytokines, like interleukin-1 (IL-1) and TNF-α. They signal the hypothalamus to produce prostaglandins (specifically PGE2). PGE2 then 'resets' the body's thermoregulatory set-point to a higher level. In response, the body conserves heat through vasoconstriction (making skin feel cool) and generates heat through shivering. This raises the core temperature to the new, higher set-point, which we measure as fever. This elevated temperature can help inhibit the growth of some pathogens and enhance the immune response.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and interactions.

Paracetamol

  • Overdose Risk: The most significant risk is accidental overdose, which can cause severe liver damage. It is essential to calculate the correct dose for children based on age and weight.
  • Hidden Ingredients: Many "all-in-one" cold and flu products contain paracetamol. Always ask "Are you taking anything else for this cold?" to prevent patients from doubling up and exceeding the maximum daily dose.

Ibuprofen

  • Contraindications: Do not recommend for patients with a history of hypersensitivity to NSAIDs, active peptic ulcer disease, or severe heart failure. Use with caution and refer if there is a history of asthma, as it can induce bronchospasm.
  • Interactions: Be aware of interactions with prescribed medicines, particularly anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin), some antihypertensives, and methotrexate.
  • Chickenpox: Current NHS advice is to use paracetamol to treat fever in chickenpox, due to a small risk of NSAIDs causing severe skin reactions.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Decongestants: Cold and flu remedies often contain decongestants (e.g., pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine). Do not recommend these for patients with hypertension, heart disease, or those taking MAOIs.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Fever management should follow NICE guidelines. A fever of >38°C in an infant under 3 months is an immediate referral. For older infants and children, use paracetamol or ibuprofen for distress, not just for the fever itself. Aspirin is contraindicated in under 16s. Ensure parents are confident with measuring liquid doses using an oral syringe.

Older adults

Be aware of atypical presentations; a serious infection may be present without a high fever. Confusion may be the primary sign. Be cautious with ibuprofen due to higher risks of renal impairment and gastrointestinal side effects. Polypharmacy increases interaction risk. The threshold for GP referral should be lower.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Paracetamol is the first-line choice and is considered safe. Ibuprofen and other NSAIDs should be avoided during pregnancy unless advised by a doctor. The underlying cause of fever needs consideration, so referral to a GP is often the safest course of action if self-care with paracetamol is insufficient.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with asthma, use ibuprofen with caution and check if they have used it safely before. For patients with hypertension or heart disease, avoid any multi-ingredient products containing decongestants. For patients with diabetes, recommend sugar-free formulations of liquid medicines.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Viral Fever & Product Sale

  • Parent: "Hello, my daughter has a temperature. She's three. Can I get some Calpol?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. To make sure that's the best thing, I just need to ask a few questions. You said she's three. What are her symptoms, apart from the fever?"
  • Parent: "She's just a bit grumpy and off her food. She has a bit of a runny nose. It started yesterday."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and has she had any medicine for it yet?"
  • Parent: "No, not yet."
  • Pharmacist: "And is she on any other medication or have any health problems?"
  • Parent: "No, she's fit and well."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a simple cold. Giving her the correct dose of paracetamol, like Calpol, is a good idea to help with her grumpiness. The most important thing is encouraging her to drink plenty of water or juice. A fever like this usually lasts a few days. You should call your GP if the fever goes on for 5 days, or if she seems to be getting more unwell, develops a rash, or you become more worried."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "Hi, I need something for my headache. I've had a fever all day and feel awful."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear that. Can I ask who this is for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 22."
  • Pharmacist: "Tell me more about your symptoms."
  • Patient: "Just a banging headache and a fever. I feel really stiff and the light in the pharmacy is hurting my eyes."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. A severe headache with a fever and a dislike of bright lights can be a sign of something more serious that needs to be checked out immediately. It is not something we can treat over the counter. You need to seek urgent medical attention. You should go to your nearest A&E department straight away. Please do not drive yourself."
