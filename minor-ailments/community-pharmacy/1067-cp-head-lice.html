Head Lice (Pediculosis Capitis) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Head lice (pediculosis capitis) is a very common reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy. The infestation is caused by the human head louse, Pediculus humanus capitis, and is particularly prevalent among primary school children. People typically present after finding live lice or their eggs (nits) in the hair of a child or family member. While scalp itching (pruritus) is a common symptom, many infestations are asymptomatic and are discovered incidentally.

The condition is self-limiting and benign. Transmission occurs via direct head-to-head contact, which is why it spreads easily within families and school groups. A key role for the pharmacy team is to provide reassurance that head lice are not a sign of poor hygiene. The primary goal is to confirm an active infestation is present, recommend appropriate OTC treatment, and provide robust advice on how to use it effectively and prevent re-infestation.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

While head lice are managed in the pharmacy, it is crucial to identify features suggesting a complication or an alternative diagnosis that requires referral to a GP.

  • Signs of secondary bacterial infection: If the person reports that the scalp is very sore, red, weeping with fluid, or has developed golden crusts, this suggests the scratched skin has become infected (impetiginisation). This requires a GP assessment for possible antibiotics.
  • Persistent treatment failure: If a person has correctly used two different types of insecticide treatments (e.g., a physical and a chemical one) and still has live lice, a referral to the GP should be considered to rule out re-infestation and consider other options.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the symptoms described (e.g., severe itching with no lice found on combing) do not match a typical head lice infestation, referral is appropriate to consider other scalp conditions like eczema or scabies.
  • Severe distress: Rarely, a chronic infestation may cause significant anxiety or sleep disturbance, warranting a GP consultation for further support.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

Using a structured questioning technique like WWHAM ensures all necessary information is gathered for a safe and effective recommendation.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. OTC head lice treatments have specific age licenses (e.g., some are suitable from 6 months, others from 2 years). This question is vital for product selection.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the signs of an active infestation. The key question is "Have you found a live louse moving in the hair?". This is essential, as treatment is not recommended for nits alone. Also ask about itching and check for signs of secondary infection (soreness, weeping).
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand the history of the infestation and whether this is a first-time episode or a recurrence, which may influence product choice.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: Crucial for investigating apparent treatment failure. Ask exactly which products have been used and, importantly, how they were applied. Key questions include "Did you use enough to cover all the hair?" and "Did you repeat the treatment after seven days?".
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for any other medical conditions, particularly scalp conditions like eczema or psoriasis which might be irritated by treatments, or any known allergies to ingredients.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

When a person complains of "white specks" in the hair, it is important to differentiate nits from other common, harmless phenomena to avoid unnecessary treatment.

Common Benign Differentials

  • Dandruff (Seborrhoeic Dermatitis): These are skin flakes which, unlike nits, are easily dislodged from the hair and will fall out if the hair is ruffled.
  • Hair Casts: These are white, tube-like structures that wrap around the hair shaft. They can be slid up and down the hair, whereas nits are cemented in place and cannot be moved.
  • Product Residue: Flakes of hairspray or gel can sometimes be mistaken for nits but can be washed or brushed out.

Referral for Other Potential Diagnoses

  • Scalp Eczema or Psoriasis: If itching is the main symptom and there are associated patches of red, inflamed, or scaly skin, these conditions are more likely.
  • Scabies: This mite infestation causes intense itching (often worse at night) and a rash, but usually also affects other parts of the body like the hands and wrists.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Treatment should only be recommended when an active infestation is confirmed by finding a live louse. Correct application and contact tracing are as important as the product choice.

First-Line Advice (All Cases)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: Advise that all household members should be checked with a detection comb. Only those with live lice should be treated, and all at the same time. Washing bedding or clothes on a hot wash is not necessary.
  • Wet Combing Method: This can be used as a non-insecticidal treatment. It involves combing wet, conditioned hair with a detection comb every 3-4 days for two weeks. It is effective but requires persistence.

First-Line Pharmacological Treatment

  • Physical Insecticides (e.g., Dimeticone, Isopropyl Myristate):
    • Mechanism: They physically coat the lice and their eggs, suffocating them or dissolving their outer shell. Resistance is not a concern.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Various brands are available, with some licensed from 6 months of age. Always check the specific product.
    • Counselling: These are the preferred first-line choice. Emphasise that two applications, 7 days apart, are essential. Warn that they can make surfaces slippery. [Rx info]

Second-Line / Alternative Options

  • Chemical Insecticides (e.g., Malathion):
    • Mechanism: A neurotoxin that chemically kills the lice.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Typically licensed for use from 6 months of age.
    • Counselling: Advise that resistance is now common in the UK. The alcohol-based lotions are flammable and must be allowed to dry naturally away from fire or hairdryers. Two applications, 7 days apart, are required. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not prescribe these medicines (unless qualified as an independent prescriber), awareness is vital for giving correct advice and explaining the need for a GP referral.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin, Fusidic Acid):
    Context: These are not for treating head lice. They are prescribed by a GP only if the scalp has become bacterially infected from scratching. The ability to spot this and refer is a key pharmacy role. [Rx advice]
  • Oral Ivermectin:
    Context: This is an oral tablet sometimes used for resistant head lice or scabies. It is an unlicensed (off-label) treatment in the UK for head lice and would only be considered by a specialist dermatologist or microbiologist after referral from a GP. It is not something a patient can or should be requesting in primary care. [Rx advice]

This knowledge allows the pharmacy team to confidently explain the limits of OTC care and the specific reasons for a GP referral, managing patient expectations effectively.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Clear, practical advice is the cornerstone of successful head lice management in the community pharmacy.

Key Counselling Points

  • Reassure and De-stigmatise: Start by saying, "Head lice are incredibly common, especially in schools, and have nothing to do with being clean. We can sort it out."
  • Explain "Live Lice Only": Emphasise that treatment is only needed if they have seen a louse moving. Nits can be empty cases from an old infestation. Recommend detection combing to check.
  • The 'Two-Treatment' Rule: Clearly explain why two applications, seven days apart, are non-negotiable. "The first treatment kills the live lice, but not always the eggs. The second treatment, a week later, kills the lice that have just hatched before they are old enough to lay new eggs."
  • Demonstrate Application: Use the product box to show how to apply it. "Make sure you use enough to completely cover all the hair, right from the roots to the tips. Set a timer on your phone so you leave it on for the right amount of time."
  • Advise on Contact Tracing: Instruct them to "check everyone in the house with a special comb tonight, and only treat the ones who have live lice. Make sure you treat everyone who is affected on the same day."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the process of advising a person on when and where to seek further help if the situation doesn't resolve as expected. This is a critical part of any pharmacy consultation.

Advise the person to consult their GP if:

  • The scalp becomes very red, sore, sticky, or develops crusts. This suggests a bacterial infection that needs medical assessment.
  • The itching is extremely severe or doesn't improve after the lice have been successfully cleared.
  • They have correctly used a full course of two different types of lice treatment (e.g., dimeticone then malathion) and still find live lice.
  • They are ever unsure about the diagnosis.

Advise the person to come back and speak to the pharmacist if:

  • They are unsure if the first treatment has worked.
  • They need advice on which product to choose for the second treatment if the first one was not effective.
  • They have any further questions about application or checking contacts.
9. Pathophysiology

Head lice infestation is caused by Pediculus humanus capitis, an obligate human ectoparasite. The louse is a wingless insect, typically 2-3 mm long, that lives exclusively on human scalp hair and feeds on blood. It cannot fly or jump; transmission occurs via direct head-to-head contact.

The life cycle is key to understanding treatment regimens. The female louse lays eggs (nits), gluing them firmly to the base of hair shafts. She lays 3-4 eggs per day and can live for up to 30 days. The nits are positioned close to the scalp to benefit from its warmth for incubation. They hatch into nymphs after 7-10 days. The nymph moults three times over about 9-12 days before becoming a sexually mature adult louse. The empty nit case remains attached to the growing hair shaft.

Itching (pruritus) is the main symptom and is caused by a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction to allergens in the louse's saliva, which is injected during feeding. This is why itching may not start until several weeks after a primary infestation has begun. In subsequent infestations, the sensitisation is already present, and itching can start much sooner. Scratching can lead to secondary bacterial infection with skin commensals like Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines for head lice requires awareness of product-specific risks and counselling points.

Physical Insecticides (e.g., Dimeticone-based)

  • Flammability: Although they work physically, some lotion or spray formulations can be flammable. Advise users to keep hair away from naked flames, cigarettes, and other ignition sources during treatment and while the hair is drying.
  • Slips and Spills: These products are often oily or silicone-based and can make bathroom floors and shower trays extremely slippery. Advise caution.
  • Skin Reactions: They are generally well-tolerated, but can occasionally cause mild skin irritation or a temporary rash.

Chemical Insecticides (e.g., Malathion)

  • Flammability: Alcohol-based lotions are highly flammable. It is essential to counsel patients to not use a hairdryer, and to keep well away from any flames or heat sources while the product is on the hair.
  • Resistance: Be aware that resistance is widespread and may be a cause of treatment failure. It is a valid reason to recommend a physical insecticide first.
  • Irritation: Can cause skin irritation. Fumes may trigger wheezing in some people with asthma.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Always check the product-specific age licence. Dimeticone 4% and malathion are licensed from 6 months. For infants under 6 months, the only suitable options are manual removal via wet combing, or referral to the GP. Ensure parents understand the importance of correct application and supervision during treatment.

Older adults

Management is the same, but it is important to ensure they are physically able to apply the treatment correctly themselves or have help to do so. Skin may be more fragile, so advise gentle combing.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

The preferred, safest option is the non-pharmacological wet combing method. If a product is requested, a physical insecticide like dimeticone 4% is the treatment of choice as it is not systemically absorbed. Chemical insecticides (malathion) should be avoided. The role of the pharmacy is to advise on the safest options and refer if the person is still concerned.

People with significant comorbidities

For individuals with pre-existing scalp conditions like severe eczema or psoriasis, or those with asthma, physical insecticides are generally better tolerated than chemical ones. Wet combing is also a good option. Recommend a patch test if there is concern about skin sensitivity.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: First-time presentation and product sale

  • Parent: "Hi, I think my daughter has nits. The school sent a letter. What should I do?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course, I can help. Who is it for and how old are they?"
  • Parent: "For my daughter, she's 6."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. The most important question is, have you seen a live louse actually moving in her hair?"
  • Parent: "Yes, I saw one this morning when I was brushing her hair. She's been a bit itchy too."
  • Pharmacist: "Thanks. Has she had them before or tried any treatments yet?"
  • Parent: "No, this is the first time."
  • Recommendation: "Okay, because you've seen a live louse, she needs treatment. I'd recommend this product here containing Dimeticone. It's very effective and works by suffocating the lice. You must apply it twice: once tonight, and then again in exactly seven days to kill any lice that have hatched from the eggs. It's also really important to check everyone else in the family with a detection comb and treat them too if you find live lice. If her scalp gets very sore or red, you should see the GP."

Scenario 2: Investigating treatment failure

  • Customer: "This head lice stuff doesn't work! I used a lotion on my son a week ago and he's still scratching."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear that, it can be very frustrating. Can I just check a few things? Which product did you use?"
  • Customer: "The one in the green box. Malathion."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and did you do a second application?"
  • Customer: "Oh. Do you have to? I just did it once."
  • Recommendation (Triage & Advice): "Ah, that's almost certainly the problem. The first treatment kills the live lice, but not all the eggs. A second treatment is needed a week later to kill the newly hatched lice. I would advise you to try again, but this time let's use a different type of product that works in a physical way, as there's a lot of resistance to the chemical ones now. This dimeticone one is excellent. Use it tonight, and then make sure you repeat it in seven days. That should break the life cycle and solve the problem."
