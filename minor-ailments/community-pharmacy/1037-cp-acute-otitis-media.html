Acute Otitis Media in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute Otitis Media (AOM) is a condition frequently encountered in community pharmacy, particularly in inquiries about infants and young children. It is defined by inflammation of the middle ear and is associated with a rapid onset of symptoms.

Patients, most often children, present with a sudden onset of ear pain (otalgia), which may manifest as irritability, crying, poor feeding, and disturbed sleep. In pre-verbal children, pulling or rubbing the ear can be a key sign. A fever is common, and other symptoms may include cough and coryza, reflecting a preceding viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI). In most cases, AOM is a self-limiting illness. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate AOM from other conditions, identify red flags requiring urgent referral, provide advice on self-care, and recommend appropriate OTC treatments for symptom relief.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of mastoiditis: Swelling, redness, or tenderness in the area behind the ear, or the ear appearing pushed forward. This suggests the infection has spread to the mastoid bone.
  • Facial nerve palsy: New onset of facial drooping or weakness on one side. This is a sign the infection is affecting the facial nerve.
  • Symptoms of intracranial complications: Severe headache, neck stiffness, dislike of bright lights (photophobia), confusion, drowsiness, or changes in consciousness. These are signs of serious conditions like meningitis.
  • Systemic signs of severe illness: A very high fever, rigors (shaking), or other signs of sepsis. This requires an emergency referral.
  • Severe vertigo: A sudden and severe spinning sensation, which may indicate the infection has spread to the inner ear (labyrinthitis).
  • Foreign body in the ear: Especially in a child, if a foreign object is suspected or seen.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. AOM is most common in young children, and OTC product licensing is often age-restricted. This also establishes if the patient is present.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the specific symptoms (ear pain, fever, irritability, ear-tugging). It is crucial for screening for red flags (e.g., "Is there any swelling behind the ear?" "Is there any weakness in their face?"). Asking about discharge helps differentiate from otitis externa or TM perforation.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To establish the acute nature. AOM pain typically improves within 3-4 days. Symptoms lasting longer without improvement, or chronic/recurrent symptoms, require a GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what analgesia has been used. This allows the pharmacist to check if the dose and frequency are correct and effective before recommending alternatives or referral.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for comorbidities that may increase risk (e.g., immunosuppression, cleft palate, Down's syndrome) and would warrant a lower threshold for referral. Also checks for any interacting medicines, although this is less common with AOM treatments.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when referral is necessary.

Common Differentials

  • Otitis Externa: An infection of the outer ear canal. Suspect this if there is pain when the outer ear (pinna) is pulled, itching, or visible swelling/debris in the ear canal. The patient is often a swimmer.
  • Otitis Media with Effusion (OME) / 'Glue Ear': Fluid in the middle ear without acute inflammation. This typically presents with hearing loss rather than severe pain or fever and is a chronic condition.
  • Referred Pain: Pain felt in the ear but originating elsewhere. Ask about toothache, jaw pain, or sore throat. An ear examination in these cases would be normal.
  • Teething: In infants, teething can cause them to be irritable and pull at their ears, but there will be no signs of acute infection.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Mastoiditis: The key serious differential to actively screen for by asking about swelling, redness or pain behind the ear.
  • Foreign Body: Always consider in young children presenting with unilateral ear discomfort or discharge.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is effective pain relief. Advice on analgesia is the most important intervention.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Simple Analgesia (Paracetamol & Ibuprofen):
    • Mechanism: Provide systemic pain and fever relief. This is the cornerstone of management.
    • Licensing: GSL. Available in formulations suitable for infants and children.
    • Counselling: Advise on correct weight-based dosing and ensuring a regular schedule for the first 24-48 hours to manage pain effectively. Reassure parents that this is the most important treatment. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options / Limited Evidence

  • Topical Anaesthetic Eardrops (e.g., containing Lidocaine):
    • Mechanism: Provide local anaesthesia to the tympanic membrane to relieve pain.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age restrictions apply (check specific product).
    • Counselling: Crucially, these must ONLY be used if the eardrum is intact. They are contraindicated if there is any ear discharge or suspected perforation. May be useful for short-term relief alongside systemic analgesia. [Rx info]

Products to Avoid

  • Decongestants and Antihistamines: These have been shown to be ineffective in treating AOM and should not be recommended for this indication.
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe for AOM, awareness of POMs is vital for managing patient expectations and explaining the patient journey after referral.

  • Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin, Clarithromycin):
    Context: A GP will only prescribe these if the patient is systemically very unwell, is at high risk of complications, or has discharge from a perforated eardrum. NICE guidance recommends against antibiotics for most uncomplicated cases. This knowledge allows pharmacists to confidently explain to parents why analgesia is the correct first step and why a "wait and see" approach is safe. Under the Pharmacy First service, AOM in children under 2 is an exclusion requiring formal GP assessment. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team to explain why a GP referral might be needed and what treatments a doctor might consider, preventing the perception that the pharmacy is a barrier to necessary medicine.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for AOM.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that AOM is a common, self-limiting illness. A key phrase is: "It's normal for the pain and fever to last for up to 3 days, but it should start to improve after that. The most important thing is keeping on top of the pain."
  • Prioritise Analgesia: Frame pain relief as the primary treatment. "The best thing you can do is give regular paracetamol or ibuprofen. This will make them much more comfortable while their body fights the infection." Ensure you check the parent's understanding of the dose and frequency.
  • Justify No Immediate Antibiotics: When a parent is anxious, provide a clear explanation. "UK guidelines recommend we treat the pain first, as most of these infections clear up on their own without antibiotics, which don't work for viruses and can cause side effects."
  • OTC Product Counselling: If recommending anaesthetic eardrops, provide the critical counselling point: "You must not use these if you see any fluid coming out of the ear, as this means the eardrum may have burst."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient or carer when they should seek further help from their GP or emergency services.

Advise the patient or their carer to consult their GP if:

  • The pain is not improving after 3 days of regular analgesia.
  • The symptoms are worsening.
  • They have recurrent episodes of AOM (e.g., 3 or more in 6 months).
  • Hearing problems persist after the infection has cleared.

Advise the patient or carer to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they or their child develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Swelling, redness or pain behind the ear.
  • Facial weakness or a drooping face.
  • A very high temperature, confusion, a stiff neck, or seems very unwell.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute otitis media is fundamentally a condition of Eustachian tube dysfunction, typically initiated by a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).

The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear space to the nasopharynx. Its functions are to equalise pressure, protect the middle ear from pathogens, and clear secretions. In young children, this tube is shorter and more horizontal, predisposing them to AOM. During a URTI, the lining of the Eustachian tube becomes inflamed and swollen, causing it to become blocked.

This blockage impairs drainage of the middle ear, causing fluid to accumulate. This fluid provides an ideal environment for viruses or bacteria (most commonly *Streptococcus pneumoniae* and *Haemophilus influenzae*) to grow. The subsequent inflammatory response causes pus to form, increasing pressure in the middle ear. This pressure pushes on the tympanic membrane (eardrum), causing it to bulge outwards, which results in the characteristic symptoms of severe pain and fever.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and patient factors.

Analgesics (Paracetamol & Ibuprofen)

  • Dosing Accuracy: Re-calculating or confirming the correct weight-based dose for children is a key safety step. Use a syringe for accurate measuring.
  • Ibuprofen Cautions: Check for a history of asthma, particularly if it's aspirin-sensitive. Avoid in patients with active peptic ulcers or renal impairment.
  • Hidden Ingredients: Remind carers to check if other 'cold and flu' remedies they are using also contain paracetamol or ibuprofen to prevent accidental overdose.

Anaesthetic Eardrops

  • Perforation: This is an absolute contraindication. Actively ask the patient about any discharge from the ear before even considering a recommendation. If there is any doubt, the product should not be supplied.
11. Use in special populations

Children

This is the most affected population. The primary intervention is ensuring effective, weight-based dosing of analgesia. NICE guidelines identify specific groups who may need referral for consideration of antibiotics (children under 2 with bilateral AOM, children with otorrhoea). Pharmacy staff should be confident in identifying these groups and explaining the need for a GP appointment.

Older adults

AOM is uncommon in older adults. A new presentation should prompt a higher suspicion of an underlying cause (e.g., a tumour obstructing the Eustachian tube) or referred pain from a dental source. A lower threshold for referral to the GP is appropriate.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Paracetamol is the first-line analgesic. Non-pharmacological measures (rest, warm compress) should be encouraged. Ibuprofen should be avoided in the third trimester of pregnancy. Anaesthetic eardrops are generally not recommended due to lack of safety data. Referral to the GP is the safest option if analgesia is insufficient.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients who are significantly immunosuppressed, or have anatomical issues like a cleft palate or cochlear implant, are at high risk of complications from AOM. These patients should be referred directly to their GP or specialist team and not managed with OTC advice alone.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Parent Seeking Advice for a Child

  • Parent: "Hello, my daughter has been up all night crying with her ear. Can I get some antibiotics for an ear infection?"
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear she's in pain. I can certainly help advise you. First, who is it for? How old is she?"
  • Parent: "It's for my daughter, Chloe. She's three."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. And what are her exact symptoms, apart from the pain?"
  • Parent: "She's been pulling her ear, and she feels a bit warm. She had a runny nose last week."
  • Pharmacist: "Thanks. Have you given her anything for the pain yet?"
  • Parent: "I gave her some Calpol a few hours ago, but she's still crying."
  • Pharmacist: "And does she take any other medication or have any health problems?"
  • Parent: "No, she's usually fit and well."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a typical acute ear infection. The guidelines don't recommend antibiotics straight away as they're usually caused by viruses and get better on their own. The most important thing is pain relief. You can give both paracetamol and ibuprofen, staggering the doses. That usually works much better than just one. If the pain isn't getting better in 3 days, or if she seems much more unwell, you should see the GP then. Look out for any swelling behind the ear or facial weakness - if you see that, she needs to be seen urgently."

Scenario 2: Referral Due to a Red Flag

  • Patient: "Hi, I need something for this awful earache. I feel terrible."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, let's see what we can do. Who is it for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 42."
  • Pharmacist: "What are your symptoms?"
  • Patient: "My ear has been throbbing for a day, but now it's really tender and swollen behind the ear as well. See?" (Points to red, swollen area behind the ear).
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for showing me that. The swelling and redness behind your ear is a 'red flag' sign. It could mean the infection has spread to the bone there, which is a condition called mastoiditis. Over-the-counter medicine isn't suitable for this, and you need to see a doctor urgently. You should contact your GP surgery right now for an emergency appointment. If you can't get one, you need to go to A&E."
