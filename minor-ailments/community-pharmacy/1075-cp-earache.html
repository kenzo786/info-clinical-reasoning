Earache (Otalgia) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Earache (otalgia) is a very common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, particularly for children. It can be caused by pathology in the ear itself (primary otalgia) or be referred from other anatomical sites (secondary otalgia). The two most common primary causes are acute otitis media (AOM) and otitis externa (OE).

AOM typically presents with rapid-onset ear pain, often following symptoms of a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI). In young children, this may manifest as non-specific signs like fever, pulling at the ear, and irritability. The condition is usually self-limiting, with most cases resolving within 3-7 days. OE presents with pain, itching, discharge, and a feeling of fullness in the ear canal. It is often triggered by swimming ('swimmer's ear') or trauma from cotton buds.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of mastoiditis: Swelling, tenderness, or redness over the mastoid process (the bone behind the ear), with displacement of the pinna forwards and outwards. This suggests spread of infection and requires emergency referral.
  • Facial nerve palsy (cranial nerve VII): New onset of facial asymmetry or weakness (e.g., drooping of one side of the face) in the context of an ear infection is a sign of serious complication needing urgent assessment.
  • Sudden onset sensorineural hearing loss: This is an otological emergency. If not clearly conductive loss from effusion or canal swelling, it requires immediate referral.
  • Necrotising (malignant) otitis externa: Suspect in immunocompromised (especially diabetic) or elderly patients with severe, deep, nocturnal pain disproportionate to the history. This is an aggressive infection of the skull base requiring emergency referral.
  • Systemic signs of severe illness: High fever (>39°C), rigors, or altered mental state may indicate sepsis or intracranial complications like meningitis.
  • Perichondritis: Inflammation of the cartilage of the pinna, often red, swollen and very tender. This can lead to cartilage necrosis ('cauliflower ear') if not treated promptly.
  • Foreign body in the ear: Particularly if it is a battery, which can cause rapid tissue necrosis, or if it cannot be easily removed.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. AOM is most common in young children, while otitis externa is more common in adults. In older adults, referred pain is a key consideration.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the earache. Is there pain, itching, discharge, or hearing loss? Is the pain sharp or dull? Is it made worse by pulling on the ear lobe (suggests OE)? Are there any red flags like swelling behind the ear or facial weakness?
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: Most acute ear infections resolve within a few days. Pain lasting longer than 3-4 days without improvement, or chronic earache, requires a GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out if effective doses of analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen) have been tried. If pain is not controlled despite this, a referral is likely needed.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify comorbidities like diabetes or immunosuppression, which increase the risk of serious infection (e.g., necrotising otitis externa). Also checks for analgesic use and potential interactions.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting viral infections causing AOM.

Common Primary Causes

  • Acute Otitis Media (AOM): Very common in children. Associated with URTI symptoms.
  • Otitis Externa (OE): Common in adults, especially swimmers. Characterised by pain on moving the pinna/tragus.
  • Eustachian Tube Dysfunction: Often causes a blocked feeling or 'popping' rather than severe pain.
  • Earwax impaction: Can cause discomfort, fullness, and conductive hearing loss.

Common Secondary Causes (Consider for Referral)

  • Referred Pain: Sources include dental problems, temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction, or pharyngitis/tonsillitis. These require referral for diagnosis.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Mastoiditis: Post-auricular swelling, tenderness, and pinna displacement.
  • Necrotising Otitis Externa: Severe pain in an at-risk individual (diabetic/immunocompromised).
  • Temporal Arteritis: In older adults (>50), consider if associated with headache or scalp tenderness.
  • Nasopharyngeal or Laryngeal Cancer: Suspect with persistent, unilateral earache in an older adult smoker.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary role of the pharmacy is to provide effective analgesia and identify patients who need referral. There are very few OTC products to treat the underlying cause of earache.

First-Line Advice (All Earache)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. A warm or cold flannel held over the ear may provide some comfort. Advise against putting anything inside the ear, such as cotton buds or oil.
  • Systemic Analgesia:
    • Mechanism: Paracetamol and ibuprofen are the mainstay of management for relieving the pain of both AOM and OE.
    • Licensing: GSL. Available in various formulations for adults and children (check product-specific age licensing).
    • Counselling: Advise patients/parents to use regular, appropriate doses to control the pain, rather than waiting for it to become severe. This is the single most effective intervention. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options (Mild Otitis Externa Only)

  • Acetic Acid 2% Spray (e.g., EarCalm):
    • Mechanism: Restores the natural acidic environment of the ear canal, making it hostile to the bacteria that cause mild OE.
    • Licensing: P medicine. For use in adults and children aged 12 years and over.
    • Counselling: For mild OE only. Advise the patient to keep the ear dry. It can cause stinging. If symptoms do not improve within 48 hours, a GP appointment is needed. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While most community pharmacists do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for managing patient expectations and explaining why a referral is necessary.

  • Topical Antibiotic/Corticosteroid Preparations:
    Context: These are the first-line prescription treatment for most cases of otitis externa. The GP or other prescriber will choose a product based on whether the eardrum is intact, as some antibiotic drops (aminoglycosides) can be ototoxic if they enter the middle ear. [Rx advice]
  • Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin):
    Context: These are not effective for most cases of AOM, which is often viral and self-limiting. NICE guidelines recommend a no-antibiotic or delayed-antibiotic strategy for most patients. They are only prescribed immediately if a child is systemically unwell, is under 2 with bilateral infection, or has otorrhoea. [Rx advice]

This knowledge allows the pharmacist to explain confidently that effective treatment often requires a proper assessment and prescription from a GP, reinforcing the value of the referral.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for earache.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations (AOM): Clearly explain the natural, self-limiting course. A key phrase is: "It's normal for the pain from a simple ear infection to last for about 3 days. The most important thing is keeping on top of the pain with paracetamol or ibuprofen."
  • Justify No Antibiotics: When a patient or parent requests antibiotics for AOM, provide a clear explanation. "UK guidelines don't recommend antibiotics for most ear infections as they are usually caused by viruses and get better on their own. Antibiotics won't speed up recovery but can cause side effects."
  • Empower with Analgesia: Frame pain relief as the active treatment. Advise on correct dosing and frequency for the patient's age and weight.
  • Explain the Referral: When a referral is made, explain why. For example, "Because the pain isn't settling with painkillers, or because of the discharge, you need a GP to have a look inside the ear and decide if a prescription for ear drops is needed."
  • Ear Care Advice (OE): For patients with suspected OE, counsel them to keep their ears dry (e.g., use cotton wool with petroleum jelly when showering) and to avoid swimming until it resolves.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient or parent when they should seek further help from a GP or A&E.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The earache is not starting to improve after 3-4 days.
  • They have been sold acetic acid spray for mild OE and there is no improvement after 2-3 days of use.
  • Hearing loss or discharge persists after the infection has cleared.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they or their child develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Pain that becomes severe and is not controlled by regular analgesia.
  • Swelling, redness, or tenderness behind the ear.
  • Any new facial weakness.
  • The patient becomes systemically unwell, with a high fever, drowsiness, or confusion.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Acute Otitis Media (AOM): This is an infection of the middle ear space, located behind the tympanic membrane. It typically begins with a viral URTI, which causes inflammation and oedema of the nasopharynx and the Eustachian tube. This tube, which connects the middle ear to the back of the throat to equalise pressure, becomes blocked. Negative pressure develops in the middle ear, leading to an effusion (fluid collection). This stagnant fluid can then become secondarily infected by bacteria (commonly *Streptococcus pneumoniae* or *Haemophilus influenzae*), leading to the formation of pus. The pressure of this purulent fluid against the tympanic membrane causes it to bulge outwards, resulting in intense pain.

Otitis Externa (OE): This is an inflammatory condition of the skin lining the external auditory canal. The canal has a protective layer of earwax (cerumen), which is acidic and has antimicrobial properties. This barrier can be disrupted by factors like moisture (swimming, humidity), trauma (cotton buds, scratching), or underlying skin conditions (eczema). When the barrier is breached, bacteria (most commonly *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* or *Staphylococcus aureus*) or, less commonly, fungi (*Aspergillus*, *Candida*) can invade the skin, causing inflammation, swelling, and pain. The narrow, enclosed space of the canal exacerbates the pain as the swelling increases.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks.

Systemic Analgesics

  • Paracetamol: A key counselling point for patients who also have cold/flu symptoms is to check the ingredients of any other remedies they are taking. Many "all-in-one" products also contain paracetamol, and taking both can lead to accidental overdose.
  • Ibuprofen: Check for contraindications such as previous peptic ulcers, and use with caution in patients with asthma. Should be taken with or after food.

Acetic Acid 2% Spray

  • Application: Can cause significant stinging or burning upon application, especially if the skin is very inflamed. It is important to warn the patient about this.
  • Referral Threshold: It is only for mild cases. If the patient reports severe pain or the canal is visibly very swollen or filled with debris, referral to the GP is more appropriate than an OTC sale.
11. Use in special populations

Children

AOM is the most common cause of earache. The primary intervention from pharmacy is advice on adequate and regular analgesia. Explain that antibiotics are not usually needed. Safety netting is crucial. Acetic acid spray is not licensed for children under 12.

Older adults

Have a high index of suspicion for referred pain from other sources (dental, TMJ, throat). Persistent, unilateral earache with no obvious cause requires GP referral to rule out malignancy. Be aware of the increased risk of necrotising otitis externa in this group, especially if they have diabetes.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care and paracetamol are the first-line recommendations. Ibuprofen should be avoided in the third trimester of pregnancy. Acetic acid spray can be used. For any symptoms that are not settling with simple measures, refer to the GP.

People with significant comorbidities

Patients with diabetes or who are immunocompromised are at high risk of necrotising otitis externa. This presents as severe pain and requires an urgent GP referral. Do not recommend acetic acid spray in these patients; they should be assessed by a doctor.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Child with Acute Otitis Media

  • Parent: "My daughter has a really bad earache. She needs antibiotics."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear she's in pain. To make sure I give you the best advice, who is the medicine for?"
  • Parent: "It's for my daughter, she's four. She's had a bit of a cold and woke up crying with the pain about two hours ago."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Has she had anything for the pain so far?"
  • Parent: "No, I came straight here."
  • Pharmacist: "And is she taking any other medication?"
  • Parent: "No."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a typical middle ear infection, which is very common after a cold. The guidelines actually don't recommend antibiotics as they don't help these infections get better any quicker. The most important thing is to give her regular paracetamol or ibuprofen to manage the pain. This pain is usually at its worst for the first 2-3 days. If the pain isn't getting better after 3 days, or if she becomes very unwell with a high fever, you should see her GP. But for now, pain relief is the best treatment."

Scenario 2: Referral for Otitis Externa

  • Patient: "Hi, my ear is killing me. It's agony if I touch it."
  • Pharmacist: "I can see you're in a lot of discomfort. Who is this for?"
  • Patient: "Me, I'm 28. It's been getting worse for two days. I went swimming last weekend."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, have you tried anything for it?"
  • Patient: "Just paracetamol, but it's not really touching it."
  • Pharmacist: "And do you take any other medicines or have any medical conditions?"
  • Patient: "No, I'm fit and well."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you. Based on what you've said—the pain on touching the ear and the link with swimming—it's very likely to be an infection of the ear canal, called otitis externa. Because simple painkillers aren't controlling it, you'll need prescription ear drops from your GP to clear up the inflammation and infection. An over-the-counter product won't be strong enough. I would advise you to make an appointment with your GP today. In the meantime, keep taking paracetamol and try ibuprofen with it as well."
