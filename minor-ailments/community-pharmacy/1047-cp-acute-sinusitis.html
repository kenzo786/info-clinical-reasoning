Acute Sinusitis in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Acute sinusitis (rhinosinusitis) is a common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, often following a recent cold. It involves inflammation of the mucosal lining of the nose and paranasal sinuses.

People typically present with symptoms of nasal blockage or congestion, and/or nasal discharge (which can be clear or purulent). This is often accompanied by facial pain or pressure, which may feel worse on bending forward, and a reduced or lost sense of smell. The condition is generally self-limiting. It is important to advise people that symptoms of acute sinusitis commonly last for two to three weeks before resolving, often outlasting the original cold symptoms.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Signs of orbital complications: Periorbital oedema, redness or cellulitis; a displaced eyeball (proptosis); double vision (diplopia); painful or restricted eye movements; or reduced visual acuity. These may indicate orbital cellulitis or abscess and require immediate A&E referral.
  • Signs of intracranial spread: A severe frontal headache that is out of proportion to the nasal symptoms; frontal swelling; signs of meningitis such as neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered consciousness; or any new focal neurological signs. These require emergency referral.
  • Severe systemic illness: High fever (above 38°C) and other signs of sepsis, or if the person appears systemically very unwell.
  • Unilateral signs: Persistent and strictly unilateral symptoms, especially with blood-stained discharge or facial swelling, can rarely indicate a tumour and warrant an urgent GP referral.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the age of the patient, as many OTC products have age restrictions. It also establishes if the patient is present or if it's a third-party consultation, which requires extra care.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the core symptoms of sinusitis (nasal blockage/discharge, facial pain/pressure, loss of smell). It helps to screen for red flags (e.g., "Is the pain severe? Any changes to your vision?") and differentiate from a simple cold or allergic rhinitis.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To determine the likely course. Symptoms <10 days are likely viral and self-limiting. Symptoms >10 days may warrant GP referral for consideration of prescription treatments (like intranasal steroids). A duration >12 weeks is chronic and requires GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments the person has already tried (e.g., painkillers, decongestants). This avoids product duplication and helps to assess the severity and progression of the condition.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for comorbidities (e.g., hypertension, heart disease, diabetes) that may contraindicate certain OTC products (especially decongestants) and to identify any potentially interacting medicines.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting are self-limiting viral infections.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Common Cold (Viral URTI): Symptoms are generally more widespread (sore throat, cough) and less focused on localised facial pain and pressure.
  • Allergic Rhinitis: Often features prominent itching, sneezing, and clear, watery rhinorrhoea, and is less likely to cause significant facial pain.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Dental Abscess: Can cause localised facial pain, but this is typically centred on a specific tooth. Urgent dental referral is needed.
  • Orbital Cellulitis: Presents with red flag eye signs and requires immediate hospital admission.
  • Intracranial Infection (e.g., Meningitis): Marked by severe headache, fever, neck stiffness, and altered mental state.
  • Nasal Tumour: A rare cause, but consider for urgent referral in cases of persistent unilateral symptoms or blood-stained discharge.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief. Non-pharmacological advice is the cornerstone of management.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise on adequate rest and hydration. Simple painkillers like paracetamol or ibuprofen are effective for facial pain and fever. Saline nasal sprays or douches can help to clear nasal passages.

Second-Line Options / Symptomatic Relief

  • Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline, Oxymetazoline):
    • Mechanism: Act as vasoconstrictors in the nasal mucosa, providing rapid relief from nasal blockage.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age restrictions apply (typically not for under 12s).
    • Counselling: Advise for short-term use only (maximum 7 days) to avoid rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa). Check for contraindications like hypertension. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While most community pharmacists do not prescribe these, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and making appropriate referrals. This context is also important for understanding national services like Pharmacy First.

  • Intranasal Corticosteroids (e.g., Mometasone, Fluticasone):
    Context: A GP may prescribe a high-dose course for patients whose symptoms have lasted more than 10 days, as they reduce mucosal inflammation. This is a key reason to refer a patient if symptoms are persistent. [Rx advice]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Phenoxymethylpenicillin, Doxycycline):
    Context: These are not effective for most cases of sinusitis which are viral. A prescriber will only consider them if symptoms are very severe, worsening significantly after an initial improvement, or the person is at high risk of complications. Under the Pharmacy First service in England, accredited pharmacists can supply antibiotics for sinusitis if specific clinical criteria are met, following a structured assessment. [Rx advice]
7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication is the most important intervention a pharmacy team can provide for acute sinusitis.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the natural course of the illness. A key phrase is: "It's very normal for sinusitis to last for two to three weeks, so please don't be alarmed if it doesn't clear up in a few days."
  • Justify No Antibiotics (when appropriate): If a patient asks for antibiotics, provide a clear explanation. "Most sinusitis is caused by a virus, similar to a cold. Antibiotics only work on bacteria, so they won't help it get better any faster and could cause side effects."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Emphasise the importance of regular painkillers and using saline nasal rinses to help with symptoms.
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain the role and risks of any recommended product, especially the 7-day limit for decongestant nasal sprays. For all-in-one products, warn about the hidden paracetamol/ibuprofen content.
  • When to See a GP: Be clear about the next step. "If the symptoms haven't started to improve after about 10 days, or are getting much worse, that would be the time to speak with your GP."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a person when and where to seek further help if their condition does not follow a normal course. This must be specific and clear.

Advise the person to contact their GP if:

  • Symptoms have not improved after 3 weeks.
  • Symptoms are worsening after an initial improvement.
  • They experience frequent episodes of sinusitis.

Advise the person to seek urgent medical advice (by calling their GP urgently, 111, or going to A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Any swelling or redness of the eyelid, or changes to vision.
  • A severe headache that is worse than a typical "sinus headache".
  • New confusion, neck stiffness, or an aversion to bright lights.
  • A very high temperature and feeling generally very unwell.
9. Pathophysiology

Acute rhinosinusitis is an inflammatory condition of the paranasal sinuses. The process typically begins with a viral URTI. The viral infection leads to inflammation and oedema of the nasal mucosa, including the narrow drainage channels of the sinuses (the ostia).

This swelling obstructs the ostia, impairing the normal process of mucociliary clearance. Mucus becomes trapped within the sinus cavity, and the oxygen concentration within the sinus decreases. This stagnant, hypoxic environment is an ideal breeding ground for bacteria that are normally harmless commensals of the upper respiratory tract, such as *Streptococcus pneumoniae* and *Haemophilus influenzae*.

While the initial trigger is viral, a secondary bacterial infection occurs in a small percentage of cases. The inflammation, mucosal oedema, and pressure from trapped secretions cause the characteristic symptoms of facial pain, pressure, and congestion. The production of purulent discharge is a result of the congregation of immune cells (neutrophils) fighting the infection.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and cautions.

Topical Decongestants (Xylometazoline, Oxymetazoline)

  • Risk of Rebound Congestion: The primary caution is rhinitis medicamentosa. Use must be limited to a maximum of 7 days, and this must be counselled clearly.
  • Contraindications: Check for hypertension, heart disease, diabetes, and hyperthyroidism, where they should be used with caution or avoided. Avoid in patients taking MAOIs.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Hidden Analgesics: Always check if "all-in-one" cold and flu remedies contain paracetamol or ibuprofen. Counsel the patient not to take separate doses of these painkillers to avoid accidental overdose.
  • Oral Decongestants (Pseudoephedrine, Phenylephrine): These have the same contraindications as topical decongestants and carry a higher risk of systemic side effects (e.g., increased blood pressure, palpitations).
11. Use in special populations

Children

Management is primarily supportive with simple analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen) and saline nasal sprays. OTC decongestants are not licensed for children under 6 and should be used with caution between 6 and 12. A higher index of suspicion for complications is required; any sign of periorbital swelling needs urgent referral.

Older adults

Be cautious with decongestants due to the increased risk of cardiovascular side effects and potential interactions. A lower threshold for GP referral is appropriate if symptoms are severe or not resolving.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with rest, fluids, and paracetamol is first-line. Saline nasal sprays are safe. Decongestants (topical and oral) should be avoided. Referral to the GP is the safest course of action if simple measures are insufficient.

People with significant comorbidities

  • Hypertension/Heart Disease: Avoid all decongestant products.
  • Diabetes: Recommend sugar-free formulations where possible.
  • Asthma: Sinusitis can worsen asthma control. Effective self-care is important.
12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Sinusitis & Self-Care Advice

  • Patient: "Hi, I've got a terrible blocked nose and my face is aching. I think it's sinusitis."
  • Pharmacist: "I can help with that. Who is it for and how long has it been going on?"
  • Patient: "It's for me. It started about 5 days ago after a cold. I don't take any medication."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like typical acute sinusitis, which is usually caused by a virus. The best things are simple painkillers like paracetamol for the facial ache, and a saline nasal spray can help to wash out your nose. It can take 2-3 weeks to clear up completely. If it's not improving after about 3 weeks, or you start to feel very unwell, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral for Persistent Symptoms

  • Patient: "I've had this sinusitis for nearly two weeks now and it's not getting any better. I've tried a decongestant spray but it didn't do much."
  • Pharmacist (after WWHAM): "Thank you. As the symptoms have been going on for more than 10 days without improving, the national guidelines recommend seeing your GP. They can assess you properly and might consider a prescription for a steroid nasal spray, which is very effective at reducing the inflammation. I can't sell you anything else over the counter that would be more effective at this stage."

Scenario 3: Urgent Referral for Red Flag

  • Patient: "My daughter has sinusitis, but this morning her eyelid has started to look a bit puffy and red."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, thank you for telling me that. Any swelling or redness around the eye is a red flag symptom with sinusitis. It means she needs to be seen by a doctor straight away. You should take her to A&E now for an urgent assessment."
