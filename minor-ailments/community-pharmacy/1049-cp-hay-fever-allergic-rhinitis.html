Allergic Rhinitis (Hay Fever) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal mucosa and is a very common reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy, particularly during the spring and summer months. People typically present with a combination of nasal and ocular symptoms. The classic tetrad of symptoms includes anterior rhinorrhoea (a clear runny nose), bouts of sneezing, nasal congestion, and nasal itching. It is often accompanied by allergic conjunctivitis, with red, itchy, and watery eyes.

The condition can be categorised by its timing. Seasonal allergic rhinitis, or hay fever, is triggered by airborne allergens like grass and tree pollens. Perennial allergic rhinitis is present year-round and is usually caused by indoor allergens like house dust mites or pet dander. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to differentiate allergic rhinitis from other conditions, identify any red flags requiring referral, recommend appropriate OTC treatments and self-care strategies, and manage expectations about the condition.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause for the symptoms. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or, in some cases, emergency services.

  • Unilateral symptoms: If symptoms such as a blocked nose, discharge, or pain are persistently on one side, this requires a GP referral to rule out a structural problem, a foreign body, or rarely, a tumour.
  • Persistent blood-stained nasal discharge: While minor nosebleeds can happen with nasal irritation, persistent or heavy bleeding is a red flag and requires a GP referral.
  • Anosmia (loss of smell): If a person has a complete loss of smell that is not explained by severe congestion, they should be referred to their GP.
  • Severe or persistent facial pain: This is not a typical symptom of hay fever and suggests other causes like bacterial sinusitis or a dental problem, warranting a GP or dentist referral.
  • Periorbital swelling or erythema: Significant redness or swelling around the eye could indicate periorbital cellulitis and requires immediate medical assessment.
  • Visual disturbance: Any change in vision requires an urgent referral to a GP or optician.
  • Nasal crusting or septal perforation: If observed or reported, this warrants a GP referral to rule out systemic disease or other causes.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. Many OTC hay fever products have specific age restrictions, and this also helps identify if the consultation is for a child, an older person, or someone who is pregnant, requiring special consideration.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the symptoms (e.g., sneezing, itchy eyes, blocked nose) to help tailor the recommendation. A blocked nose responds best to a corticosteroid nasal spray, whereas itchy eyes are best treated with eye drops. It is also key to screen for red flags at this stage.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To determine if the pattern is seasonal (hay fever) or perennial (year-round allergy). This helps confirm the diagnosis and distinguish it from a short-lived viral cold.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what the person has already tried (e.g., other antihistamines, nasal sprays). This avoids duplication and can uncover issues like incorrect nasal spray technique, which is a common reason for treatment failure.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for comorbidities and potential interactions. Key conditions to identify are asthma (as uncontrolled rhinitis can worsen it), hypertension (decongestants must be avoided), and glaucoma. It is also important to know if the person is taking other medicines that may cause drowsiness.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are straightforward allergic rhinitis.

Common Differentials

  • Infective Rhinitis (Common Cold): Usually lasts less than 10 days and may be accompanied by fever, sore throat, or muscle aches. Symptoms of allergy (like itching) are absent.
  • Non-Allergic Rhinitis: A key differential is vasomotor rhinitis, where symptoms are triggered by environmental changes like temperature or fumes, rather than allergens.
  • Rhinitis Medicamentosa: Rebound nasal congestion caused by overusing topical decongestant sprays for more than seven days. It is important to ask about their use.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Sinonasal Tumours: A crucial consideration if a patient reports unilateral symptoms, persistent bleeding, or facial pain.
  • Nasal Polyps: Can cause severe blockage and loss of smell. They require a GP diagnosis and management plan.
  • Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA): A rare but serious condition that can present with nasal crusting and bleeding. Requires urgent referral.
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Leak: Extremely rare, but presents as a one-sided, constant, watery nasal drip that tastes salty, often after head trauma. Requires emergency referral.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

Management focuses on symptomatic relief through a stepwise approach. Non-pharmacological advice should accompany all recommendations.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise on allergen avoidance (e.g., monitoring pollen counts, wearing wrap-around sunglasses). Saline nasal sprays or douches can help by washing allergens out of the nose.

OTC Medicines

  • Oral Antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine, Fexofenadine):
    • Mechanism: Block the H1 receptor, reducing histamine-mediated symptoms like itching, sneezing, and runny nose. Less effective for nasal blockage.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available for adults and children (check product-specific age licensing).
    • Counselling: Advise that they are best taken regularly. Although "non-drowsy", they can cause drowsiness in a small number of people. [Rx info]
  • Intranasal Corticosteroids (INCS) (e.g., Beclometasone, Fluticasone Propionate):
    • Mechanism: Reduce local inflammation in the nasal mucosa. They are the most effective treatment for all nasal symptoms, especially blockage.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age restrictions apply (typically 18+ for OTC purchase).
    • Counselling: Essential to explain that they need to be used regularly and can take several days to reach full effect. Correct spray technique is vital for success. [Rx info]
  • Sodium Cromoglicate (Eye drops / Nasal Spray):
    • Mechanism: A mast cell stabiliser, preventing the release of histamine.
    • Licensing: P or GSL.
    • Counselling: Less effective than other options but has an excellent safety profile. Requires frequent use (4-6 times daily) to be effective. A good option for pregnant people. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While most community pharmacy teams do not prescribe these medicines, awareness is vital for managing patient expectations and providing context for why a GP referral may be necessary if OTC treatment fails.

  • Intranasal Corticosteroid (INCS) Sprays:
    Context: A GP can prescribe different and sometimes more potent INCS sprays (e.g., Mometasone, Fluticasone furoate) which may also be licensed for younger children. This is a common next step after a referral. [Rx advice]
  • Combination Nasal Sprays:
    Context: If a patient fails to respond to an INCS alone, a GP may prescribe a combination product containing both an intranasal corticosteroid and an intranasal antihistamine (e.g., Dymista). [Rx advice]
  • Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs):
    Context: A GP may consider adding montelukast, an oral tablet, to a patient's regimen. This is more likely if the patient also suffers from asthma. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps the pharmacy team explain the patient journey and the value of a GP assessment for accessing different treatment tiers, reinforcing the pharmacy's role in a structured care pathway.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for someone suffering from allergic rhinitis.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that treatments control symptoms, they don't cure the allergy. It's crucial to explain the time it takes for treatments to work, especially nasal sprays: "This nasal spray is the most effective treatment for a blocked nose, but you need to use it every day, and it might take over a week to feel the full benefit."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame allergen avoidance as an active treatment. Give practical tips like checking the pollen forecast and showering after being outdoors on high-pollen days.
  • Correct Nasal Spray Technique: This is the most common reason for treatment failure. Actively demonstrate the technique: look down at your toes, use your right hand for your left nostril (and vice-versa), and aim outwards towards your ear. Sniff gently, don't snort it back.
  • Explain the Stepped Approach: "Let's start you on this nasal spray. Use it correctly every day for two to three weeks. If your symptoms are still not under control, then you should see your GP, as they can consider prescription-only options."
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to make sure I've been clear, can you show me how you're going to use the nasal spray?" can make a huge difference.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a person when they should seek further help from their GP. This ensures they know when to act if their condition doesn't improve as expected or worsens.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the person to consult their GP if:

  • Symptoms do not improve after 2-3 weeks of continuous, correct use of an OTC intranasal corticosteroid spray.
  • Their quality of life, sleep, or work performance is still significantly affected despite treatment.
  • They have co-existing asthma and are noticing their asthma symptoms are getting worse.
  • They are concerned about the diagnosis and would like to be assessed for allergy testing.

Advise the person to seek urgent medical advice from their GP if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • One-sided nasal symptoms (blockage or discharge).
  • Persistent facial pain.
  • Any bleeding from the nose that isn't just minor spotting.
  • Any changes to their vision.

Finally, always invite them to speak to the pharmacist again if they have more questions or if their chosen product isn't suitable.

9. Pathophysiology

Allergic rhinitis is a classic example of a Type 1 hypersensitivity reaction. The process occurs in two main phases.

The first stage is sensitisation. On initial exposure to an allergen (e.g., a grass pollen grain), an antigen-presenting cell processes the allergen and presents it to a T-helper cell (Th2). These Th2 cells stimulate B lymphocytes to differentiate into plasma cells, which then produce allergen-specific Immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies. This specific IgE circulates and binds to the surface of mast cells and basophils in the nasal mucosa, sensitising the individual.

Upon subsequent exposure to the same allergen, the allergen binds to and cross-links the IgE antibodies on the surface of these sensitised mast cells. This triggers mast cell degranulation, releasing a cascade of inflammatory mediators. This constitutes the early-phase reaction (occurring within minutes) and is driven by mediators like histamine, causing the characteristic symptoms of sneezing, itching, and clear rhinorrhoea.

The late-phase reaction occurs 4-6 hours later. It is characterised by the recruitment of other inflammatory cells, including eosinophils, to the nasal mucosa. These cells release further pro-inflammatory mediators, leading to a more sustained inflammatory response. This phase is primarily responsible for the prolonged and troublesome symptom of nasal congestion.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and interactions.

Intranasal Corticosteroids (INCS)

  • Side Effects: Local side effects like nasal stinging and minor nosebleeds are the most common issues. They are often caused by incorrect technique where the spray is aimed at the nasal septum. Re-counselling on technique is the best first step.
  • Systemic Effects: Systemic absorption from OTC nasal sprays is very low and clinically insignificant for most people when used at the recommended dose.

Oral Antihistamines

  • Sedation: Even "non-drowsy" second-generation antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine) can cause drowsiness in some people. This effect is more pronounced with cetirizine than loratadine or fexofenadine. Always provide a "may cause drowsiness" warning.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Hidden Ingredients: Some hay fever products contain a decongestant like pseudoephedrine. These are subject to legal restrictions on quantity and must not be sold to patients with hypertension or heart disease. Always check the active ingredients.

Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline)

  • Rhinitis Medicamentosa: These should not be recommended for allergic rhinitis. If a patient is buying them, counsel them on the risk of rebound congestion if used for more than 5-7 days.
11. Use in special populations

Children

Check product licensing carefully as it varies by age. For OTC sale, non-drowsy antihistamines like loratadine and cetirizine are available in liquid form for children from 2 years. Beclometasone and fluticasone nasal sprays are only licensed for sale to those 18+. Sodium cromoglicate eye drops are a safe option for most children.

Older adults

Avoid recommending older, sedating antihistamines (e.g., chlorphenamine) due to the increased risk of confusion, falls, and anticholinergic side effects. Second-generation antihistamines are much safer. Ensure they have the dexterity to use nasal sprays and eye drops correctly.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care and allergen avoidance are first-line. If medication is needed, a stepwise approach is used. Saline nasal sprays are safe. Sodium cromoglicate eye drops are a good first choice. If these are insufficient, an intranasal corticosteroid (like beclometasone) is considered the next safest option. If an oral antihistamine is required, loratadine and cetirizine are preferred. Always check the latest specialist resources and refer to the GP if symptoms are severe.

People with significant comorbidities

For people with asthma, actively treating their hay fever is important as it can improve their asthma control. For those with hypertension or heart disease, ensure any multi-ingredient product does not contain a decongestant. For people with diabetes, recommend sugar-free liquid formulations where available.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: First-time Presentation & INCS Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I think I have hay fever for the first time. My nose is so blocked up and I can't stop sneezing."
  • Pharmacist: "I can certainly help with that. Who is it for?" (W)
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 25."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, and you've got a blocked, sneezy nose. Any other symptoms, like itchy eyes?" (W)
  • Patient: "Yes, my eyes are really itchy too."
  • Pharmacist: "And have you tried anything yet, or do you take any other medicines?" (A, M)
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet, and I don't take any other tablets."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like classic hay fever. The best treatment for a blocked nose is a steroid nasal spray, used every day. I'll show you how to use it properly as that's key. For your itchy eyes, you can use sodium cromoglicate eye drops. Remember, the spray can take a few days to work fully. If things aren't better in a couple of weeks, you should see your GP."

Scenario 2: Treatment Failure & Technique Counselling

  • Patient: "This hay fever spray you sold me isn't working. I'm still just as blocked up."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear that. Can you tell me exactly how you've been using it?" (A)
  • Patient: "Well I tilt my head back and give it a good sniff to make sure it gets in."
  • Recommendation (Counselling): "Thanks for explaining. That's actually a really common mistake people make. The spray is meant to work on the inside of your nose, not go down your throat. Let me show you the right way: you look down, put the nozzle just inside your nostril, and aim it towards your ear on that side. Then just a gentle sniff is all you need. Can you try that for me? Let's give it another two weeks using this technique every day and see how you get on. If it's still not working, then we should get you to see the GP."

Scenario 3: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "Can I have a hay fever spray? My left nostril has been completely blocked for a month."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help. Is it just the one side that's blocked?" (W)
  • Patient: "Yes, the right one is completely fine."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. Because the symptoms are only on one side and have been there for a while, it's really important that a doctor takes a look to see what's causing it. An over-the-counter medicine isn't the right approach here. You should make an appointment with your GP this week to get it checked out. Does that make sense?"
