Warts and Verrucas in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Warts and verrucas are a common reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy. They are benign growths caused by the Human Papillomavirus (HPV) and are most common in children and young adults. A verruca is simply a wart on the sole of the foot.

Patients typically present with single or multiple firm, rough papules. Their appearance varies by type:

  • Common warts (verruca vulgaris): Raised, cauliflower-like lesions, typically on the hands and knees.
  • Plantar warts (verrucas): Flattened due to pressure, can be painful. Paring the surface reveals tiny black dots (thrombosed capillaries), which is a key sign to look for.
  • Plane warts: Smooth, flat-topped, skin-coloured papules, usually seen in multiples on the face and hands.
  • Filiform warts: Long, thin growths, common on the face and neck.

The condition is contagious, spread by direct contact or contaminated surfaces like swimming pool floors. The natural history is favourable, with most resolving spontaneously without treatment, particularly in children. However, they can persist for months or even years, which often prompts the request for treatment.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the pharmacy team is to identify features suggesting a more serious condition. The presence of any red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP.

  • Suspicion of malignancy: Refer urgently if the lesion is growing quickly, bleeding, ulcerated, painful, or has an irregular border or colour. This is particularly important in older adults or on sun-exposed skin.
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: If the lesion does not look like a typical wart and you cannot confidently rule out a more serious cause, referral is the safest option.
  • Anogenital warts: Always refer patients with warts in the genital or anal area to a GP or a sexual health (GUM) clinic. Do not attempt to treat these in the pharmacy.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Patients on immunosuppressant therapy (e.g., for an organ transplant) or with conditions like HIV should be referred to their GP, as they have a higher risk of complications.
  • Significant pain or disability: Refer if a verruca is causing severe pain or affecting mobility, especially if simple measures have failed.
  • Diabetes or poor circulation: Patients with diabetes or peripheral vascular disease should be referred to their GP or a podiatrist before attempting to self-treat any foot lesion.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique like WWHAM is essential to ensure a safe and appropriate recommendation in the pharmacy.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age and identity. Many OTC wart treatments have age restrictions. It is crucial to identify patients who should not self-treat, such as those with diabetes, poor circulation, or immune system disorders.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the diagnosis. Ask what it looks like, where it is, and if it has black dots. Screen for red flag symptoms like recent changes, rapid growth, bleeding, or ulceration. Differentiate between a painful verruca and a corn.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: A long-standing, stable lesion is reassuring. A new lesion that has appeared and grown rapidly is a red flag and requires referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what the patient has tried (e.g., other OTC products, duct tape). This avoids duplicating therapy and helps understand why previous treatments may have failed (e.g., inconsistent use).
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify any relevant comorbidities or prescribed medicines. Specifically check for immunosuppressant drugs, diabetes medication, and anticoagulants or other drugs that affect circulation or healing.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of differential diagnoses is key for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a GP referral is necessary.

Common Benign Differentials

  • Corns and Calluses: Hyperkeratotic areas caused by pressure. Unlike verrucas, they do not have black dots, and the skin lines continue over them. They are typically painful on direct pressure.
  • Seborrhoeic Keratosis: Common in older adults, these have a benign "stuck-on" appearance and can be mistaken for warts.
  • Molluscum Contagiosum: Small, pearly papules, often with a central dimple, caused by a different virus. Common in children.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC): Can appear as a persistent, crusty, or ulcerated nodule. If a "wart" in an older adult is growing or bleeding, suspect SCC and refer urgently.
  • Amelanotic Melanoma: A rare but important differential. A new, changing, or unusual pink or red nodule requires urgent referral.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is resolution of the wart, but it requires patience and persistence. Self-care advice is as important as the product choice.

First-Line Advice (All Warts)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This can be an active choice. Advise on the high chance of spontaneous resolution. To prevent spread, advise wearing flip-flops in communal areas, not sharing towels, and covering the wart with a plaster. Duct tape occlusion can be an effective, low-risk option.

Primary OTC Treatment

  • Keratolytics (e.g., Salicylic Acid):
    • Mechanism: Salicylic acid is a keratolytic agent that works by breaking down the keratin protein that makes up the wart and the thick layer of dead skin on top of it.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL or P. Available as paints, gels, and medicated plasters. Check product for specific age licence.
    • Counselling: Advise daily application for up to 12 weeks. The key to success is preparation: soak the area, file down the dead skin with an emery board, apply the product to the wart only, and protect surrounding skin with petroleum jelly. [Rx info]

Other OTC Options

  • Freezing Sprays (e.g., Dimethyl Ether Propane):
    • Mechanism: Works by freezing the wart tissue, causing cryonecrosis. It does not reach the same low temperatures as the liquid nitrogen used by GPs.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Not suitable for all ages or for use in diabetes.
    • Counselling: Explain that it can be painful and may cause a blister. Its efficacy is generally considered to be lower than for salicylic acid. Multiple applications may be needed. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not supply these treatments (unless they are prescribers), awareness is vital for managing patient expectations after a referral.

  • Cryotherapy with Liquid Nitrogen:
    Context: This is a common treatment performed by GPs or practice nurses. It is significantly colder and often more effective than OTC freezing sprays. Knowing this allows you to explain to patients why a GP visit might be beneficial if OTC options fail. [Rx advice]
  • Stronger Keratolytics or other topical agents:
    Context: GPs or dermatologists can prescribe products with higher concentrations of salicylic acid or other agents if standard treatments are unsuccessful.
  • Curettage and Cautery:
    Context: For very stubborn warts, a specialist may physically scrape the wart away (curettage) and use heat to stop bleeding (cautery). This is a hospital-based procedure. Awareness helps in explaining the full patient pathway. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain why a referral is the appropriate next step and what might happen at the GP surgery, positioning the pharmacy as a knowledgeable and integrated part of the healthcare system.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for warts and verrucas.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that treatment is a slow process. A key phrase is: "This isn't a quick fix. You need to be patient and use the treatment every single day for it to work, and it can take up to three months." Also stress the high chance of them disappearing on their own.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame the preparation process (soaking and filing) as the most important part of the treatment. Sell it as a package: "The paint will work much better if you use an emery board first."
  • Counselling on Application: Demonstrate how to protect healthy skin using petroleum jelly. Explain that mild irritation is a sign the product is working, but to stop if the skin becomes very sore.
  • Explain Product Differences: If asked, explain that OTC freeze sprays are not as strong as the liquid nitrogen used by the doctor, which helps manage expectations.
  • Check Understanding: After explaining, check for understanding: "So, just to be sure, how many months should you try this for before we consider other options?"
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a person on when to seek further help. This ensures they know when to act if the lesion changes or fails to improve.

Advise the person to consult their GP if:

  • The lesion starts to look different, especially if it grows quickly, changes colour, bleeds, itches, or becomes painful.
  • The wart or verruca has not resolved after 3 months of continuous, correct use of an OTC treatment.
  • The surrounding skin becomes very red, sore, or shows signs of infection (e.g., pus).
  • They have diabetes or poor circulation and develop any new problem with their feet.

Advise the person to seek urgent medical advice if they notice any significant red flag symptoms as previously discussed.

Finally, encourage them to return to the pharmacy if they have any questions about the treatment or if they are unsure whether it is working.

9. Pathophysiology

Warts are caused by infection of the skin's top layer (epidermis) by the Human Papillomavirus (HPV). The virus enters through tiny cuts or scratches.

Once inside, HPV causes the host's skin cells (keratinocytes) to multiply rapidly, creating the rough, thick growth that is characteristic of a wart. The virus is adept at hiding from the body's immune system, which is why warts can persist for a long time.

The small black dots often visible in verrucas are not "roots", but rather tiny blood vessels (capillaries) that have grown into the wart and become thrombosed (clotted). Eventually, the body's immune system recognises the virus and clears it, leading to spontaneous resolution of the wart. Treatments aim to destroy the abnormal skin cells and stimulate a local immune response.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks.

Salicylic Acid Preparations

  • Cautions: Do not sell for use on the face, neck, anogenital areas, moles, or birthmarks. Products should not be sold to patients with diabetes or poor blood circulation without medical advice, due to the risk of creating a non-healing ulcer.
  • Side Effects: Can cause local skin irritation. Advise the user to apply carefully and protect the surrounding skin.

OTC Freezing Sprays

  • Cautions: Not suitable for young children (check product for specific age). Not for use by people with diabetes or circulatory problems. Can be very painful, so warn users before they purchase.
  • Side Effects: Pain, blistering (which can be large), and skin pigment changes (lighter or darker patches) can occur after use. These are more common with improper application.
11. Use in special populations

Children

The "wait and see" approach is often best as spontaneous resolution is very likely. If treatment is requested, a salicylic acid product suitable for their age is the preferred OTC choice. Advise parents that treatment requires their dedication and can take a long time.

Older adults

Maintain a high index of suspicion for skin cancer. Any unusual or changing "wart" in this age group warrants an immediate GP referral. Be cautious about selling treatments for use on the lower legs where skin may be fragile and healing can be poor.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care and non-drug options like duct tape should be encouraged first. While cautious, short-term use of salicylic acid on a small, single wart is generally considered low-risk, it is best to advise referral to a GP for confirmation before starting treatment. OTC freezing sprays should generally be avoided.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, or who are immunocompromised, the correct advice is always to refer to their GP or podiatrist. Do not sell wart treatments to these patients for self-management, especially for lesions on the feet.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Verruca & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, have you got anything for a verruca? I think my daughter has one."
  • Pharmacist: "Certainly. So I can help, who is it for?"
  • Patient: "My daughter, she's 10."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. And what makes you think it's a verruca?"
  • Patient: "It's on the bottom of her foot, it's flat and has little black dots in it. She says it hurts a bit when she walks on it. She's had it for a few months."
  • Pharmacist: "Has she tried anything for it yet, and is she on any regular medication or have any health problems?"
  • Patient: "No, we haven't tried anything, and she's fit and well."
  • Recommendation: "That does sound exactly like a verruca. The best evidence is for products containing salicylic acid. It's important to know that it can take up to 3 months of daily use to work. You'll need to soak her foot, gently file the top of the verruca, and then apply the paint carefully just to the verruca itself. If it's not gone after 3 months, or it starts to look very sore, pop back or see the GP."

Scenario 2: Urgent Referral for a 'Wart'

  • Patient: "I need some of that freezing stuff for a wart on my husband's arm."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Can I just ask a few questions to make sure that's the right thing? Who is the patient?"
  • Patient: "My husband, he's 72."
  • Pharmacist: "And what does this 'wart' look like? How long has he had it?"
  • Patient: "Well, he's had it a while, maybe 6 months. But it seems to have gotten bigger in the last few weeks and it bled a little bit yesterday when he caught it on his shirt."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for that information. Because it has changed recently and started bleeding, it's really important that a doctor looks at it. An over-the-counter treatment isn't appropriate when a skin lesion changes like that. He needs to make an urgent appointment to see his GP this week to get it checked properly. Please tell the receptionist it's a skin lesion that has grown and bled so they know it's important."
