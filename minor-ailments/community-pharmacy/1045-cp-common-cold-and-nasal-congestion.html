Common Cold (Nasal Congestion) in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

The common cold is one of the most frequent reasons for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, particularly in autumn and winter. It is an acute, self-limiting viral infection of the upper respiratory tract.

Patients typically present with a combination of symptoms including a blocked or runny nose (rhinorrhoea), sneezing, sore throat, and often a mild cough. The nasal discharge may initially be clear, becoming thicker and yellow or green after a few days; this change reflects inflammatory cell influx and does not reliably indicate bacterial infection. A low-grade fever and general malaise are common. Symptoms usually peak within 2-3 days and resolve within 7-10 days, although a residual cough may persist longer.

Common triggers are numerous viral pathogens, most commonly Rhinoviruses. The condition is benign and typically resolves without medical intervention. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations regarding the condition's natural duration.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or complication. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Systemic signs of severe illness: Such as rigors, persistent high fever (>38°C), marked breathlessness, or chest pain. This may indicate pneumonia or sepsis.
  • Signs of meningitis: Stiff neck, photophobia, non-blanching rash, or new-onset confusion require emergency assessment.
  • Unilateral facial pain and swelling: Severe, localised pain over the sinuses, particularly if associated with facial swelling, fever, and purulent discharge, may suggest severe acute bacterial sinusitis or a complication like orbital cellulitis.
  • Severe sore throat with stridor or drooling: May indicate a deep neck space infection or epiglottitis (rare in adults due to Hib vaccination), which are airway emergencies.
  • Marked shortness of breath: A respiratory rate >30 breaths/min, inability to complete sentences, or cyanosis suggests significant respiratory compromise (e.g., pneumonia, acute exacerbation of asthma/COPD).
  • Symptoms in an at-risk individual: Immunocompromised patients, the very elderly, or those with significant cardiorespiratory comorbidity who appear systemically unwell require a low threshold for concern and referral.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the age of the patient, as many OTC cold and flu products have specific age restrictions. This also establishes if the patient is present or if it's a third-party consultation, which requires extra care.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the nature of the main complaint (e.g., blocked nose, runny nose, sinus pressure) and identify any associated symptoms (fever, aches, cough) or red flags (chest pain, breathlessness). This helps tailor the product recommendation and ensure it is appropriate.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To confirm the condition is acute. Symptoms lasting more than 10-14 days without improvement may suggest a secondary infection (like sinusitis) or an alternative diagnosis (like allergic rhinitis) and warrant a referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what treatments or self-care measures the patient has already tried. This avoids recommending a product that has already failed and helps understand the progression of the illness. It is also a key safety check for products containing paracetamol or ibuprofen.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for potential drug interactions with OTC products (e.g., decongestants and beta-blockers or MAOIs) and to identify comorbidities (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, asthma) where certain products are contraindicated or require caution.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are self-limiting viral infections.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Common Cold (Viral URTI): The most common cause. Characterised by a mix of nasal symptoms, sneezing, sore throat, and mild malaise over 7-10 days.
  • Influenza: Typically marked by a more abrupt onset with high fever, significant myalgia, headache, and prostration.
  • COVID-19: Presentation is highly variable but can be indistinguishable from a common cold.

Other Common Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Acute Bacterial Sinusitis: Suspect if symptoms last >10 days with no improvement, or worsen after initial improvement. Features include purulent discharge, severe localised facial pain (often unilateral), and fever.
  • Allergic Rhinitis: Often has a clear temporal pattern (e.g., seasonal). Associated with prominent itching of the nose and eyes, and sneezing.

Serious Differentials to Exclude

  • Pneumonia: Suspect if symptoms include high fever, productive cough, breathlessness, and pleuritic pain.
  • Meningitis: Suspect if symptoms include headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, and altered mental state.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief. Non-pharmacological advice should always be provided alongside any product recommendation.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise adequate rest and hydration. Steam inhalation can be soothing.
  • Simple Analgesia (e.g., Paracetamol, Ibuprofen):
    • Mechanism: Provide relief from associated symptoms like headache, sore throat, and muscle aches.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Check product-specific age licensing.
    • Counselling: Crucial to check for hidden paracetamol/ibuprofen in any other "all-in-one" remedies being used to prevent overdose. [Rx info]
  • Saline Preparations (e.g., Sprays, Drops):
    • Mechanism: Help to thin and clear mucus, and moisturise nasal passages.
    • Licensing: GSL. Suitable for all patient groups, including infants and pregnant people.
    • Counselling: An excellent and safe first-line choice for congestion. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options (for Nasal Congestion)

  • Decongestants - Topical (e.g., Xylometazoline):
    • Mechanism: Act locally as vasoconstrictors to reduce nasal swelling.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age restrictions apply (typically not for under 6s).
    • Counselling: Highly effective but MUST be limited to 5-7 days' use to avoid rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa). [Rx info]
  • Decongestants - Oral (e.g., Pseudoephedrine):
    • Mechanism: Act systemically as vasoconstrictors.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age restrictions apply.
    • Counselling: Check for contraindications like hypertension, heart disease, and relevant interacting medicines. Advise on MHRA alerts regarding rare risks of PRES/RCVS. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists (without an independent prescribing qualification) do not prescribe these for a common cold, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Antibiotics:
    Context: These are not effective for viral colds. A GP will only prescribe them if a secondary bacterial infection, such as severe acute sinusitis or pneumonia, is suspected. Pharmacists play a key role in upholding antimicrobial stewardship by explaining this to patients. Under a service like Pharmacy First, pharmacists may be able to prescribe antibiotics for confirmed sinusitis, but not for an uncomplicated cold. [Rx advice]
  • Intranasal Corticosteroids:
    Context: A GP may prescribe these if there is significant persistent inflammation or to treat an underlying condition like allergic rhinitis which may have been exacerbated by a viral infection. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral might be needed if symptoms persist, and what treatments a doctor might consider for complications or alternative diagnoses.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for the common cold.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the natural, self-limiting course of a viral cold. A key phrase is: "It's normal for cold symptoms to last for about a week to ten days, so don't be alarmed if it doesn't clear up in a day or two."
  • Justify No Antibiotics: When a patient requests antibiotics, provide a clear, confident explanation. "This cold is caused by a virus, and antibiotics only work against bacteria. They won't help you get better any faster but could cause side effects."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as an active treatment. Recommend rest, plenty of fluids, and using saline nasal spray to help with congestion.
  • OTC Product Counselling: Explain what the recommended product does (e.g., "This nasal spray will help unblock your nose quickly, but it's important you don't use it for more than seven days"). Highlight key warnings and check for understanding.
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've explained that clearly, what symptoms would make you decide to see a doctor?" can reinforce safety netting.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a person when they should seek further help. This ensures that someone with a self-limiting illness knows when to act if their condition deviates from the normal course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the person to consult their GP if:

  • Symptoms show no improvement after 10-14 days.
  • They start to feel much more unwell after an initial period of improvement ("double-sickening").
  • They develop severe earache, or discharge from the ear.
  • They have a persistent fever that isn't resolving.

Advise the person to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Significant shortness of breath or difficulty breathing.
  • Sharp chest pain.
  • Severe headache with a stiff neck or dislike of bright lights.
  • New and unexplained confusion.
  • Severe, one-sided facial swelling.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

The common cold is an acute viral infection of the upper respiratory tract epithelium, primarily affecting the nose and nasopharynx. Rhinoviruses are responsible for 30-50% of cases, with other pathogens including coronaviruses, influenza viruses, and adenoviruses.

The virus invades the mucosal cells, leading to direct cell damage and triggering a local inflammatory response. Inflammatory mediators such as bradykinin and prostaglandins are released. This leads to vasodilation of blood vessels in the nasal submucosa and increased vascular permeability, resulting in oedema (nasal congestion) and transudation of fluid (rhinorrhoea). There is also stimulation of mucous glands, further contributing to the nasal discharge.

Sneezing is a reflex stimulated by histamine release from mast cells. A sore throat is caused by inflammation of the pharyngeal mucosa. Systemic symptoms like malaise and low-grade fever are mediated by cytokines (e.g., interleukins, TNF-alpha) that are released as part of the host's innate immune response to the viral infection. The change in nasal discharge colour from clear to yellow/green is primarily due to the influx of neutrophils, and is not in itself a sign of bacterial superinfection.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and interactions.

Oral Decongestants (e.g., Pseudoephedrine, Phenylephrine)

  • Interactions & Cautions: Contraindicated in patients with severe hypertension, heart disease, or those taking MAOIs. Use with caution in diabetes, hyperthyroidism, and benign prostatic hyperplasia.
  • MHRA Safety Alerts: Be aware of the small risk of PRES and RCVS. Advise patients to stop treatment and see a doctor if they develop a sudden severe headache, nausea, vomiting or seizures.

Topical Decongestants (e.g., Xylometazoline)

  • Rebound Congestion: The most significant clinical issue is rhinitis medicamentosa. It is vital to counsel every patient on the need to limit use to a maximum of seven days.

Multi-Ingredient Products ("All-in-One")

  • Hidden Ingredients: Many "all-in-one" cold and flu remedies contain paracetamol or ibuprofen. It is essential to counsel patients not to take additional separate doses of these analgesics to avoid accidental overdose. Always ask "what have you taken for this already?".
  • Unnecessary Ingredients: Counsel patients to choose products that only treat the symptoms they actually have, rather than taking a multi-ingredient product with components they do not need.
11. Use in special populations

Children

OTC cold medicines containing decongestants are not for use in children under 6. For children 6-12, they should be used with caution on pharmacist advice only. The first-line recommendation for all children is self-care and saline nasal drops/spray to relieve congestion. Simple analgesics (paracetamol/ibuprofen) can be recommended for fever and pain, checking age-appropriate formulations.

Older adults

Avoid recommending oral decongestants where possible due to the high prevalence of cardiovascular comorbidities and risk of urinary retention in men. A referral to the GP should be considered sooner in this group if symptoms are severe or not resolving. Saline nasal spray is a safe choice.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with rest, fluids, and steam inhalation is first-line. Saline nasal spray is the safest first-line product to recommend for congestion. Paracetamol is the analgesic of choice. Oral and topical decongestants are generally not recommended. The pharmacy's role is to advise on safe self-care and refer to the GP if symptoms are severe.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients with hypertension or heart disease, oral decongestants are contraindicated. For patients with diabetes, recommend sugar-free formulations where possible. For patients with asthma, a cold can be a trigger, so ensure they know what to do if their asthma symptoms worsen.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Cold & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I'm just after something for this blocked nose. I can't breathe."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "Of course. To get you the right thing, can I ask who it's for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 42."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "Thanks. How long has it been blocked for?"
  • Patient: "About two days. It started with a sore throat, now it's just this."
  • Pharmacy Staff: "Okay, and have you tried anything yet, or are you taking any other medication?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet. I take a blue inhaler for asthma when I need it, but that's all."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a typical cold. A saline nasal spray is a good, safe option to help clear the congestion. If you want something stronger, a decongestant nasal spray like xylometazoline works very well. You just need to make sure you don't use it for more than a week. It won't affect your asthma. Remember to drink plenty of fluids. If you're not feeling better in a week or so, or feel worse, pop back in or see your GP."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "Can I get some advice? I've had a cold for over a week, and I feel dreadful."
  • Pharmacist: "Let's see if we can help. What are your symptoms now?"
  • Patient: "Well the cold was getting better, but yesterday this pain started in my face, just here [points to cheek]. It's throbbing, and my nose is pouring out green stuff. I feel hot and shivery too."
  • Pharmacist: "And are you taking any other medicines or have any medical conditions?"
  • Patient: "No, I'm generally healthy."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you. Based on what you've said – particularly the one-sided facial pain and feeling feverish after starting to get better – it suggests you might have a sinus infection that needs more than an over-the-counter medicine. This needs a proper assessment. You should contact your GP surgery today and explain these symptoms. Under the new Pharmacy First service, I may be able to assess and treat this, would you like me to check if you're eligible?"
