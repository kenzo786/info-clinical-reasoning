Constipation in Community Pharmacy
Context: Constipation in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Constipation is a common reason for a person to seek advice in a community pharmacy, affecting individuals of all ages. It is not a disease in itself but a symptom. Presentation varies, but is typically defined by infrequent bowel movements (fewer than three per week) and difficulty passing stools. Patients may use the Bristol Stool Chart to describe their stools as lumpy or hard (types 1-2) and complain of straining, a sensation of incomplete evacuation, or bloating.

Patients often report associated symptoms like abdominal bloating, cramping, and general discomfort. While for most it is a functional issue, it can cause significant distress and impact quality of life. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to exclude serious pathology through effective questioning, provide advice on self-care, recommend appropriate OTC treatments, and manage patient expectations regarding treatment response.

Common triggers include a low-fibre diet, inadequate fluid intake, reduced physical activity, and certain medications (e.g., opioids, anticholinergics, iron supplements). In many cases, constipation is self-limiting and responds well to lifestyle advice and simple laxatives.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Persistent, unexplained rectal bleeding: Any bleeding not clearly attributable to benign anal causes like fissures is a red flag for colorectal cancer.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Significant, unintentional weight loss alongside a change in bowel habit is a key warning sign for malignancy.
  • New onset in older adults: A persistent change in bowel habit to constipation lasting more than 6 weeks in a person aged over 50 years warrants investigation for colorectal cancer.
  • Palpable abdominal or rectal mass: While a rectal exam is not performed in a pharmacy, a patient reporting a lump in their abdomen requires urgent referral.
  • Unexplained iron-deficiency anaemia: A patient mentioning this diagnosis alongside constipation requires referral as it can be a sign of occult gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Severe, persistent pain with vomiting and bloating: These features may indicate intestinal obstruction, which is a surgical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission.
  • Failure to respond to treatment: Persistent constipation that does not respond to multiple OTC laxatives used correctly may require GP investigation.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age, as many laxatives have specific age restrictions. This also establishes if the consultation is for a third party, which requires additional caution.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To understand the specific symptoms. Use the Bristol Stool Chart concept ("Are the stools hard and pellet-like?"). Ask about straining, bloating, or pain. Crucially, screen for red flag symptoms like blood in the stool, weight loss, or severe pain.
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To differentiate between recent onset and chronic constipation. A persistent change in bowel habit for over 6 weeks, especially in someone over 50, is a red flag and requires mandatory GP referral.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To discover what self-care (diet, fluids) or treatments the patient has already tried. This avoids product duplication and helps assess the severity and treatment-resistance of the constipation.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for medicines known to cause constipation (e.g., codeine, amitriptyline, iron supplements). It also identifies comorbidities and potential interactions (though laxative interactions are uncommon).
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage and identifying when a referral is necessary. Most cases presenting in the pharmacy are functional or lifestyle-related.

Common Self-Limiting Causes

  • Functional Constipation: The most frequent cause, related to low dietary fibre, poor fluid intake, and a sedentary lifestyle.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C): Suspect if abdominal pain is a key feature and is relieved by defecation. This is a GP diagnosis but can be managed long-term with pharmacy support.
  • Drug-Induced Constipation: A very common cause in pharmacy. A link between starting a new medicine and symptom onset is the key clue.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Colorectal Cancer: The primary concern when red flag symptoms like weight loss, rectal bleeding, or new onset in an older adult are present.
  • Hypothyroidism: Consider referral if constipation is accompanied by fatigue, weight gain, and dry skin.
  • Bowel Obstruction: An emergency referral is needed for severe abdominal pain, vomiting, and absolute constipation (no wind or stool).
  • Faecal Impaction: Suspect in the elderly or immobile, especially if there is overflow diarrhoea and confusion. This requires referral for assessment.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is to achieve a soft, well-formed stool (Bristol Stool Chart type 4) without straining. Lifestyle advice should underpin any product recommendation.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise a gradual increase in fibre, adequate fluid intake (1.5-2L/day), regular exercise, and establishing a good toilet routine.

Stepwise Laxative Options

  • Bulk-forming laxatives (e.g., Ispaghula husk):
    • Mechanism: Increase faecal mass and retain water, stimulating peristalsis.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL. Suitable for adults and children over 6 years (check product-specific license).
    • Counselling: First-line treatment choice. Advise that they can take 2-3 days to work. Must be taken with plenty of water to avoid obstruction. [Rx info]
  • Osmotic laxatives (e.g., Macrogols, Lactulose):
    • Mechanism: Draw water into the bowel via osmosis, softening hard stools.
    • Licensing: Macrogols and lactulose are available as GSL or P medicines. Age licenses vary, so check individual products.
    • Counselling: A good first-line choice if stools are particularly hard, or second-line if a bulk-forming laxative is insufficient. Macrogols tend to cause less bloating than lactulose. [Rx info]
  • Stimulant laxatives (e.g., Senna, Bisacodyl):
    • Mechanism: Directly stimulate the nerves in the colon to increase motility.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Not for use in children under 12 without medical advice.
    • Counselling: For short-term use only (no more than one week). Useful for predictable, overnight relief. Can cause abdominal cramps. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While community pharmacists do not initiate these medicines (unless qualified as a prescriber), awareness is vital for counselling patients on the next steps after a GP referral and managing expectations.

  • Prucalopride:
    Context: This is a specialist drug prescribed by GPs for chronic constipation when at least two different types of laxatives from different classes have failed. It works by stimulating gut motility. Knowing this helps explain to a patient why a GP may want to try optimising standard laxatives first. [Rx advice]
  • Linaclotide:
    Context: This is another specialist option, licensed for moderate-to-severe Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C). A patient might mention this if they have been seen by a gastroenterologist. [Rx advice]
  • Opioid-induced constipation treatments:
    Context: Patients on strong, long-term opioids who have failed on standard laxatives (osmotic + stimulant) may be prescribed drugs like naloxegol by their GP. Pharmacists can support by ensuring new opioid prescriptions are appropriately co-prescribed with preventative laxatives. [Rx advice]
7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for constipation.

Key Counselling Points

  • Explain the Stepwise Approach: Let the patient know that management is a ladder. "Let's start with lifestyle changes and this first type of laxative. If that's not enough after a week or so, come back and we can try adding a different type."
  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain that laxatives are not instant. "A bulk-forming laxative like this one can take 2 or 3 days to start working properly." This prevents disappointment and incorrect use.
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame lifestyle advice as the foundation of treatment, not an afterthought. Use the Bristol Stool Chart to help them understand the goal (a type 4 stool).
  • Explain Product Use: Give specific instructions. "It's really important to drink a large glass of water with this sachet, otherwise it won't work correctly." Or for stimulants, "Take this at bedtime, and it should work by morning."
  • Justify Referral: If a referral is necessary, explain why clearly and calmly. "Because the constipation has started recently and you're over 50, the national guidelines recommend that you see your GP to get checked out properly."
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient when they should seek further help. This ensures that someone treating a simple case of constipation knows when to act if their condition is not improving or might be serious.

Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The constipation does not improve after 1-2 weeks of correct laxative use and lifestyle changes.
  • They are needing to use stimulant laxatives for more than a week without medical advice.
  • They develop any other symptoms that are concerning them.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (GP or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Passing any blood in the stool (not from a known, minor fissure).
  • Developing severe abdominal pain, swelling, or vomiting.
  • Unexplained weight loss.
  • Feeling systemically unwell with a fever.

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns about their treatment.

9. Pathophysiology

Constipation results from stool moving too slowly through the colon (slow-transit) or from an inability to effectively evacuate stool from the rectum (defecatory disorder).

The colon's main function is to absorb water from digested food to form stool. The transit of contents is controlled by enteric nerves and colonic muscle contractions. If transit time is prolonged (slow-transit constipation), excessive water is absorbed, leading to hard, dry stools that are difficult to pass. This can be idiopathic or secondary to conditions like hypothyroidism or drugs like opioids that reduce gut motility.

Defecatory disorders involve a functional problem with the pelvic floor or anal sphincter. In pelvic floor dyssynergia, the muscles do not coordinate correctly during attempted defecation, creating a functional outlet obstruction. This leads to symptoms of excessive straining and a feeling of incomplete evacuation.

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and correct patient selection.

Bulk-forming Laxatives

  • Cautions: Should be avoided in patients with swallowing difficulties or those who are immobile with poor fluid intake, as this increases the risk of oesophageal or intestinal obstruction. Always stress the need for adequate hydration.

Stimulant Laxatives

  • Side Effects & Risks: Can cause predictable abdominal cramps. Should not be used in suspected bowel obstruction. Reinforce that they are for short-term use only to avoid colonic atony.

Osmotic Laxatives

  • Lactulose: May cause significant flatulence and bloating. Use with caution in patients who are lactose intolerant.
  • Macrogols: Some formulations contain significant amounts of potassium or sodium and should be used with caution in patients with renal impairment or those on electrolyte-modifying drugs, although risk is low with short-term use.

Sugar-free Formulations

  • Many linctuses and liquid laxatives contain sugar. For patients with diabetes, it is important to check for and recommend sugar-free versions where available.
11. Use in special populations

Children

First-line advice is dietary change and behavioural support. An osmotic laxative (macrogols are licensed OTC from 2 years, lactulose from 1 month) is the pharmacological treatment of choice. Stimulants should be avoided unless on medical advice. Chronic constipation (>8 weeks) or faecal impaction requires GP assessment.

Older adults

Polypharmacy is a major cause, so an MUR or NMS can be valuable. Faecal impaction risk is high; be vigilant for symptoms like confusion or overflow diarrhoea. Osmotic laxatives like macrogols are often a good choice. Avoid bulk-forming laxatives in frail, bedbound individuals with low fluid intake.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Self-care with diet and fluids is first-line. If medication is needed, bulk-forming and osmotic laxatives (lactulose, macrogols) are safe to recommend as they are not absorbed systemically. Stimulant laxatives should generally be avoided.

People with significant comorbidities

For patients starting opioid painkillers, advise them to discuss preventative laxatives with their GP, or recommend they buy a stimulant and osmotic laxative to have ready. For patients with diabetes, recommend sugar-free formulations of liquid laxatives. For patients with renal impairment, caution is needed with long-term use of macrogols containing electrolytes.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Opioid-Induced Constipation & Product Sale

  • Patient: "Hi, I've just picked up my prescription for co-codamol and the GP said I should get a laxative."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. That's very sensible advice. Who is the prescription for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me, I'm 45."
  • Pharmacist: "And have you become constipated yet, or is this to prevent it?"
  • Patient: "It's already started. I've been straining and my stools are like hard little pebbles."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Have you tried anything yet?"
  • Patient: "No, not yet."
  • Pharmacist: "And do you take any other medication?"
  • Patient: "Just ramipril for my blood pressure."
  • Recommendation: "Because codeine slows your bowel down, you usually need two types of laxative to get things moving. I'd suggest a stool softener like a macrogol, which draws water in to make things softer, and a stimulant like senna to give your bowel a push. You take the macrogol sachet during the day and the senna at night. Remember to drink plenty of fluids. If things don't improve in a week, let your GP know."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "Hello, my husband has sent me in for some laxatives. He's 71."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay, I can help with that. What symptoms has he been having?"
  • Patient: "He's just been really bunged up for the last couple of months, it's not like him at all. He says his clothes feel a bit loose too."
  • Pharmacist: "Thank you for sharing that. Has he tried any laxatives already?"
  • Patient: "No, he's a bit stubborn."
  • Pharmacist: "And does he take any other medication?"
  • Patient: "Just his statin."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Because this is a new problem for him, it's been going on for a couple of months, and you've mentioned he's lost some weight, the national health guidelines say he must see his GP for a check-up before trying any treatments. It's really important that he gets properly assessed. Please ask him to make an appointment with his GP as soon as possible and tell them those exact symptoms."
