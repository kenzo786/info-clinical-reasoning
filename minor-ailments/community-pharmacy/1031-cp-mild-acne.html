Mild Acne in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Mild acne (acne vulgaris) is an extremely common chronic inflammatory skin condition, and a frequent reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy. Patients typically present with a combination of non-inflammatory lesions (open and closed comedones, i.e., blackheads and whiteheads) and a small number of inflammatory lesions (papules and pustules). In mild acne, there should be no nodules or cysts. The condition most frequently affects areas with a high density of sebaceous follicles, such as the face, neck, upper chest, back, and shoulders.

The onset is most common during puberty, coinciding with hormonal changes, but it can persist into or even start in adulthood. While often considered a cosmetic issue, it can cause significant psychological distress. The primary role of the pharmacy team is to correctly identify mild acne, differentiate it from conditions requiring referral, recommend appropriate self-care and over-the-counter (OTC) treatments, and manage patient expectations about treatment timelines.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause or a more severe form of acne that requires management by a GP or dermatologist. The presence of red flags warrants urgent referral.

  • Severe or nodulocystic acne: The presence of numerous, large, painful, deep nodules or cysts, or any evidence of scarring, requires mandatory GP referral for prescription treatment.
  • Acne fulminans: A rare but serious presentation with an abrupt onset of inflamed, ulcerative nodules accompanied by systemic symptoms like fever, joint pain, or feeling unwell. This requires immediate A&E referral.
  • Signs of hyperandrogenism: In female patients, acne presenting with features like excessive facial/body hair (hirsutism), hair thinning on the scalp, or a deepening voice, suggests an underlying hormonal condition like PCOS and requires GP investigation.
  • Significant psychological distress: If the patient expresses feelings of severe low mood, social isolation, body dysmorphic disorder, or suicidal thoughts related to their acne, they require an urgent GP appointment.
  • Treatment failure: A patient who has already tried appropriate first-line prescription treatments from their GP without success should be referred back for a medication review and potential step-up in therapy.
  • Atypical presentation: A sudden onset of uniform-looking (monomorphic) spots could indicate drug-induced acne or folliculitis, warranting a GP review.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique like WWHAM is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation for acne in the pharmacy.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age, as product licensing for acne treatments varies. It establishes if the patient is present, and if not, highlights the limitations of a third-party consultation. It also prompts consideration of special populations (e.g., pregnancy).
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To determine the type of lesions (comedones, papules, pustules) and confirm it is mild acne. Ask about skin sensitivity. Crucially, this is the stage to screen for the psychological impact of the condition and ask about any red flag symptoms (e.g., pain, cysts, scarring).
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To understand if this is a new or persistent problem. If a patient has been using a treatment, it helps to establish if it has been used for long enough (e.g., 8-12 weeks) to be effective.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out which OTC or prescribed products the patient has already tried and for how long. This prevents recommending a product that has already failed and helps to understand treatment adherence and expectations.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To check for any prescribed medicines that could be causing drug-induced acne (e.g., systemic steroids). This is also a critical safety check for pregnancy or breastfeeding, which would dictate which, if any, product can be recommended.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral to the GP is necessary. Most presentations will be acne, but mimics must be ruled out.

Common Differentials for Referral

  • Rosacea: A key differential, especially in adults over 30. The main distinguishing feature is the complete absence of comedones (blackheads/whiteheads). Rosacea typically presents with persistent facial redness (erythema), flushing, and telangiectasia.
  • Perioral/Periorificial Dermatitis: Presents as clusters of small red papules around the mouth, nose or eyes, characteristically sparing the skin border directly next to the lips. Comedones are absent.
  • Bacterial or Malassezia (Pityrosporum) Folliculitis: An inflammation of hair follicles, presenting as uniform, often itchy, papules and pustules. It lacks the variety of lesion types (comedones, papules) seen in acne.

Less Common Differentials to Consider

  • Drug-Induced Acne: Consider this if there is a sudden onset of monomorphic inflammatory papules. Check if the patient is taking medications like corticosteroids, anabolic steroids, or lithium.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The goal of OTC treatment is to manage mild acne symptoms. Self-care advice is the foundation of any recommendation.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Self-Care: Advise on a consistent skincare routine. This includes using a gentle, non-comedogenic cleanser twice daily and a suitable oil-free moisturiser. Over-washing or using harsh scrubs should be avoided. Crucially, advise the patient not to pick or squeeze spots to prevent scarring.

OTC Product Recommendations

  • Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO):
    • Mechanism: An effective antimicrobial agent against C. acnes which also helps to unblock pores (comedolytic). It is the gold-standard OTC ingredient.
    • Licensing: Available as P or GSL in various strengths (e.g., 2.5%, 5%, 10%). Age restrictions apply (typically for use in adolescents and adults).
    • Counselling: Warn that it can bleach hair and fabrics. Advise starting with a low strength on alternate days to build tolerance. Common side effects are dryness and irritation. [Rx info]
  • Salicylic Acid:
    • Mechanism: A beta-hydroxy acid that acts primarily by exfoliating the skin and unblocking pores (comedolytic).
    • Licensing: GSL. Commonly found in cleansers, washes, and spot treatments.
    • Counselling: Generally less effective than BPO for inflammatory lesions but can be useful for comedonal acne. Can cause mild skin irritation. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While not supplied OTC, awareness of prescription treatments is vital for community pharmacy teams to manage patient expectations, explain the rationale for a GP referral, and counsel patients on their prescribed medicines.

  • Topical Retinoids (e.g., Adapalene):
    Context: A first-line prescription treatment that normalises skin cell turnover to prevent pores from blocking. Often used in combination with benzoyl peroxide. Knowing this helps explain to a patient why a GP visit is a necessary next step if OTC treatments fail. [Rx advice]
  • Topical Combination Products (e.g., Adapalene/BPO, Clindamycin/BPO):
    Context: GPs frequently prescribe these fixed-dose combinations for convenience and efficacy. Explaining that GPs use antibiotic-BPO combinations to prevent resistance helps reinforce antimicrobial stewardship messages. [Rx advice]
  • Oral Antibiotics (e.g., Lymecycline, Doxycycline):
    Context: Used for moderate-to-severe acne. Pharmacy teams can support adherence and counsel on side effects (e.g., photosensitivity with doxycycline) when dispensing. [Rx advice]
  • Combined Oral Contraceptives (COC):
    Context: Certain COCs are licensed for acne in women and can be an effective prescription option from the GP. [Rx advice]
  • Oral Isotretinoin:
    Context: A highly effective treatment for severe, cystic, or treatment-resistant acne, initiated and monitored only by a consultant dermatologist. [Rx advice]
7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for mild acne.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: This is the most crucial point. Clearly state: "Acne treatments take time to work. You need to use this consistently for at least 8-12 weeks before you will see a real improvement."
  • Justify Recommendation: Explain why you have chosen a product. For example: "I'm recommending this cream with benzoyl peroxide because it works by unblocking pores and killing the bacteria that contribute to spots."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame skincare as an active treatment. "Using a gentle cleanser and an oil-free moisturiser every day is just as important as the treatment cream itself."
  • Counsel on Application: Advise applying a thin layer (pea-sized amount) to the entire affected area, not just individual spots, once or twice daily on clean, dry skin.
  • Warn about Side Effects: Proactively mention common side effects and how to manage them. "It's normal for your skin to feel a bit dry or red at first. You can manage this by starting with application every other night and always using a moisturiser." Remind them about BPO bleaching fabrics.
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just to be sure I've explained that clearly, when should you come back and speak to us or see a GP?" reinforces the safety netting advice.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a person when they should seek further help from the pharmacy or their GP. This ensures they know what to do if their condition does not follow the expected course.

All advice should be specific and clear. Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • Their acne does not improve after 8-12 weeks of consistent use of an appropriate OTC product.
  • Their acne gets significantly worse (e.g., becomes more widespread, or painful cysts develop).
  • They notice any signs of scarring.
  • The acne is having a significant impact on their mental health or wellbeing.
  • The skin becomes severely irritated, red, or painful from the treatment, and this is not settling.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice from their GP if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • A sudden, severe eruption of painful spots associated with feeling unwell, feverish or having joint pains.
  • Developing signs of a hormonal imbalance (for females).

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions about their skin or treatment.

9. Pathophysiology

Acne vulgaris is a multifactorial inflammatory disease of the pilosebaceous unit. Its development is understood to involve four key interconnected processes.

  1. Sebum Production: Sebaceous glands, particularly on the face and upper trunk, are sensitive to androgen hormones. During puberty, rising androgen levels cause these glands to enlarge and produce increased amounts of sebum (seborrhoea).
  2. Follicular Hyperkeratinisation: There is an abnormal proliferation and reduced shedding of keratinocytes lining the hair follicle. This blocks the follicle opening, forming a plug known as a microcomedone, the primary lesion of acne.
  3. Microbial Colonisation: The blocked, lipid-rich follicle creates an ideal anaerobic environment for the bacterium Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) to proliferate.
  4. Inflammation and Immune Response: C. acnes and the breakdown of sebum trigger an innate immune response, attracting inflammatory cells. This leads to the development of inflammatory papules and pustules. If the follicle wall ruptures, it causes a larger inflammatory reaction in the surrounding skin.
10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and patient factors.

Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO)

  • Irritation: The most common issue. Advise starting with a lower strength (2.5% or 5%) and less frequent application. Always recommend use with a gentle cleanser and a non-comedogenic moisturiser.
  • Bleaching: Always provide a clear warning that BPO can permanently bleach hair, clothing, towels, and bed linen.
  • Photosensitivity: BPO can make the skin more sensitive to the sun. Advise use of a broad-spectrum sunscreen.

Salicylic Acid

  • Systemic Absorption: While minimal with OTC products, widespread use of high-concentration peels is not recommended, especially in pregnancy.
  • Irritation: Can cause dryness and irritation, particularly when combined with other active ingredients like BPO. Advise a simple routine.

General Skincare Products

  • Non-Comedogenic: Counsel patients to look for "non-comedogenic" or "oil-free" on any moisturisers or make-up they use, to ensure their routine is not contributing to the problem.
  • Harsh Scrubs: Advise against using harsh, abrasive facial scrubs as these can disrupt the skin barrier and worsen inflammation.
11. Use in special populations

Children & Adolescents

This is the most common population. OTC products containing BPO are generally appropriate for adolescents. It is essential to provide clear, simple instructions and manage expectations to encourage adherence. Pre-pubertal acne is a red flag requiring GP referral.

Older adults

Acne is less common and rosacea is a more likely diagnosis. Older skin is often drier and more sensitive, so irritating products like BPO should be recommended with caution, advising use with plenty of moisturiser. Always perform a medication history check for drug-induced causes.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

This is a key group requiring cautious advice. Non-pharmacological measures (gentle cleansing) are first-line. If treatment is requested, most OTC products should be avoided. A GP referral is the safest and most appropriate action. Patients should be explicitly told that retinoids (available on prescription) are harmful in pregnancy. Azelaic acid (POM) is often the preferred agent prescribed by GPs in this situation.

People with significant comorbidities

For most patients, OTC acne treatments are safe. However, in those with very sensitive skin conditions, like eczema affecting the face, any irritating product should be avoided. The most important consideration is psychological comorbidity; always screen for distress and refer to the GP if concerned.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple OTC Sale for Mild Acne

  • Patient: "Hi, can I get something for my son's spots? He's 15."
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. Can you tell me what his spots are like?"
  • Patient: "They're mostly blackheads on his nose and a few red ones on his forehead. He's had them for a few months."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. Has he tried anything for them yet, and is he on any other medication?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing yet, and he takes no other medicines."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like typical mild teenage acne. A good starting point would be a cream containing 5% benzoyl peroxide. He should apply a thin layer to the whole affected area once a day after washing his face. It's really important he knows it can take 2-3 months to see a good result, so he needs to stick with it. It might make his skin a bit dry or red at first, so using an oil-free moisturiser will help. Please also warn him that it can bleach clothes and bedsheets. If it's not better after 3 months, or it gets worse, he should see his GP."

Scenario 2: Referral Due to Psychological Impact

  • Patient: "Hi, I'm looking for the strongest acne cream you have."
  • Pharmacist: "I can certainly talk you through the options. Who is this for?"
  • Patient: "It's for me. I'm 22. I'm just sick of my skin, I've tried everything."
  • Pharmacist: "I'm sorry to hear you're feeling that way. What have you tried so far?"
  • Patient: "All sorts of washes. I just don't want to go out anymore, it looks so horrible."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "It sounds like this is really getting you down, and that's a very important symptom for us to address. When acne has that much of an impact on how you're feeling, and when you've already tried things from the pharmacy without success, the best and most effective thing to do is to see your GP. They have access to a much wider range of prescription treatments, like tablets and stronger creams, that can make a real difference. An over-the-counter product is not the right step for you today. I'd really encourage you to make a GP appointment to discuss this."
