Chickenpox in Community Pharmacy
1. Clinical presentation and overview

Chickenpox (varicella) is a common reason for people to seek advice in a community pharmacy. It is a highly infectious illness caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), most often affecting children. The illness typically starts with a prodrome of fever and malaise for 1-2 days before a characteristic vesicular rash appears.

The rash classically begins on the head, chest, or back before spreading. It evolves in stages: starting as macules, progressing to papules, then fluid-filled vesicles (blisters), which then crust over. The presence of lesions in different stages of development simultaneously ('cropping') is a key feature. The main symptom is intense itching (pruritus). Most healthy children recover fully within one to two weeks.

The incubation period is 10 to 21 days after exposure. An individual is infectious from about 48 hours before the rash appears until all vesicles have crusted over (usually 5-7 days from rash onset). The primary role of the pharmacy team is to confirm the likely diagnosis through effective questioning, exclude serious pathology, recommend appropriate self-care and OTC treatments, and manage expectations about the illness's natural duration.

2. Red flags and urgent referral criteria

The primary role of the community pharmacy team is to identify any features that suggest a more serious underlying cause for the illness. The presence of red flags warrants an urgent referral to a GP or emergency services (A&E).

  • Neurological symptoms: Signs such as new-onset drowsiness, confusion, seizures, ataxia (unsteadiness), or severe headache may indicate varicella encephalitis or meningitis. This requires emergency referral.
  • Respiratory symptoms: Significant cough, shortness of breath, or chest pain, particularly in adults, smokers, or immunocompromised individuals, may suggest varicella pneumonitis.
  • Severe skin signs: A widespread, painful, or haemorrhagic (bleeding into vesicles) rash. Spreading erythema, warmth, and tenderness around lesions suggests severe secondary bacterial infection, such as cellulitis or necrotising fasciitis.
  • Systemic signs of severe illness: A high, persistent fever, rigors, or signs of dehydration (e.g., reduced urine output). These may indicate severe secondary infection or sepsis.
  • At-risk populations presenting with chickenpox:
    • Immunocompromised patients: (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids). They are at risk of severe disease and require urgent referral.
    • Pregnant people: Any pregnant person without a definite history of chickenpox who has been exposed, or any pregnant person who develops the rash, requires an urgent GP appointment.
    • Neonates (infants <28 days old): Can develop severe illness and require urgent medical assessment.
3. Focused history taking (WWHAM framework)

A structured questioning technique is essential to ensure a safe and effective consultation in the pharmacy. The WWHAM framework provides a reliable structure for this.

  • W – Who is the medicine for?
    Reasoning: To confirm the patient's age. This is critical as OTC treatments for itch (like sedating antihistamines) have specific age licenses. It also establishes if the patient is present or if it's a third-party consultation.
  • W – What are the symptoms?
    Reasoning: To confirm the nature of the rash (vesicles, different stages) and to screen for red flags. Key questions include "Are they drowsy or confused?", "Are any of the spots looking very red or angry?", "Are they having any trouble breathing?".
  • H – How long have the symptoms been present?
    Reasoning: To confirm the timeline of the illness. Onset within the last 24 hours in an at-risk patient (e.g., an adult) may prompt a faster referral as antiviral treatment is time-sensitive.
  • A – Action taken already?
    Reasoning: To find out what has already been tried. Crucially, this is an opportunity to check for and advise against the use of NSAIDs like ibuprofen.
  • M – Medication being taken?
    Reasoning: To identify any prescribed medicines that indicate the patient is in a high-risk group (e.g., immunosuppressants, steroids) or has comorbidities. It also allows checking for the possibility of pregnancy.
4. Differential diagnosis in primary care

An awareness of the differential diagnosis is essential for effective triage in the pharmacy and identifying when a referral is necessary. The classic 'cropping' rash of chickenpox is highly characteristic.

Common Differentials

  • Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease: Vesicles are typically confined to the palms, soles, and mouth area.
  • Impetigo: A bacterial infection causing vesicles that rupture to form honey-coloured crusts; often localised.
  • Insect bites: Usually lack a prodromal fever and do not evolve through vesicular and crusted stages.
  • Scabies: Intensely itchy rash with burrows, often in finger webs and wrists. Itch is typically worse at night.

Serious Differentials to Consider for Referral

  • Disseminated Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV): Can mimic chickenpox, especially in children with eczema (eczema herpeticum) or immunocompromised patients. Requires urgent referral.
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN): A rare but severe reaction, often to medication. Involves severe mucosal blistering and skin detachment. This is a medical emergency.
5. Over-the-counter (OTC) treatment options

The primary goal of OTC treatment is symptomatic relief of itch and fever. Non-pharmacological advice is paramount.

First-Line Advice (All Patients)

  • Non-Pharmacological / Self-Care: This should be the first recommendation. Advise adequate fluid intake, rest, and keeping cool. Keeping fingernails short prevents damage from scratching.
  • Topical Cooling Preparations (e.g., Calamine Lotion, Cooling Gels):
    • Mechanism: Provide a soothing effect on the skin through evaporation, which can temporarily relieve itching.
    • Licensing: Generally GSL. Suitable for all ages.
    • Counselling: Advise that they help with itch but do not treat the infection. [Rx info]
  • Analgesia (Paracetamol):
    • Mechanism: For relief of fever and general aches/pains.
    • Licensing: GSL or P. Available in formulations suitable for children from 2 months old.
    • Counselling: Advise on correct age-appropriate dosing. Crucially, advise that ibuprofen should NOT be used. [Rx info]

Second-Line Options for Itch

  • Sedating Antihistamines (e.g., Chlorphenamine):
    • Mechanism: An H1-receptor antagonist. Its sedating effect is beneficial for relieving itch and aiding sleep.
    • Licensing: P medicine. Age restrictions apply (typically not for children under 1 year).
    • Counselling: For troublesome itching, especially at night. Must warn about drowsiness and to avoid driving or operating machinery. [Rx info]
6. Prescription-only medicines (POMs) — for educational context

While most community pharmacists do not prescribe these for chickenpox, awareness is vital for patient counselling, managing expectations, and understanding the patient journey after referral.

  • Oral Antivirals (e.g., Aciclovir):
    Context: These are not effective or necessary for uncomplicated chickenpox in healthy children. A GP may prescribe them for individuals at higher risk of severe disease, such as adults, adolescents, or the immunocompromised. Treatment must be started within 24 hours of rash onset. This knowledge helps explain why a referral for a healthy child seeking an antiviral is not appropriate. [Rx advice]
  • Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin):
    Context: Antibiotics are ineffective against the chickenpox virus itself. Pharmacists play a key role in upholding antimicrobial stewardship by explaining this. A GP will only prescribe antibiotics if they diagnose a secondary bacterial skin infection, such as cellulitis around one of the spots. [Rx advice]

This knowledge helps pharmacists explain to a patient why a GP referral is needed for certain groups and what treatments a doctor might consider.

7. Pharmacist counselling and advice

Effective communication and counselling are the most important interventions a pharmacy team can provide for chickenpox.

Key Counselling Points

  • Manage Expectations: Clearly explain the natural course of the illness. A key phrase is: "It's normal for chickenpox to last about a week or so from start to finish. The most important thing is to wait until every single spot has scabbed over before going back to school or work."
  • The Ibuprofen Warning: Provide a clear, direct, and confident explanation. "It is very important that you do not give ibuprofen for chickenpox. There is evidence linking it to nasty skin infections. Please use paracetamol instead if you need something for the fever."
  • Empower with Self-Care: Frame self-care as the main treatment. Recommend keeping cool, drinking plenty of fluids, and using cooling lotions. Keeping nails short is an active step to prevent infection.
  • Infection Control: Advise on the infectious period and the need to avoid contact with people who are pregnant, newborn babies, or those on chemotherapy.
  • Check Understanding: After providing advice, a simple check like "Just so I know I've explained it well, can you remind me which painkiller you need to avoid?" can reinforce key safety messages.
8. Safety netting and follow-up

Safety netting is the critical process of advising a patient or carer when they should seek further help. This ensures they know when to act if the condition deviates from the normal course.

Advise the patient to consult their GP if:

  • The patient is not drinking or showing signs of dehydration.
  • The skin around any of the spots becomes red, hot, swollen or painful, as this may be a bacterial infection.
  • The patient seems to be getting more unwell after the first few days, instead of better.

Advise the patient to seek urgent medical advice (call GP immediately or A&E) if they develop any red flag symptoms, such as:

  • Difficulty breathing or a cough.
  • A severe headache, drowsiness, or confusion.
  • Difficulty walking or unsteadiness.
  • A seizure (fit).

Finally, advise them they can always speak to the pharmacist again if they have any further questions or concerns.

9. Pathophysiology

Chickenpox is the clinical manifestation of a primary infection with the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a DNA virus from the human herpesvirus family. Transmission occurs primarily via inhalation of airborne respiratory droplets from an infected individual or, less commonly, by direct contact with the fluid from skin vesicles.

The virus initially replicates in the nasopharynx and regional lymph nodes. This is followed by a primary viraemia, where the virus spreads via the bloodstream to organs of the reticuloendothelial system, such as the liver and spleen, for further replication. A second, more significant viraemia then occurs approximately 10-21 days after initial infection. During this secondary viraemic phase, the virus disseminates throughout the body and seeds the skin.

Viral replication within capillary endothelial cells and the epidermis leads to cellular degeneration and the formation of characteristic vesicles. The fluid within these vesicles is rich in infectious virions. The host immune response, involving both cellular and humoral immunity, is crucial for controlling the infection and leads to the crusting of lesions and recovery. Following the primary infection, VZV establishes lifelong latency in the dorsal root ganglia of the sensory nervous system. Reactivation of this latent virus later in life causes herpes zoster (shingles).

10. Product-specific considerations and cautions

Safe supply of OTC medicines requires awareness of product-specific risks and cautions.

NSAIDs (Ibuprofen)

  • Critical Safety Warning: There is a strong association between ibuprofen use during chickenpox and an increased risk of severe necrotising soft-tissue infections. It must be actively advised against. Paracetamol is the only recommended agent for fever/pain.

Sedating Antihistamines (Chlorphenamine)

  • Interactions: Additive sedative effects with other CNS depressants, including alcohol.
  • Side Effects: The primary side effect is drowsiness. A "Do not drive or operate machinery" warning is essential for adult patients.
  • Cautions: Be particularly cautious in older adults due to increased risk of confusion and falls.

Multi-Ingredient Products

  • Hidden Ingredients: While not specific to chickenpox remedies, patients may be taking other cold and flu products concurrently. Always check for hidden paracetamol to prevent accidental overdose.
11. Use in special populations

Children

This is the most common patient group. The primary recommendation is supportive care with fluids and cooling lotions/gels. Paracetamol can be used for fever. A sedating antihistamine like chlorphenamine (P medicine) can be considered for troublesome itch, adhering to age-specific licensing (usually for >1 year old).

Older adults

Chickenpox is more severe in adults. While self-care advice is the same, there should be a very low threshold for referring to a GP, as they are candidates for antiviral therapy. Caution is needed with sedating antihistamines due to the increased risk of falls and confusion.

Pregnant or breastfeeding people

Immediate referral to the GP is mandatory if a pregnant person develops a rash or has been exposed and is non-immune. Do not recommend any OTC products beyond simple emollients or cooling gels. Self-care with rest and fluids is appropriate while awaiting medical review. Paracetamol is considered safe.

People with significant comorbidities

An immediate GP referral is required for any patient known to be significantly immunocompromised (e.g., on chemotherapy, high-dose steroids). For patients with diabetes, sugar-free formulations of paracetamol or antihistamines should be recommended where possible.

12. Illustrative pharmacy consultation scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Viral Rash & Self-Care Advice

  • Patient: "Hi, my daughter has chickenpox. What can I get for her?"
  • Pharmacist: "Of course. First, who is it for and how old is she?"
  • Patient: "It's for my daughter, she's 5."
  • Pharmacist: "And what are her symptoms, just the spots?"
  • Patient: "Yes, spots everywhere and she's very itchy and a bit grumpy. She has a bit of a temperature."
  • Pharmacist: "How long has she had the spots?"
  • Patient: "They started yesterday."
  • Pharmacist: "Have you given her anything yet?"
  • Patient: "Just some paracetamol for the fever."
  • Pharmacist: "Perfect. And is she on any other medication?"
  • Patient: "No, nothing."
  • Recommendation: "It sounds like a classic case of chickenpox. The best thing is plenty of fluids and keeping her cool. For the itch, a cooling gel or calamine lotion can really help. The most important advice is to not give her any ibuprofen, as it can cause skin problems with chickenpox - stick with the paracetamol. If any of the spots look infected - so very red, hot and angry - or she becomes very drowsy or unwell, you should call the GP straight away."

Scenario 2: Referral due to Red Flag

  • Patient: "I need something for my husband's chickenpox. He feels awful."
  • Pharmacist: "Okay. How old is your husband?"
  • Patient: "He's 42."
  • Pharmacist: "And what are his symptoms exactly?"
  • Patient: "He's covered in spots, but he's also developed a nasty cough and says he's finding it a bit hard to breathe."
  • Recommendation (Triage): "Thank you for telling me that. Because he is an adult and has developed a cough and difficulty breathing, he needs to see a doctor urgently. An over-the-counter medicine isn't suitable, as those symptoms can be a sign of a chest complication that is more common in adults. You should contact his GP surgery right away and tell them about the rash and the breathing problems. If you can't get an appointment in the next few hours, you should call 111 for advice."
