Endocrine System Examination
1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary aim of the endocrine examination is to identify the physical manifestations of hormonal excess or deficiency. Unlike other systems, it is not an examination of a single organ, but rather a targeted general examination looking for a constellation of signs across the whole body. The goal is to detect patterns suggestive of specific endocrine syndromes, such as thyrotoxicosis, hypothyroidism, Cushing's syndrome, or acromegaly. The examination also seeks to identify the complications of endocrine disease, most notably the peripheral signs of diabetes mellitus. Findings must be interpreted in the context of the patient's history to build a coherent diagnostic hypothesis.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A focused endocrine examination requires knowledge of specific anatomical locations where signs are most apparent.

  • The Thyroid Gland: This butterfly-shaped gland is located in the anterior neck. The two lobes wrap around the trachea, connected in the midline by a thin piece of tissue called the isthmus. The isthmus typically lies inferior to the cricoid cartilage. The key surface landmark for locating the gland is the thyroid cartilage (the 'Adam's apple'); the gland sits below this.
  • The Eyes: The periorbital tissues, conjunctiva, and extraocular muscles are crucial. Protrusion of the eyeball (proptosis) and the position of the upper eyelid relative to the limbus are key observations in thyroid eye disease.
  • Visual Pathways: The pituitary gland sits in the sella turcica, directly beneath the optic chiasm where the optic nerves cross. A pituitary tumour can compress the chiasm, classically causing a bitemporal hemianopia (loss of the outer visual fields for both eyes).
  • Skin Creases and Folds: The palmar creases of the hands and the skin of the axillae are important sites to inspect for abnormal pigmentation associated with Addison's disease (hyperpigmentation) or insulin resistance (acanthosis nigricans).
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional and effective endocrine examination requires specific equipment and clear communication.

  1. Equipment:
    • A glass of water for the patient to sip.
    • Stethoscope (to listen for a thyroid bruit).
    • Tendon hammer (to assess reflexes).
    • Cotton wool or a neurotip (to test sensation in the feet).
    • An ophthalmoscope or a pin with a red top (to assess visual fields).
    • Blood pressure cuff and sphygmomanometer.
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • The patient should ideally be sitting on the side of the examination couch for most of the assessment.
    • Ensure good lighting. Preserve dignity with appropriate draping, but ensure you can adequately see the hands, arms, face, and neck.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, my name is [Name] and I am a foundation doctor").
    • Explain the examination in simple terms and gain consent. A suitable phrase is: "I've been asked to perform a general examination that focuses on your hormone systems. This will involve looking at your hands, face and eyes, examining your neck, and checking your reflexes. Would that be okay?"
    • Always wash your hands before you begin.
4. The systematic sequence

The endocrine examination is a systematic survey from head to toe, looking for specific syndromic patterns. A consistent approach is key.

  1. General Inspection: From the end of the bed, observe the patient's overall appearance. Note their body habitus (e.g., central obesity, weight loss), stature, and any obvious facial features (e.g., plethora, coarseness), a visible goitre, or signs of anxiety.
  2. Hands and Arms:
    • Inspect the hands for thyroid acropachy (clubbing-like changes), palmar erythema (red palms), and check for a fine tremor by placing a piece of paper on their outstretched hands. Feel the palms for sweat (hyperthyroidism) or dryness (hypothyroidism).
    • Check the radial pulse for rate and rhythm (tachycardia/bradycardia). Measure blood pressure and assess for a postural drop if adrenal insufficiency is suspected.
  3. Face and Eyes:
    • Inspect the face for coarsening of features (acromegaly), a rounded 'moon' face with plethora (red cheeks) in Cushing's, or thinning of the outer third of the eyebrows in hypothyroidism.
    • Perform a focused eye examination: Check for lid retraction (stare), lid lag, and proptosis (exophthalmos). Assess eye movements for ophthalmoplegia.
    • Assess visual fields to confrontation, checking for a bitemporal hemianopia.
  4. The Neck (Thyroid Examination):
    • Inspect from the front for any masses or a goitre. Ask the patient to sip some water and hold it in their mouth, then swallow, while you watch for movement of any mass.
    • Palpate the thyroid gland from behind. Use the flats of your fingers to feel the lobes for size, consistency, and any nodules. Ask the patient to swallow again as you palpate.
    • Auscultate over the thyroid lobes with the bell of your stethoscope for a thyroid bruit (a sound indicating high blood flow).
  5. Trunk and Legs:
    • Inspect the chest and abdomen for skin changes like purple striae (Cushing's) or acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety patches in the axilla or neck).
    • Inspect the shins for pretibial myxoedema (a lumpy, discoloured rash seen in Graves' disease). Check for pitting oedema.
    • Assess reflexes at the knees or ankles; they may be brisk in hyperthyroidism or show delayed relaxation in hypothyroidism.
  6. Completing the Examination: To complete, a diabetic foot examination (testing sensation) should be performed if relevant.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Knowing the normal baseline is crucial for spotting pathology. A normal examination is reassuring but does not exclude all endocrine disease, much of which is diagnosed biochemically.

  • General: The patient is of normal stature and body habitus for their age. They appear calm and comfortable.
  • Peripheries: Hands are warm and dry. Pulse is regular, between 60-100 bpm. No tremor, clubbing, or abnormal pigmentation.
  • Face and Eyes: Normal facial features. Sclera are white with no lid retraction or proptosis. Eye movements are full and painless. Visual fields are full to confrontation.
  • Neck: The thyroid gland is neither visible nor palpable. There are no audible bruits over the gland.
  • Trunk and Legs: Skin is clear of striae or rashes. Reflexes are normal, without delayed relaxation.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Specific signs, especially when clustered together, are strong indicators of underlying endocrine syndromes.

Goitre
  • The Sign: A visibly or palpably enlarged thyroid gland. A goitre can be diffuse (smoothly enlarged) or nodular.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates an abnormality of the thyroid gland. This could be associated with hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease, toxic multinodular goitre), hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's thyroiditis), or euthyroid states (simple colloid goitre).
Signs of Thyrotoxicosis
  • The Sign: A collection of signs including a fine tremor, warm sweaty palms, tachycardia (a fast heart rate), and anxiety.
  • Pathology Link: Caused by an excess of circulating thyroid hormones, which increases the body's metabolic rate and adrenergic (adrenaline-like) activity.
Thyroid eye disease (Ophthalmopathy)
  • The Sign: Lid retraction (a 'staring' look), lid lag (upper eyelid lags behind the eye on downward gaze), proptosis (bulging eyes), and restricted or painful eye movements (ophthalmoplegia).
  • Pathology Link: An autoimmune process, almost exclusively associated with Graves' disease, where antibodies attack the tissues behind the eye, causing inflammation and swelling.
  • Red Flag: Severe eye pain, reduced visual acuity, or signs of corneal exposure are ophthalmological emergencies requiring urgent specialist review.
Signs of Hypothyroidism
  • The Sign: A collection of signs including dry, coarse skin, hair loss (especially outer third of eyebrows), bradycardia (a slow heart rate), a hoarse voice, and characteristically delayed relaxation of reflexes.
  • Pathology Link: Caused by a deficiency of thyroid hormone, leading to a global slowing of metabolic and bodily functions.
Cushingoid features
  • The Sign: A combination of central obesity, a rounded 'moon' face, plethora (a ruddy complexion), thin skin with easy bruising, and wide, purple abdominal striae.
  • Pathology Link: These are all manifestations of prolonged exposure to excess cortisol, either from medication (iatrogenic) or an endogenous tumour. Cortisol affects fat distribution, breaks down skin collagen, and weakens blood vessels.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A structured summary conveys your findings logically and professionally. Report pertinent positives and negatives in sequence, finishing with a concluding statement.

On general inspection, the patient appears [e.g., anxious and fidgety / lethargic], with a body habitus suggestive of [e.g., central obesity].

Their hands were [e.g., warm and sweaty with a fine tremor]. The pulse was [rate] and [rhythm].

On examination of the face, there was [e.g., evidence of thyroid eye disease with lid retraction and proptosis / coarse facial features]. Visual fields were [full / showed a bitemporal defect].

Examination of the neck revealed [e.g., a diffusely enlarged, non-tender goitre with an audible bruit / no palpable goitre].

To complete the examination, there were [e.g., brisk reflexes and pretibial myxoedema / no relevant peripheral signs].

In summary, the findings are consistent with a diagnosis of [e.g., thyrotoxicosis, likely due to Graves' disease].
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

Clustering signs into a recognisable pattern is the key to clinical diagnosis in endocrinology.

9. Relevant next-step investigations

Physical findings should always be confirmed with appropriate biochemical or imaging tests.

  • If you find signs of thyroid disease (hyper- or hypothyroidism):
    • Logical Next Steps: The key investigation is a Thyroid Function Test (TFT), measuring TSH and free T4/T3. Thyroid autoantibodies may also be useful.
  • If you find signs of Cushing's syndrome:
    • Logical Next Steps: First-line tests include a 24-hour urinary free cortisol or a low-dose dexamethasone suppression test to confirm cortisol excess.
  • If you find signs of acromegaly:
    • Logical Next Steps: The initial blood test is Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1). If raised, this is followed by a glucose tolerance test with growth hormone measurements.
  • If you find a visual field defect:
    • Logical Next Steps: This is a red flag finding requiring an urgent MRI of the pituitary gland and referral to ophthalmology.
  • If you suspect Addison's disease:
    • Logical Next Steps: A 9 am serum cortisol is a screening test. The definitive investigation is a short Synacthen test (ACTH stimulation test).
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

The endocrine examination is nuanced. Avoiding common mistakes is key to a good performance, particularly in an OSCE setting.

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to ask the patient to swallow water when inspecting and palpating the neck.
    • Palpating the thyroid gland from the front, which is less sensitive than palpating from behind.
    • Not assessing the full range of eye movements or forgetting to check for lid lag specifically.
    • Forgetting to check for delayed relaxation of the reflexes in suspected hypothyroidism.
      • - Forgetting to auscultate for a thyroid bruit.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start with hand washing, introduction, and gaining consent.
    • Verbalise your findings as you go. For example: "I am looking at the hands for tremor or thyroid acropachy... I can see no evidence of these."
    • Be slick with your thyroid exam: stand behind the patient and have the glass of water ready.
    • If you find signs of thyroid eye disease, state that you would perform a full cranial nerve examination, especially of nerves II, III, IV, and VI, and check visual acuity with a Snellen chart.
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant next steps, such as measuring blood pressure (including postural), testing urine (for glucose), and performing a full diabetic foot screen.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected signs is a vital clinical reasoning skill.

  • Case 1: Graves' Disease
    • Scenario: A 28-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of weight loss, anxiety, and palpitations.
    • Expected Findings: She appears anxious and may have a fine tremor of her outstretched hands. Her palms may be warm and sweaty. Her pulse is likely fast (tachycardia). She may have a visible, diffusely enlarged goitre, and on auscultation, a bruit may be present. Key findings would be signs of thyroid eye disease, such as lid retraction and lid lag.
  • Case 2: Long-standing Hypothyroidism
    • Scenario: A 65-year-old man attends a check-up complaining of tiredness and "slowing down" for the past year.
    • Expected Findings: He may appear lethargic with a puffy (myxoedematous) face and some hair loss from the outer third of his eyebrows. His skin may feel dry and cool. His pulse would be slow (bradycardia). The pathognomonic sign would be delayed relaxation of his ankle or knee jerk reflexes.
  • Case 3: Acromegaly
    • Scenario: A 50-year-old woman complains that her rings and shoes no longer fit, and her husband says her face has changed. She also mentions headaches and poor peripheral vision.
    • Expected Findings: You would note coarse facial features, including a prominent brow (frontal bossing) and an enlarged jaw (prognathism). Her hands would be large and 'spade-like'. Examination of her visual fields would likely reveal a bitemporal hemianopia.
