1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary aim of the neurological examination is to localise a lesion within the nervous system. Following a detailed history, the examination seeks to elicit physical signs that confirm or refute diagnostic hypotheses. It is a systematic process designed to answer the question, "Where is the problem?". The findings help differentiate between central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system (nerve root, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction, muscle) pathologies. A key distinction sought is between upper motor neurone (UMN) and lower motor neurone (LMN) patterns of weakness.

Furthermore, the examination establishes an objective baseline of a patient's neurological function, which is critical for monitoring disease progression or recovery. It aims to identify red flag signs, such as those indicating acute spinal cord compression or raised intracranial pressure, which require immediate investigation and intervention.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A structured neurological examination is built upon a foundation of functional neuroanatomy. Understanding the relationship between anatomical structures and the signs elicited is key to localisation.

  • Upper vs Lower Motor Neurones (UMN/LMN): This is a fundamental concept. UMNs originate in the cerebral cortex and brainstem, and their axons travel within the central nervous system. They synapse on LMNs, whose cell bodies are in the brainstem (for cranial nerves) or spinal cord. The LMN axon then travels out to the periphery to innervate muscle fibres. The characteristics of weakness, changes in tone, and reflexes differ depending on which type of neurone is affected.
  • Dermatomes and Myotomes:
    • A dermatome is an area of skin supplied by a single spinal nerve root. Testing sensation in key dermatomes helps localise a lesion to a specific spinal level.
    • A myotome is a group of muscles supplied by a single spinal nerve root. Testing power in specific muscle groups allows for localisation. Key examples include: C5 (shoulder abduction), C7 (elbow extension), L2 (hip flexion), L4 (knee extension), L5 (foot dorsiflexion), and S1 (foot plantarflexion).
  • Tendon Reflexes and Nerve Roots: Tapping a tendon stretches the muscle, activating a spinal reflex arc. The briskness of the reflex provides information about the state of the UMNs and LMNs at that level.
    • Biceps Reflex: C5, C6
    • Triceps Reflex: C7, C8
    • Knee (Patellar) Reflex: L3, L4
    • Ankle (Achilles) Reflex: S1, S2
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional and accurate examination requires careful preparation and clear communication. The neurological examination can be lengthy, so it is important to ensure the patient is comfortable and understands the process.

  1. Equipment: Gather all necessary equipment before you start.
    • Tendon (patellar) hammer
    • Neuro-tip (for testing pain sensation)
    • Cotton wool or a neuro-tip brush (for light touch)
    • 128 Hz tuning fork (for vibration sense)
    • Ophthalmoscope
    • Pen torch
    • Snellen chart (for visual acuity)
    • A cup of water (for swallowing)
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to sit on the side of the bed or lie on the examination couch. Ensure they are comfortable.
    • Explain that you will need them to move their arms and legs, so they should have loose-fitting clothing. Maintain dignity with appropriate draping, exposing only the area being examined at that time.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Begin by washing your hands.
    • Introduce yourself with your name and role.
    • Explain the examination in simple terms and gain consent. A suitable phrase is: "I've been asked to perform an examination of your nervous system. This will involve looking at your eyes and face, and then testing the feeling and strength in your arms and legs. It shouldn't be painful, but please tell me if you feel any discomfort. Would that be okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A neurological examination is typically broken down into sections: general inspection, gait, cranial nerves, upper limbs, and lower limbs. A consistent sequence ensures no steps are missed.

  1. General Inspection & Gait: Start by observing the patient. Note any walking aids, muscle wasting, fasciculations (muscle twitches), or abnormal postures. Ask the patient to walk, turn, and walk back. Observe their gait, arm swing, and balance. Ask them to perform a tandem gait (walking heel-to-toe).
  2. Cranial Nerves (I-XII): Systematically assess each nerve in turn, comparing left to right. This includes sense of smell, visual acuity and fields, fundoscopy, eye movements, facial sensation and power, hearing (including Rinne's and Weber's tests), palate movement, and tongue movement.
  3. Upper Limb Examination:
    • Inspection: Look for wasting, fasciculations, abnormal posture.
    • Tone: Assess tone at the elbow and wrist by passively moving the joints.
    • Power: Test power against resistance for key movements (shoulder, elbow, wrist, fingers). Grade using the MRC scale (0-5).
    • Reflexes: Elicit the biceps, triceps, and supinator reflexes.
    • Coordination: Test with the finger-to-nose test and by assessing for dysdiadochokinesia (difficulty with rapid, alternating movements).
    • Sensation: Test light touch, pinprick, vibration sense, and proprioception (joint position sense).
  4. Lower Limb Examination:
    • Inspection: As for upper limbs.
    • Tone: Assess tone at the knee and ankle. Test for clonus at the ankle.
    • Power: Test power for key movements (hip, knee, ankle, big toe).
    • Reflexes: Elicit the knee, ankle, and plantar reflexes.
    • Coordination: Test with the heel-to-shin test.
    • Sensation: As for upper limbs.
  5. Completing the Examination: Thank the patient. Remember to consider assessing higher cognitive functions and examining the spine if indicated.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising normal findings is crucial for identifying pathology. A normal examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • General & Gait: Patient is alert and oriented. Gait is steady with normal arm swing and turning. Able to perform tandem gait without difficulty.
  • Cranial Nerves: All 12 cranial nerves are intact. Pupils are equal and reactive, visual fields are full, eye movements are full with no nystagmus, face is symmetrical, hearing is normal, and palate elevates symmetrically.
  • Motor System (Limbs):
    • Tone: Normal tone throughout, with no spasticity or rigidity. No clonus.
    • Power: MRC grade 5/5 in all myotomes tested.
    • Reflexes: All reflexes present and symmetrical (graded as ++). Plantar responses are down-going.
  • Sensation & Coordination:
    • Sensation: Sensation to light touch, pinprick, vibration, and proprioception is intact and symmetrical.
    • Coordination: Finger-nose and heel-shin testing are performed smoothly and accurately. No dysdiadochokinesia.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Pathological signs are the objective evidence of neurological disease. Understanding their significance is the key to clinical localisation.

Upper motor neurone (UMN) signs
  • The Signs: Spasticity (velocity-dependent increase in tone), hyperreflexia (brisk reflexes), clonus, and an upgoing plantar response (Babinski sign). Weakness is in a "pyramidal" distribution (extensors weaker than flexors in arms; flexors weaker than extensors in legs).
  • Pathology Link: Indicates a lesion in the central nervous system (brain or spinal cord) affecting the descending motor pathways. Examples include stroke, multiple sclerosis, and spinal cord injury.
Lower motor neurone (LMN) signs
  • The Signs: Hypotonia (reduced tone) or flaccidity, hyporeflexia (reduced or absent reflexes), muscle wasting, and fasciculations (visible muscle twitches). Weakness affects the muscles supplied by the damaged nerve or nerve root.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates a lesion in the peripheral nervous system, affecting the anterior horn cell, nerve root, plexus, or peripheral nerve itself. Examples include peripheral neuropathy and motor neurone disease.
Cerebellar signs
  • The Signs: Remembered by the mnemonic DANISH: Dysdiadochokinesia, Ataxia (gait and truncal), Nystagmus, Intention tremor, Slurred (staccato) speech, Hypotonia.
  • Pathology Link: These signs point to a lesion within the cerebellum or its connecting pathways. Causes can include stroke, tumours, or degenerative conditions.
Extrapyramidal (Parkinsonian) signs
  • The Signs: Remembered by the mnemonic TRAP: Tremor (typically a resting, "pill-rolling" tremor), Rigidity (a "lead-pipe" or "cogwheel" increase in tone), Akinesia (or bradykinesia - poverty and slowness of movement), and Postural instability.
  • Pathology Link: These signs are characteristic of Parkinson's disease and other Parkinsonian syndromes, resulting from dysfunction of the basal ganglia.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating findings. Start with a general statement about the patient's higher functions and gait, then systematically describe the cranial nerve, upper limb, and lower limb findings. Conclude with a localisation.

On examination, the patient was alert and oriented with a [describe gait, e.g., normal / ataxic] gait.

Cranial nerves [e.g., were grossly intact / showed a specific deficit, e.g., a left lower motor neurone 7th nerve palsy].

In the upper limbs, tone was [e.g., normal]. Power was [e.g., 5/5 throughout]. Reflexes were [e.g., symmetrical and present]. Sensation and coordination were intact.

In the lower limbs, there were signs of a [e.g., right-sided upper motor neurone pattern weakness]. Tone was increased on the right, with power 4/5 in the flexors and extensors. Reflexes were brisker on the right, with an upgoing right plantar response.

In summary, the findings are consistent with a [e.g., right-sided hemiparesis of an upper motor neurone pattern].
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

Individual signs are clues, but constellations of signs are what allow for robust clinical localisation and diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses or localisations.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis / Localisation
Unilateral UMN signs affecting face, arm, and leg. Contralateral Cerebral Hemisphere Lesion (e.g., Stroke)
Bilateral UMN signs in the legs (spastic paraparesis) with a clear sensory level on the trunk. Spinal Cord Lesion (Myelopathy)
Asymmetrical resting tremor, bradykinesia, and rigidity. Parkinson's Disease
Gait ataxia, intention tremor, and dysdiadochokinesia. Cerebellar Syndrome
Distal, symmetrical sensory loss ("glove and stocking") with absent ankle reflexes. Peripheral Neuropathy
A mix of UMN and LMN signs in multiple limbs. Motor Neurone Disease
9. Relevant next-step investigations

Findings on examination directly guide the choice of investigations to confirm a diagnosis and determine the underlying cause.

  • If you find signs of an acute stroke (sudden focal deficit):
    • Logical Next Steps: An immediate CT head scan is required to differentiate between an ischaemic and haemorrhagic stroke, as this dictates management. An MRI offers more detail later.
  • If you find signs of spinal cord compression (e.g., spastic legs, sensory level):
    • Logical Next Steps: This is a neurological emergency. An urgent MRI of the entire spine is the investigation of choice to identify and locate the cause of the compression.
  • If you find signs of a peripheral neuropathy:
    • Logical Next Steps: Investigations are aimed at finding the cause. Key tests include bloods (e.g., HbA1c for diabetes, B12/folate levels, thyroid function) and neurophysiology (nerve conduction studies and EMG).
  • If you suspect meningitis (e.g., headache, fever, neck stiffness):
    • Logical Next Steps: After an initial assessment and neuroimaging to exclude raised intracranial pressure, a lumbar puncture is performed to analyse the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Success in a neurological examination, particularly in an OSCE, depends on a slick, systematic technique and awareness of common errors.

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting essential equipment like a tuning fork or ophthalmoscope.
    • Failing to adequately compare the left and right sides for every test.
    • Incorrect technique for eliciting reflexes (e.g., wrong grip on hammer, patient not relaxed).
    • Hurting the patient with the neuro-tip by applying too much pressure.
    • Forgetting to test for clonus or plantar responses in the lower limb exam.
    • Not providing clear instructions to the patient.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start with the basics: Wash hands, introduce yourself, explain, and gain consent.
    • Verbalise as you go. State what you are testing and what you are looking for (e.g., "I am now assessing tone in the upper limbs, feeling for any spasticity or rigidity.").
    • Be systematic and have a routine. This prevents you from omitting steps under pressure.
    • At the end, provide a concise summary of your positive and negative findings.
    • Crucially, conclude with a localisation: "In summary, the findings localise to the..." (e.g., right cerebellum, peripheral nerves in a stocking distribution).
    • State what you would do "to complete the examination," such as check gait, perform fundoscopy, or assess cognition.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected examination findings is a key skill for developing clinical reasoning.

  • Case 1: Acute Ischaemic Stroke
    • Scenario: A 72-year-old right-handed man presents with a 2-hour history of sudden-onset right-sided weakness and difficulty speaking.
    • Expected Findings: He may have a dysphasia. Examination would reveal UMN signs on the right side: increased tone (developing over time), pyramidal weakness (power perhaps 1/5), brisk reflexes, and an upgoing plantar response. He would also have a right UMN 7th nerve palsy (forehead spared).
    • Summary: The findings are consistent with an acute left middle cerebral artery territory stroke.
  • Case 2: Cerebellar Syndrome
    • Scenario: A 55-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of worsening balance and clumsiness, particularly when reaching for objects.
    • Expected Findings: Her speech may be slurred and "scanning". She would have a broad-based, ataxic gait and be unable to perform tandem walking. Examination would show a marked intention tremor on the finger-nose test, and she would be clumsy on rapid alternating movements (dysdiadochokinesia).
    • Summary: These are classic signs of a cerebellar syndrome.
  • Case 3: Lumbar Radiculopathy ('Sciatica')
    • Scenario: A 45-year-old man presents with a 2-week history of severe pain radiating from his lower back down the back of his left leg to his foot.
    • Expected Findings: Examination of the left lower limb would reveal LMN signs localised to a specific nerve root. For an S1 radiculopathy, you might find weakness of plantarflexion (S1 myotome), a diminished or absent ankle reflex (S1), and sensory loss over the sole and lateral border of the foot (S1 dermatome).
    • Summary: These focal LMN signs in the leg are consistent with an S1 radiculopathy.
