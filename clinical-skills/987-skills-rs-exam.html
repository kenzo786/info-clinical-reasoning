Cardivascular System Examination
1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the respiratory examination is to identify physical signs that confirm or refute diagnoses suspected from the patient's history. It is a systematic process designed to detect evidence of common respiratory pathologies such as obstructive lung disease (COPD, asthma), infection (pneumonia), pleural disease (effusion, pneumothorax), interstitial lung disease, and malignancy. The findings must always be integrated with the history to form a cohesive clinical picture. A key aim is to identify signs requiring urgent intervention, such as tracheal deviation in a tension pneumothorax or the signs of acute severe asthma.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

Accurate performance and interpretation of the respiratory examination relies on a sound knowledge of the underlying thoracic anatomy and its corresponding surface landmarks.

  • Chest Wall Lines: These vertical lines are used to localise findings.
    • Anterior: Mid-sternal, mid-clavicular, anterior axillary lines.
    • Lateral: Mid-axillary line.
    • Posterior: Mid-scapular, vertebral lines.
  • The Sternal Angle (Angle of Louis): A palpable ridge on the sternum where the manubrium joins the body. It lies level with the 2nd costal cartilage, providing the reference point for counting ribs and intercostal spaces. It also marks the approximate level of the tracheal bifurcation (the carina).
  • Lung Lobes & Fissures:
    • The apex of each lung extends above the clavicle into the supraclavicular fossa.
    • The right lung has three lobes (upper, middle, lower), separated by the horizontal and oblique fissures. The horizontal fissure runs from the 4th costal cartilage to meet the oblique fissure in the mid-axillary line at the 5th rib.
    • The left lung has two lobes (upper, lower), separated by the oblique fissure.
  • The Trachea: This should be palpable in the midline within the suprasternal notch. Deviation from this position is a significant sign.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A smooth, professional, and safe examination requires careful preparation and clear communication.

  1. Equipment:
    • Stethoscope (with both bell and diaphragm).
    • Alcohol wipes to clean the stethoscope.
  2. Infection Control:
    • Begin by washing your hands or using alcohol gel in front of the patient.
    • Clean the diaphragm and bell of your stethoscope with an alcohol wipe. This is a key step that demonstrates good practice and provides patient reassurance.
  3. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to sit upright on the edge of the examination couch. This allows for optimal examination of both the front and back of the chest. If the patient cannot sit, position them at a 45-degree angle.
    • Preserve the patient's dignity by asking them to undress to the waist and providing a sheet or blanket to cover themselves when not being actively examined. Explain that you will need to examine both the front and back.
  4. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself clearly with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, my name is [Name] and I am a foundation doctor").
    • Explain the examination in simple terms and gain consent. A good phrase is: "I've been asked to examine your breathing. This will involve me looking at your chest, feeling for the airflow, tapping on your back, and then having a listen with my stethoscope. Would that be okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent, systematic approach (Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, Auscultation) reduces the chance of missing important signs. The examination should be performed on both the anterior and posterior chest wall, always comparing left to right.

  1. General Inspection: From the end of the bed, look for breathlessness (and use of accessory muscles), audible wheeze, cough, stridor, pallor, or cachexia. Note any equipment like oxygen masks, nebulisers, or sputum pots.
  2. The Hands: Inspect for finger clubbing, tar staining (from smoking), and peripheral cyanosis. Check for a fine tremor (can be caused by salbutamol use) and asterixis (CO2 retention flap).
  3. The Face & Neck: Check for central cyanosis (under the tongue) and signs of Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhydrosis), which can indicate an apical lung tumour. Assess the Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP) as a sign of right heart strain (cor pulmonale). Palpate for lymphadenopathy, especially in the supraclavicular fossae.
  4. The Chest - Inspection: Look for scars (thoracotomy, chest drain), chest wall deformities (barrel chest, pectus excavatum/carinatum), and asymmetry of chest wall movement. Count the respiratory rate for one full minute.
  5. The Chest - Palpation:
    • Assess tracheal position to check if it is central.
    • Assess chest expansion by placing your hands on the anterior and posterior chest and asking the patient to take a deep breath. Note the degree and symmetry of movement.
    • Assess tactile vocal fremitus by feeling for transmitted vibrations as the patient repeats "ninety-nine".
  6. The Chest - Percussion: Percuss symmetrically across all lung zones on the front and back, comparing the note from side to side. Listen for a normal resonant note, a dull note (consolidation/effusion), or a hyper-resonant note (pneumothorax).
  7. The Chest - Auscultation: Using the diaphragm of the stethoscope, listen to all lung zones as the patient breathes deeply through their mouth.
    • Identify the quality of the breath sounds (e.g., normal vesicular, bronchial).
    • Listen for any added sounds, such as wheezes, crackles (crepitations), or a pleural rub.
    • Assess vocal resonance by listening for the clarity of transmitted sound as the patient whispers "ninety-nine".
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising what is normal is a prerequisite for identifying pathology. A normal respiratory examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • General Appearance: The patient is comfortable at rest, with a respiratory rate of 12-20 breaths per minute and no use of accessory muscles.
  • Peripheries: No finger clubbing, tar staining, or cyanosis.
  • Chest Inspection: Symmetrical chest wall movement during respiration. No visible deformities or scars.
  • Palpation: Trachea is central. Chest expansion is equal bilaterally. Tactile vocal fremitus is present and equal.
  • Percussion: A resonant percussion note is heard symmetrically over all lung zones.
  • Auscultation: Normal vesicular breath sounds are heard throughout both lungs. There are no added sounds (wheezes, crackles, or rubs). Vocal resonance is normal.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Pathological signs are objective evidence of underlying disease. The key is to interpret individual signs in the context of the whole clinical picture.

Tracheal deviation
  • The Sign: The trachea is felt to be shifted from its central position in the suprasternal notch.
  • Pathology Link: It can be 'pushed' away from the lesion (e.g., by a large pleural effusion or tension pneumothorax) or 'pulled' towards the lesion (e.g., by upper lobe collapse or fibrosis).
Dull percussion note
  • The Sign: A thud-like, non-resonant sound on percussion.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates increased density of the tissue beneath, replacing the normal air-filled lung. This is found in consolidation (pneumonia), pleural effusion, collapse, or a large tumour. A very dull note is described as "stony dull" and is characteristic of a pleural effusion.
Hyper-resonant percussion note
  • The Sign: A booming, low-pitched sound on percussion.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates an abnormal amount of air in the thoracic cavity, most classically found in a pneumothorax. It may also be present in severe emphysema due to air trapping.
Bronchial breathing
  • The Sign: Harsh, hollow-sounding breath sounds with a distinct pause between inspiration and expiration, heard in an area where normal vesicular sounds are expected.
  • Pathology Link: It indicates that sound is being transmitted more effectively through consolidated (solidified) lung tissue than through normal air-filled lung. It is a key sign of pneumonia.
Wheeze (rhonchi)
  • The Sign: A continuous, musical, high-pitched sound, usually heard on expiration.
  • Pathology Link: Caused by airflow through narrowed lower airways. A diffuse, "polyphonic" wheeze is characteristic of widespread airway narrowing in asthma or COPD. A single, "monophonic" wheeze is a red flag for a focal obstruction, such as a tumour or foreign body.
Crackles (crepitations)
  • The Sign: Discontinuous, "popping" sounds heard during inspiration.
  • Pathology Link: Can be fine or coarse. Fine crackles are caused by the explosive opening of small airways and alveoli collapsed by fluid or fibrosis (e.g., pulmonary fibrosis, pulmonary oedema). Coarse crackles are from air bubbling through secretions in the larger airways (e.g., pneumonia, bronchiectasis).
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating your findings. Report the pertinent positive and negative findings in a logical sequence, avoiding simple lists of what you did.

On general inspection, the patient appeared [e.g., comfortable at rest / breathless with use of accessory muscles], with a respiratory rate of [rate].
There were no peripheral stigmata of respiratory disease such as clubbing or cyanosis.
On examination of the chest, there were [e.g., no scars or deformities].
The trachea was [central / deviated to the...]. Chest expansion was [equal / reduced on the...].
The percussion note was [resonant throughout / dull over the... / hyper-resonant over the...].
On auscultation, breath sounds were [e.g., vesicular and equal bilaterally / bronchial over the...].
There were [no added sounds / description of wheeze, crackles etc. and their location].
Vocal resonance was [normal / increased / decreased].
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

A constellation of signs is more powerful than a single finding. Correlating patterns of signs from inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation allows you to build a strong diagnostic hypothesis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Fever, localised reduced expansion, dull percussion, bronchial breathing, and coarse crackles. Lobar Pneumonia (Consolidation)
Reduced expansion, stony dull percussion, and reduced/absent breath sounds, typically at a lung base. Pleural Effusion
Unilateral reduced expansion, hyper-resonant percussion, and absent breath sounds. Trachea may be deviated. Pneumothorax
Bilateral fine end-inspiratory crackles (like Velcro), often with finger clubbing. Interstitial Lung Disease (e.g., Pulmonary Fibrosis)
Barrel-shaped chest, pursed-lip breathing, symmetrically reduced expansion, widespread polyphonic wheeze. COPD / Severe Asthma
9. Relevant next-step investigations

The examination findings are crucial for guiding the selection of appropriate investigations to confirm a diagnosis, assess severity, and plan management.

  • If you find signs of consolidation (pneumonia):
    • Logical Next Steps: A Chest X-ray (CXR) is essential. Blood tests including a Full Blood Count (FBC) and C-Reactive Protein (CRP) are required. A sputum sample for microscopy and culture is also vital.
  • If you find signs of a pleural effusion or pneumothorax:
    • Logical Next Steps: A Chest X-ray is the key initial investigation. An ultrasound of the chest can be very useful for characterising and guiding drainage of an effusion.
  • If you suspect airway obstruction (asthma/COPD):
    • Logical Next Steps: Spirometry (lung function tests) with reversibility testing is the definitive investigation. A Chest X-ray is often performed to exclude other pathology.
  • If you suspect interstitial lung disease:
    • Logical Next Steps: A Chest X-ray is the first step, but a High-Resolution CT (HRCT) scan of the chest is required for detailed assessment. Lung function tests are also essential.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good exam technique is essential for a competent performance, especially in an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to examine the back of the chest, or not exposing it adequately.
    • Failing to compare one side with the other for every step (palpation, percussion, auscultation).
    • Percussing over the scapula, which will always be dull, instead of in the intercostal spaces around it.
    • Forgetting to examine the supraclavicular fossae for lymph nodes and lung apices.
    • Not asking the patient to take deep breaths through their mouth during auscultation, which makes breath sounds much harder to hear.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start with: Wash hands, introduce yourself, explain, and gain consent.
    • Be systematic and follow the same IPPA sequence every time.
    • Verbalise your actions and findings as you go (e.g., "I am now percussing the anterior chest, comparing left to right... the percussion note is resonant bilaterally."). This shows the examiner your thought process.
    • If you find an abnormality (e.g., reduced breath sounds), demonstrate you know how to assess it further (e.g., by checking vocal resonance in that area).
    • At the end, always state how you would complete the examination: "To complete my examination, I would check the patient's oxygen saturations and temperature."
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings helps to solidify understanding and build clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Right Lower Lobe Pneumonia
    • Scenario: A 68-year-old man presents with a 3-day history of fever, shortness of breath, and a cough productive of rusty-coloured sputum.
    • Expected Findings: He may be tachypnoeic (fast respiratory rate). Examination of the right lower chest would likely reveal reduced chest expansion, a dull percussion note, increased vocal resonance/fremitus, and bronchial breathing with coarse crackles over the affected area.
    • Summary: These are the classic signs of consolidation, consistent with a right lower lobe pneumonia.
  • Case 2: Acute Severe Asthma
    • Scenario: A 22-year-old known asthmatic presents to the emergency department with severe breathlessness, unable to speak in full sentences.
    • Expected Findings: The patient will be visibly distressed, using accessory muscles of respiration with a very high respiratory rate. There will be a widespread, loud, polyphonic expiratory wheeze. In very severe attacks, the chest may become "silent" as airflow is too poor to generate a wheeze - a life-threatening sign.
    • Summary: These signs indicate severe airway obstruction requiring immediate emergency treatment.
  • Case 3: Large Left-Sided Pleural Effusion
    • Scenario: A 70-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer presents with a month of progressive, non-pleuritic breathlessness.
    • Expected Findings: On inspection, there may be reduced movement of the left side of the chest. The trachea may be deviated to the right. Palpation would confirm reduced expansion on the left. Percussion would be stony dull over the left base, and auscultation would reveal absent breath sounds and reduced vocal resonance in the same area.
    • Summary: The findings are classical for a large left-sided pleural effusion.
