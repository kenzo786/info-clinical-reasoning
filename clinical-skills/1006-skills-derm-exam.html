1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of a dermatological examination is to accurately identify, describe, and categorise skin lesions in order to form a differential diagnosis. It is a visual and tactile process designed to detect signs of inflammatory conditions (e.g., eczema, psoriasis), infections (bacterial, fungal, viral), and skin malignancies (e.g., melanoma, basal cell carcinoma). The examination encompasses not only the skin but also the hair, nails, and mucous membranes. Findings must be integrated with the patient's history to build a complete clinical picture and identify signs requiring urgent investigation, such as a changing mole suspicious for melanoma.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

In dermatology, the "anatomy" is the specific language used to describe lesions (morphology) and their layout (distribution and configuration). A shared vocabulary is essential for accurate communication and diagnosis.

  • Primary Lesions (unaltered skin):
    • Macule: A flat area of altered colour, <1cm.
    • Patch: A macule >1cm.
    • Papule: A solid, raised lesion <1cm.
    • Plaque: A palpable, flat-topped lesion >1cm (a larger papule).
    • Nodule: A solid, raised lesion with a deeper component, >1cm.
    • Vesicle: A fluid-filled blister <1cm.
    • Bulla: A fluid-filled blister >1cm.
    • Pustule: A pus-containing lesion.
    • Weal: A transient, raised lesion due to dermal oedema (as in urticaria).
  • Secondary Lesions (changes from primary lesions or trauma):
    • Scale: A visible flake of shedding skin.
    • Crust: Dried exudate (serum, blood, or pus) on the skin surface.
    • Erosion: A superficial loss of epidermis.
    • Ulcer: A deeper loss of epidermis and dermis.
    • Lichenification: Thickening of the skin with accentuation of skin markings, from chronic rubbing.
  • Distribution: The location of lesions on the body (e.g., flexural, extensor, photodistributed, dermatomal).
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional and effective dermatological examination requires good preparation and clear communication to ensure patient comfort and dignity.

  1. Equipment:
    • Excellent lighting, preferably natural daylight.
    • A flexible, transparent ruler to measure lesions.
    • A magnifying lens or, if trained, a dermatoscope.
    • Gloves.
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ensure the room is warm and private. A chaperone should always be offered.
    • Ask the patient to undress to their underwear, providing a gown or sheet to maintain dignity, explaining that you will need to look at all areas of their skin.
    • The examination may require the patient to sit, stand, and lie down to visualise all skin surfaces.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself clearly with your name and role.
    • Explain the examination in simple terms and gain consent. A good phrase is: "To get a full picture of your skin problem, I need to perform a head-to-toe examination. This will involve looking at your scalp, nails, and in between your toes. A chaperone is available if you would like one. Would that be okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A systematic head-to-toe approach ensures no area is missed. The examination is primarily one of inspection, supplemented by palpation.

  1. General Inspection: From a short distance, assess the overall distribution and pattern of any rash or lesions. Note any obvious scars or signs of systemic illness.
  2. The Head and Neck: Inspect the face, ears, and neck. Carefully examine the scalp by parting the hair. Check the mucous membranes of the mouth and conjunctivae.
  3. The Trunk: Inspect the anterior chest, abdomen, and back. Remember to check skin folds, such as under the breasts and in the axillae.
  4. The Limbs:
    • Arms & Hands: Examine the arms, paying attention to the extensor (elbows) and flexural (antecubital fossae) surfaces. Inspect the palms and the spaces between the fingers.
    • Legs & Feet: Examine the legs, paying close attention to the shins, knees, and ankles. Inspect the soles of the feet and the spaces between the toes.
  5. Nails: Inspect the fingernails and toenails for any changes like pitting, onycholysis (lifting of the nail plate), or splinter haemorrhages.
  6. Detailed Lesion Assessment: For any significant lesion or rash identified, perform a detailed "close-up" examination:
    • Inspect: Describe the lesion using formal terminology (Size, Colour, Shape, Border). For pigmented lesions, systematically apply the ABCDE criteria (Asymmetry, Border, Colour, Diameter >6mm, Evolution).
    • Palpate: Wearing gloves, gently feel the lesion's texture (e.g., scaly, smooth), consistency (soft, firm, indurated), and temperature. Note any tenderness.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising what is normal is fundamental to identifying pathology. A normal examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • General Appearance: The skin appears intact, well-hydrated, and of a uniform colour appropriate for the patient's ethnicity.
    • Significance: The absence of rashes, ulcers, or suspicious growths makes a significant active dermatological process less likely.
  • Lesions: No suspicious (e.g., ABCDE-positive) lesions are present. Benign lesions like simple freckles or seborrhoeic keratoses may be present.
    • Significance: The absence of lesions concerning for malignancy is a key and reassuring finding.
  • Appendages: The hair is of normal texture and evenly distributed. The nails are smooth and translucent, with a pink nail bed, and no pitting or deformity.
    • Significance: Normal hair and nails suggest the absence of many systemic diseases, autoimmune conditions, and fungal infections.
  • Mucous Membranes: The conjunctivae and oral mucosa are pink and moist, with no evidence of pallor, ulceration, or blistering.
    • Significance: Normal mucous membranes make severe drug reactions or blistering disorders less likely.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Specific lesions and patterns of distribution are objective evidence of underlying disease processes. Some findings are red flags that necessitate urgent referral.

Malignant melanoma features
  • The Sign: A pigmented lesion with features of the ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, an irregular Border, Colour variegation, Diameter >6mm, and Evolution (history of change).
  • Pathology Link: Reflects the uncontrolled proliferation of malignant melanocytes.
  • Red Flag: Any lesion with these features, particularly a changing mole, is suspicious for malignant melanoma and requires an urgent two-week-wait referral for excision and biopsy.
Non-melanoma skin cancer features
  • The Sign: For Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC), a pearly or translucent nodule with a rolled edge and surface telangiectasia (fine blood vessels). For Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC), a persistent, firm, keratotic (scaly/crusty) papule or nodule, which may ulcerate.
  • Pathology Link: Malignant proliferation of basal cells (BCC) or keratinocytes (SCC), strongly linked to cumulative sun exposure.
Psoriatic plaques
  • The Sign: Well-demarcated, erythematous (red) plaques with an overlying silvery scale. Typically on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees) and scalp. May be associated with nail pitting.
  • Pathology Link: An immune-mediated condition causing hyperproliferation of keratinocytes.
Eczematous patches
  • The Sign: Ill-defined, erythematous patches with dryness, scale, and possibly excoriations or lichenification. Typically affects flexural surfaces in atopic eczema.
  • Pathology Link: Inflammation of the epidermis, often related to a compromised skin barrier and allergic sensitisation.
Burrows
  • The Sign: Fine, wavy, greyish lines on the skin, typically found in the finger web spaces, on the wrists, and in the axillae.
  • Pathology Link: This sign is pathognomonic (uniquely characteristic) of Scabies, caused by the mite burrowing into the epidermis.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary using standardised dermatological language is essential for communicating your findings to colleagues.

On examination of the skin, this patient has [e.g., a solitary lesion / a widespread polymorphic rash].

Focusing on the main presentation, there are [description of morphology, e.g., well-demarcated erythematous plaques with a silver scale], measuring up to [size] cm in diameter.

The lesions are distributed [symmetrically/asymmetrically] on the [location, e.g., extensor surfaces of the elbows and knees, and scalp].

On examination of the nails, there is evidence of [e.g., pitting / onycholysis]. The hair and mucous membranes are [unremarkable].

There are no other skin lesions of concern. In particular, there are no lesions with features of malignant melanoma.
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

A constellation of signs, including morphology and distribution, is powerful evidence for a specific diagnosis.

9. Relevant next-step investigations

The findings on examination guide the selection of appropriate investigations to confirm a diagnosis and guide management.

  • If you find a lesion suspicious for Melanoma:
    • Logical Next Step: An urgent excision biopsy (removing the entire lesion with a 2mm margin) is the gold-standard investigation. This is typically done via a two-week-wait referral to dermatology.
  • If you find a lesion suspicious for BCC or SCC:
    • Logical Next Step: A diagnostic biopsy (e.g., a punch, shave, or incisional biopsy) is required to confirm the histology before planning definitive treatment.
  • If you suspect a fungal infection (e.g., tinea):
    • Logical Next Steps: Skin scrapings, nail clippings, or plucked hairs should be sent for microscopy and culture (M&C) to identify the fungal species.
  • If you are uncertain about a diagnosis of an inflammatory dermatosis:
    • Logical Next Step: A punch biopsy can be taken from a representative area of the rash for histopathological analysis to differentiate between conditions like psoriasis, eczema, and lichen planus.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good exam technique is essential for a safe and accurate examination, especially in a practical assessment.

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to offer a chaperone or ensure the room is warm, making the patient uncomfortable.
    • Inadequate exposure, leading to missed lesions on the back, scalp, or intertriginous areas.
    • Not having adequate lighting to properly assess lesion colour and morphology.
    • Forgetting to examine the nails, hair, and mucous membranes.
    • Vague descriptions of lesions instead of using precise dermatological terminology.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start by washing your hands, introducing yourself, explaining the procedure, and gaining consent.
    • Verbalise what you are doing as you go (e.g., "I am now inspecting the posterior trunk..."). This shows the examiner your systematic approach.
    • When you find a lesion, describe it fully using a structured approach like "Size, Shape, Colour, Border..." before offering a diagnosis.
    • If you find a pigmented lesion, state that you would use the ABCDE criteria to assess it.
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant next steps, such as using a dermatoscope (if trained) or taking a biopsy if indicated.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings helps to build clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Nodular Basal Cell Carcinoma
    • Scenario: An 80-year-old man presents with a non-healing "spot" on his temple that sometimes bleeds.
    • Expected Findings: On the right temple, you would expect to find a solitary, skin-coloured or pinkish papule, approximately 8mm in diameter, with a classic pearly appearance and a slightly rolled border. Fine, linear blood vessels (telangiectasia) would be visible on the surface. The lesion would be firm and non-tender on palpation.
  • Case 2: Guttate Psoriasis
    • Scenario: A 22-year-old student develops a sudden, widespread rash two weeks after having a sore throat.
    • Expected Findings: You would expect to find multiple, discrete, small (2-10mm), 'tear-drop' shaped erythematous papules and small plaques scattered widely across the trunk and proximal limbs. A fine, silvery scale may be present on the surface of the lesions.
  • Case 3: Scabies
    • Scenario: A 28-year-old healthcare assistant presents with a intensely itchy rash, which is worse at night and has also affected her partner.
    • Expected Findings: The key findings would be multiple, excoriated papules and fine, grey, thread-like lines (burrows) located predominantly in the finger web spaces, on the flexor aspects of the wrists, and in the axillae.
