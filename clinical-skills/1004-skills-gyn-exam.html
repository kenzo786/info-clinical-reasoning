1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the gynaecological examination is to identify physical signs of pathology within the female reproductive system, serving as a crucial counterpart to the clinical history. The examination is a systematic process designed to detect evidence of infection (e.g., pelvic inflammatory disease, vaginitis), structural abnormalities (e.g., uterine fibroids, ovarian cysts, pelvic organ prolapse), and malignancy (e.g., cervical or uterine cancer). A key aim is to correlate physical findings, such as localised tenderness, palpable masses, or visible lesions, with the patient's reported symptoms. This allows the clinician to narrow the differential diagnosis and identify urgent conditions requiring immediate action, such as an ectopic pregnancy or tubo-ovarian abscess.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A sound knowledge of pelvic anatomy is essential for performing and interpreting the gynaecological examination correctly.

  • External Genitalia (The Vulva): This includes the mons pubis, the labia majora and minora, the clitoris anteriorly, and the vestibule, which contains the urethral meatus and the vaginal introitus (opening).
  • Vagina and Fornices: The vagina is a muscular tube leading to the cervix. The gutters around the cervix are known as the fornices (anterior, posterior, and two lateral). These are important during bimanual palpation for accessing the adnexa and assessing for tenderness.
  • The Cervix: The "neck of the womb" that protrudes into the top of the vagina. It should appear pink and smooth. The external os is the opening in the centre; it is a small round hole in women who have not given birth vaginally (nulliparous) and a horizontal slit in those who have (parous).
  • The Uterus: A pear-shaped organ that sits behind the bladder. In most women, it is 'anteverted' (tilted forward) and 'anteflexed' (flexed forward). Its size, shape, position, and mobility are assessed during bimanual palpation.
  • The Adnexa: This term refers to the structures adjacent to the uterus, namely the ovaries and fallopian tubes. In a healthy state, they are often not palpable. Tenderness or a palpable mass in the adnexal regions is a key pathological finding.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional, sensitive, and safe examination requires meticulous preparation and clear communication.

  1. Equipment:
    • Examination couch with a disposable sheet.
    • An appropriate light source.
    • Non-sterile gloves.
    • Water-based lubricant.
    • Cusco's speculums of various sizes (small, medium, large).
    • Any necessary sampling equipment (e.g., swabs for microbiology, cervical screening kit).
  2. Preparation and Chaperoning:
    • Ask the patient to empty her bladder before the examination for comfort and ease of palpation.
    • A chaperone must be offered and present for any intimate examination. This is a professional and legal requirement to protect both the patient and the clinician. Document the presence and name of the chaperone.
  3. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to lie on the examination couch and provide a sheet to cover themselves. For the examination, they should lie on their back with their heels drawn up towards their bottom, and then let their knees fall open to the sides.
  4. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself and the chaperone clearly.
    • Explain the procedure in simple terms, what it involves, and why it is necessary. A good phrase is: "Following our discussion, I need to perform an internal examination. A chaperone will be present with us throughout. It will involve me first looking at the outside, then using a small plastic instrument to look at the neck of your womb, and finally using my fingers to feel your womb and ovaries. It shouldn't be painful, but please tell me to stop at any time. Would that be okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent sequence ensures all steps are completed thoroughly. The examination begins with a general assessment and abdominal palpation before proceeding to the pelvic examination.

  1. General Inspection: From the end of the bed, note if the patient appears comfortable or in pain, and look for signs of pallor (anaemia) or cachexia.
  2. Abdominal Examination: This must be performed first. Inspect for scars or masses, then palpate all nine regions, paying close attention to the suprapubic area. Feel for any pelvic masses arising into the abdomen and assess for tenderness, guarding, or rebound tenderness.
  3. Inspection of the External Genitalia: With the patient correctly positioned, gently inspect the vulva for any ulcers, warts, sores, rashes, abnormal discharge, or visible signs of prolapse.
  4. Speculum Examination:
    • Select an appropriately sized speculum, warm it with water, and lubricate it if necessary.
    • Gently part the labia and insert the speculum with the blades closed, angled downwards and backwards. Rotate it 90 degrees to its final position and slowly open the blades to visualise the cervix.
    • Inspect the cervix for its overall appearance, noting any lesions, ulcers, polyps, or abnormal discharge from the os. If required, take any necessary swabs or samples at this point.
    • As you slowly withdraw the speculum, inspect the vaginal walls for redness, discharge, or other lesions.
  5. Bimanual Palpation:
    • Put on a new pair of gloves and lubricate the index and middle fingers of your dominant hand. Inform the patient you are about to begin.
    • Gently insert your two fingers into the vagina. Place your other hand on the lower abdomen (suprapubic region).
    • Palpate the cervix to assess its position and consistency. Gently move the cervix side-to-side to check for cervical motion tenderness (also known as cervical excitation), a key sign of pelvic inflammation.
    • Palpate the uterus by elevating the cervix with your internal hand and pressing down with your external hand. Assess its size, shape, position (anteverted/retroverted), and mobility. Note any tenderness.
    • Palpate the adnexa by moving your internal fingers into the left lateral fornix and then the right, sweeping downwards with your external hand to feel for any adnexal masses or tenderness.
  6. Completing the Examination: Gently withdraw your fingers. Clean any excess lubricant from the patient with a paper towel. Cover the patient with the sheet and allow them to dress in private before discussing the findings.
5. Interpreting normal findings

A normal examination provides a reassuring baseline. It is vital to be able to recognise and document normal findings confidently.

  • Abdomen: Soft and non-tender, with no palpable masses arising from the pelvis.
  • External Genitalia: Normal appearance with no visible lesions, rashes, or swelling.
  • Vaginal Walls: Appear pink and well-rugae (wrinkled) in a pre-menopausal woman. No inflammation or lesions. A small amount of non-offensive, white or clear discharge can be physiological.
  • Cervix: Appears pink and smooth, with no visible lesions. The os is closed and round (nulliparous) or slit-like (parous).
  • Bimanual Palpation:
    • The uterus is typically anteverted, smooth, non-tender, and not enlarged (approximately the size of a pear).
    • The cervix is mobile and non-tender (no cervical motion tenderness).
    • The adnexal areas (fornices) are soft and non-tender, with no palpable masses.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Deviations from the normal findings are key to diagnosis. Some represent red flags requiring urgent action.

Cervical motion tenderness (CMT)
  • The Sign: The patient experiences sharp pain upon gentle movement of the cervix during bimanual examination. Also known as "cervical excitation".
  • Pathology Link: Indicates inflammation of the peritoneum overlying the pelvic organs.
  • Clinical Significance: A classic sign of Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID). It is also a key red flag sign for a possible ectopic pregnancy.
Adnexal mass or tenderness
  • The Sign: A palpable mass or distinct tenderness felt in the area of the ovaries and fallopian tubes (the adnexa).
  • Pathology Link: Suggests the presence of an ovarian cyst, an ovarian tumour (benign or malignant), a tubo-ovarian abscess, or a hydrosalpinx.
  • Red Flag: An adnexal mass in the presence of a positive pregnancy test is highly suspicious of an ectopic pregnancy until proven otherwise.
Uterine enlargement
  • The Sign: On bimanual palpation, the uterus feels larger than its normal pear size. It may feel smooth and uniform, or irregular and "knobbly".
  • Pathology Link: Can be physiological (pregnancy) or pathological. A bulky, irregular uterus is characteristic of uterine fibroids. A symmetrically enlarged, tender uterus can suggest adenomyosis.
Visible cervical abnormality
  • The Sign: An obvious lesion on the cervix seen during speculum examination, such as an ulcer, a raised mass, or an irregular growth that may bleed easily on contact.
  • Pathology Link: Highly concerning for cervical cancer. Other possibilities include large cervical polyps or ectropion.
  • Red Flag: Any suspicious visible lesion on the cervix requires urgent referral to a colposcopy clinic for biopsy, regardless of cervical screening history.
Abnormal vaginal discharge
  • The Sign: Discharge that is purulent (pus-like), blood-stained, malodorous, or has a characteristic appearance.
  • Pathology Link: A thick, white, "cottage cheese" discharge is classic for vaginal candidiasis. A thin, grey-white, fishy-smelling discharge suggests bacterial vaginosis. A yellow-green, frothy discharge suggests trichomoniasis. Purulent discharge from the cervical os suggests cervicitis (e.g. from chlamydia).
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A structured summary conveys the findings clearly and professionally. Report pertinent positive and negative findings in a logical sequence.

A chaperone was present throughout the examination.
On general inspection, the patient appeared well.
The abdomen was soft and non-tender with no palpable masses.
On inspection of the external genitalia, there were no abnormalities noted.
Speculum examination revealed a [e.g., healthy-looking / erythematous] cervix and vaginal walls. There was [e.g., no abnormal discharge / a description of discharge].
On bimanual examination, the uterus was [size, e.g., not enlarged], [position, e.g., anteverted] and [mobile/non-tender].
There was [no / significant] cervical motion tenderness.
The adnexa were [soft and non-tender / description of any mass or tenderness].
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

Combining signs from the examination with the patient's history creates a powerful diagnostic tool.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Unilateral adnexal tenderness, cervical motion tenderness, positive pregnancy test. Ectopic Pregnancy
Bilateral lower abdominal and adnexal tenderness, fever, purulent cervical discharge, cervical motion tenderness. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
Bulky, irregular, non-tender uterus on bimanual palpation. Uterine Fibroids
Visible, hard, irregular cervical lesion that bleeds on contact. Cervical Cancer
9. Relevant next-step investigations

Examination findings directly guide the choice of initial investigations to confirm a diagnosis and plan management.

  • If you find signs of PID or vaginitis (e.g., discharge, tenderness):
    • Logical Next Steps: High vaginal and endocervical swabs for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity, plus specific tests (NAAT) for chlamydia and gonorrhoea. Bloods including FBC and CRP.
  • If you suspect an ectopic pregnancy or adnexal mass:
    • Logical Next Steps: Urgent urine human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) test. If positive, or if suspicion remains high, an urgent transvaginal ultrasound scan is the definitive imaging modality.
  • If you find a bulky uterus or suspect fibroids/polyps:
    • Logical Next Steps: A transvaginal ultrasound scan is the primary investigation to assess uterine and endometrial morphology.
  • If you find a suspicious cervical lesion:
    • Logical Next Steps: An urgent 'two-week wait' referral to a colposcopy clinic for detailed examination and biopsy of the lesion.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Success in a gynaecological examination, particularly in an OSCE, depends on a slick, systematic, and sensitive approach.

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to offer and document the presence of a chaperone.
    • Failing to perform an abdominal examination before the pelvic examination.
    • Causing unnecessary discomfort by using a speculum that is too large or not lubricated.
    • Failing to correctly identify the cervix, often due to not inserting the speculum at the correct angle.
    • Forgetting to assess for cervical motion tenderness, a critical sign.
    • Poor communication, leading to patient anxiety and muscular tension, making the examination difficult or impossible.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Start perfectly: Wash hands, introduce yourself and the chaperone, explain the procedure clearly, and gain explicit consent.
    • Verbalise your actions throughout (e.g., "I am now inspecting the cervix, looking for any abnormalities..."). This demonstrates your knowledge to the examiner.
    • Always be gentle and communicate with your patient, checking they are comfortable.
    • If you cannot visualise the cervix, withdraw the speculum slightly and re-angle it before opening again.
    • At the end, always state how you would complete your assessment: "To complete my examination, I would take relevant swabs, perform a urinalysis, and review the patient's previous notes and investigations."
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected examination findings helps to build robust clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Acute Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
    • Scenario: A 22-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of worsening bilateral lower abdominal pain, fever, and a new vaginal discharge.
    • Expected Findings: She may look unwell and have a fever. The abdomen would be tender in the suprapubic region. Speculum exam would likely show a purulent discharge from the cervical os. The key finding on bimanual examination would be significant cervical motion tenderness and bilateral adnexal tenderness.
  • Case 2: Uterine Fibroids
    • Scenario: A 42-year-old woman presents with a long history of heavy, prolonged menstrual periods and a feeling of pressure in her pelvis.
    • Expected Findings: Abdominal palpation may reveal a firm, non-tender mass arising from the pelvis. Bimanual examination would confirm a significantly enlarged, bulky, and irregularly shaped uterus, often described as "knobbly". The uterus would be non-tender.
  • Case 3: Atrophic Vaginitis
    • Scenario: A 68-year-old postmenopausal woman reports vaginal soreness, dryness, and some spotting of blood.
    • Expected Findings: The vulva may appear pale and thin. Speculum examination would reveal pale, smooth, and shiny vaginal walls with a loss of the normal rugae. The tissue may look inflamed and friable, with small petechial haemorrhages or areas that bleed easily on contact with the speculum.
