Neurological System History
1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a neurological history is to answer two fundamental questions: "Where is the lesion?" and "What is the lesion?". This involves first localising the problem to a specific part of the nervous system (e.g., cerebral hemisphere, brainstem, spinal cord, peripheral nerve, or muscle) and then determining the nature of the pathology. The temporal pattern of the symptoms is the most powerful clue to the underlying aetiology. For example, a sudden onset suggests a vascular event like a stroke, a subacute progression points towards an inflammatory or infective cause, and a chronic, insidious course is typical of a degenerative condition.

Unlike other histories, a neurological history often relies heavily on a collateral history from a family member, friend, or witness. This is essential when the patient has cognitive impairment, altered consciousness, or a lack of insight into their condition. The process is a form of clinical detective work, piecing together clues from the symptom timeline, associated features, and risk factors to build a coherent neurological formulation.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective and sensitive communication is paramount. Neurological symptoms can be frightening, stigmatising, and profoundly impact a person's identity and independence. It is crucial to create a safe, non-judgemental environment where patients feel comfortable discussing sensitive issues like memory loss, incontinence, or behavioural changes.

  • Collateral History: Always consider the need for a collateral history. When assessing a patient with a seizure, blackout, or cognitive decline, an account from a witness or family member is invaluable. Frame this as a routine part of the assessment: "To get the clearest possible picture, it's often helpful to speak to someone who has seen what happens. Is there anyone with you today who could help with that?"
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Begin with broad, open-ended questions ("Tell me in your own words what has been happening?"). Allow the patient to describe their experience, then use more focused, closed questions to clarify specific details of timing, location, and character.
  • Active Listening & Empathy: Neurological symptoms are often bizarre and difficult to describe. Listen actively, show empathy, and validate the patient's experience. This builds trust and encourages them to share more information.
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): Exploring ICE is vital. Patients often harbour deep-seated fears about conditions like brain tumours, stroke, or dementia. Directly asking, "Was there anything specific you were worried this might be?" allows you to address these fears and tailor your explanation and management plan.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

The neurological system presents with a wide array of symptoms. The key is to characterise them fully and recognise patterns that point to urgent underlying pathology.

  • Headache: A very common symptom. The goal is to identify features of a secondary, and potentially life-threatening, cause.
    • Red Flag: A sudden, severe, "thunderclap" headache (peaking within a minute) suggests a subarachnoid haemorrhage. Other red flags include new-onset headache in someone over 50, headache with fever and neck stiffness (meningitis), or a headache that is progressively worsening and associated with new neurological signs (space-occupying lesion).
  • Weakness: Loss of motor power. The distribution of weakness is key to localisation.
    • Red Flag: Sudden onset of unilateral weakness suggests a stroke. Rapidly ascending bilateral weakness could be Guillain-Barré syndrome. Weakness associated with a clear sensory level and bladder/bowel dysfunction indicates acute spinal cord compression or cauda equina syndrome.
  • Seizures or Blackouts: A transient alteration of consciousness or behaviour. A witness account is essential.
    • Red Flag: A first-ever seizure in an adult requires urgent investigation for a structural cause. Status epilepticus (a seizure lasting >5 minutes, or >2 seizures without recovery in between) is a medical emergency.
  • Dizziness: It is crucial to differentiate true vertigo (a spinning sensation) from pre-syncope (feeling faint) or general imbalance.
    • Red Flag: Acute, persistent vertigo associated with other brainstem signs (e.g., new slurred speech, facial weakness, limb ataxia) is a red flag for a posterior circulation stroke.
  • Sensory Disturbance: Numbness (anaesthesia) or abnormal sensations like tingling (paraesthesia).
    • Red Flag: "Saddle anaesthesia" (numbness in the perineal area) combined with sphincter disturbance is a hallmark of cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

A structured framework is needed to analyse neurological symptoms. While a mnemonic like SOCRATES can be a starting point, a more nuanced approach focusing on timing, distribution, and associated features is often more effective. This framework helps build the picture of localisation and aetiology.

  • Timing & Onset: This is the most important clue to the cause.
    • Sudden (seconds/minutes): Suggests a vascular cause (e.g., ischaemic stroke, haemorrhage) or seizure.
    • Subacute (days/weeks): Suggests an inflammatory (e.g., multiple sclerosis relapse) or infective cause (e.g., abscess).
    • Chronic (months/years): Suggests a degenerative (e.g., Parkinson's disease), neoplastic, or genetic condition.
    • Pattern: Is it monophasic, progressive, or relapsing-remitting?
  • Distribution & Localisation: Where are the symptoms?
    • Is the weakness upper or lower motor neurone in pattern? Is it proximal or distal? Does it respect the midline?
    • Does the sensory loss fit a specific dermatome (nerve root), a peripheral nerve distribution ("glove and stocking"), or a cerebral pattern (e.g., hemisensory loss)?
  • Associated Symptoms (Positive & Negative):
    • Positive phenomena: The presence of abnormal sensations or movements (e.g., tingling, tremor, visual aura, epileptic phenomena).
    • Negative phenomena: The absence or loss of a function (e.g., weakness, numbness, visual field loss).
  • Triggers & Relieving Factors: Are symptoms worse with a specific action (e.g., tremor at rest vs. on intention), posture, or time of day?
5. System-specific review of systems

A focused review of the nervous system is essential for localisation. The questions are designed to screen for deficits in different anatomical areas, from the cerebral cortex down to the muscles.

  • Higher Cerebral Function: Any changes in memory, personality, or behaviour? Difficulties with speech (finding the right words or articulating them)? Problems with complex tasks like managing finances?
  • Cranial Nerves: Changes to sense of smell (anosmia)? Any blurring or loss of vision, double vision (diplopia), or flashing lights? Facial droop, numbness, or asymmetry? Problems with hearing, swallowing (dysphagia), or voice hoarseness?
  • Motor System: Any weakness, clumsiness, or heaviness in the limbs? Any muscle wasting or twitching (fasciculations)? Any stiffness or slowness of movement? Problems with balance or coordination (ataxia)?
  • Sensory System: Any numbness, tingling, or pins and needles? Does it affect one area, or is it in a "glove and stocking" distribution? Have you noticed difficulty feeling hot or cold?
  • Autonomic & Sphincters: Any problems with bladder control (hesitancy, urgency, incontinence)? Any issues with bowel function? Any dizziness on standing up?
  • General/Constitutional: Unexplained weight loss, fevers, or night sweats? These can suggest malignancy, systemic infection, or inflammatory conditions affecting the nervous system.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides the context for their symptoms and is essential for determining their risk profile for certain neurological conditions.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Focus on vascular risk factors: Hypertension, Diabetes, Atrial Fibrillation, Ischaemic Heart Disease, and Hypercholesterolaemia are critical in assessing stroke risk.
    • A history of malignancy is vital, as it could indicate brain or spinal metastases.
    • Ask about previous head injuries, autoimmune diseases, and prior neurological problems.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • Enquire about anticoagulants or antiplatelets (risk of intracranial bleeding).
    • Review medications that can lower the seizure threshold (e.g., some antidepressants, tramadol) or cause movement disorders (e.g., antipsychotics, metoclopramide) or peripheral neuropathy (e.g., some chemotherapies, isoniazid).
    • Specifically ask about alcohol intake and recreational drug use.
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask specifically about conditions that can be inherited, such as Epilepsy, Parkinson's disease, Huntington's disease, Motor Neurone Disease, Dementias, and Muscular Dystrophies.
    • A family history of migraine is also very common and relevant.
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Handedness: Crucial to establish as language function is localised to the dominant hemisphere (the left in >95% of right-handed individuals).
    • Function: This is a cornerstone of the neurological history. How do the symptoms affect activities of daily living (ADLs)? Can they wash, dress, cook? Can they still work or drive? Establishing a baseline is key.
    • Occupation and travel history can provide clues to toxic exposures or rare infections.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

A problem representation, or neurological formulation, is a concise summary that integrates the key historical features into a coherent statement. It should specify the patient's demographics, the temporal profile of the illness, the syndrome (the cluster of symptoms and signs), and the likely anatomical localisation and aetiology. This synthesis is the bridge from history taking to investigation and management.

The formula is often: [Age/Handedness/Sex] with [key risk factors] presents with a [temporal profile] of [syndrome], consistent with a lesion at [anatomical location] due to a likely [aetiology].

  • Example 1 (Stroke): "A 72-year-old, right-handed man with a history of hypertension and atrial fibrillation presents with a sudden-onset dysphasia and right hemiparesis, consistent with an acute ischaemic stroke in the left middle cerebral artery territory."
  • Example 2 (Spinal Cord Compression): "A 65-year-old woman with a known history of breast cancer presents with a four-week history of progressive thoracic back pain, bilateral leg weakness, and a sensory level at T8, highly suspicious for malignant spinal cord compression."
  • Example 3 (Multiple Sclerosis): "A 28-year-old woman presents with a five-day history of painful, blurring vision in her left eye, consistent with optic neuritis, likely representing a first demyelinating event of multiple sclerosis."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The differential diagnosis in neurology is generated by combining the anatomical location with the likely pathological process (often remembered by the mnemonic VINDICATE: Vascular, Infective, Neoplastic, Degenerative, Inflammatory, Congenital, Autoimmune, Traumatic, Endocrine/Metabolic).

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Sudden onset of a focal neurological deficit (e.g., hemiparesis, aphasia, hemisensory loss) in a patient with vascular risk factors. Stroke (Ischaemic or Haemorrhagic)
"Thunderclap" headache reaching maximum intensity in under one minute. Subarachnoid Haemorrhage
Headache, fever, neck stiffness, and photophobia developing over hours to days. Meningitis / Encephalitis
Subacute, progressive limb weakness with a sensory level and/or sphincter disturbance. Spinal Cord Compression
Slowly progressive, asymmetric resting tremor, bradykinesia, and rigidity in an older adult. Idiopathic Parkinson's Disease
Episodes of distinct neurological deficits (e.g., optic neuritis, transverse myelitis) separated in time and space in a younger adult. Multiple Sclerosis
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all key information is conveyed clearly, leading with the patient's details and the core problem before filling in the background context. This is vital for safe handovers and clear ward round presentations.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [right/left]-handed [man/woman].

They presented with [chief complaint], which began [timing, e.g., acutely this morning, or progressively over 6 months].

The primary symptoms are [describe the key positive and negative symptoms, e.g., weakness of the right arm and leg, with associated slurred speech].

Key features from the history of presenting complaint are [mention temporal profile, key characteristics, e.g., the weakness was sudden in onset and maximal at onset]. A collateral history from [e.g., their spouse] confirmed [e.g., a 2-minute witnessed tonic-clonic seizure].

There are no symptoms of [mention key negative findings, e.g., no headache, no sensory loss, no visual disturbance].

Relevant past medical history includes [list key conditions, e.g., atrial fibrillation, hypertension].
Current medications include [list key medications].
There is a family history of [e.g., early-onset dementia].
Socially, they live with [partner/alone] and were fully independent prior to this event.

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [handedness] [man/woman] with a background of [risk factors] presenting with [temporal profile] of [symptom cluster], with my main concern being a [top differential] localised to the [anatomical location].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly improve the quality and accuracy of the history, leading to a more precise neurological localisation and diagnosis.

  • Pitfall: Forgetting to ask for a collateral history, especially in patients with altered awareness, cognition, or consciousness.
  • Expert Tip: Make obtaining a collateral history a default step in cases of seizure, syncope, or suspected dementia. If no one is present, ask for a contact number to speak with a relative or witness.
  • Pitfall: Not clarifying what the patient means by "dizziness". This can refer to vertigo, pre-syncope, or imbalance, each with a different set of causes.
  • Expert Tip: Use direct questions to differentiate: "When you say dizzy, do you feel that you or the room is spinning (vertigo)? Or is it more a feeling that you might faint or pass out (pre-syncope)?"
  • Pitfall: Failing to accurately establish the temporal profile of the symptoms. This is often the single most important diagnostic clue.
  • Expert Tip: Create a timeline. Ask the patient "When were you last completely well?". Then map the sequence of events from that point. This is especially useful for complex, chronic, or relapsing conditions.
  • Pitfall: Missing the "cauda equina questions" in a patient with back pain or leg weakness.
  • Expert Tip: In any patient with significant back pain, bilateral leg symptoms, or leg weakness, always ask explicitly about bladder control (specifically asking about painless urinary retention), bowel control, and any numbness in the "saddle area" (perineum and buttocks).
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with symptoms suggestive of a first presentation of multiple sclerosis.

  • Clinician: "Hello Ms. Jones, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've been having some problems with your vision. Could you tell me more about that from the beginning?"
  • Patient: "It started about three days ago. I noticed the vision in my right eye was getting blurry, and the colours seem really washed out compared to my left eye."
  • Clinician: "Okay, blurry vision and washed-out colours in the right eye. Is there any pain?"
  • Patient: "Yes, it's quite sore behind the eye, especially when I look from side to side. It's a dull ache."
  • Clinician: "Thank you. Have you ever had anything like this before? Or any other strange neurological symptoms, perhaps numbness or weakness anywhere that came and went?"
  • Patient: "Actually, yes. About a year ago, the whole of my left leg went numb and tingly for a few weeks. It felt really heavy. It just got better on its own so I didn't see a doctor."
  • Clinician: "That's a very important piece of information. Thank you for telling me. Can I ask, what have you been worried this might be?"
  • Patient: "I'm not sure, I Googled it and something called MS came up, which has worried me a bit."

Problem Representation: A 29-year-old woman presents with a three-day history of painful, subacute visual loss in the right eye, consistent with optic neuritis, with a past history of a transient sensory disturbance in her left leg a year ago, raising suspicion for a diagnosis of Multiple Sclerosis.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Unsure, but has read about Multiple Sclerosis.
  • Concerns: Worried about what MS would mean for her future.
  • Expectations: Hopes to find out what is wrong and if there is any treatment for her eye.
