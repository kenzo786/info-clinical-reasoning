1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of an ophthalmology history is to gather specific information to localise the cause of a patient's symptoms to a specific part of the eye or visual pathway. This structured approach is essential for differentiating between benign and sight-threatening conditions, which often present with similar symptoms like a red eye or loss of vision. Unlike a general medical history, the focus is on a rapid but thorough assessment of visual function and specific ocular symptoms. The history guides the subsequent examination and helps determine the urgency of referral, whether it be to a community optometrist, an eye casualty department, or a GP for management of a related systemic condition.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective and empathetic communication is the foundation of an accurate ophthalmic history. It is essential to create a safe, confidential environment where the patient feels comfortable sharing their concerns, which often include significant anxiety about vision loss. Remember that a patient may be partially or severely visually impaired, so verbal cues are paramount. Always introduce yourself and explain your role clearly. Ensure any carers or relatives are included in the conversation if the patient consents. Adopting a patient-centred approach ensures you understand what matters most to them, which is crucial for shared decision-making and managing their expectations.

  • Establish Rapport: Begin with a clear introduction ("Hello, I'm one of the foundation doctors"). Address the patient by name. If you are not in their direct line of sight, make them aware of your location in the room.
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open questions ("Tell me about the trouble you're having with your vision?") to allow the patient to describe the problem in their own words. Gradually narrow down with more focused, closed questions ("Is the vision loss in one eye or both?") to clarify specific details.
  • Active Listening & Empathy: Actively listen to the patient. Acknowledge and validate their concerns ("It sounds very frightening to lose vision so suddenly"). This builds trust and encourages the sharing of more detailed information.
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): Exploring ICE is critically important given the anxiety associated with eye problems.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing this problem?"
    • Concerns: Directly address the patient's main worry. A crucial question is, "Is there anything specific you've been worried this could be?" This often reveals a fear of permanent blindness.
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?" This helps align the management plan with the patient's goals.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

A small number of cardinal symptoms account for the majority of ophthalmology presentations. A thorough exploration of these is essential, with an immediate focus on identifying any red flags that require urgent, specialist assessment to prevent irreversible sight loss.

  • Vision Loss: The most critical symptom. The history aims to determine the timing, nature, and pattern of the loss.
    • Red Flag: Sudden, profound, and persistent vision loss suggests a vascular occlusion (central retinal artery or vein), retinal detachment, or giant cell arteritis (GCA) and requires immediate emergency assessment.
  • Painful Red Eye: Can range from benign conjunctivitis to sight-threatening acute angle-closure glaucoma or keratitis.
    • Red Flag: A painful red eye associated with reduced visual acuity, a fixed and mid-dilated pupil, haloes around lights, or a hazy cornea points towards acute angle-closure glaucoma, a true ophthalmic emergency.
    • Red Flag: Severe pain, photophobia (light sensitivity), and a history of contact lens wear suggests microbial keratitis.
  • Flashes & Floaters: The sudden onset of new floaters (spots or cobwebs in vision) or photopsia (flashes of light).
    • Red Flag: New, persistent flashes and/or floaters, especially if accompanied by a "curtain" or "shadow" in the peripheral vision, are highly suspicious for a retinal tear or detachment and need urgent review.
  • Diplopia (Double Vision): Can be monocular (persists when one eye is closed) or binocular (resolves when one eye is closed).
    • Red Flag: Sudden onset binocular diplopia, especially if associated with pain, headache, or neurological symptoms (e.g., ptosis, pupil abnormalities), suggests a cranial nerve palsy or other serious neurological problem requiring urgent investigation.
  • Trauma: Any history of injury to the eye, even if it seems minor.
    • Red Flag: High-velocity injury (e.g., from grinding metal), chemical splash, or penetrating trauma requires immediate referral to an eye casualty department.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

Each cardinal symptom must be broken down systematically. For pain, the SOCRATES mnemonic (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, and Severity) is useful. For visual symptoms, a modified approach is needed to capture the key details.

  • For Vision Loss:
    • Laterality: Is it in one eye (monocular) or both (binocular)?
    • Onset: Was it sudden (seconds/minutes), acute (hours/days), or gradual (weeks/months)?
    • Progression: Is the vision loss getting worse, better, or is it static? Is it intermittent?
    • Pattern of Loss: Is it total loss (blackout), blurred vision, or a field defect? A "curtain falling" suggests retinal detachment. Central loss (scotoma) suggests macular disease.
    • Associated Symptoms: Is it painful? Are there flashes or floaters? Are there any neurological symptoms?
  • For Diplopia (Double Vision):
    • Monocular vs Binocular: Ask the patient to cover one eye, then the other. If the double vision disappears with either eye covered, it is binocular. If it persists in one eye when the other is covered, it is monocular. This is a crucial diagnostic step.
    • Direction: Is the double vision worse when looking in a particular direction (gaze-evoked)? Are the two images horizontal, vertical, or tilted?
    • Associated Symptoms: Is there pain on eye movement, ptosis (eyelid droop), headache, or any other weakness or numbness?
5. System-specific review of systems

Beyond the cardinal symptoms, asking about other related symptoms helps to build a complete clinical picture, as many systemic diseases manifest in the eyes.

  • Neurological: Ask about headaches (especially a new type of headache or one localised to the temple in older adults, for GCA), weakness, numbness, or problems with balance. Ask about ptosis (eyelid droop) or facial weakness.
  • Rheumatological: Ask about joint pain, stiffness, or swelling (associated with uveitis in conditions like ankylosing spondylitis). Ask about skin rashes (e.g., psoriasis, rosacea) or dry mouth and dry eyes (Sjögren's syndrome).
  • Endocrine: Ask about symptoms of diabetes (polyuria, polydipsia) or thyroid disease (heat intolerance, palpitations, weight changes, stare/lid retraction for thyroid eye disease).
  • Vascular: Specifically for older patients with vision loss, ask about jaw claudication (pain in the jaw when chewing), scalp tenderness, and proximal muscle stiffness (polymyalgia rheumatica). These are key symptoms of Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA).
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides essential context for their symptoms and is vital for narrowing the differential diagnosis and assessing risk.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Focus on key systemic diseases: Diabetes, Hypertension, Thyroid Disease, Autoimmune conditions (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis, Sarcoidosis), and Malignancy.
    • Ask about any previous eye conditions, trauma, surgery (especially cataract surgery), or lazy eye (amblyopia).
    • Crucially, ask: "Do you wear glasses or contact lenses?". If so, "When was your last eye test with an optometrist?".
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • Ask about all medications, including over-the-counter drugs.
    • Specifically ask about eye drops (e.g., for glaucoma, dry eyes).
    • Note drugs with ocular side effects: steroids (cataracts, glaucoma), hydroxychloroquine/chloroquine (maculopathy), amiodarone (corneal deposits, optic neuropathy), ethambutol (optic neuropathy), and tamsulosin (floppy iris syndrome).
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask specifically about eye conditions in the family, particularly glaucoma, strabismus (squint), amblyopia (lazy eye), and inherited retinal conditions (e.g., retinitis pigmentosa).
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Occupation: Are they exposed to foreign bodies (e.g., metalwork, grinding) or UV light?
    • Driving: Do they drive? Have their symptoms affected their ability to meet the legal standard for vision? This has significant safety and legal implications.
    • Smoking & Alcohol: Smoking is a major risk factor for age-related macular degeneration (AMD), cataracts, and thyroid eye disease.
    • Contact Lens Wear: If they wear contact lenses, ask about their lens hygiene practices (e.g., daily/monthly lenses, sleeping in them, showering in them). This is a major risk factor for microbial keratitis.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

A problem representation is a concise, one-sentence summary that encapsulates the most critical elements of the history. It is the bridge between data gathering and clinical reasoning. A good problem representation includes the patient's demographics, key risk factors, and the temporal pattern of their main symptoms. The formula is often: [Age/sex] with [key risk factors] presenting with [symptom profile] affecting [which eye/both] suggestive of [most likely location of problem].

  • Example 1 (Retinal Detachment): "A 58-year-old myopic man presents with a 12-hour history of new floaters and flashing lights in his right eye, now with a shadow progressing across his peripheral vision, highly suspicious for a retinal detachment."
  • Example 2 (Anterior Uveitis): "A 34-year-old man with known ankylosing spondylitis presents with a two-day history of a painful, red, and light-sensitive left eye with blurred vision, suggestive of acute anterior uveitis."
  • Example 3 (Giant Cell Arteritis): "An 82-year-old woman presents with a 24-hour history of sudden, profound vision loss in her left eye, preceded by several weeks of headache and jaw claudication, concerning for giant cell arteritis."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The pattern of historical features, particularly the timing of vision loss and the presence or absence of pain, is a powerful tool for shaping the differential diagnosis.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Sudden, painless, monocular vision loss. Vascular Occlusion (CRAO/CRVO), Retinal Detachment, Vitreous Haemorrhage. In an older patient, consider Giant Cell Arteritis.
Sudden, painful, monocular vision loss with a red eye. Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma, Microbial Keratitis, Anterior Uveitis.
Gradual, painless, monocular or binocular vision loss. Cataract (blur/glare), Age-Related Macular Degeneration (central distortion/blur), Diabetic Retinopathy, Glaucoma (peripheral loss, often asymptomatic until late).
Painful red eye with minimal/no vision loss. Conjunctivitis, Episcleritis, Corneal Abrasion, Foreign Body.
Sudden onset flashes and/or floaters. Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD). If a visual field defect is present, suspect a Retinal Tear/Detachment.
Binocular horizontal diplopia in an older adult with vascular risk factors. Microvascular Cranial Nerve Palsy (e.g., VI nerve palsy). Must rule out Giant Cell Arteritis if pain or other GCA symptoms are present.
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all key information is conveyed clearly and concisely to senior colleagues or specialist teams, which is vital for safe and effective patient care.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented to [e.g., A&E] with [chief complaint], affecting the [right/left/both] eye(s), which began [timing, e.g., three hours ago].

The main symptom is [e.g., vision loss, pain, double vision]. They describe it as [e.g., a curtain falling over their vision].
The onset was [sudden/gradual] and it is [improving/worsening/static].
Associated symptoms include [flashes, floaters, pain, redness, headache].
There is no [mention key negative findings, e.g., no pain, no photophobia, no trauma].

Their visual acuity was measured at [e.g., 6/60 in the affected eye].

Their past medical history includes [list key conditions, e.g., diabetes, high myopia].
Current medications are [list key medications, e.g., apixaban].
There is a family history of [e.g., glaucoma].
Regarding their social history, they [e.g., wear monthly contact lenses, are a welder]. They do/do not drive.

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] with a background of [risk factors] presenting with [key symptom cluster], which is highly suspicious for [top differential, e.g., a right retinal detachment].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly improve the quality and accuracy of the history, leading to safer and more effective clinical decision-making.

  • Pitfall: Not specifying the laterality of symptoms. Always clarify if it affects the right, left, or both eyes.
  • Expert Tip: If a patient says "my vision is blurry", ask them to elaborate. Does "blurry" mean cloudy like a cataract, distorted like macular disease, or double vision? This clarification is diagnostically crucial.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting to ask about contact lens wear in any patient with a red eye. This history is critical as it raises the possibility of serious microbial keratitis.
  • Expert Tip: Always ask about trauma, even if it is not the presenting complaint. Patients may not volunteer information about an injury they feel was minor but could be the underlying cause.
  • Pitfall: Neglecting the systemic review for an apparently isolated eye problem. Forgetting to ask about jaw claudication in an elderly patient with vision loss could mean missing a diagnosis of Giant Cell Arteritis.
  • Expert Tip: Differentiating monocular vs binocular diplopia is fundamental. Have the patient physically cover each eye in turn. Monocular diplopia is an optical problem (e.g., cataract, astigmatism); binocular diplopia is a misalignment problem (neurological or muscular).
  • Pitfall: Failing to ask about the impact on function, particularly driving. This is a patient safety and legal requirement.
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with a classic presentation of a posterior vitreous detachment (PVD).

  • Clinician: "Hello Mrs. Jones, I'm one of the doctors. I hear you've been having some problems with your vision today. Can you tell me what's been happening in your own words?"
  • Patient: "Yes, it's my right eye. This morning I suddenly saw a flash of light, like a camera flash in the corner of my eye. It only lasted a second but it happened a few times."
  • Clinician: "A flash of light in the corner of your right eye. After the flashes, did you notice anything else?"
  • Patient: "Well yes, now I've got this big black spot, like a fly, that just seems to be drifting around. It's very annoying. When I try to look at it, it moves away."
  • Clinician: "Okay, so a new, large, floating spot. Have you had anything like this before?"
  • Patient: "No, never. It's quite worrying."
  • Clinician: "I understand. Can I just check, apart from the flashes and the new floater, have you noticed any blurring of your vision, or what looks like a shadow or a curtain coming across your vision at all?"
  • Patient: "No, my vision seems clear otherwise. No curtain, thank goodness."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, that's very clear. Is there anything you've been worried this might be?"
  • Patient: "Well you hear terrible things don't you? I'm worried I might be losing my sight."

Problem Representation: A 65-year-old woman presents with an acute onset of photopsia and a new, large floater in her right eye, without any visual field loss, consistent with an acute posterior vitreous detachment.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Unsure of the cause.
  • Concerns: Worried about losing her eyesight permanently.
  • Expectations: Hopes to find out what is wrong and to be reassured that it is not serious.
