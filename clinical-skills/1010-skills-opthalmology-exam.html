1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the ophthalmology examination is to objectively assess the structure and function of the eyes and visual pathways. It is a systematic process designed to screen for pathology, localise lesions, and identify signs of both ocular and systemic disease, such as diabetes, hypertension, and raised intracranial pressure. The examination must be integrated with the patient's history to build a complete clinical picture. A key aim is to identify urgent, sight-threatening signs, such as a relative afferent pupillary defect or papilloedema, which require immediate investigation and management.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A basic understanding of the anatomy visible with a direct ophthalmoscope (fundoscopy) is essential for a competent examination. The 'fundus' refers to the interior surface of the eye opposite the lens, and includes the retina, optic disc, macula, fovea, and posterior pole.

  • The Optic Disc: This is the entry point for the optic nerve and retinal blood vessels. It is the first structure to seek during fundoscopy. It is normally a pale pink-orange colour with clear, crisp margins. It is located nasally from the centre of the retina.
  • The Macula & Fovea: The macula is a small, specialised area of the retina lateral (temporal) to the optic disc, responsible for high-acuity central vision. At its centre is the fovea, which provides the sharpest vision. It appears slightly darker than the surrounding retina and may have a bright foveal reflex in younger individuals.
  • Retinal Vessels: Arteries and veins emerge from the optic disc and branch out to supply the retina. Retinal arteries are typically brighter red and narrower (in a 2:3 ratio) than the darker, wider retinal veins.
  • The Red Reflex: This refers to the reddish-orange reflection of light from the eye's retina. When viewed through an ophthalmoscope from a distance, it should be uniform and symmetrical between the two eyes.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A smooth, professional, and safe examination requires careful preparation and clear communication.

  1. Equipment:
    • Snellen Chart: For testing visual acuity, positioned 6 metres from the patient.
    • Pinhole Occluder: To differentiate refractive errors from other causes of reduced acuity.
    • Pen Torch: For assessing pupillary reflexes.
    • Direct Ophthalmoscope: For fundoscopy. Ensure the batteries are working and check the light source before you begin.
    • (Optional) Mydriatic eye drops (e.g., tropicamide) for dilating the pupils to get a better view. You must have senior approval, check for contraindications (e.g., narrow angles), and warn the patient they cannot drive for several hours afterwards.
  2. Positioning and Environment:
    • The patient should be seated comfortably. The examination can be performed in a chair or on the side of the bed.
    • The room should be able to be darkened for fundoscopy, as this helps to dilate the pupils and improves the clarity of your view.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself clearly ("Hello, I am [Name], a foundation doctor").
    • Explain what you would like to do and gain consent. A good phrase is: "I've been asked to perform an examination of your eyes. This will involve me testing your vision, checking your eye movements, and then shining a bright light into your eyes to look at the back. Is that okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent sequence ensures no steps are missed. The exam proceeds from general function to detailed internal structures. Always examine the 'good' or asymptomatic eye first.

  1. General Inspection: From the front, observe for obvious abnormalities like ptosis (drooping eyelid), strabismus (squint), proptosis (bulging eyes), redness, or discharge.
  2. Visual Acuity: Test each eye separately using a Snellen chart, starting with the right eye. The patient should wear their distance glasses. Record the lowest line they can read. If acuity is below 6/12, re-test with a pinhole.
  3. Visual Fields: Perform confrontation testing to assess peripheral vision. Sit opposite the patient and ask them to cover one eye while you cover your own opposite eye. Test each quadrant by bringing a moving finger or red hatpin from the periphery to the centre.
  4. Pupils: In a dimly lit room, inspect for size, shape, and symmetry. Check the direct and consensual light reflexes with a pen torch. Then perform the swinging light test to look for a Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD).
  5. Eye Movements: Ask the patient to keep their head still and follow your finger as you trace a large 'H' shape. Observe for any restriction of movement or nystagmus (involuntary eye movements). Ask if they experience any diplopia (double vision).
  6. Fundoscopy (Ophthalmoscopy):
    • Dim the room lights to dilate the pupils.
    • Set the ophthalmoscope diopter to 0.
    • From a distance of ~30 cm, look for the red reflex in both eyes.
    • Approach the patient's right eye with your right eye, and their left eye with your left eye.
    • Place your free hand on their forehead or shoulder for stability.
    • Come in closer until you can see the fundus. Focus the ophthalmoscope to get a clear view of the optic disc. Assess its colour, contour (margins), and cup.
    • Systematically examine the four retinal vascular arcades.
    • Finally, ask the patient to look directly at the light to bring the macula into view.
  7. Completing the Examination: Briefly check sensation over the forehead and cheeks (trigeminal nerve, V1/V2 distribution) if relevant.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising what is normal is essential to identifying pathology. A normal ophthalmology examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • Visual Acuity: Normal acuity is 6/6 (or better) in each eye, with or without glasses.
  • Visual Fields: Full visual fields on confrontation testing in all quadrants.
  • Pupils: Pupils are Equal, Round, and Reactive to Light (PERRLA). There is no Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD).
  • Eye Movements: Full and smooth eye movements in all directions of gaze, with no nystagmus or diplopia.
  • Red Reflex: Present and symmetrical in both eyes.
  • Fundoscopy:
    • Optic Disc: Pink in colour with a clearly defined, sharp contour (margin). The central cup should occupy less than half the disc diameter (cup-to-disc ratio <0.5).
    • Retinal Vessels: Arteries are narrower and lighter in colour than veins (A:V ratio approx 2:3). No haemorrhages, exudates, or cotton wool spots.
    • Macula: Appears as a slightly darker area temporal to the disc, with a uniform appearance.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Pathological signs are objective evidence of underlying disease. Any new, unexplained finding requires careful consideration and often urgent referral.

Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)
  • The Sign: On the swinging light test, the affected pupil dilates when the light is swung across to it from the normal eye. Also known as a Marcus Gunn pupil.
  • Pathology Link: A key sign of unilateral or asymmetrical damage to the optic nerve (afferent pathway) anterior to the optic chiasm. The affected eye detects less light, so the consensual response from the good eye is stronger than the direct response of the bad eye.
  • Red Flag: A new RAPD is a critical sign of significant optic nerve disease (e.g., optic neuritis, retinal artery occlusion) and requires urgent ophthalmology referral.
Papilloedema
  • The Sign: Swelling of the optic disc on fundoscopy. Key features are blurring and elevation of the disc margins, loss of the optic cup, and hyperaemia (redness) of the disc. Haemorrhages may be present on or around the disc.
  • Pathology Link: Occurs due to raised intracranial pressure being transmitted along the optic nerve sheath, causing blockage of axoplasmic flow and venous stasis at the nerve head. It is almost always bilateral.
  • Red Flag: Papilloedema is a neurological emergency, indicating pathologically high intracranial pressure. It requires immediate investigation (e.g., neuroimaging) to rule out a space-occupying lesion.
Optic atrophy
  • The Sign: The optic disc appears chalky-white or pale on fundoscopy.
  • Pathology Link: Represents the death of optic nerve axons from a long-standing pathological process, such as advanced glaucoma, previous optic neuritis, or chronic compression of the nerve. It is the end-stage sign of optic nerve damage.
Diabetic retinopathy
  • The Sign: A spectrum of findings including microaneurysms (tiny red dots), dot and blot haemorrhages, hard exudates (yellow lipid deposits), and cotton wool spots (small, fluffy white patches indicating retinal ischaemia).
  • Pathology Link: Caused by microvascular damage from chronic hyperglycaemia. Can progress to a proliferative stage with abnormal new vessel growth (neovascularisation), which can lead to vitreous haemorrhage and tractional retinal detachment.
Hypertensive retinopathy
  • The Sign: Findings include silver or copper wiring (arterial wall thickening), arteriovenous (AV) nipping (veins appear constricted where arteries cross them), flame haemorrhages, and cotton wool spots. Severe cases can present with papilloedema.
  • Pathology Link: Results from vascular damage due to chronically elevated blood pressure. The presence of these signs indicates end-organ damage from hypertension.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating your findings. Start with visual acuity, then comment on fields, pupils, movements, and finally fundoscopy. Report pertinent positive and negative findings.

On examination of the eyes, visual acuity was [e.g., 6/9 in the right eye, 6/12 in the left eye], improving to [e.g., 6/6] with pinhole.
Visual fields were [e.g., full to confrontation].
Pupils were equal and reactive to light, with [e.g., no RAPD / a left RAPD].
Eye movements were full, with no diplopia.
On fundoscopy, the red reflex was present bilaterally.
The optic discs appeared [e.g., pink with sharp margins]. The vessels, maculae, and peripheries were [e.g., normal / describe any pathology seen, such as dot haemorrhages and hard exudates].
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

Individual signs are useful, but a constellation of signs pointing in the same direction is powerful evidence for a specific diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Reduced acuity in one eye, poor colour vision, a central field defect, and a relative afferent pupillary defect. The disc may look normal or be swollen. Optic Neuritis (especially in a younger female, consider MS)
Bilateral swollen optic discs (papilloedema) with enlarged blind spots on field testing, in a patient with headaches. Vision may be preserved initially. Raised Intracranial Pressure (needs urgent investigation)
Gradual reduction in vision in a diabetic patient, with dot/blot haemorrhages, microaneurysms, and hard exudates seen on fundoscopy. Diabetic Retinopathy / Maculopathy
Sudden, profound, painless vision loss in one eye, with a relative afferent pupillary defect and a pale retina with a 'cherry-red spot' at the macula. Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)
9. Relevant next-step investigations

The findings on examination guide the selection of appropriate investigations to confirm a diagnosis and assess severity.

  • If you find papilloedema:
    • Logical Next Steps: This is a neurological emergency. The patient needs an urgent CT or MRI scan of the brain to rule out a space-occupying lesion. Blood pressure measurement is also critical.
  • If you find signs of diabetic or hypertensive retinopathy:
    • Logical Next Steps: Check blood pressure and HbA1c. The patient requires routine or urgent referral to an ophthalmologist for formal assessment and grading, often with retinal photography and OCT scanning.
  • If you find a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD):
    • Logical Next Steps: This requires urgent referral to ophthalmology for further investigation to determine the cause of the optic neuropathy. This may involve visual field testing, OCT scanning of the optic nerve, and potentially neuroimaging.
  • If you cannot view the fundus due to a poor red reflex:
    • Logical Next Steps: This often indicates a dense cataract. The patient should be referred to an optometrist or ophthalmologist for a slit-lamp examination to confirm the diagnosis and assess for surgery.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good exam technique can dramatically improve performance, especially in a practical setting like an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to test visual acuity first - it is the 'vital sign' of the eye.
    • Performing the swinging light test too quickly; you should hold the light on each eye for 2-3 seconds to observe the response.
    • Not dimming the room lights sufficiently for fundoscopy, resulting in a poor view.
    • Holding the ophthalmoscope with the wrong hand or using the wrong eye (remember: your right eye to the patient's right eye).
    • Forgetting to examine the macula at the end of fundoscopy.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start by introducing yourself, explaining the examination, and gaining consent.
    • Check that your ophthalmoscope is working before you approach the patient.
    • Verbalise your findings as you go (e.g., "Visual acuity is 6/6 in the right eye... Fields are full to confrontation... There is no relative afferent pupillary defect.").
    • Be systematic and follow the same sequence every time.
    • If you see an abnormality on fundoscopy, do not panic. Take a moment, describe it systematically (e.g., "I can see several dot haemorrhages in the inferotemporal quadrant"), and then continue your examination.
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant further steps, such as checking blood pressure, performing a full cranial nerve exam, or using a slit lamp.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings helps to solidify understanding and build clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
    • Scenario: A 45-year-old man with a 20-year history of Type 1 Diabetes attends for a routine check-up. He has noticed some new 'floaters' recently.
    • Expected Findings: Visual acuity may be reduced. Fundoscopy is the key part of the examination. You would expect to see signs of background retinopathy (dot/blot haemorrhages, hard exudates) but also signs of proliferation, such as fine networks of new vessels (neovascularisation), particularly on the optic disc. There may be evidence of a pre-retinal or vitreous haemorrhage.
  • Case 2: Malignant Hypertension
    • Scenario: A 55-year-old man presents to A&E with a severe headache and blurred vision. His blood pressure is 220/140 mmHg.
    • Expected Findings: Fundoscopy would likely show signs of severe hypertensive retinopathy, including bilateral flame haemorrhages, multiple cotton wool spots, and swelling of both optic discs (papilloedema).
  • Case 3: Ischaemic Optic Neuropathy (due to GCA)
    • Scenario: An 80-year-old woman presents with sudden, painless vision loss in her right eye. She also reports headache and jaw pain when eating for the past week.
    • Expected Findings: Visual acuity would be severely reduced in the right eye (e.g., counting fingers). A significant right relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) would be present. Fundoscopy of the affected eye would reveal a pale, swollen optic disc, often with a few splinter haemorrhages on the disc margin.
