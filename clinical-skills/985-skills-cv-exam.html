Cardiovascular System Examination
1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the cardiovascular examination is to detect physical signs of cardiac disease. It is a systematic process designed to identify evidence of conditions such as heart failure, valvular heart disease, congenital abnormalities, infective endocarditis, and peripheral arterial disease. The examination does not happen in isolation; its findings must be integrated with the patient's history to build a complete clinical picture. A key aim is to identify signs that may require urgent investigation or intervention, such as a new heart murmur in a septic patient or the signs of critical aortic stenosis.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

Accurate performance of the cardiovascular examination relies on knowledge of underlying structures and their corresponding surface markings on the praecordium (the area of chest overlying the heart).

  • The Apex Beat: This is the most lateral and inferior point at which the cardiac impulse is felt. It is normally located in the 5th left intercostal space in the mid-clavicular line and corresponds to the apex of the left ventricle.
  • Auscultation Areas: These are not directly over the valves themselves, but are the areas on the chest wall where the sound from each valve is best heard due to the direction of blood flow.
    • Aortic Area: 2nd intercostal space, right sternal edge.
    • Pulmonary Area: 2nd intercostal space, left sternal edge.
    • Tricuspid Area: 4th or 5th intercostal space, lower left sternal edge.
    • Mitral Area: 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line (at the apex).
  • The Sternal Angle (Angle of Louis): A palpable ridge on the sternum where the manubrium joins the sternal body. It lies level with the 2nd rib and is the crucial reference point for measuring the Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP).
  • The Jugular Veins: The internal jugular vein runs between the two heads of the sternocleidomastoid muscle. Visualising its pulsation is key to estimating central venous pressure.
3. Preparation, positioning & infection control

A smooth, professional, and safe examination requires careful preparation.

  1. Infection Control:
    • Begin by washing your hands or using alcohol gel.
    • Clean the diaphragm and bell of your stethoscope with an alcohol wipe in front of the patient. This demonstrates good practice and provides reassurance.
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to lie on the examination couch and position them at a 45-degree angle. This is the standard position for assessing the JVP and examining the praecordium.
    • Preserve the patient's dignity with appropriate draping (e.g., a sheet or blanket), but ensure you have adequate exposure of the chest from the waist up when required.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself clearly with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, my name is [Name] and I am a foundation doctor").
    • Explain what you would like to do in simple terms and gain consent. A good phrase is: "I've been asked to perform an examination of your heart. This will involve me looking at your hands and face, feeling the pulses in your neck, and then having a listen to your chest. Would that be okay?"
4. Step-by-step examination sequence

A consistent, systematic approach reduces the chance of missing important signs. The sequence generally moves from general inspection, to the periphery, and finally to the praecordium.

  1. General Inspection: From the end of the bed, look for signs of distress, breathlessness, obvious cyanosis, or pallor. Note any monitoring equipment, intravenous lines, or supplemental oxygen. Are they comfortable at rest?
  2. The Hands: Inspect for clubbing, splinter haemorrhages (tiny linear bleeds under the nails), Janeway lesions (non-tender spots on the palms), and Osler's nodes (tender nodules on the fingertips). Check for temperature and test capillary refill time (should be <2 seconds).
  3. The Pulses & Blood Pressure: Palpate the radial pulse, assessing its rate and rhythm. Palpate the brachial pulse and check for a collapsing pulse (Water-hammer pulse), a sign of aortic regurgitation. Blood pressure should also be measured.
  4. The Face & Neck: Check the eyes for conjunctival pallor (anaemia), xanthelasma (cholesterol deposits), and corneal arcus. Check for central cyanosis under the tongue. Then, assess the Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP) and the character of the carotid pulse (one side at a time).
  5. The Praecordium - Inspection: Look for any scars (e.g., midline sternotomy, lateral thoracotomy), visible pulsations, or implanted devices like pacemakers.
  6. The Praecordium - Palpation: Locate the apex beat. Feel for heaves (forceful impulses at the left sternal edge, indicating right ventricular hypertrophy) and thrills (palpable murmurs).
  7. The Praecordium - Auscultation: Using the diaphragm, listen sequentially over the four valve areas (Aortic, Pulmonary, Tricuspid, Mitral). Identify S1 and S2, then listen for added sounds or murmurs. Repeat with the bell at the tricuspid area and the apex for low-pitched sounds.
  8. Special Manoeuvres:
    • To accentuate an aortic regurgitation murmur, ask the patient to sit up, lean forward, and hold their breath in expiration, while listening at the lower left sternal edge with the diaphragm.
    • To accentuate a mitral stenosis murmur, ask the patient to roll onto their left side, and listen at the apex with the bell.
    • Auscultate the carotid arteries for radiation of an aortic stenosis murmur and the axilla for radiation of a mitral regurgitation murmur.
  9. Completing the Examination: Check for sacral and pitting pedal oedema. Auscultate the lung bases for fine crackles (pulmonary oedema).
5. Normal findings & their significance

Recognising what is normal is essential to identifying pathology. A normal cardiovascular examination provides a reassuring baseline against which abnormal findings can be compared.

  • General Appearance: The patient is comfortable at rest.
    • Significance: The absence of breathlessness, distress, or pallor makes an acute, severe cardiorespiratory event less likely.
  • Peripheries: Warm peripheries with a capillary refill time under 2 seconds. No clubbing, splinter haemorrhages, oedema, or signs of hyperlipidaemia.
    • Significance: Suggests adequate peripheral perfusion and the absence of chronic hypoxia, infective endocarditis, or severe heart failure.
  • Pulse & JVP: A regular pulse between 60-100 bpm and a non-elevated JVP (≤ 3cm from the sternal angle).
    • Significance: A regular rhythm and normal JVP suggest the absence of a major arrhythmia or significant right heart pressure overload.
  • Praecordium: Apex beat is in the 5th left intercostal space, mid-clavicular line. No palpable heaves or thrills.
    • Significance: A non-displaced apex beat suggests the heart is not significantly enlarged. Absence of heaves or thrills makes significant ventricular hypertrophy or a very loud murmur unlikely.
  • Auscultation: Clear first (S1) and second (S2) heart sounds with no audible murmurs or added sounds (S3/S4).
    • Significance: This is the most reassuring finding, suggesting the absence of significant valvular disease or overt heart failure.
6. Key abnormal findings & red flags

Pathological signs are objective evidence of underlying disease. Any new, unexplained finding requires careful consideration. Some findings are red flags that necessitate immediate action.

Raised jugular venous pressure (JVP)
  • The Sign: A vertical height of the JVP pulsation more than 3-4 cm above the sternal angle.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates elevated right atrial pressure, most commonly caused by right-sided heart failure (secondary to left-sided failure or lung disease) or fluid overload.
Heart murmurs
  • The Sign: Extra sounds generated by turbulent blood flow.
  • Pathology Link: The key sign of valvular heart disease. Examples include an ejection-systolic murmur of aortic stenosis or a pansystolic murmur of mitral regurgitation.
  • Red Flag: A new heart murmur in a patient with a fever or signs of sepsis is highly suspicious for infective endocarditis, a medical emergency.
Added heart sounds (S3/S4)
  • The Sign: Low-pitched sounds in diastole creating a "gallop" rhythm.
  • Pathology Link: An S3 gallop is caused by rapid filling of a dilated, non-compliant ventricle and is a classic sign of congestive heart failure. An S4 gallop is from atrial contraction against a stiff ventricle, seen in long-standing hypertension or aortic stenosis.
Displaced apex beat
  • The Sign: The apex beat is located lateral to the mid-clavicular line.
  • Pathology Link: This is a reliable sign of cardiomegaly (an enlarged heart), most commonly due to left ventricular dilatation from conditions like heart failure or significant valve disease.
Peripheral oedema and pulmonary crackles
  • The Sign: Pitting oedema in the ankles/sacrum and fine, "popping" crackles at the lung bases.
  • Pathology Link: Cardinal signs of decompensated heart failure, caused by fluid moving into the interstitial space due to high venous pressures.
  • Red Flag: The combination of basal crackles and peripheral oedema suggests biventricular failure and significant fluid overload requiring urgent treatment.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating your findings. Start with a general statement, mention the pulse and JVP, describe praecordium findings, and finish with the periphery. Avoid simply listing everything you did; report the pertinent positive and negative findings.

On examination, the patient appeared [e.g., comfortable at rest / breathless].

The pulse was [rate] and [rhythm, e.g., regular / irregularly irregular]. The JVP was [not elevated / elevated to X cm]. There were no peripheral stigmata of infective endocarditis.

On examination of the praecordium, the apex beat was [located in the 5th IC space, MCL / displaced]. There were no [heaves / thrills]. On auscultation, heart sounds I and II were present, with [no added sounds / a description of any murmur or added sound].

To complete the examination, there was [no / description of] peripheral oedema, and the lung bases were [clear / had crackles].
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

Individual signs are useful, but a constellation of signs pointing in the same direction is powerful evidence for a specific diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Raised JVP, displaced apex beat, S3 gallop, bilateral fine basal crackles, pitting pedal oedema. Congestive Heart Failure (Biventricular)
Slow-rising pulse, forceful apex beat, harsh ejection systolic murmur radiating to carotids. Severe Aortic Stenosis

9. Relevant next-step investigations

The findings on examination guide the selection of appropriate investigations to confirm a diagnosis and assess its severity.

  • If you find a new murmur or signs of valvular disease:
    • Logical Next Steps: An Electrocardiogram (ECG) is the first step. The definitive investigation is a Transthoracic Echocardiogram (Echo) to visualise valve structure and function.
  • If you find signs of heart failure (e.g., raised JVP, oedema, crackles):
    • Logical Next Steps: Initial tests include an ECG, blood tests including B-type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP), and a Chest X-ray. An Echocardiogram is essential to confirm the diagnosis and determine the underlying cause.
  • If you find signs of infective endocarditis (e.g., fever, new murmur, stigmata):
    • Logical Next Steps: This is a medical emergency. Immediate investigations include multiple sets of blood cultures, bloods (FBC, CRP, U&Es), an ECG, and an urgent Echocardiogram.
  • If you detect an irregular pulse (suggesting an arrhythmia):
    • Logical Next Steps: An ECG is required immediately to identify the rhythm (e.g., Atrial Fibrillation). If symptoms are intermittent, a 24-hour ambulatory ECG (Holter monitor) may be needed.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good exam technique can dramatically improve performance, especially in a practical setting like an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to position the patient correctly at 45 degrees from the start.
    • Inadequate exposure of the chest, making inspection and palpation difficult.
    • Pressing too hard with the bell of the stethoscope – this creates a seal, turning it into a diaphragm, and you will miss low-pitched sounds like an S3 or the murmur of mitral stenosis.
    • Forgetting to perform the special manoeuvres for diastolic murmurs (sitting forward for aortic regurgitation, left lateral for mitral stenosis).
    • Forgetting to complete the examination by checking for oedema and listening to the lung bases.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start with the "magic formula": Wash hands, introduce yourself, state your name and role, explain the examination, and gain consent.
    • Verbalise what you are doing as you go along (e.g., "I am now palpating the apex beat..."). This tells the examiner you know what you are looking for.
    • Be systematic. Follow the same sequence every time so you do not forget any steps under pressure.
    • If you find a murmur, demonstrate that you know how to assess it properly (e.g., check for radiation, perform manoeuvres).
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant further steps, such as measuring blood pressure, examining the abdomen for hepatomegaly, and checking all peripheral pulses.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings helps to solidify understanding and build clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Acute Decompensated Heart Failure
    • Scenario: A 75-year-old woman with a history of hypertension and a previous heart attack presents with several days of worsening breathlessness and new ankle swelling.
    • Expected Findings: She may be visibly breathless at rest. You would expect to find a raised JVP, a laterally displaced apex beat, a possible S3 gallop sound on auscultation, bilateral pitting pedal oedema, and fine crackles at both lung bases.
    • Summary: This patient has clinical signs consistent with decompensated biventricular heart failure.
  • Case 2: Severe Aortic Stenosis
    • Scenario: A 78-year-old man is referred by his GP after an episode of fainting while gardening. He mentions some recent chest tightness on exertion.
    • Expected Findings: You would expect to find a slow-rising, low-volume carotid pulse. The apex beat may be non-displaced but forceful (a heave). The key finding would be a harsh ejection systolic murmur, loudest in the aortic area and radiating clearly to the carotid arteries in the neck.
    • Summary: The examination findings are highly suggestive of critical aortic stenosis.
  • Case 3: Mitral Regurgitation & Atrial Fibrillation
    • Scenario: A 68-year-old woman presents with palpitations and feeling more tired and breathless than usual when doing her shopping.
    • Expected Findings: The pulse would be irregularly irregular. The apex beat may be displaced. Auscultation would reveal a pansystolic (holosystolic) murmur, loudest at the apex, which radiates towards the axilla.
    • Summary: These signs indicate the presence of atrial fibrillation with likely co-existent significant mitral regurgitation.
