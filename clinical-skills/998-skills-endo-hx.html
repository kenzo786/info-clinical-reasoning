Endocrine System History
1. Overview & Aims of this History

The primary aim of an endocrine history is to identify symptom constellations that suggest an underlying hormonal excess or deficiency. Unlike organ-specific histories, endocrine problems often manifest with vague, gradual, and multi-systemic symptoms, such as fatigue, weight change, or mood disturbance. The clinician's task is to piece together seemingly unrelated clues from the patient's narrative to form a coherent hypothesis about which endocrine axis might be dysfunctional. A key objective is to screen for 'red flag' symptoms that could indicate a life-threatening endocrine emergency, such as an adrenal crisis or thyroid storm. The history also serves to establish a baseline of the patient's functional status and quality of life, which is essential for gauging response to treatment.

2. Core Principles & Communication

An endocrine history requires particular sensitivity. Patients may be discussing significant changes to their body image (e.g., weight gain, hair loss, skin changes), sexual function, or mental health, which can be distressing. It is vital to create a confidential and non-judgemental space. Start with open questions and listen actively. Acknowledge and validate the patient's concerns, as they may have been feeling dismissed or misunderstood for a long time. It can be incredibly helpful to ask for an old photograph (e.g., a driving licence) to objectively assess changes in appearance over time. Always explain why you are asking sensitive questions, linking them to the potential underlying medical issue (e.g., "Sometimes, changes in hormone levels can affect mood or energy, I wonder if you've noticed anything like that?").

3. Key Presenting Complaints & Cardinal Symptoms

Endocrine disorders present in varied ways. Key complaints often revolve around changes in energy, metabolism, and physical appearance.

  • Weight Change (Unintentional):
    • Weight Gain: Often associated with fluid retention or changes in fat distribution (e.g., central obesity in Cushing's syndrome). Key features are rate of gain and associated symptoms like fatigue and cold intolerance (suggesting hypothyroidism).
    • Weight Loss: Despite a normal or increased appetite ('weight loss with good appetite') is a classic feature of hyperthyroidism. Also seen in poorly controlled Type 1 diabetes or adrenal insufficiency.
    • Red Flags: Rapid, significant weight loss; signs of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) like vomiting and abdominal pain; profound weakness and postural dizziness (adrenal crisis).
  • Fatigue & Malaise:
    • A non-specific but very common symptom. In hypothyroidism, it's often associated with lethargy and need for more sleep. In hyperthyroidism, patients may feel 'tired but wired'. In Addison's disease, it is profound and often associated with postural hypotension.
    • Red Flags: Lethargy progressing to stupor or coma; associated hypotension or hypoglycaemia.
  • Altered Mood or Cognition:
    • Anxiety, irritability, and emotional lability are common in hyperthyroidism. Depression and 'brain fog' are characteristic of hypothyroidism. Psychosis is a rare but serious feature of severe Cushing's syndrome or myxoedema coma.
    • Red Flags: Acute confusion, delirium, or psychosis could indicate a severe metabolic disturbance (e.g., DKA, severe hypercalcaemia) or hormonal crisis (thyroid storm, myxoedema coma).
  • Sweating & Temperature Intolerance:
    • Heat Intolerance & Sweating: Classic features of thyrotoxicosis. May also be seen in phaeochromocytoma (episodic) or acromegaly.
    • Cold Intolerance: A key symptom of hypothyroidism, where patients feel cold even in a warm environment.
    • Red Flags: Fever and profuse sweating in a patient with known hyperthyroidism may signal an impending thyroid storm.
4. Deconstructing the Presenting Complaint

To understand the core symptoms, a structured approach is essential. A mnemonic like SOCRATES can be adapted to explore a vague complaint like fatigue or a specific one like palpitations.

Let's apply it to a patient presenting with "palpitations":

  • Site: "Where do you feel the palpitations? In your chest, or is it more of a pounding in your ears?"
  • Onset: "When did they first start? Did they come on suddenly or gradually? Was there any particular trigger?" (e.g., stress, caffeine).
  • Character: "Can you describe the feeling? Is it like a fluttering, a racing, a pounding, or a skipped beat?"
  • Radiation: (Less relevant for palpitations).
  • Associated Symptoms: "When you get the palpitations, do you also notice anything else? For example, feeling sweaty, shaky, short of breath, anxious, or losing weight without trying?" (This screens for thyrotoxicosis).
  • Timing: "How often do they happen? How long do they last for? Are they there all the time or do they come and go?" (Episodic symptoms are key for phaeochromocytoma).
  • Exacerbating / Relieving Factors: "Does anything seem to bring them on or make them worse? Does anything help them settle?"
  • Severity: "How much do they bother you? Do they stop you from doing things?"
5. System-Specific Review of Systems

This is the cornerstone of the endocrine history, where patterns are found. It's best organised by gland/hormone system, moving from head to toe.

  • General: Weight change (quantify), appetite change, fatigue, fever.
  • Pituitary/Hypothalamus:
    • Visual: Any changes to your eyesight? Double vision? Loss of peripheral vision (suggests bitemporal hemianopia from pituitary adenoma pressure on the optic chiasm)?
    • Growth Hormone (Acromegaly): "Have you noticed your hands or feet getting bigger? Do your rings or shoes still fit? Any change in your facial features?" (Ask to see ID/old photos). Headaches? Sweating?
    • Prolactin: (In women) "Have your periods become irregular or stopped? Any milky discharge from your nipples (galactorrhoea)?" (In men) "Any problems with sexual function or reduced libido?"
    • ADH (Diabetes Insipidus): "Are you thirsty all the time? How much do you drink in a day? Are you passing urine very frequently, even overnight?"
  • Thyroid:
    • Hyperthyroidism: Weight loss (with good appetite), heat intolerance, sweating, palpitations, anxiety, irritability, diarrhoea, tremor, new-onset atrial fibrillation.
    • Hypothyroidism: Weight gain, cold intolerance, lethargy, dry skin, hair thinning (especially outer third of eyebrows), constipation, low mood, hoarse voice.
    • Goitre: Any swelling in your neck? Discomfort? Difficulty swallowing or breathing?
  • Parathyroid (Calcium):
    • Hypercalcaemia: "Stones, bones, groans, and psychiatric overtones." Kidney stones? Bone pain? Abdominal pain/constipation? Low mood, confusion?
    • Hypocalcaemia: Tingling in your lips or fingertips? Muscle cramps or spasms?
  • Adrenal:
    • Cushing's (Cortisol Excess): Weight gain (especially on your face, neck and torso), skin changes (easy bruising, stretch marks - especially purple), muscle weakness (difficulty standing from a chair), mood changes, increased infections.
    • Addison's (Cortisol/Aldosterone Deficiency): Profound fatigue, dizziness on standing, weight loss, abdominal pain, vomiting, skin pigmentation (especially in skin creases like the palms, or on gums). "Have you developed a tan without being in the sun?"
    • Phaeochromocytoma (Adrenaline Excess): The "spells". Episodic headache, sweating, and palpitations.
  • Pancreas (Diabetes): "The 3 Polys". Excessive thirst (polydipsia), passing lots of urine (polyuria), and increased hunger (polyphagia). Blurred vision? Recurrent infections (e.g., thrush)?
  • Gonads (Sex Hormones):
    • Women: Ask about menstrual cycle regularity, flow, and date of last menstrual period (LMP). Enquire about excess facial or body hair (hirsutism) and acne (suggests androgen excess, e.g., PCOS).
    • Men: Ask about libido (sex drive), erectile dysfunction, and loss of body hair.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides essential clues to the aetiology of a suspected endocrine disorder.

  1. Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Known Endocrine Conditions: Diabetes, thyroid disorders, etc.
    • Autoimmune Diseases: Type 1 diabetes, pernicious anaemia, vitiligo, coeliac disease. These often cluster together (autoimmune polyglandular syndrome).
    • Previous Surgery: Neck (thyroidectomy), head/brain (pituitary surgery), adrenal surgery.
    • Radiation Treatment: Especially to the head, neck, or chest, which can cause delayed pituitary or thyroid failure.
    • Obstetric History: Postpartum haemorrhage can be complicated by Sheehan's syndrome (pituitary infarction).
  2. Drug History (DHx):
    • Prescribed: Steroids (can cause iatrogenic Cushing's and suppress the adrenal axis), Amiodarone (can cause hypo/hyperthyroidism), Lithium (can cause hypothyroidism/hyperparathyroidism), dopamine agonists. Ask about any hormone replacement therapy (thyroxine, hydrocortisone, HRT).
    • Over-the-counter/Herbal: Some supplements (e.g., those containing biotin) can interfere with thyroid function assays.
  3. Family History (FHx):
    • Many endocrine conditions have a genetic component. Ask specifically about:
    • Thyroid disease, Diabetes, Addison's disease.
    • Autoimmune disorders in first-degree relatives.
    • Rarely, a history of endocrine cancers may point towards a familial syndrome like Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN).
  4. Social History (SHx):
    • Occupation & Function: How have the symptoms affected their ability to work and perform activities of daily living? This is vital for assessing severity and for management planning (e.g., a bus driver with hypoglycaemic episodes).
    • Diet & Lifestyle: Note any significant dietary changes.
    • Alcohol & Smoking: Smoking is a major risk factor for thyroid eye disease.
7. Synthesising the History: The Problem Representation

The problem representation is a one-sentence summary that encapsulates the core clinical features and relevant background. It is the bridge between data gathering and diagnostic reasoning. A good problem representation for an endocrine history should name the key symptoms, specify their duration, and mention any highly relevant contextual factors like a key past medical or family history. It should implicitly point towards the likely dysfunctional endocrine axis.

Structure: [Age and sex] with a [duration] history of [key symptom 1, 2, 3...] in the context of [relevant PMHx/FHx/Medication], creating a picture consistent with [suspected syndrome, e.g., thyrotoxicosis, adrenal insufficiency].

Example 1 (Thyroid): "A 34-year-old woman presents with a three-month history of weight loss despite increased appetite, palpitations, and heat intolerance, consistent with new-onset thyrotoxicosis."

Example 2 (Adrenal): "A 52-year-old man with a history of long-term steroid use for asthma presents with profound fatigue, postural dizziness, and muscle aches, raising suspicion of iatrogenic adrenal insufficiency."

8. Forming a Differential Diagnosis from the History

After synthesising the history, formulate a differential diagnosis. In endocrinology, it's helpful to think both syndromically (e.g., "the diagnosis is Cushing's syndrome") and anatomically/aetiologically (e.g., "the cause could be an adrenal adenoma, a pituitary adenoma, or exogenous steroids").

9. Presenting the History

A clear, structured presentation is crucial. It should begin with the problem representation, followed by a logical narrative of the history, and conclude with a summary of key findings and a provisional diagnostic plan.

"This is Mrs. Jane Doe, a 45-year-old teacher, who presents with a 4-month history of increasing fatigue, weight gain, and feeling cold.

History of Presenting Complaint:
- The fatigue has been progressive, leaving her feeling exhausted by the end of the school day. She has gained 5kg in weight over this period despite no change in her diet.
- She constantly feels cold, even wearing jumpers indoors, and has noticed her skin has become very dry and her hair seems thinner.
- She also reports significant constipation, opening her bowels only twice a week compared to her usual once daily. Her mood has been low, and she struggles with concentration.
- There are no symptoms of palpitations, heat intolerance, tremor or neck swelling.

Systemic Enquiry:
- No visual changes, headaches, or galactorrhoea.
- No polydipsia or polyuria.

Past Medical History:
- She has vitiligo, diagnosed 5 years ago. Otherwise fit and well.

Drug History:
- No regular medications. No allergies.

Family History:
- Her mother has an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism).

Social History:
- She lives with her husband and two children. She works full-time as a primary school teacher but has taken some days off due to fatigue. She does not smoke and drinks alcohol rarely.

To summarise, this is a 45-year-old woman with a subacute history of fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance. Given her personal history of vitiligo and family history of thyroid disease, the presentation is highly suggestive of primary hypothyroidism."
10. Common Pitfalls & Expert Tips
  • Attribution Error: Ascribing vague symptoms like fatigue or anxiety solely to psychological stress or depression without considering an organic cause. Always complete a full endocrine systems review.
  • Forgetting Drugs: Failing to identify medications that can cause endocrine dysfunction is a common pitfall. Always take a meticulous drug history, including over-the-counter and herbal remedies. Key culprits are steroids, amiodarone, and lithium.
  • Ignoring Subtle Clues: Gradual changes may be missed by the patient. Ask about changes in glove, ring, or shoe size (acromegaly), or skin pigmentation (Addison's). Asking to see a driver's licence or an old photo can be remarkably revealing.
  • Insufficient Quantification: Vague statements like "drinks a lot" are unhelpful. Quantify symptoms where possible: "How many litres do you drink a day?", "How much weight have you gained/lost over what period?".
  • Avoiding Sensitive Topics: Don't shy away from asking about menstrual function, libido, and erectile dysfunction. These are key diagnostic clues. A professional, matter-of-fact approach is usually effective. Explain why you're asking.
  • Expert Tip: When you suspect an adrenal crisis (a "can't miss" diagnosis), one of the most useful questions is about postural symptoms: "Do you feel dizzy, lightheaded, or like you might black out when you stand up from sitting or lying?". A clear "yes" is a major red flag.
11. Illustrative History Example
  • Clinician: "Hello, I'm one of the junior doctors. I understand you've been feeling unwell recently. Can you tell me what's been happening?"
  • Patient: "I just feel completely wired and on edge all the time. I've lost about a stone in weight over the last two months, which is strange because I'm eating more than ever."
  • Clinician: "That sounds unsettling. When you say 'wired and on edge', what does that feel like?"
  • Patient: "My heart feels like it's racing a lot of the time, even when I'm just sitting watching TV. And my hands have started shaking, which is embarrassing."
  • Clinician: "I see. Have you noticed any other changes? For example, in your body temperature or energy levels?"
  • Patient: "Yes, I'm boiling hot all the time. My husband is complaining about me having the windows open in the middle of the night! And I feel tired, but I just can't switch off or relax."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, that's very clear. Can I ask, have you had any trouble with your bowels, perhaps looser stools than usual?"
  • Patient: "Funnily enough, yes. I have been going to the toilet more often."
  • Clinician: "And have you noticed any swelling in your neck, or any problems with your eyes, like they feel gritty or look more prominent?"
  • Patient: "My neck feels fine, but my colleagues have said my eyes look a bit 'starey' recently."

Problem Representation: A 28-year-old woman presents with a two-month history of unintentional weight loss despite a good appetite, associated with palpitations, heat intolerance, tremor, and anxiety, highly suggestive of thyrotoxicosis, possibly due to Graves' disease given the eye signs.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Thinks it might be severe anxiety or stress from work.
  • Concerns: Worried that the palpitations mean there is something seriously wrong with her heart.
  • Expectations: Hopes to get some tests to find out the cause and get treatment to feel normal again.
