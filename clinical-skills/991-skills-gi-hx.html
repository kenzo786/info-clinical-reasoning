1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a gastrointestinal (GI) history is to methodically gather information to construct a clear narrative of the patient's symptoms. This allows a clinician to localise the problem (e.g., upper GI, lower GI, hepatobiliary, or pancreatic) and differentiate between organic disease and functional disorders. A key objective is the rapid identification of surgical emergencies and other life-threatening conditions, such as a GI haemorrhage, bowel perforation, or obstruction. Unlike a general medical history, the GI history is highly structured to explore symptoms that can often be vague or non-specific. The process is essential for building an accurate differential diagnosis, guiding investigations, and formulating a safe, effective management plan.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective and sensitive communication is the cornerstone of a successful GI history. Many symptoms relate to personal and private topics, such as bowel habits, alcohol intake, and body weight. It is essential to create a safe, confidential environment where the patient feels comfortable enough to share this sensitive information. Acknowledging the personal nature of these questions and adopting a professional, non-judgemental tone is crucial. Building this therapeutic rapport through active listening and empathy ensures you understand what truly matters to the patient, which is vital for shared decision-making about their care.

  • Establish Rapport: Begin with a warm introduction ("Hello, I'm one of the foundation doctors") and ensure the patient is comfortable. Sit down, maintain open body language, and explain the need to ask some personal questions.
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open-ended questions ("Tell me what’s brought you in today?") to allow the patient to describe their problems in their own words. Gradually transition to more focused, closed questions ("Is the pain worse after eating?") to clarify specific details.
  • Active Listening & Signposting: Show you are listening by nodding and summarising key points. Use signposting to structure the consultation ("Okay, we've talked about the pain. Now I'm going to ask some questions about your waterworks and bowel habits.").
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): It is vital to understand the patient's perspective.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing these symptoms?"
    • Concerns: Directly address the patient's main worry. A key question is, "Was there anything specific you were worried this might be?" This often uncovers fears of cancer or the need for major surgery.
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we would be able to do for you today?" This helps align the management plan with the patient's goals.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

The gastrointestinal system signals distress through a number of cardinal symptoms. A thorough exploration of these is essential, with an immediate focus on identifying red flags that require urgent senior review and intervention.

  • Abdominal Pain: The most common GI symptom. The location, character, and timing provide critical diagnostic clues.
    • Red Flag: Severe, sudden-onset pain ("thunderclap" headache equivalent), or signs of peritonitis (pain worse on movement, coughing) suggest perforation, ischaemia, or a ruptured aneurysm and require immediate surgical assessment.
  • Nausea & Vomiting: Enquire about the volume, content (food, bile, blood), and timing of any vomit.
    • Red Flag: Vomiting fresh blood (haematemesis) or "coffee grounds" (altered blood) indicates an upper GI bleed. Persistent vomiting with abdominal distension and inability to pass flatus or faeces suggests bowel obstruction.
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing): A critical symptom that must be taken seriously. Differentiate between problems initiating a swallow (oropharyngeal) and food getting stuck (oesophageal).
    • Red Flag: Progressive dysphagia (starting with solids, progressing to liquids) accompanied by weight loss is a classic presentation of oesophageal cancer and requires urgent investigation.
  • Change in Bowel Habit: Ask about diarrhoea, constipation, or alternating patterns. Clarify frequency, consistency (the Bristol Stool Chart is a useful tool), and associated features like urgency or tenesmus (a feeling of incomplete evacuation).
    • Red Flag: A persistent and unexplained change in bowel habit for six weeks or more in a patient over 50 years old is a red flag for colorectal cancer.
  • Rectal Bleeding & Melaena: Clarify the nature of the blood: fresh red blood on the paper or in the pan (haematochezia) versus black, tarry, offensive-smelling stools (melaena), which signifies altered blood from an upper GI source.
    • Red Flag: Any haemodynamically unstable patient with active bleeding requires immediate resuscitation and emergency endoscopy. Melaena combined with dizziness or collapse is a medical emergency.
  • Jaundice: A yellow discolouration of the sclera (eyes) and skin due to high bilirubin levels. Ask about associated pale stools and dark urine, which indicates an obstructive cause.
    • Red Flag: Painless, progressive jaundice in an older patient is highly suspicious for malignancy (pancreatic or cholangiocarcinoma) until proven otherwise.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

Each cardinal symptom must be methodically explored. For pain, a useful mnemonic is SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, and Severity). Using such a framework ensures all critical details are captured.

  • For Abdominal Pain (SOCRATES):
    • Site: Ask the patient to point with one finger. Epigastric (stomach, duodenum), Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) (liver, gallbladder), Left Iliac Fossa (LIF) (sigmoid colon).
    • Onset: Sudden (perforation, rupture) vs. gradual (inflammatory, infectious).
    • Character: Burning (peptic ulcer), colicky (waves of pain from obstruction), constant and severe (ischaemia, peritonitis).
    • Radiation: To the back (pancreatitis, aortic aneurysm), to the right shoulder/scapula (biliary colic).
    • Associated Symptoms: Fever, vomiting, diarrhoea, jaundice, weight loss.
    • Timing: Relationship to meals (worse after food suggests peptic ulcer/biliary colic; better after food suggests duodenal ulcer).
    • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: Worsened by fatty foods (biliary colic), relieved by defecation (colonic pain), worse on lying flat (reflux).
    • Severity: Score out of 10. Severe pain (10/10) suggests serious pathology like pancreatitis or mesenteric ischaemia.
  • For a Change in Bowel Habit:
    • Consistency & Form: Use the Bristol Stool Chart (1=hard lumps, 7=liquid) to get an objective measure.
    • Frequency: "How many times a day do you typically go? And how about now?"
    • Colour: Brown, black (melaena), pale (obstructive jaundice), or silvery (ampullary carcinoma).
    • Blood & Mucus: Is blood on the paper, in the toilet bowl, or mixed in with the stool? Is there slimy mucus?
    • Associated features: Urgency, tenesmus, or a feeling of incomplete emptying.
5. System-specific review of systems

Beyond the cardinal symptoms, asking about other related symptoms helps to screen for complications, identify the underlying cause, or point towards an alternative diagnosis.

  • General: Ask about unintentional weight loss (malignancy, malabsorption, IBD), loss of appetite (anorexia), fevers, or rigors (infection, abscess, cholangitis).
  • Oesophago-gastric: Ask about heartburn or acid reflux (GORD), indigestion (dyspepsia), and odynophagia (pain on swallowing).
  • Hepatobiliary: Ask about pruritus (itching, common in cholestasis), pale stools, and dark urine (both suggest obstructive jaundice).
  • Gynaecological: In any female presenting with lower abdominal pain or bloating, a gynaecological history is mandatory. Ask about their last menstrual period (LMP), cycle regularity, heavy or painful periods, and the possibility of pregnancy. This helps rule out ectopic pregnancy, ovarian cysts, or pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Urological: Ask about dysuria (pain on urination), frequency, and haematuria (blood in the urine) to exclude a urinary tract source for abdominal pain.
  • Extra-intestinal Manifestations: Enquire about mouth ulcers, eye redness/pain (uveitis), and joint pains/swelling (arthropathy), which can be associated with Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD).
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides the essential context for their symptoms. This information is vital for risk stratification and helps determine the pre-test probability of a particular GI diagnosis.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Focus on previous GI diagnoses: GORD, peptic ulcer disease, IBD (Crohn's/Ulcerative Colitis), gallstones, pancreatitis, diverticular disease, or known liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis).
    • Ask specifically about previous abdominal or pelvic surgeries, as adhesions are a major cause of bowel obstruction.
    • Note any history of diabetes, vascular disease (risk for mesenteric ischaemia), or autoimmune conditions.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • This is critical. Ask specifically about:
      • NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen) and aspirin: can cause peptic ulcers and GI bleeding.
      • Anticoagulants/Antiplatelets (e.g., warfarin, apixaban, clopidogrel): increase bleeding risk.
      • Steroids: can also cause peptic ulceration and mask signs of peritonitis.
      • Opioids (e.g., codeine, morphine): a common cause of constipation.
      • Recent antibiotic use: a risk factor for Clostridioides difficile colitis.
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask specifically about any family history of bowel cancer, noting the relative and age of diagnosis.
    • Also ask about IBD, coeliac disease, or hereditary liver conditions.
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Alcohol: This must be quantified accurately in units per week. It is a major risk factor for liver disease, pancreatitis, and gastritis. Frame this non-judgementally.
    • Smoking: A key risk factor for peptic ulcer disease, GI cancers, and Crohn's disease. Quantify in pack-years. (Pack-years = [cigarettes per day / 20] x years smoked).
    • Travel History: Ask about recent foreign travel, which may suggest an infectious cause of diarrhoea.
    • Diet: Note any specific trigger foods. Is their diet generally balanced?
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

The problem representation is a concise, one-sentence summary that encapsulates the most critical elements of the history. It forms the bridge between gathering data and clinical reasoning, forcing the clinician to synthesise information and commit to a diagnostic direction. A good problem representation includes the patient's demographics, key risk factors, and the temporal pattern of their main symptoms. The formula is often: [Age/sex] with [key risk factors] presenting with [symptom profile] suggestive of [most likely diagnostic category].

  • Example 1 (Bowel Obstruction): "An 82-year-old woman with a history of a previous hysterectomy presents with a three-day history of colicky central abdominal pain, vomiting, and absolute constipation, in keeping with acute small bowel obstruction secondary to adhesions."
  • Example 2 (Upper GI Bleed): "A 68-year-old man with a history of osteoarthritis on long-term naproxen presents with a 24-hour history of passing black, tarry stools and one episode of coffee-ground vomiting, consistent with an upper GI bleed secondary to NSAID-induced peptic ulcer disease."
  • Example 3 (Inflammatory Bowel Disease): "A 24-year-old man presents with a six-week history of bloody diarrhoea, crampy lower abdominal pain, urgency, and a 5kg weight loss, concerning for a new presentation of inflammatory bowel disease, most likely ulcerative colitis."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The pattern of historical features is the most powerful tool for shaping the differential diagnosis and determining which conditions are most likely.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Burning epigastric pain, worse after meals or at night. History of NSAID use. Relieved by antacids. Peptic Ulcer Disease / Gastritis
Severe, colicky RUQ pain, radiating to the right shoulder, triggered by fatty meals, lasting for hours. Biliary Colic / Acute Cholecystitis
Severe central abdominal pain radiating to the back, associated with vomiting. History of gallstones or heavy alcohol use. Acute Pancreatitis
Colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, abdominal distension, and absolute constipation (no flatus or stool). Bowel Obstruction
Crampy LIF pain and fever in an older patient, often with a change in bowel habit. Acute Diverticulitis
Chronic or relapsing bloody diarrhoea, abdominal pain, urgency, and weight loss in a younger patient. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all key information is conveyed clearly and concisely. This is vital for safe and effective handovers, ward round presentations, or referrals to other specialities like surgery or gastroenterology.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented with a chief complaint of [e.g., abdominal pain], which began [timing, e.g., yesterday evening].

The pain is located in the [site, e.g., right upper quadrant]. The patient describes it as [character, e.g., a severe, constant ache]. It radiates to the [radiation, e.g., right shoulder]. On a scale of 1 to 10, the severity is [score]. It was triggered by [e.g., a fatty meal]. Associated symptoms include [e.g., nausea and two episodes of vomiting].

Specifically regarding their GI system, their bowels last opened [timing], and it was [normal/abnormal]. There has been no diarrhoea or rectal bleeding.

Their past medical history includes [list key conditions, e.g., type 2 diabetes].
Current medications are [list key medications, e.g., metformin].
There is no significant family history.
Regarding their social history, they drink [number] units of alcohol per week and are a non-smoker.

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] with a background of [risk factors] presenting with [key symptom cluster], which is highly suspicious for [top differential, e.g., acute cholecystitis].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly improve the quality and accuracy of the GI history, leading to better and safer clinical decision-making.

  • Pitfall: Accepting "indigestion" as a diagnosis. This is a symptom, not a cause.
  • Expert Tip: Break it down. Ask the patient what they mean by indigestion. Is it burning pain (dyspepsia), acid reflux, bloating, or something else? This clarifies the underlying symptom.
  • Pitfall: Taking a vague alcohol or drug history. Patients may under-report alcohol intake or forget over-the-counter medications.
  • Expert Tip: Frame alcohol questions gently ("To get a full picture, I need to ask about lifestyle factors like alcohol. Can you tell me what you might drink in a typical week?"). For drugs, specifically ask about painkillers like ibuprofen, naproxen, or aspirin, as patients often don't consider them 'real' medicines.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting a gynaecological history in a female with abdominal pain.
  • Expert Tip: Make it a routine. For any female of child-bearing age with abdominal pain, always ask for their Last Menstrual Period (LMP) and if there is any chance they could be pregnant. This can avoid missing a dangerous ectopic pregnancy.
  • Pitfall: Not clarifying the nature of rectal bleeding.
  • Expert Tip: Ask specific questions to differentiate. "Was the blood on the toilet paper after wiping, splashed in the pan, or mixed in with the stool itself? What colour was it - bright red or a darker colour?" This helps distinguish between haemorrhoids, diverticular bleeds, and cancer.
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with a likely flare of inflammatory bowel disease.

  • Clinician: "Hello Mrs. Jones, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've come in today because your tummy problems have been acting up. Can you tell me what's been happening in your own words?"
  • Patient: "It's my colitis, doctor. It's come back. For the last two weeks, I've been having to run to the toilet, maybe ten times a day. It's just diarrhoea."
  • Clinician: "Ten times a day is a lot. I'm sorry to hear that. When you go, what is the diarrhoea like? Is there anything else with it?"
  • Patient: "It's very loose, watery, and there's always blood and a sort of slime with it. And even after I've been, I feel like I need to go again straight away."
  • Clinician: "That sounds very distressing. That feeling of needing to go again, we call tenesmus. Are you getting any tummy pain with it?"
  • Patient: "Yes, I get these horrible cramps in the lower part of my tummy before I have to rush to the loo. They go away for a bit after."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, that's very clear. I just need to ask a couple more things. Have you noticed any fevers, or have you lost any weight recently?"
  • Patient: "I've been feeling hot and cold at night, and my trousers feel a bit looser. I weighed myself yesterday and I've lost half a stone."
  • Clinician: "Okay. Last question for now. What have you been worried about with all this going on?"
  • Patient: "I'm just worried I'll need to go back on the strong steroids, I hate the side effects. I was hoping there might be something else."

Problem Representation: A 34-year-old woman with a known history of ulcerative colitis presents with a two-week history of worsening bloody diarrhoea, tenesmus, crampy abdominal pain, and subjective fevers, consistent with an acute flare of her IBD.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Knows this is a flare of her ulcerative colitis.
  • Concerns: Worried about needing steroid treatment and the associated side effects.
  • Expectations: Hopes for an alternative treatment to control the flare.
