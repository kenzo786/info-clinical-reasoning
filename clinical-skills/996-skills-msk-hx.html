1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a musculoskeletal (MSK) history is to determine the anatomical origin of the problem and its underlying pathology. This involves distinguishing between articular (within the joint) and peri-articular (around the joint) structures, and differentiating between four main pathological processes: mechanical/degenerative, inflammatory, infective, or neoplastic. The history is also crucial for assessing the functional impact of the condition on the patient's life and, most importantly, for identifying 'red flag' symptoms that signify serious spinal or systemic pathology requiring urgent investigation and management.

2. Core principles & communication

Building trust is essential for an effective MSK consultation. Pain and disability can be sensitive topics, so creating a confidential and empathetic environment is key. Always begin by introducing yourself and your role, and ensure the patient is physically comfortable. A patient-centred approach, focusing on how the symptoms affect their life, will provide a richer diagnostic narrative and facilitate shared decision-making. Asking the patient to "point with one finger to where the pain is worst" can be an invaluable starting point, localising the issue far more accurately than verbal descriptions alone.

  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad questions ("Tell me about the trouble you've been having with your knee?") and then use more focused questions to clarify details ("Does the knee lock or give way?").
  • Active Listening: Acknowledge the patient's story by summarising key points back to them. This confirms your understanding and validates their experience.
  • Signposting: Guide the patient through the consultation. For example, "Okay, we have talked about the pain in your hands. Now I’d like to ask some questions about your general health to see if anything else is connected."
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): This is vital for understanding the patient's perspective.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing these symptoms?"
    • Concerns: "What is your biggest worry about this problem?" (e.g., "I'm worried I'll have to stop working" or "I'm worried it's arthritis like my mother had").
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?"
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

A focused exploration of cardinal MSK symptoms is the cornerstone of diagnosis. It is crucial to identify red flags that may indicate a serious underlying condition.

  • Pain: The most common symptom. It's essential to differentiate inflammatory pain (often worse with rest, improves with activity) from mechanical pain (worse with activity, improves with rest).
    • Red Flag: Severe, constant, progressive, non-mechanical pain, especially pain that wakes the patient at night. In the context of back pain, this could indicate malignancy or infection.
  • Stiffness: A sensation of resistance to movement. The key feature is its duration, especially early morning stiffness.
    • Red Flag: Early morning stiffness lasting more than 30-60 minutes is a hallmark of inflammatory arthritis (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis).
  • Swelling: May be articular (in the joint) or peri-articular. Ask about the timing, persistence, and location of any swelling.
    • Red Flag: A single hot, red, swollen, and exquisitely painful joint is septic arthritis until proven otherwise and requires immediate hospital assessment.
  • Weakness & Locking: Differentiate true neurological weakness from weakness due to pain inhibition. Locking describes a joint getting stuck in one position, suggesting an internal derangement like a meniscal tear. Giving way suggests instability.
  • Loss of Function: This is arguably the most important outcome for the patient. Enquire specifically how symptoms affect their work, hobbies, and activities of daily living (e.g., washing, dressing, cooking).
  • Systemic Symptoms: Fever, night sweats, and unintentional weight loss are constitutional symptoms.
    • Red Flag: The presence of these symptoms alongside musculoskeletal pain is highly suspicious for infection, malignancy, or a systemic inflammatory disease.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

For pain, a systematic approach is vital. The SOCRATES mnemonic (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, and Severity) is an excellent framework.

  • Site: Where is the pain? Ask the patient to point. Is it one joint (monoarthritis), 2-4 joints (oligoarthritis), or 5+ joints (polyarthritis)? Is it symmetrical?
  • Onset: Was it sudden (e.g., trauma, gout, infection) or gradual (e.g., osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis)?
  • Character: How would you describe the pain? (e.g., dull, aching, sharp, stabbing, burning).
  • Radiation: Does the pain travel anywhere? (e.g., sciatica is pain radiating from the back down the leg in a dermatomal pattern).
  • Associated Symptoms: Is there swelling, redness, stiffness, or weakness? Are there any extra-articular features (see review of systems)?
  • Timing: Is there a daily pattern? Inflammatory pain and stiffness are typically worse in the morning and after rest. Mechanical pain is worse after use and at the end of the day.
    • Specifically for stiffness, ask: "How long does it take for you to 'loosen up' in the mornings?"
  • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: What makes it better or worse? (e.g., rest, movement, specific activities, painkillers).
  • Severity: Ask the patient to rate the pain on a scale of 1 to 10.
5. System-specific review of systems

Many musculoskeletal diseases are systemic. A thorough "top-to-toe" review is essential to uncover clues pointing towards a specific rheumatological diagnosis. Think "joint plus one other system".

  • General: Ask about fevers, night sweats, fatigue, and unintentional weight loss. These can indicate systemic inflammation, infection, or malignancy.
  • Neurological (Spinal): Crucial for back pain. Ask about leg weakness, numbness or altered sensation in the saddle area (inner thighs, buttocks, perineum), and any disturbance to bladder or bowel function (incontinence or retention). These are red flags for Cauda Equina Syndrome.
  • Dermatological: Ask about rashes (e.g., the butterfly rash of lupus, scaly plaques of psoriasis), skin thickening (scleroderma), or nail changes (pitting in psoriatic arthritis).
  • Eyes: Ask about red, painful eyes (uveitis, seen in spondyloarthropathies) or dry eyes (Sjögren's syndrome).
  • ENT/Respiratory: Ask about dry mouth (Sjögren's syndrome), or shortness of breath and cough which could indicate lung involvement in conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or connective tissue diseases.
  • Gastrointestinal: Ask about mouth ulcers or any symptoms of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) like diarrhoea or abdominal pain, as this is associated with enteropathic arthritis.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

Understanding the patient's background is vital for interpreting their symptoms and assessing risk factors.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Ask about previous trauma, fractures, or surgeries related to the presenting complaint.
    • Enquire about known diagnoses like osteoarthritis, gout, psoriasis, or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • List all current medications, including over-the-counter drugs like NSAIDs.
    • Note previous treatments tried and their effect.
    • Be aware of drugs that can cause MSK side effects (e.g., statins causing myalgia, long-term steroids causing osteoporosis, fluoroquinolones causing tendon rupture).
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask specifically about inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, or ankylosing spondylitis in first-degree relatives.
    • A family history of gout or osteoarthritis can also be relevant.
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Occupation & Hobbies: What does their job entail? (e.g., manual labour, office work). Are there repetitive movements?
    • Functional Impact: This is key. How do the symptoms affect activities of daily living (ADLs)? Can they wash and dress, cook, shop? Has it affected their work or hobbies?
    • Living Situation: Do they live in a house or flat? Are there stairs? Do they use any mobility aids? Who is at home to help?
    • Ask about smoking and alcohol intake. Smoking is a significant risk factor for developing rheumatoid arthritis and for poorer outcomes.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

The problem representation is a one-sentence summary that encapsulates the patient's story. It should include demographics, the key temporal and qualitative features of the symptoms, and the most likely pathological process. This sharpens clinical reasoning and guides investigation. The formula is typically: [Age/sex] with [key risk factors/PMHx] presenting with [symptom pattern] suggestive of [most likely diagnostic category].

  • Example 1 (Inflammatory): "A 45-year-old woman presents with a three-month history of a symmetrical, inflammatory polyarthritis affecting the small joints of her hands, associated with over an hour of early morning stiffness, highly suggestive of new-onset rheumatoid arthritis."
  • Example 2 (Mechanical): "A 68-year-old retired builder with a history of knee injuries presents with a two-year history of gradual-onset, right-sided knee pain that is worse on walking, relieved by rest, and associated with minimal morning stiffness, in keeping with osteoarthritis."
  • Example 3 (Red Flag): "A 55-year-old man presents with a six-week history of new-onset, constant low back pain that wakes him from sleep, associated with bilateral leg weakness and saddle numbness, concerning for cauda equina syndrome."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The pattern of historical features is the most powerful tool for shaping the differential diagnosis and determining which conditions are most likely.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Symmetrical polyarthritis, small joints of hands/feet, morning stiffness >60 mins, gradual onset. Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
Pain worse with use, better with rest. Affects large weight-bearing joints (knee, hip) or small joints of hands (DIPs). Morning stiffness <30 mins. Osteoarthritis (OA)
Acute, exquisitely painful, hot, red, swollen single joint (often big toe or knee). Rapid onset. Gout or Septic Arthritis
Joint pain in a patient with a known psoriatic rash or typical nail changes (pitting, onycholysis). Can be asymmetrical. Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA)
Low back pain and stiffness in a young man (<45), worse in the morning, improves with exercise, not rest. Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS) / Axial Spondyloarthritis
Low back pain with neurological signs in the legs (weakness, numbness) and/or sphincter disturbance. Cauda Equina Syndrome, Spinal Cord Compression
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all key information is conveyed clearly and concisely, which is vital for safe and effective handovers and referrals.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented with a [duration] history of [joint pain/stiffness/etc].

The primary site is the [location e.g., small joints of hands, symmetrically]. The onset was [gradual/sudden]. It is exacerbated by [triggers, e.g., activity/rest] and relieved by [relieving factors]. The main associated symptom is [e.g., morning stiffness lasting for 2 hours]. On a scale of 1 to 10, the severity is [score].

There is a significant impact on function, for example [describe functional limitation].

A systemic review is positive for [e.g., rash] but negative for [e.g., eye or GI symptoms].

Their past medical history includes [list key conditions]. Current medications are [list key medications].
There is a family history of [e.g., rheumatoid arthritis in their mother].
Regarding their social history, they work as a [occupation] and smoke [number] cigarettes per day.

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] presenting with [key symptom cluster, e.g., a symmetrical inflammatory polyarthritis], which is suspicious for [top differential, e.g., new-onset rheumatoid arthritis].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly improve the diagnostic accuracy of the history.

  • Pitfall: Vague localisation of pain. A patient saying they have "hip pain" may be pointing to their lower back or buttock.
    • Expert Tip: Always ask the patient to "point with one finger" to the site of the pain. This is known as the "C-sign" for true hip joint pathology, where they cup their hand around the side of their hip.
  • Pitfall: Not quantifying stiffness. Simply noting "morning stiffness" is insufficient.
    • Expert Tip: Always ask for the duration: "How long, in minutes or hours, does the stiffness last from when you wake up?". Stiffness lasting >30-60 minutes suggests an inflammatory cause.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting the full systemic review. It is easy to focus only on the joint.
    • Expert Tip: Have a simple checklist for extra-articular features (Skin, Eyes, Lungs, Gut, GU). Remember the phrase "joint plus one": if you find an extra-articular feature, it strongly points to a specific systemic disease.
  • Pitfall: Neglecting the functional and social history.
    • Expert Tip: Frame questions in a practical way: "What could you do three months ago that you cannot do now?". Ask about specific activities: "Can you turn a key in a lock? Can you brush your hair?". This provides a clear measure of impact.
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with new-onset inflammatory arthritis.

  • Clinician: "Hello Mrs. Jones, I'm one of the foundation doctors. I understand you've been sent by your GP with some joint pains. Can you tell me about that in your own words?"
  • Patient: "It started a few months ago, mainly in my hands. It's just so hard to get going in the mornings. My fingers are stiff and puffy."
  • Clinician: "Okay, stiff and puffy hands. When you say stiff in the morning, how long does that last for?"
  • Patient: "Ages. It takes a good couple of hours before I can make a proper fist. I'm really struggling with things like opening jars."
  • Clinician: "I see. And which joints in your hands are most affected?"
  • Patient: "It's mainly these ones, the knuckles. On both hands. And my wrists feel sore too."
  • Clinician: "Does anything seem to make the pain and stiffness better?"
  • Patient: "Moving about helps a bit. Sitting still for too long makes them seize up."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, that's very clear. I need to ask some general questions. Have you noticed any other problems, say with your skin, eyes, or general energy levels?"
  • Patient: "I've just been feeling exhausted, completely wiped out, which isn't like me at all."
  • Clinician: "And is there anything you've been worried this might be?"
  • Patient: "My mum had terrible rheumatoid arthritis, and I'm terrified I'm going the same way."

Problem Representation: A 48-year-old woman presents with a three-month history of a symmetrical, inflammatory polyarthritis affecting her metacarpophalangeal (MCP) and wrist joints, associated with over two hours of morning stiffness and significant fatigue, highly suggestive of new-onset rheumatoid arthritis.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Thinks it might be arthritis.
  • Concerns: Worried it is rheumatoid arthritis, like her mother had, and is fearful of the potential disability.
  • Expectations: Wants a diagnosis and to start treatment to prevent her from getting worse.
