1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the renal and urological examination is to detect physical signs of kidney disease, fluid imbalance, and urinary tract abnormalities. It is a systematic process designed to identify evidence of conditions such as chronic kidney disease (CKD), acute kidney injury (AKI), urinary retention, polycystic kidney disease, and renal artery stenosis. The examination integrates findings from a general assessment with a focused abdominal examination. Its findings must be combined with the patient's history to build a complete clinical picture and guide further investigation, such as identifying a palpable bladder in a patient with urinary symptoms or signs of fluid overload in someone with known kidney disease.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A precise renal and urological examination requires a good understanding of the underlying anatomy, particularly the retroperitoneal location of the kidneys.

  • The Kidneys: These are retroperitoneal organs, meaning they lie behind the abdominal peritoneum. The right kidney typically sits slightly lower than the left due to the presence of the liver. They are not usually palpable in healthy, slim adults.
  • Costovertebral Angle (CVA): This is a key surface landmark, formed on the back by the 12th rib and the vertebral column. It is the site for percussing for kidney tenderness (renal angle tenderness), which can indicate inflammation as seen in pyelonephritis.
  • The Bladder: When empty, the bladder sits deep within the pelvis and is not palpable or percussable. When it fills and rises out of the pelvis, it may be palpable and percussable in the suprapubic region, the area of the abdomen just above the pubic bone.
  • Renal Arteries: These branch from the abdominal aorta. Bruits (abnormal sounds of turbulent blood flow) suggesting renal artery stenosis are best heard on either side of the midline, a few centimetres superior and lateral to the umbilicus.
  • Renal Transplant Scar: A surgically placed kidney is typically located in the right or left iliac fossa. A curved scar, known as a "hockey-stick" incision, may be visible here.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional, safe, and effective examination requires careful preparation and clear communication.

  1. Equipment:
    • Stethoscope
    • Gloves
    • Alcohol wipes to clean stethoscope
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to lie on the examination couch, ideally flat on their back (supine) to relax the abdominal muscles. Have one pillow for their head.
    • Preserve the patient's dignity with a sheet or blanket, ensuring adequate exposure of the abdomen from the pubic symphysis to the xiphisternum when required.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself clearly with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, my name is [Name] and I am a foundation doctor").
    • Explain the examination in simple terms and gain consent. It is crucial to mention the more intimate parts of a full examination.
    • A good phrase is: "I've been asked to perform an examination of your tummy and kidneys. This will involve me looking, feeling and listening to your tummy, and tapping on your back. To be thorough, it can also involve examining the genitalia and performing an internal examination of the back passage. Would that be okay? We will ensure you have a chaperone present for these parts."
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent sequence ensures no signs are missed. The examination moves from general inspection to a detailed assessment of the periphery and abdomen.

  1. General Inspection: From the end of the bed, look for signs of ill health. Note the patient's colour (sallow yellow-grey complexion of uraemia), any signs of breathlessness (fluid overload), cachexia, or scratching (pruritus of CKD). Note any equipment like fluid charts, catheters, or dialysis lines.
  2. The Hands and Arms: Inspect for nail changes like Beau's lines or leukonychia. Check for asterixis ('uraemic flap'), a sign of metabolic encephalopathy. Look for an arteriovenous (AV) fistula or graft in the arm; if present, palpate for a thrill and auscultate for a bruit.
  3. The Face & Neck: Check the eyes for conjunctival pallor (anaemia of CKD). Assess the Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP) with the patient at 45 degrees as a measure of fluid status.
  4. The Abdomen - Inspection: Look for any scars (especially a renal transplant scar in the right or left iliac fossa), masses, or abdominal distension (which could be ascites or a very full bladder).
  5. The Abdomen - Palpation: Palpate lightly then deeply across the nine abdominal regions. The key manoeuvre is to ballot each kidney. Place one hand behind the patient in the renal angle and the other on the abdomen below the costal margin. Gently push your hands together to try and feel the kidney between them. Also, palpate the suprapubic area for a firm, tense bladder indicative of retention.
  6. The Abdomen - Percussion: Percuss the suprapubic area, starting from the umbilicus and moving downwards. A change from a resonant to a dull note suggests a distended bladder.
  7. The Abdomen - Auscultation: Listen with the diaphragm approximately 2cm superior and lateral to the umbilicus on both sides for renal artery bruits.
  8. The Back: Ask the patient to sit forward. Gently percuss each costovertebral angle with the heel of your hand to elicit any tenderness, which is a key sign of pyelonephritis. While they are sitting forward, inspect the sacral area for pitting oedema.
  9. Completing the Examination: Check the legs for pitting pedal oedema. Auscultate the lung bases for crackles (a sign of pulmonary oedema). State that you would also, with consent and a chaperone, examine the external genitalia and perform a digital rectal examination (DRE) in men to assess the prostate gland.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising normality is key to identifying pathology. A normal examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • General Appearance: The patient is comfortable at rest, with normal skin colour, and not breathless.
    • Significance: Absence of uraemic signs or fluid overload makes severe, decompensated kidney disease less likely.
  • Peripheries and Fluid Status: No peripheral oedema. The JVP is not elevated (≤ 3cm from the sternal angle).
    • Significance: Suggests the patient is clinically euvolaemic (has a normal fluid volume).
  • Abdomen: Soft and non-tender on palpation.
    • Significance: No evidence of peritonitis or organomegaly.
  • Kidneys and Bladder: The kidneys are not palpable or ballotable. The bladder is not palpable and the suprapubic area is resonant to percussion.
    • Significance: This is a normal finding. Palpable kidneys or bladder are always pathological.
  • Auscultation and Back: No renal artery bruits are heard. There is no tenderness on percussion of the costovertebral angles.
    • Significance: Makes significant renal artery stenosis and acute pyelonephritis unlikely.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Abnormal signs are objective evidence of underlying disease. Any unexplained finding requires careful consideration and further investigation.

Palpable or ballotable kidney
  • The Sign: The kidney can be felt between the two hands on bimanual examination (ballotment).
  • Pathology Link: Suggests the kidney is enlarged. Unilateral enlargement could be due to a tumour or hydronephrosis (swelling from blocked urine flow). Bilateral enlargement is the classic sign of Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD).
Palpable, percussable bladder
  • The Sign: A firm, rounded suprapubic mass that is dull to percussion.
  • Pathology Link: A key sign of urinary retention, where urine is unable to leave the bladder, causing it to distend significantly. Common causes include benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) or neurological disease.
Costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness
  • The Sign: The patient experiences sharp pain when the renal angle is percussed.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates inflammation of the kidney. It is the cardinal physical sign of acute pyelonephritis (a kidney infection).
  • Red Flag: In a patient who is febrile or appears septic, CVA tenderness is a major red flag for severe pyelonephritis requiring urgent treatment.
Signs of fluid overload
  • The Sign: A combination of raised JVP, sacral oedema, pitting pedal oedema, and crackles at the lung bases.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates an excess of total body fluid. This is a common consequence of both acute and chronic kidney disease, where the kidneys fail to excrete salt and water effectively. It is also a feature of nephrotic syndrome.
Arteriovenous (AV) fistula
  • The Sign: A surgically created connection between an artery and a vein in the arm, with a palpable thrill and audible bruit.
  • Pathology Link: This is not a sign of disease itself, but of its treatment. An AV fistula is created to provide robust vascular access for haemodialysis in patients with end-stage renal disease. Its presence immediately tells you the patient has severe CKD.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating your findings. Start with a general statement on the patient's appearance and fluid status, then describe the key abdominal and other findings. Report pertinent positives and negatives.

On general inspection, the patient appeared [e.g., comfortable at rest / sallow and unwell].
Clinically, they appeared to be [euvolaemic / fluid overloaded], with a JVP of [X cm] and [presence/absence of] peripheral oedema.
There were no peripheral stigmata of chronic kidney disease.
On examination of the abdomen, it was soft and non-tender.
The kidneys were [not palpable / describe findings]. The bladder was [not palpable / palpable and percussable to the umbilicus].
There were no audible renal bruits.
On examination of the back, there was no tenderness at the costovertebral angles.
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

A constellation of signs pointing in the same direction is powerful evidence for a specific diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Raised JVP, sacral and pedal oedema, pulmonary crackles. Fluid Overload (secondary to AKI or CKD)
Bilateral, large, irregular, ballotable kidneys. Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)
Palpable, firm suprapubic mass, which is dull to percussion. Chronic Urinary Retention
Fever, systemic upset, and marked unilateral costovertebral angle tenderness. Acute Pyelonephritis
9. Relevant next-step investigations

Findings on examination are crucial for guiding the selection of appropriate next-step investigations.

  • If you find signs of fluid overload:
    • Logical Next Steps: Initial tests include a Urine dipstick (for protein/blood), blood tests including Urea & Electrolytes (U&Es) to assess kidney function, and a Chest X-ray to look for pulmonary oedema.
  • If you find a palpable kidney or abdominal mass:
    • Logical Next Steps: The key investigation is imaging, starting with a Renal Tract Ultrasound. A CT scan may be required for further characterisation. U&Es are also essential.
  • If you find signs of urinary retention (palpable bladder):
    • Logical Next Steps: An immediate bladder scan is needed to confirm the diagnosis and quantify the volume. This is usually followed by catheterisation. Blood tests should include U&Es (to check for post-renal AKI) and a Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) test in men (before a DRE if possible).
  • If you find signs of pyelonephritis (CVA tenderness):
    • Logical Next Steps: Urgent investigations include a urine dipstick and sending a mid-stream urine (MSU) for culture. Blood tests should include a Full Blood Count (FBC), C-Reactive Protein (CRP), U&Es, and blood cultures if sepsis is suspected.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good exam technique is vital for demonstrating competence, especially in an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to position the patient supine (flat) for the abdominal examination.
    • Not kneeling or crouching to be at the same level as the abdomen when inspecting and palpating.
    • Forgetting the key manoeuvre of balloting the kidneys.
    • Forgetting to percuss for the bladder.
    • Forgetting to ask the patient to sit forward to examine their back for CVA tenderness and sacral oedema. This is a frequently missed step.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start with the "magic formula": Wash hands, introduce yourself, state your name and role, explain the examination, and gain consent.
    • Verbalise what you are doing (e.g., "I am now going to feel for your kidneys..."). This shows the examiner your thought process.
    • Be systematic in your approach to the abdomen: inspect, then palpate, percuss, and auscultate.
    • If you find an AV fistula, demonstrate you know what it is by palpating for the thrill and auscultating for the bruit.
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant further steps, such as checking a urine dipstick, examining the external genitalia (with a chaperone), and performing a digital rectal examination.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings helps to solidify understanding and build clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)
    • Scenario: A 45-year-old man with a family history of kidney problems presents with high blood pressure and vague loin pain.
    • Expected Findings: The most striking finding would be bilateral, large, irregular kidneys that are ballotable on examination. His blood pressure would likely be elevated.
    • Summary: The presence of bilaterally enlarged kidneys in a patient with a relevant family history is highly suggestive of ADPKD.
  • Case 2: Acute Pyelonephritis
    • Scenario: A 28-year-old woman presents with a high fever, rigors, and severe left-sided back pain.
    • Expected Findings: She would likely appear unwell and flushed. The abdomen might be generally soft, but there would be marked tenderness on percussion of the left costovertebral angle.
    • Summary: The constellation of systemic sepsis and focal renal angle tenderness is classic for acute pyelonephritis.
  • Case 3: Chronic Urinary Retention
    • Scenario: An 80-year-old man with a known history of prostate problems presents with confusion and a lower abdominal swelling.
    • Expected Findings: On general inspection, he may have signs of uraemic flap (asterixis). The key finding would be a smooth, firm, palpable mass arising from the pelvis into the abdomen, which is dull to percussion. This is the distended bladder.
    • Summary: These signs are consistent with chronic urinary retention causing a post-renal acute kidney injury.
