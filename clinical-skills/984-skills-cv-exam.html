Cardivascular System History
1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a cardiovascular history is to gather specific information to differentiate between cardiac and non-cardiac causes of symptoms, formally assess a patient's cardiovascular risk profile, and identify evidence of cardiac end-organ consequences like heart failure. Unlike a general medical history, this process is highly structured to rapidly identify potentially life-threatening conditions such as acute coronary syndrome (ACS). The key is to build a narrative that not only describes the symptom but also places it in the context of the patient's background risk and functional status. This focused approach is essential for accurate diagnosis and timely, appropriate management.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective communication is the foundation of accurate diagnosis. It is essential to create a safe, confidential environment where the patient feels able to share sensitive information, and their concerns are heard and validated. Adopting a patient-centred approach ensures you understand what matters most to them, which is crucial for shared decision-making.

  • Establish Rapport: Begin with a warm introduction ("Hello, I'm one of the foundation doctors") and ensure the patient is comfortable before starting. Sit at their level and maintain open body language.
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open questions ("Tell me what's been happening?") to allow the patient to tell their story in their own words. Gradually narrow down with more focused, closed questions ("Is the pain sharp or dull?") to clarify specific details.
  • Active Listening & Signposting: Actively listen to the patient, providing verbal and non-verbal cues (e.g., nodding, summarising back). Use signposting to structure the consultation ("Okay, we've talked about the pain. Now I'm going to ask some questions about your general health.").
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): Exploring ICE is critically important in a cardiovascular context.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing this?"
    • Concerns: Directly address the patient's main worry. A crucial question is, "Is there anything specific you've been worried this could be?" This often reveals a fear of a heart attack.
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?" This helps align the management plan with the patient's goals.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

The cardiovascular system typically signals distress through a limited number of cardinal symptoms. A thorough exploration of these is essential, with an immediate focus on identifying any red flags that require urgent action.

  • Chest Pain: The most critical cardiovascular symptom. The goal is to determine if it is ischaemic in nature.
    • Red Flag: Chest pain that is new, severe, crushing in character, associated with sweating or nausea, or lasts longer than 15 minutes suggests an Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) and requires immediate emergency assessment.
  • Dyspnoea (Breathlessness): Can be an 'anginal equivalent' (a symptom of ischaemia without pain) or a primary symptom of heart failure.
    • Red Flag: Sudden, severe breathlessness, especially if associated with chest pain, could indicate a pulmonary embolism or acute heart failure and necessitates urgent review.
  • Palpitations: An awareness of one's own heartbeat. The history aims to define its rate and rhythm.
    • Red Flag: Palpitations associated with chest pain, pre-syncope (light-headedness), or syncope (fainting) suggest a haemodynamically unstable arrhythmia that requires immediate investigation.
  • Syncope / Pre-syncope: A transient loss of consciousness (or near-faint) due to temporary interruption of blood flow to the brain.
    • Red Flag: Syncope occurring *during* exertion is a major warning sign for structural heart disease (e.g., aortic stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy) and requires urgent cardiological assessment.
  • Oedema: Typically peripheral oedema (swelling of the ankles or legs), which can indicate fluid retention from right-sided heart failure.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

Each cardinal symptom must be broken down systematically. A useful mnemonic is SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, and Severity). Using this framework ensures no critical detail is missed.

  • For Chest Pain:
    • Site: Central, retrosternal, or diffuse pain is more likely to be cardiac.
    • Onset: Sudden onset is typical for ACS or pulmonary embolism.
    • Character: "Heavy", "tight", "crushing", or "pressure" are classic words for ischaemic pain.
    • Radiation: Classic radiation is to the jaw, neck, shoulders, or down one or both arms.
    • Associated Symptoms: Nausea, sweating (diaphoresis), and breathlessness are common in ACS.
    • Timing: Pain lasting >20 minutes suggests MI. Pain for 2-10 minutes suggests angina. Fleeting pains (seconds) are rarely cardiac.
    • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: Worsened by exertion and relieved by rest or GTN spray is the hallmark of angina. Pain relieved by sitting forward suggests pericarditis.
    • Severity: A high severity score (e.g., 8/10 or higher) increases suspicion for a serious cause like MI or aortic dissection.
  • For Dyspnoea:
    • Quantify Exertional Tolerance: "How far can you walk on the flat before you have to stop?" This helps grade severity.
    • Orthopnoea (breathlessness when lying flat): "How many pillows do you sleep with at night?" Needing more pillows suggests worsening left heart failure.
    • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnoea (PND): "Do you ever wake up suddenly at night feeling breathless, needing to open a window?" This is a highly specific sign of heart failure.
5. System-specific review of systems

Beyond the cardinal symptoms, asking about other related symptoms helps to build a complete clinical picture and screen for complications or alternative diagnoses that may present in a similar way.

  • General: Ask about fevers, rigors, or night sweats, which could suggest an underlying infection such as infective endocarditis. Enquire about unintentional weight loss, which may indicate cardiac cachexia in advanced heart failure or an underlying malignancy.
  • Respiratory: Ask about cough, particularly if it is productive of frothy white or pink sputum, a classic sign of acute pulmonary oedema. Ask about haemoptysis (coughing up blood), which is a red flag for pulmonary embolism or malignancy.
  • Neurological: Ask about dizziness, blackouts (syncope), or any stroke-like symptoms such as unilateral weakness, facial droop, or slurred speech. These can result from arrhythmias like Atrial Fibrillation (AF) causing a thromboembolic event.
  • Peripheral Vascular: Ask about pain in the calves on walking that is relieved by rest. This is known as intermittent claudication and its presence indicates peripheral arterial disease. It is a powerful marker of widespread atherosclerosis, significantly increasing the likelihood of co-existing coronary artery disease.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides the context for their symptoms and is essential for formal cardiovascular risk stratification. This information helps to determine the pre-test probability of a cardiac diagnosis.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Focus on the "big six" cardiovascular risk factors: Ischaemic Heart Disease (IHD), Hypertension, Hypercholesterolaemia, Diabetes Mellitus, Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD), and Cerebrovascular Disease (Stroke/TIA). A history of any of these significantly raises the likelihood of a cardiac cause.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • Ask about specific cardiovascular medications (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel, statins, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors).
    • For patients with known angina, it is crucial to ask about GTN spray use: "How often do you use it? Does it relieve the pain? How quickly?".
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask specifically about IHD in a first-degree relative, noting the age of onset. Premature disease (defined as <55 years in a male relative or <65 in a female relative) is a significant independent risk factor.
    • Ask about any family history of sudden cardiac death or inherited conditions like cardiomyopathies or channelopathies (e.g., Long QT syndrome).
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Smoking: This is a critical, modifiable risk factor. It must be quantified in pack-years. (Pack-years = [number of cigarettes smoked per day / 20] x number of years smoked).
    • Recreational Drugs: In a younger patient with chest pain, it is vital to ask about cocaine and amphetamine use, as they can cause coronary vasospasm and MI.
    • Function: Ask what the patient's life was like before this illness and how their symptoms impact their daily activities (e.g., work, hobbies, washing, dressing).
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

A problem representation is a concise, one-sentence summary that encapsulates the most critical elements of the history. It is the bridge between data gathering and clinical reasoning, forcing the clinician to synthesise information and commit to a diagnostic direction. A good problem representation includes the patient's demographics, key risk factors, and the temporal pattern of their main symptoms. The formula is often: [Age/sex] with [key risk factors] presenting with [symptom profile] suggestive of [most likely diagnostic category].

  • Example 1 (Ischaemic): "A 65-year-old male smoker with known hypertension presents with three hours of central, crushing chest pain radiating to his jaw, consistent with an acute coronary syndrome."
  • Example 2 (Heart Failure): "An 80-year-old woman with a history of a previous myocardial infarction presents with a three-week history of worsening exertional breathlessness, now present after walking 20 yards, and three-pillow orthopnoea."
  • Example 3 (Arrhythmia): "A 72-year-old man with a history of hypertension presents with a sudden-onset episode of rapid, irregular palpitations associated with light-headedness, concerning for new-onset atrial fibrillation."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The pattern of historical features is the most powerful tool for shaping the differential diagnosis and determining which conditions are most likely.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Exertional, central chest tightness (<10 mins), relieved by rest, in a patient with multiple cardiovascular risk factors. Stable Angina
Similar pain to above, but new onset, worsening, or occurring at rest and lasting >20 minutes, with associated nausea/sweating. Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
Gradual onset of exertional breathlessness, orthopnoea, PND, and bilateral ankle swelling. Congestive Heart Failure
Sudden onset of palpitations described as "irregular" or "fluttering", associated with dizziness. Atrial Fibrillation / Atrial Flutter
Sharp, central chest pain, worse on lying flat and relieved by sitting forward, often preceded by a viral illness. Acute Pericarditis
Sudden-onset, severe "tearing" chest or back pain in a patient with hypertension. Aortic Dissection
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all key information is conveyed clearly and concisely, which is vital for safe and effective handovers, ward round presentations, or referrals to other specialities.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented with [chief complaint], which began [timing, e.g., three hours ago].

The patient describes the symptom as [character of symptom, e.g., central crushing chest pain]. It radiates to the [radiation]. On a scale of 1 to 10, the severity is [score]. It is exacerbated by [triggers] and relieved by [relieving factors]. Associated symptoms include [list key associated symptoms].

There are no symptoms of [mention key negative findings from review of systems].

Their past medical history includes [list key conditions, e.g., hypertension, diabetes].
Current medications are [list key medications].
There is a family history of [e.g., IHD in their father at age 52].
Regarding their social history, they are a [current/ex-]smoker with a [number]-pack-year history.

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] with a background of [risk factors] presenting with [key symptom cluster], which is highly suspicious for [top differential, e.g., an acute coronary syndrome].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly improve the quality and accuracy of the history taken, leading to better clinical decision-making.

  • Pitfall: Failing to quantify symptoms. "Breathless" is not enough; you need to know their exertional tolerance. "Smokes" is not enough; you need a pack-year history.
  • Expert Tip: Frame functional questions in terms of everyday activities. "Could you walk up a flight of stairs without stopping before this started? And how about now?" This provides a clear, objective measure of decline.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting to ask about recreational drugs, especially cocaine, in a young patient presenting with ischaemic-sounding chest pain.
  • Expert Tip: Frame the question non-judgementally as part of routine screening: "We ask everyone this as some substances can be relevant to the heart - have you used any recreational drugs recently?".
  • Pitfall: Not exploring the GTN spray response fully.
  • Expert Tip: Ask three specific questions: "Do you use a GTN spray?", "Does it work?", and "How quickly does it work?". Relief within 1-2 minutes is suggestive of angina; delayed, partial, or no relief is less so and may suggest oesophageal spasm or a non-ischaemic cause.
  • Pitfall: Using leading questions early in the consultation, such as "Is the pain a crushing feeling?".
  • Expert Tip: Always start with open questions: "Can you describe the feeling to me in your own words?". Let the patient provide the descriptors first.
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with new-onset stable angina.

  • Clinician: "Hello Mr. Smith, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've been having some trouble with chest discomfort. Could you tell me more about that in your own words?"
  • Patient: "Well, for the last month or so, whenever I walk up the hill from the shops, I get this tight feeling right in the middle of my chest."
  • Clinician: "Okay, a tight feeling in the middle of your chest when you walk up the hill. Can you describe that tightness for me?"
  • Patient: "It's like a pressure, like someone is sitting on me. It's not a sharp pain."
  • Clinician: "And when you get that pressure, does it seem to travel anywhere else?"
  • Patient: "Sometimes I feel it in my jaw, and my left arm feels a bit heavy."
  • Clinician: "And what do you do when you feel that?"
  • Patient: "I have to stop walking. If I stand still for a minute or two, it goes away completely."
  • Clinician: "And if you were to score it out of 10, with 10 being the worst, how bad is the pressure?"
  • Patient: "Oh, about a 6 or 7 out of 10. It's enough to make me stop."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, that's very clear. Can I just check, is there anything you've been worried this might be?"
  • Patient: "Well, my father had a heart attack, so I've been worried it's my heart."

Problem Representation: A 62-year-old man presents with a one-month history of exertional, central chest pressure radiating to his jaw, which is reliably relieved by rest, consistent with new-onset stable angina.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Thinks it could be his heart.
  • Concerns: Worried he is having a heart attack like his father.
  • Expectations: Hopes to find out what is wrong and get some treatment for it.
