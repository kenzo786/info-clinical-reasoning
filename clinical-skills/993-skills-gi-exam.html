Gastrointestinal System Examination
1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the gastrointestinal (GI) examination is to detect physical signs of abdominal or systemic disease. It is a systematic process designed to identify evidence of pathology in the digestive tract and associated organs like the liver, gallbladder, spleen, and pancreas. Key aims include identifying organomegaly (enlargement of organs), abnormal fluid collections (ascites), signs of inflammation or peritonitis, and the stigmata of chronic liver disease. The findings must be integrated with the patient's history to formulate a differential diagnosis and guide further investigation. A crucial objective is to identify signs of an acute abdomen, which may require urgent surgical intervention.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A sound knowledge of abdominal topography is essential for localising findings. The abdomen is typically divided into nine regions or four quadrants.

  • Nine Regions: These are created by two vertical mid-clavicular lines and two horizontal lines (the subcostal and intertubercular planes).
    • Top row: Right Hypochondrium, Epigastrium, Left Hypochondrium.
    • Middle row: Right Lumbar (Flank), Umbilical, Left Lumbar (Flank).
    • Bottom row: Right Iliac Fossa (RIF), Suprapubic (or Hypogastrium), Left Iliac Fossa (LIF).
  • Key Structures by Region:
    • Epigastrium: Stomach, pancreas, aorta.
    • Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) / Right Hypochondrium: Liver, gallbladder.
    • Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ) / Left Hypochondrium: Spleen, stomach.
    • Right Iliac Fossa (RIF): Appendix, caecum.
    • Left Iliac Fossa (LIF): Sigmoid colon.
  • Surface Landmarks:
    • McBurney's Point: Located two-thirds of the way from the umbilicus to the anterior superior iliac spine. Tenderness here is a classic sign of appendicitis.
    • Costal Margin: The lower edge of the rib cage, which the edge of a normal liver and spleen should not project below.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional, safe, and effective examination requires careful preparation and clear communication to ensure the patient is comfortable and relaxed.

  1. Equipment & Infection Control:
    • Ensure you have a stethoscope, measuring tape, and gloves.
    • Begin by washing your hands or using alcohol gel. Warm your hands if necessary.
    • Clean your stethoscope with an alcohol wipe in front of the patient.
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to lie supine (flat on their back) on the examination couch, ideally with only one pillow to help relax the abdominal muscles.
    • Ask the patient to place their arms by their sides, not behind their head, as this tenses the abdomen.
    • Preserve the patient's dignity with a sheet or blanket, ensuring adequate exposure of the abdomen from the pubic symphysis to the xiphisternum.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Introduce yourself with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, my name is [Name] and I am a foundation doctor").
    • Explain what you are going to do and gain consent. A good phrase is: "I've been asked to perform an examination of your tummy. This will involve me looking, feeling, and having a listen. Before we start, are you in any pain at the moment?"
    • Asking about pain at the start is a critical safety step; the painful area must always be examined last.
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent, systematic approach (Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, Auscultation) ensures no signs are missed. Remember to continually observe the patient's face for signs of discomfort.

  1. General Inspection: From the end of the bed, assess if the patient is comfortable or in pain, their nutritional status (e.g., cachectic), and note any yellow discolouration (jaundice) or pallor. Look for paraphernalia like stoma bags or feeding tubes.
  2. Hands and Arms: Look for signs of chronic liver disease such as clubbing, palmar erythema (red palms), Dupuytren's contracture, or leukonychia (white nails). Check for asterixis ('liver flap'), a sign of hepatic encephalopathy.
  3. Face and Neck: Check the eyes for jaundice (in the sclera) and conjunctival pallor. Look in the mouth for ulcers or poor dentition. Palpate for the left supraclavicular node (Virchow's node), a sign of gastric cancer.
  4. Abdomen - Inspection: Squat down to eye level. Look for distension, scars, hernias, prominent veins (caput medusae), masses, rashes, or striae (stretch marks). Note the location of any stomas.
  5. Abdomen - Palpation:
    • Light palpation: With a flat hand, systematically palpate all nine regions, watching the patient's face. Assess for tenderness and guarding.
    • Deep palpation: Repeat, pressing more firmly to identify deeper masses or organomegaly. Palpate the painful area last.
    • Organ palpation: Palpate for the liver and spleen edge, asking the patient to take deep breaths. Palpate the kidneys bimanually. Feel for a pulsatile, expansile mass in the epigastrium (suggesting an abdominal aortic aneurysm).
  6. Abdomen - Percussion: Percuss all nine regions to assess for dullness (suggesting a mass or fluid) versus normal resonance. Percuss to determine the liver span and test for shifting dullness if you suspect ascites.
  7. Abdomen - Auscultation: Use the diaphragm to listen for bowel sounds (should be normoactive). Listen over the aorta and renal arteries for bruits (vascular turbulence).
  8. Completing the Examination: Check for pitting oedema over the sacrum and ankles, a sign of fluid overload or low albumin.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising what is normal is fundamental to identifying pathology. A normal GI examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • General Appearance: The patient is comfortable, not in pain, and appears well-nourished. There is no jaundice.
  • Peripheries: No stigmata of chronic liver disease on the hands, face, or chest.
  • Inspection: The abdomen is soft, not distended. No visible scars, masses, or prominent veins.
    • Significance: Suggests the absence of significant pathology like gross ascites, large tumours, or portal hypertension.
  • Palpation: The abdomen is non-tender on both light and deep palpation. No guarding or rebound tenderness.
    • Significance: A soft, non-tender abdomen makes acute intra-abdominal inflammation (peritonitis) highly unlikely.
  • Organ Palpation: The liver, spleen, and kidneys are not palpable. The aorta is not expansile.
    • Significance: A non-palpable liver or spleen is normal (unless the patient is very thin). This makes significant organomegaly unlikely.
  • Percussion & Auscultation: The abdomen is resonant throughout. Bowel sounds are present and normoactive. No bruits are heard.
    • Significance: Suggests the absence of large solid masses, significant ascites, or vascular abnormalities. Normal bowel sounds make complete bowel obstruction less likely.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Pathological signs are objective evidence of underlying disease. Any new or unexplained finding requires careful consideration and correlation with the clinical history.

Stigmata of chronic liver disease (CLD)
  • The Signs: A collection of signs including spider naevi (on the chest), palmar erythema, clubbing, gynaecomastia (male breast development), and testicular atrophy.
  • Pathology Link: These are caused by liver dysfunction, primarily the failure to metabolise oestrogen and other hormones.
Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
  • The Sign: The liver edge is palpable more than 2cm below the right costal margin. Note its consistency (smooth, irregular, firm).
  • Pathology Link: Causes are numerous and include liver metastases, alcoholic liver disease, heart failure (congestive hepatomegaly), and haematological malignancies.
Splenomegaly (enlarged spleen)
  • The Sign: A palpable mass emerging from under the left costal margin that moves towards the RIF on inspiration and has a palpable 'notch'.
  • Pathology Link: Can be caused by portal hypertension (due to liver cirrhosis), haematological disorders (leukaemia, lymphoma), or infections (e.g., malaria, glandular fever).
Ascites (abdominal fluid)
  • The Sign: Abdominal distension with dullness to percussion in the flanks. This dullness 'shifts' when the patient rolls onto their side (shifting dullness).
  • Pathology Link: Fluid in the peritoneal cavity, most commonly caused by portal hypertension secondary to liver cirrhosis, but also by malignancy or heart failure.
Peritonism (Guarding / Rebound Tenderness)
  • The Signs: Guarding is the involuntary tensing of abdominal muscles on palpation. Rebound tenderness is pain upon the rapid withdrawal of the examining hand.
  • Pathology Link: These indicate inflammation of the peritoneum, the lining of the abdomen.
  • Red Flag: These are hard signs of an acute surgical abdomen (e.g., from appendicitis, perforation, diverticulitis) and require immediate surgical referral.
Positive Murphy's Sign
  • The Sign: The patient suddenly catches their breath in pain when the examiner presses on the right upper quadrant as the patient inspires deeply.
  • Pathology Link: This indicates inflammation of the gallbladder (acute cholecystitis), as the inflamed organ hits the examiner's hand.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating your findings clearly. Start with a general statement, then systematically report the positive and negative findings from inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation.

On general inspection, the patient appeared [e.g., comfortable at rest / jaundiced]. There were no peripheral stigmata of chronic liver disease.

On examination of the abdomen, it was [e.g., soft and non-distended / distended]. There were [e.g., no scars / a midline laparotomy scar].

On palpation, the abdomen was [e.g., soft and non-tender / tender in the right iliac fossa]. There was no [guarding or rebound tenderness]. The liver and spleen were not palpable, and the kidneys were not ballotable.

On percussion, the abdomen was [e.g., resonant throughout / dull in the flanks].

On auscultation, bowel sounds were [present and normoactive / absent]. There were no audible bruits.
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

A constellation of signs is more powerful than an isolated finding. Linking signs together helps to build a strong case for a specific diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Jaundice, ascites, spider naevi, splenomegaly, palmar erythema, asterixis. Decompensated Chronic Liver Disease (Cirrhosis)
Fever, localised tenderness in the RIF, guarding, positive Rovsing's sign. Acute Appendicitis
Tenderness in the RUQ, fever, positive Murphy's sign. Acute Cholecystitis

9. Relevant next-step investigations

Findings on examination are crucial for guiding the appropriate use of investigations to confirm a diagnosis and plan management.

  • If you find signs of liver disease (jaundice, stigmata, organomegaly, ascites):
    • Logical Next Steps: Blood tests including Liver Function Tests (LFTs), coagulation screen, and FBC. An abdominal ultrasound is essential to assess liver texture, identify biliary obstruction, and confirm ascites/splenomegaly.
  • If you find signs of appendicitis (localised RIF tenderness/peritonism):
    • Logical Next Steps: This is a clinical diagnosis requiring urgent surgical review. Investigations include bloods (FBC, CRP) and often an ultrasound or CT scan to confirm the diagnosis, especially if there is diagnostic uncertainty.
  • If you suspect bowel obstruction (distension, tinkling bowel sounds):
    • Logical Next Steps: Initial investigation is an erect abdominal X-ray and supine film to look for dilated loops of bowel and air-fluid levels. A CT scan is often required to identify the level and cause of obstruction.
  • If you suspect an Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (pulsatile expansile mass):
    • Logical Next Steps: An urgent abdominal ultrasound is the best initial imaging modality to confirm the presence and measure the diameter of an aneurysm. If symptomatic, this becomes an emergency requiring immediate vascular surgical input and CT angiography.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good examination technique can dramatically improve performance, especially in an OSCE setting.

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to ask if the patient has any pain before you start palpating.
    • Palpating the painful area first, causing the patient to tense up for the rest of the exam.
    • Having cold hands, which can cause involuntary guarding.
    • Inadequate exposure of the abdomen, missing hernias or scars.
    • Forgetting to squat down to inspect the abdomen from the side for subtle masses or pulsations.
    • Forgetting to complete the examination by checking for hernias and peripheral oedema.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always start with the "magic formula": Wash hands, introduce yourself, state name/role, explain, and gain consent.
    • Verbalise what you are looking for as you go along (e.g., "I'm looking for any scars, stomas, or signs of distension..."). This demonstrates your knowledge.
    • Be systematic: Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, Auscultation. Following a routine prevents omissions under pressure.
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant further steps, such as examining the hernial orifices, external genitalia, and performing a digital rectal examination (DRE).
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings is a key skill for developing robust clinical reasoning.

  • Case 1: Decompensated Liver Disease
    • Scenario: A 62-year-old man with a history of heavy alcohol use presents with abdominal swelling and confusion.
    • Expected Findings: You would expect to find jaundice, multiple spider naevi, gynaecomastia, and palmar erythema. He may have asterixis. His abdomen would be distended with demonstrable shifting dullness (ascites). The liver may be small and shrunken or enlarged, and the spleen may be palpable.
  • Case 2: Acute Appendicitis
    • Scenario: A 19-year-old student presents with a 24-hour history of central abdominal pain that has now moved to the right iliac fossa. He has a fever and has lost his appetite.
    • Expected Findings: The patient may be lying still to avoid movement. The key finding would be maximal tenderness over McBurney's point in the RIF. There would likely be localised guarding and rebound tenderness. Percussion over the RIF would cause sharp pain.
  • Case 3: Large Bowel Obstruction
    • Scenario: An 80-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of not opening her bowels, abdominal bloating, and vomiting.
    • Expected Findings: The abdomen would be significantly distended and tympanitic (like a drum) on percussion. Bowel sounds may be high-pitched and "tinkling" early on, or absent later. There may be generalised tenderness, but not necessarily localised peritonism unless there is ischaemia or perforation.
