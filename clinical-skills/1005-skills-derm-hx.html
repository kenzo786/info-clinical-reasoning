Dermatological System History
1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a dermatological history is to gather specific information to construct a differential diagnosis between inflammatory, infectious, malignant, and iatrogenic skin conditions. A key goal is to accurately characterise the presenting lesion(s) and their evolution over time. Unlike many other histories, the dermatological history is intimately linked with visual description and subsequent examination. The narrative should place the skin complaint in the context of the patient's general health, systemic symptoms, environmental exposures, and psychosocial wellbeing. This structured approach is crucial for identifying potentially life-threatening conditions like melanoma or severe cutaneous drug reactions, and for recognising skin signs of underlying systemic disease.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective and sensitive communication is vital. Skin conditions are often visible and can cause significant psychological distress, embarrassment, and social isolation. It is essential to create a private, non-judgemental environment where the patient feels comfortable discussing their symptoms and their impact. Acknowledging the patient's concerns validates their experience and is a cornerstone of a patient-centred approach, which is crucial for building rapport and agreeing on a management plan.

  • Establish Rapport: Begin with a warm introduction and ensure the patient is comfortable. Acknowledge the sensitivity of skin issues and reassure them of confidentiality before asking to see the affected area.
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open questions ("Tell me about the problem with your skin?") to let the patient describe the issue in their own terms. Gradually narrow the focus with more specific, closed questions ("Is the rash itchy or painful?") to clarify details about the lesions and symptoms.
  • Active Listening & Signposting: Show you are listening by summarising key points. Structure the consultation clearly ("Now that we've talked about the rash itself, I'm going to ask a few questions about your general health.").
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): Exploring ICE is critically important in dermatology.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might have caused this?"
    • Concerns: Directly address the patient's main worry. A crucial question is, "Was there anything specific you were worried this might be?" This often reveals fears of cancer, contagion, or permanent scarring.
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?" This helps to manage expectations regarding diagnosis, treatment, and cure.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

Dermatological problems manifest in a limited number of ways. A thorough exploration of these cardinal symptoms and signs is essential, with an immediate focus on identifying any red flags that require urgent specialist assessment.

  • Rash or Lesion: The most common presentation. The history aims to define its characteristics and evolution.
    • Red Flag: A new or changing pigmented lesion. Use the ABCDE criteria: Asymmetry, irregular Border, varied Colour, Diameter >6mm, Evolving (changing or bleeding). This suggests malignant melanoma and requires an urgent two-week-wait referral.
  • Pruritus (Itch): A cardinal symptom of many skin diseases.
    • Red Flag: Severe, persistent, widespread itch without a primary rash can be a sign of underlying systemic disease (e.g., lymphoma, liver failure, thyroid disease) and requires further investigation.
  • Blistering (Vesicles/Bullae):
    • Red Flag: Sudden, widespread blistering, especially if accompanied by fever, mucosal involvement (sore eyes, mouth, genitals), or feeling unwell. This may indicate a dermatological emergency such as Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS), Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN), or pemphigus.
  • Painful Lesion(s):
    • Red Flag: A rapidly expanding, hot, swollen, and painful area of skin suggests cellulitis or necrotising fasciitis, a surgical emergency. Shingles (herpes zoster) also presents with pain, often before the rash appears.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

Every dermatological complaint must be broken down systematically. For a lesion or rash, a useful mnemonic is SCRIPT (Site, Character, Radiation/Spread, Inception/Onset, Progression/Timing, Triggers/Relieving Factors). This framework ensures all critical details about the skin problem are captured.

  • For a Rash/Lesion (SCRIPT):
    • Site: Where did it first appear? Where is it located now? Note the distribution (e.g., symmetrical, sun-exposed areas, flexures, extensor surfaces).
    • Character: What does it look and feel like? (e.g., "flat spots," "raised bumps," "scaly patches," "fluid-filled blisters"). What is the colour, shape, and border?
    • Radiation / Spread: Has it spread since it started? If so, in what pattern and how quickly?
    • Inception / Onset: When exactly did it start? Was the onset sudden or gradual?
    • Progression / Timing: Has it changed in size, shape, or colour over time? Is it persistent, or does it come and go?
    • Triggers / Relieving Factors: Does anything make it better (e.g., creams, cold) or worse (e.g., sunlight, stress, heat, certain foods)?
  • For Pruritus (Itch):
    • SOCRATES: Use this for a detailed analysis of the itch itself.
    • Timing: When is the itch worst? Itch that is exclusively or significantly worse at night is a classic feature of scabies.
    • Severity: How severe is it? Does it interfere with sleep or daily activities?
5. System-specific review of systems

Beyond the skin itself, a targeted review of other systems is crucial because many dermatological conditions are manifestations of underlying systemic disease ('the skin is a window to internal medicine').

  • General: Ask about fever, night sweats, and unintentional weight loss. These can be associated with malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, melanoma) or systemic inflammatory conditions.
  • Musculoskeletal: Enquire about joint pain, swelling, or stiffness. This is essential for diagnosing psoriatic arthritis, reactive arthritis, lupus, or dermatomyositis.
  • Gastrointestinal: Ask about mouth ulcers, diarrhoea, or abdominal pain, which can be linked to inflammatory bowel disease (presenting with pyoderma gangrenosum) or coeliac disease (presenting with dermatitis herpetiformis).
  • Respiratory: A history of cough or dyspnoea could be relevant in conditions like sarcoidosis, which has cutaneous manifestations.
  • Genitourinary: Ask about genital ulcers or urethritis, which can be seen in conditions like Behçet's disease or reactive arthritis.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides essential context for their skin complaint. This information is key to understanding their risk factors and identifying potential causes.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Focus on the "atopic triad": Eczema, Asthma, and Hay Fever.
    • Ask about pre-existing autoimmune conditions (e.g., thyroid disease, lupus), immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, transplant), and any previous history of skin cancer.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • A meticulous drug history is vital. Ask about all medications: prescribed, over-the-counter, supplements, and herbal remedies.
    • Crucially, ask about newly started medications in the weeks or months preceding rash onset, as drug eruptions are common. Note any previous reactions to drugs.
    • Ask what topical treatments (creams, ointments) they have tried, how they used them, and whether they helped.
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask about skin conditions in the family, such as psoriasis, eczema, or atopy.
    • A family history of melanoma or other skin cancers in a first-degree relative is a significant risk factor.
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Occupation and Hobbies: Explore potential exposure to irritants, chemicals, or allergens (e.g., hairdresser, cleaner, construction worker).
    • Sun Exposure: Quantify lifetime sun exposure, including holidays abroad, sunbed use, and history of peeling or blistering sunburns. This is critical for assessing skin cancer risk.
    • Travel History: Recent travel may suggest infectious causes.
    • Pets: Contact with animals can be a source of infestations or allergies.
    • Impact on Life (Quality of Life): Ask how the condition affects their mood, sleep, work, and relationships.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

The problem representation is a concise, one-sentence summary that encapsulates the most critical elements of the history. It bridges data gathering and clinical reasoning, forcing the clinician to synthesise information and commit to a diagnostic direction. A good problem representation includes the patient's demographics, key risk factors, and the morphology and timeline of their main complaint.

  • Example 1 (Inflammatory): "A 28-year-old woman with a personal and family history of atopy presents with a multi-year history of a chronic, itchy, scaly rash affecting the flexural surfaces of her elbows and knees, consistent with atopic eczema."
  • Example 2 (Malignant): "A 68-year-old retired gardener with a significant history of sun exposure presents with a new pigmented lesion on his back, which has grown and developed an irregular border over the last four months, concerning for a malignant melanoma."
  • Example 3 (Infectious): "A 45-year-old man with type 2 diabetes presents with a three-day history of a rapidly spreading, hot, tender, and red area on his left lower leg, highly suggestive of cellulitis."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The pattern of historical features, particularly the timeline, distribution, and morphology described by the patient, is a powerful tool for shaping the differential diagnosis before you even examine the patient.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Chronic, itchy, scaly rash on flexural surfaces (e.g., inside elbows, behind knees) with a personal/family history of atopy. Atopic Eczema
Well-demarcated, red, scaly plaques on extensor surfaces (e.g., front of knees, outer elbows) and scalp. May have associated joint pain. Plaque Psoriasis
New or changing pigmented lesion in an adult, with features like asymmetry, border irregularity, colour change, or growth. Malignant Melanoma
Slowly growing, pearly or translucent nodule on a sun-exposed site in an older person, may describe central ulceration or bleeding. Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)
Sudden onset of intensely itchy bumps and lines (burrows), worse at night, affecting finger webs, wrists, and groin. Often other household members are itchy. Scabies
Acute, widespread, symmetrical rash appearing days to weeks after starting a new medication. Drug Eruption
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all key information is conveyed clearly and concisely, which is vital for safe and effective handovers and referrals to dermatology.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented with [chief complaint, e.g., a new rash] on their [site], which began [timing, e.g., two weeks ago].

The patient describes the lesion(s) as [character of lesion, e.g., itchy, red patches with a silvery scale]. It first appeared on the [site of onset] and has since spread to [current distribution].

Associated symptoms include [e.g., itch, pain]. There has been no [bleeding, discharge].

A systemic review is notable for [e.g., painful, swollen joints]. There is no fever or weight loss.

Their past medical history is significant for [list key conditions].
Current medications are [list key medications, esp. new ones].
There is a family history of [e.g., psoriasis in their mother].
Regarding their social history, they have a history of [e.g., smoking] and report [e.g., significant stress at work].

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] with a background of [risk factors] presenting with [key symptom cluster], suggestive of [top differential, e.g., new-onset plaque psoriasis with psoriatic arthropathy].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly improve the quality and accuracy of the history, leading to better clinical decision-making and more appropriate management.

  • Pitfall: Taking an incomplete drug history. Forgetting to ask about over-the-counter drugs, supplements, or timings.
  • Expert Tip: Frame the question specifically: "In the three months before this rash started, did you start any new medications, tablets, or remedies of any kind, even if you don't think it's related?".
  • Pitfall: Not asking about sun exposure. This is a critical risk factor for the most common cancers in the world.
  • Expert Tip: Quantify it. "Have you ever used sunbeds? Lived somewhere hot? Had any really bad, blistering sunburns?".
  • Pitfall: Forgetting the systemic review in a patient with a rash.
  • Expert Tip: As a minimum, always ask about the 'big three' systemic clues: fever/weight loss, joint pains, and mouth ulcers. This simple screen can unmask a serious underlying diagnosis.
  • Pitfall: Underestimating the impact on quality of life.
  • Expert Tip: Ask a direct but open question: "How has this skin problem been affecting you day-to-day?". This can reveal significant psychological distress that needs addressing as part of the management plan.
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with a suspicious pigmented lesion.

  • Clinician: "Hello Mr. David, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've come in today because you're worried about a mole on your back."
  • Patient: "That's right. My wife noticed it a few months ago and she says it's definitely got bigger."
  • Clinician: "Okay, and apart from getting bigger, have either of you noticed any other changes? For instance, in its shape or colour?"
  • Patient: "Well, it used to be a neat circle, but now the edge looks a bit fuzzy, not a clean line. And one part of it seems to have gone almost black, while the rest is dark brown."
  • Clinician: "And has it caused you any trouble? Any itchiness, pain, or has it ever bled?"
  • Patient: "It has been a bit itchy over the last few weeks. It did bleed once when I caught it on a chair."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, that's very clear. Can I just ask, what's your relationship with the sun been like over the years? Do you burn easily?"
  • Patient: "I'm a builder, so I've worked outdoors my whole life. I used to burn all the time as a lad, peeling every summer."
  • Clinician: "Finally, was there anything specific you were worried this could be?"
  • Patient: "My wife's been on at me to get it checked. She's worried it's skin cancer."

Problem Representation: A 65-year-old builder with a lifelong history of high sun exposure presents with a pigmented lesion on his back that has enlarged and developed an irregular border and colour variegation over several months, with recent onset of itch and bleeding, features highly suspicious for malignant melanoma.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: That the mole has changed and isn't right.
  • Concerns: Worried it could be skin cancer.
  • Expectations: To have the lesion examined and find out what it is, and what needs to be done about it.
