Respiratory System History
1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a respiratory history is to distinguish between pulmonary, cardiac, and other causes of a patient's symptoms. It provides a structured framework for identifying the nature of the underlying respiratory disease, which may be obstructive (like asthma or COPD), restrictive (like pulmonary fibrosis), infective, or malignant. The history-taking process is crucial for rapidly identifying life-threatening conditions such as a pulmonary embolism, acute severe asthma, or tension pneumothorax. A detailed narrative, placing symptoms in the context of the patient's risk factors (like smoking and occupational exposure) and functional limitations, is the cornerstone of accurate diagnosis and effective management.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective communication is fundamental to obtaining an accurate and comprehensive history. The clinician must build trust and create a confidential space where the patient feels comfortable discussing their symptoms and fears, particularly concerns about cancer or progressive breathlessness. A patient-centred approach ensures the consultation addresses what is most important to the patient, facilitating shared decision-making about investigations and treatment.

  • Establish Rapport: Begin with a polite introduction ("Hello, I'm one of the junior doctors looking after you today") and ensure the patient is as comfortable as possible. If they are breathless, allow them to speak in short sentences and do not rush them. Sit at their level to create a less confrontational environment.
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open-ended questions ("Tell me about the trouble you've been having with your breathing?") to encourage the patient to describe their experience. Then, use more focused, closed questions ("Is the cough productive?" or "Does the pain get worse when you breathe in?") to clarify specific details of the cardinal symptoms.
  • Active Listening & Signposting: Pay close attention to verbal and non-verbal cues. Summarise information back to the patient to check your understanding ("So, it sounds like the breathlessness started suddenly two days ago, is that right?"). Use signposting to guide the consultation logically ("Okay, we've discussed the cough in detail. I'm now going to ask you some questions about your past health.").
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): This is vital for understanding the patient's perspective.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing these symptoms?"
    • Concerns: Directly asking, "Was there anything you were specifically worried this could be?" is crucial and often reveals fears of lung cancer or being unable to breathe.
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" This clarifies the patient's goals for the consultation.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

The respiratory system typically signals pathology through a core set of cardinal symptoms. A detailed exploration of these is essential, with an immediate focus on identifying any red flags that necessitate urgent intervention.

  • Dyspnoea (Breathlessness): The subjective sensation of difficult or uncomfortable breathing. The history must define its onset, duration, and severity.
    • Red Flag: Sudden onset of severe breathlessness, especially if accompanied by chest pain or stridor (a high-pitched inspiratory noise), requires immediate emergency assessment. This could indicate a pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, or acute airway obstruction.
  • Cough: A critical symptom. It is vital to establish its duration (acute <3 weeks, subacute 3-8 weeks, chronic >8 weeks) and character. Ask if it is productive of sputum, and if so, its colour, consistency, and volume.
    • Red Flag: The new onset of a persistent cough in a smoker or ex-smoker, or the presence of haemoptysis (coughing up blood), is a red flag for malignancy and requires urgent investigation.
  • Haemoptysis (Coughing up blood): This is always a significant finding. It's important to quantify the amount (from streaks in the sputum to frank blood) and confirm it is not from the nose or stomach (haematemesis).
    • Red Flag: All cases of haemoptysis are considered a red flag until proven otherwise, with potential causes including lung cancer, pulmonary embolism, and tuberculosis.
  • Wheeze: A high-pitched whistling sound produced during breathing, typically on expiration. It is a sign of narrowed lower airways.
    • Red Flag: A persistent, unilateral wheeze suggests focal airway obstruction (e.g., from an inhaled foreign body or tumour). New-onset wheeze in a middle-aged or older adult is also a concern.
  • Chest Pain: Can be a primary respiratory symptom. It is often 'pleuritic' in nature, meaning it is sharp, localised, and exacerbated by deep inspiration or coughing.
    • Red Flag: Sudden-onset, severe pleuritic chest pain associated with breathlessness is a classic presentation of pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax and needs urgent review.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

To ensure a comprehensive understanding of each key symptom, a structured mnemonic such as SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, and Severity) should be employed. This systematic approach prevents critical details from being overlooked.

  • For Dyspnoea:
    • Onset: Sudden (e.g., pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax) vs. gradual over weeks/months (e.g., COPD, interstitial lung disease).
    • Timing: Is it constant or intermittent? Does it vary seasonally (asthma) or during the day (worse in the morning in COPD)?
    • Severity/Progression: Quantify functional limitation. The MRC Dyspnoea Scale is a formal tool, but simple questions are effective: "How far could you walk on the flat a month ago versus today?" or "Can you still manage a flight of stairs?".
    • Associated Symptoms: Ask about cough, wheeze, fever (infection), and chest pain. Also ask about orthopnoea (breathlessness on lying flat) and PND (paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea) to screen for a cardiac cause.
  • For Cough:
    • Character: Is it dry and hacking, or wet and productive? A 'bovine' cough (non-explosive) suggests vocal cord paralysis.
    • Sputum: If productive, clarify colour (clear, yellow, green, rusty), consistency (mucoid, purulent), and volume (e.g., a teaspoonful, a cupful). Green/yellow sputum suggests infection.
    • Timing: Is it worse at night (can suggest asthma or GORD) or in the morning (typical for COPD/bronchiectasis)?
    • Triggers: Is it brought on by cold air, exercise, or allergens (asthma)? Or after eating (aspiration)?
5. System-specific review of systems

Beyond the cardinal symptoms, enquiring about other systems can reveal extra-pulmonary manifestations of a respiratory disease, identify alternative diagnoses, or uncover important comorbidities.

  • General: Ask about fevers, rigors, and night sweats (suggesting infection like pneumonia or TB, or malignancy like lymphoma). Enquire about unintentional weight loss and loss of appetite (concerning for lung cancer or advanced chronic disease). Ask about finger clubbing.
  • Ear, Nose & Throat (ENT): Ask about chronic sinusitis, nasal discharge, or polyps, which are associated with asthma. Enquire about hoarseness of the voice, as this can be caused by a tumour affecting the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
  • Cardiovascular: Screen for cardiac causes of breathlessness by asking about orthopnoea, PND, and peripheral oedema (swollen ankles). Swelling in one leg could suggest a deep vein thrombosis (DVT), a major risk factor for pulmonary embolism.
  • Musculoskeletal/Rheumatological: Ask about joint pain, swelling, or stiffness. Some connective tissue diseases, like rheumatoid arthritis, have significant pulmonary manifestations (e.g., interstitial lung disease).
  • Gastrointestinal: Ask about symptoms of acid reflux or heartburn, as gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) is a well-known cause of chronic cough.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

Understanding the patient's background is essential for interpreting their symptoms and assessing their risk of specific respiratory conditions. This context helps establish the pre-test probability of a diagnosis.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Specifically ask about known respiratory diseases: Asthma, COPD, Bronchiectasis, previous Pneumonia, Tuberculosis (TB), or Pulmonary Embolism (PE).
    • Ask about atopy (eczema, hay fever) as it is linked to asthma.
    • Enquire about any cardiac history, particularly heart failure.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • Ask about all inhaler use (relievers and preventers). Critically, ask the patient to demonstrate their inhaler technique.
    • Enquire about home oxygen therapy, nebulisers, and recent courses of antibiotics or oral steroids.
    • Certain drugs can cause lung disease (e.g., methotrexate, amiodarone, beta-blockers can worsen asthma).
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask about asthma, eczema, or hay fever in first-degree relatives.
    • Enquire about inherited conditions such as cystic fibrosis and alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency (a rare cause of early-onset emphysema).
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Smoking: This is the single most important risk factor. Quantify it in pack-years ([cigarettes per day / 20] x years smoked). Record current status (smoker, ex-smoker, never-smoker). Ask about passive smoking exposure.
    • Occupational History: A lifetime history is crucial. Ask about exposure to dusts, fumes, or chemicals. Key examples include asbestos (shipbuilding, construction), coal dust, silica, and farming (mouldy hay).
    • Pets & Hobbies: Ask specifically about keeping birds (pigeons, budgies) which can cause hypersensitivity pneumonitis ("bird fancier's lung").
    • Travel History: Ask about recent travel, especially long-haul flights (risk for PE) or to areas with high rates of TB.
    • Functional Status: Assess the impact of symptoms on daily life, work, and sleep.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

The problem representation is a one-sentence summary that captures the essence of the clinical case. It acts as a bridge between the data gathered and the process of diagnostic reasoning. An effective summary includes the patient's key demographics, major risk factors, and the primary symptom cluster with its timeline. The typical formula is: [Age/sex] with [key risk factors] presenting with [symptom profile] suggestive of [most likely diagnostic category].

  • Example 1 (Infection/Exacerbation): "A 68-year-old female ex-smoker with known COPD presents with a three-day history of worsening breathlessness, increased cough productive of green sputum, and wheeze, consistent with an acute infective exacerbation."
  • Example 2 (Malignancy): "A 70-year-old lifelong smoker presents with a six-week history of a persistent cough, haemoptysis, and unintentional weight loss, concerning for an underlying lung malignancy."
  • Example 3 (Acute Vascular): "A 35-year-old woman, who takes the combined oral contraceptive pill, presents with the sudden onset of pleuritic chest pain and severe breathlessness two days after returning from a long-haul flight."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The patterns emerging from the history are the most powerful tool for building a relevant differential diagnosis and deciding on the most probable cause.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Long-term smoker with chronic, progressive exertional breathlessness and a daily morning cough productive of sputum. Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
Episodic symptoms of wheeze, breathlessness, and cough. Worse at night or early morning, with identifiable triggers (e.g., cold air, exercise, allergens). Personal or family history of atopy. Asthma
Acute onset of cough, fever, purulent sputum, and breathlessness. May be associated with pleuritic chest pain. Pneumonia / Acute Bronchitis / Infective Exacerbation of COPD
Sudden onset of sharp, pleuritic chest pain and breathlessness. Risk factors present (e.g., recent surgery, immobility, active cancer, long-haul travel). Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
Subacute or chronic history of persistent new cough, haemoptysis, weight loss, or fatigue in a current or former smoker. Lung Malignancy
Gradual onset of progressive exertional dyspnoea and a dry, persistent cough in an older adult. May have finger clubbing. Interstitial Lung Disease (e.g., Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis)
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all critical information is conveyed logically and efficiently, which is vital for safe handovers, ward round presentations, or when making referrals.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented with a [duration] history of [chief complaint, e.g., worsening shortness of breath].

The patient describes the breathlessness as [describe functional limitation, e.g., now occurring at rest]. Associated symptoms include a cough, which is productive of [sputum colour/character], and [wheeze/chest pain/fever]. There has been no haemoptysis.

There are no symptoms of [mention key negative findings, e.g., no leg swelling, orthopnoea or PND].

Their past medical history is significant for [list key conditions, e.g., COPD, asthma].
Current medications include [list key respiratory medications, e.g., Salbutamol and a steroid inhaler].
There is a family history of [e.g., asthma].
Regarding their social history, they are a [current/ex-]smoker with a [number]-pack-year history, and worked as a [mention key occupation].

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] with a background of [risk factors] presenting with [key symptom cluster], which is highly suspicious for [top differential, e.g., an infective exacerbation of COPD].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common mistakes during history-taking can dramatically improve diagnostic accuracy and patient management.

  • Pitfall: Failing to quantify symptoms. "Smokes a bit" is insufficient; you must get a pack-year history. "Breathless" is too vague; you need to know their functional limitation or MRC grade.
  • Expert Tip: Use the patient's own home as a reference for function. "Can you walk from your living room to the kitchen without stopping? Could you do that last week?". This makes the decline concrete.
  • Pitfall: Taking an incomplete social history and missing a crucial occupational or hobby-related exposure (e.g., asbestos, pigeons).
  • Expert Tip: Always ask, "Tell me about all the jobs you've ever had, even short-term ones." Frame it as a routine but important question.
  • Pitfall: Not assessing inhaler use and technique properly. The patient may have the right prescription but be getting no benefit from it.
  • Expert Tip: Ask the patient to physically show you how they use their inhaler. This is more revealing than just asking if they know how to use it.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting to screen for cardiac causes of breathlessness, such as heart failure.
  • Expert Tip: Always ask the "cardiac two-step": "How many pillows do you sleep with?" (orthopnoea) and "Do you ever wake up suddenly at night gasping for air?" (PND).
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient presenting with an acute exacerbation of COPD.

  • Clinician: "Hello Mrs. Jones, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've been finding it very hard to breathe. Can you tell me what's been happening?"
  • Patient: (Speaking in short sentences) "It's my chest... my COPD. It's been getting worse... for three days now. Much worse than usual."
  • Clinician: "Okay. Tell me about 'worse than usual'. What can you normally do, and what can you do now?"
  • Patient: "Normally I can get washed and dressed... but now... I can barely make it to the toilet. The cough is terrible too."
  • Clinician: "And what about the cough? Is anything coming up?"
  • Patient: "Yes, horrible green, thick stuff. Much more than my usual morning phlegm."
  • Clinician: "Have you had any fevers or sweats? Or any pain in your chest when you breathe?"
  • Patient: "I've been feeling all hot and bothered. No real pain, just tight, like a band around my chest."
  • Clinician: "Thanks, that's very clear. Just before we move on, was there anything in particular you were worried this might be?"
  • Patient: "I just get so scared when it gets this bad. I was worried I'd end up back in hospital... and here I am."

Problem Representation: A 74-year-old female ex-smoker with a known history of severe COPD presents with a three-day history of acute-on-chronic breathlessness, an increased productive cough with purulent sputum, and subjective fever, consistent with an acute infective exacerbation of COPD.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Believes this is a flare-up of her known COPD, likely a chest infection.
  • Concerns: Fear of significant deterioration and hospitalisation.
  • Expectations: Hopes to receive treatment (likely antibiotics and nebulisers) to help her breathing and allow her to go home.
