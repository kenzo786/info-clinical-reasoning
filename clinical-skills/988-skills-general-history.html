General Medical History
1. Overview & aims of the medical history

The medical history is the single most powerful diagnostic tool in clinical medicine. It is a structured conversation through which the clinician gathers information to understand a patient's health problems in their own context. More than just a checklist of questions, it is the patient's narrative, and listening carefully is paramount. A well-taken history allows the clinician to build a strong therapeutic relationship, understand the patient's perspective, and often, arrive at the correct diagnosis before any physical examination or investigation is even performed. Studies have consistently shown that the history alone can lead to the final diagnosis in over 75% of cases.

The primary aims are to:

  • Establish a professional and trusting rapport with the patient.
  • Elicit an accurate and chronological account of the presenting problem(s).
  • Gather essential background information about past health, medications, and family/social context.
  • Formulate a relevant list of differential diagnoses to guide subsequent examination and investigation.
  • Understand the patient's Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE) regarding their illness.
2. Core principles: communication & rapport

The quality of the history depends entirely on effective communication. The initial moments of the consultation set the tone for the entire encounter. A calm, empathetic, and professional approach will put the patient at ease and facilitate the sharing of sensitive information. Building rapport is not a separate step but a continuous process woven throughout the consultation.

  • Preparation: Before entering, quickly review the patient's basic details if available. Ensure the environment is private, quiet, and comfortable.
  • Introduction and Consent: Introduce yourself clearly, stating your name and role (e.g., "Hello, I'm Sarah, one of the foundation doctors"). Confirm the patient's identity. Explain the purpose of the consultation ("I'd like to ask you some questions about your health to understand what's been going on") and gain consent before proceeding.
  • Active Listening: Use open-ended questions at the start ("Tell me what's brought you in today?"). Pay attention to both verbal and non-verbal cues. Use techniques like summarising and paraphrasing ("So, it sounds like the pain started on Monday...") to show you are listening and to check your understanding.
  • Empathy: Acknowledge the patient's feelings with simple, genuine phrases ("That sounds very difficult," or "I can see that's been worrying for you"). This validates their experience and strengthens the therapeutic alliance.
3. Section 1: The presenting complaint (PC)

The Presenting Complaint (PC) is the main reason, or reasons, for the patient's consultation. It is the "headline" of the medical history and should be a concise statement that captures the core problem. The goal is to elicit this in the patient's own words, not in medical jargon. This ensures the consultation is centred on what is most important to the patient from the outset.

  • Eliciting the PC: Use a broad, open-ended question like "What's brought you along today?" or "How can I help you?".
  • Listen carefully: Allow the patient to answer without interruption. They may present multiple issues.
  • Clarify and Prioritise: If there are several complaints, list them and then ask the patient to prioritise. For example, "You've mentioned headaches, tiredness, and a sore knee. Which of those is troubling you the most right now?".
  • Documentation: Record the PC using the patient's own words where possible (e.g., "crushing chest pain" or "couldn't catch my breath"). Note the duration of the complaint (e.g., "Headaches for 2 weeks").
4. Section 2: History of the presenting complaint (HPC)

The History of the Presenting Complaint (HPC) is the detailed, chronological exploration of the main problem identified in the PC. This is the heart of the diagnostic process. A systematic framework is essential to ensure all relevant details are gathered. The most widely used mnemonic for this is SOCRATES, which is particularly useful for analysing pain but can be adapted for most other symptoms.

  • Site: "Where exactly is the pain/symptom? Can you point to it with one finger?"
  • Onset: "When did it start? Was it sudden or gradual? What were you doing at the time?"
  • Character: "What does it feel like? Can you describe it for me?" (e.g., sharp, dull, burning, crushing).
  • Radiation: "Does the pain move anywhere else?"
  • Associated Symptoms: "Have you noticed anything else that comes on with it?" (e.g., nausea, breathlessness, fever).
  • Timing: "Is it constant or does it come and go? How long does each episode last? Is there a pattern to it?"
  • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: "Does anything make it better or worse?" (e.g., movement, posture, medication).
  • Severity: "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 is the worst pain imaginable, what would you score it?" Or, "How does it affect your daily activities?".

It is also vital to ask about similar episodes in the past and to document relevant negative findings (e.g., for chest pain, "no associated breathlessness or sweating").

5. Section 3: The systematic review of systems (ROS)

The Review of Systems (ROS) is a "top-to-toe" inventory of key symptoms, organised by physiological system. Its purpose is twofold: firstly, to identify any associated symptoms that the patient may not have considered relevant to their presenting complaint, and secondly, to act as a safety net to screen for evidence of disease in other systems that might otherwise be missed. This ensures a holistic assessment of the patient's health.

Ask a brief series of screening questions for each system:

  • General/Constitutional: Fevers, night sweats, change in weight or appetite, fatigue.
    • Red Flag: Unexplained weight loss is a significant red flag for underlying malignancy or chronic disease.
  • Cardiovascular: Chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, orthopnoea (breathlessness on lying flat), peripheral oedema (ankle swelling).
  • Respiratory: Cough, sputum, haemoptysis (coughing up blood), wheeze, pleuritic chest pain.
    • Red Flag: Haemoptysis is always a red flag that requires urgent investigation.
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), indigestion, change in bowel habit, blood in stool.
  • Genitourinary: Dysuria (pain on urination), urinary frequency, haematuria (blood in urine), incontinence.
  • Neurological: Headaches, dizziness, seizures, weakness, numbness, changes in vision or speech.
  • Musculoskeletal: Joint pain, swelling, stiffness, back pain.
  • Dermatological: Rashes, new or changing skin lesions.
6. Section 4: Past medical history (PMH)

The Past Medical History (PMH) provides essential context, helping you to understand the patient's baseline health and identify risk factors for their current problem. A patient's existing conditions may directly relate to their presentation, influence your differential diagnosis, or affect your management plan.

Begin with a general question, such as, "Do you have any ongoing medical conditions that you see your doctor for?". Then, it is good practice to specifically probe for major chronic illnesses, as patients may not always consider well-controlled conditions as ongoing problems.

  • Major Conditions to ask about specifically:
    • Asthma or COPD
    • Hypertension (high blood pressure)
    • Ischaemic Heart Disease (angina, heart attacks)
    • Stroke or Transient Ischaemic Attacks (TIAs)
    • Diabetes (Type 1 or 2)
    • Epilepsy
    • Jaundice, hepatitis, or liver problems
    • Anaemia or blood clots (DVT/PE)
    • Cancer (current or in remission)
    • Mental health conditions like depression or anxiety
  • Previous Surgeries & Hospitalisations: Ask, "Have you ever had any operations?" and "Have you ever been admitted to hospital?". For any surgery, note the procedure, date, and any complications.
7. Section 5: Drug history (DHx) & allergies

A comprehensive drug history is vital for patient safety. It helps identify potential causes for the patient's symptoms (side effects), prevent harmful drug interactions, and avoid prescribing medications to which the patient is allergic. It must include all medications, not just those prescribed by a doctor.

A helpful framework is to ask about the "4 Ps":

  1. Prescribed Medications: "Are you on any regular prescribed medications?". Ask for their repeat prescription list if possible. For each drug, confirm the name, dose, frequency, and route. Ask about compliance ("How are you getting on with taking them?").
  2. P-O-C (Purchased Over-the-Counter): Ask specifically about any medicines or remedies bought from a pharmacy or supermarket, such as painkillers (paracetamol, ibuprofen), vitamins, or supplements.
  3. P-Herbal (Phytotherapeutic): Ask about any herbal or alternative remedies (e.g., St John's Wort), as these can have significant interactions.
  4. P-Recreational: Ask about recreational drug use in a sensitive and non-judgemental manner.

Allergies: This is a critical safety step. Ask, "Are you allergic to any medications?". If the answer is yes, you must clarify the nature of the reaction (e.g., "What happens when you take penicillin?"). A simple rash is different from life-threatening anaphylaxis. Always document the reaction clearly. Ask about food or other environmental allergies as well.

8. Section 6: Family history (FHx)

The Family History (FHx) is taken to identify any potential genetic predisposition to illness. Many common and serious conditions have a heritable component, and a positive family history can significantly increase a patient's risk, altering your diagnostic reasoning and screening recommendations. The focus should primarily be on first-degree relatives (parents, siblings, and children), as their shared genetic material is highest.

Start with a general enquiry: "Are there any medical conditions that run in your family?". Then, ask specifically about key conditions:

  • Cardiovascular Disease: Ask about ischaemic heart disease (angina/heart attacks) or strokes, particularly if they occurred at a young age (e.g., under 60).
  • Cancer: Ask about any family history of cancer, noting the type of cancer and the age of onset of the affected relative (e.g., breast, ovarian, bowel, prostate).
  • Diabetes: Note whether this is Type 1 or Type 2.
  • Other significant conditions: Ask about hypertension, thyroid disease, or any known genetic disorders such as cystic fibrosis, Huntington's disease, or muscular dystrophy.
  • For each affected relative, it is good practice to document their age, their relationship to the patient, and the specific condition.
9. Section 7: Social history (SHx)

The Social History (SHx) provides a picture of the patient as an individual within their environment. It helps you to understand their personal circumstances, lifestyle risk factors, and the functional impact of their illness. This information is crucial for holistic care and for planning a safe and appropriate management plan (e.g., a discharge plan). Questions should be asked sensitively and non-judgementally.

A useful structure includes:

  • Home & Personal Life: "Who do you live with at home?", "What sort of accommodation do you live in (e.g., house, flat, stairs)?", "Is there anyone at home who helps to look after you?".
  • Occupation & Hobbies: "What is your current or former occupation?", "What do you like to do in your spare time?". This can reveal occupational exposures or the impact of illness on their quality of life.
  • Lifestyle Risk Factors:
    • Smoking: "Do you smoke?". If yes, quantify in pack-years ([cigarettes per day / 20] x years). If no, "Have you ever smoked?".
    • Alcohol: "Do you drink alcohol?". Quantify intake in units per week. Be aware of screening tools for harmful drinking if concerns arise.
    • Recreational Drugs: As mentioned in the drug history, this can be revisited if appropriate.
  • Functional Status: "How has this problem been affecting your ability to manage day-to-day?". This assesses the true impact of the illness on the patient's life.
10. Synthesising & presenting the history

After gathering all the information, the final step is to synthesise it into a coherent narrative. This is essential for communicating the case clearly to colleagues (e.g., on a ward round or in a referral letter) and for creating a logical record in the patient's notes. The presentation should follow the same structure in which the history was taken.

  • Structure of Presentation:
    1. Start with the patient's name, age, and a one-sentence opening statement including the presenting complaint (e.g., "This is Mrs. Jane Doe, a 65-year-old lady who presented with a 2-day history of shortness of breath.").
    2. Present the History of Presenting Complaint in a chronological, structured way.
    3. Briefly list any significant positive or negative findings from the Review of Systems.
    4. Summarise the Past Medical History.
    5. List the current medications and any allergies.
    6. Mention any significant Family or Social History.
  • The Problem Representation: This is a powerful technique where you create a one-sentence summary that encapsulates the core clinical problem. It usually takes the form: [Age, sex] with [key risk factors/PMH] presenting with [key symptom cluster] suggestive of [diagnostic category]. For example: "A 65-year-old male smoker with a history of hypertension presents with a 2-hour history of sudden-onset, crushing central chest pain radiating to his jaw."
  • Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE): Always conclude by stating the patient's ICE. This demonstrates a patient-centred approach.
11. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Taking a good history is a skill that improves with practice. Being aware of common pitfalls can help you refine your technique more quickly.

  • Pitfall: Asking leading questions. (e.g., "You're not coughing up any blood, are you?"). This biases the patient's answer.
    • Tip: Rephrase as neutral, open questions. ("Have you noticed anything in your sputum when you cough?").
  • Pitfall: Using medical jargon. This can confuse patients and lead to inaccurate information.
    • Tip: Use simple language. Instead of "orthopnoea," ask "Do you find it harder to breathe when you lie flat?".
  • Pitfall: Not clarifying the allergy. Simply writing "allergic to penicillin" is unsafe. The reaction could be a mild rash or fatal anaphylaxis.
    • Tip: Always ask "What happens when you take it?" and document the specific reaction.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting to quantify. "Smokes" or "drinks" is not enough information.
    • Tip: Quantify smoking in pack-years and alcohol in units per week. This is essential for risk assessment.
  • Pitfall: Not exploring Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE). Missing the patient's agenda can lead to an unsatisfying consultation and poor adherence to plans.
    • Tip: Make it a habit to end the history-taking portion with: "Was there anything you were particularly worried this might be?" and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?".
