1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of an Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) history is to systematically evaluate symptoms in these interconnected regions to distinguish between benign, self-limiting conditions and those requiring urgent intervention. This involves characterising symptoms with precision, identifying red flags for malignancy or airway compromise, and understanding the functional impact on the patient's hearing, balance, voice, and breathing. Unlike a general medical history, it drills down into specific anatomical areas and functions. The history is the cornerstone of diagnosis, guiding whether the likely cause is inflammatory, infective, structural, or neoplastic, and thereby determining the necessary investigations and management plan.

2. Core principles & communication

Effective communication is crucial, as ENT symptoms can be distressing and impact a patient's self-image and ability to communicate. It is vital to create a confidential and empathetic environment where the patient feels comfortable discussing issues like hearing loss, facial asymmetry, or changes in their voice. A patient-centred approach ensures you address their primary concerns, which is essential for building rapport and facilitating shared decision-making. Be mindful that patients with significant hearing impairment may require you to speak clearly, face them directly, and minimise background noise.

  • Establish Rapport: Begin with a clear introduction ("Hello, I'm one of the foundation doctors"). Ensure the patient is comfortable and that you are positioned to communicate effectively, especially if there is hearing loss. Sit at their level and maintain open body language.
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open questions ("Tell me about the trouble you've been having with your ear?") to allow the patient to frame the issue. Gradually transition to more focused, closed questions ("Is the pain sharp or a dull ache?") to clarify specific details of each symptom.
  • Active Listening & Signposting: Demonstrate you are listening by summarising key points ("So, the dizziness started last Tuesday after a cold..."). Use signposting to guide the consultation logically ("Okay, we have talked about your ear pain. Now I'm going to ask some questions about your hearing.").
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): Exploring ICE is fundamental in ENT.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing these symptoms?"
    • Concerns: Directly ask about their main worry. A critical question is, "Was there anything specific you were worried this could be?" This often reveals fears of cancer or permanent hearing loss.
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" This clarifies goals, from reassurance to specialist referral.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

A focused exploration of cardinal ENT symptoms is essential. For each symptom, you must consider its laterality (unilateral or bilateral), as unilateral symptoms are often more concerning. The immediate priority is to screen for red flags that indicate a serious underlying pathology.

  • Ear (Otology):
    • Otalgia (Ear Pain): Can be primary (from the ear) or referred (e.g., from teeth or throat).
    • Otorrhoea (Discharge): Note the colour, consistency, and smell.
    • Hearing Loss: Distinguish between conductive and sensorineural patterns. Is it sudden or gradual?
    • Tinnitus: A sound in the ear without an external source. Is it pulsatile?
    • Vertigo: A true sensation of movement (often spinning). Distinguish from general dizziness.
    • Red Flag: Sudden onset sensorineural hearing loss requires immediate ENT assessment and high-dose steroids. Any new cranial nerve palsy (e.g., facial droop) alongside ear symptoms is an emergency.
  • Nose (Rhinology):
    • Nasal Obstruction: Unilateral or bilateral? Constant or intermittent?
    • Rhinorrhoea (Runny Nose): Note character (clear, purulent, bloody) and triggers.
    • Epistaxis (Nosebleed): Anterior or posterior? Amount and frequency?
    • Facial Pain/Pressure: A key feature of sinusitis.
    • Anosmia (Loss of Smell): Often associated with nasal polyps or viral infections.
    • Red Flag: Unilateral bloody discharge, persistent blockage, or swelling in an adult could indicate a nasal or sinus tumour.
  • Throat (Laryngology) & Neck:
    • Sore Throat / Odynophagia (Painful Swallowing): A common symptom of infection.
    • Dysphagia (Difficulty Swallowing): Is it for solids, liquids, or both?
    • Hoarseness (Dysphonia): A change in voice quality.
    • Stridor: A high-pitched, noisy breathing sound indicating upper airway obstruction.
    • Neck Lump: Note its location, size, consistency, and how long it has been present.
    • Red Flag: Stridor is a medical emergency requiring immediate airway assessment. Hoarseness lasting >3 weeks (especially in a smoker), unexplained dysphagia, or a persistent, firm neck lump are red flags for head and neck cancer.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

Each cardinal symptom must be methodically explored to build a detailed picture. A useful mnemonic for this is SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, and Severity). Using this framework for every key symptom ensures a comprehensive and detailed history.

  • For a Neck Lump:
    • Site: Where exactly is the lump? Anterior or posterior triangle? Midline?
    • Onset: When did you first notice it? Has it grown since?
    • Character: Is the lump soft, firm, or rock-hard? Is the surface smooth or irregular?
    • Radiation: Is there any associated pain that radiates elsewhere (e.g., to the ear - otalgia)?
    • Associated Symptoms: Ask about 'B symptoms' (fever, night sweats, unexplained weight loss), hoarseness, dysphagia, or skin changes over the lump.
    • Timing: Is the lump always present or does it change in size (e.g., with meals, suggesting a salivary gland issue)?
    • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: Does anything make it bigger or smaller? Is it tender? (Painful lumps are more often inflammatory).
    • Severity: How much does it bother you? Is it causing any functional problems?
  • For Vertigo:
    • Onset & Timing: Was the onset sudden or gradual? How long do episodes last (seconds, minutes, hours, days)? Is it continuous or episodic?
    • Character: Can you describe the sensation? Is it a feeling of the room spinning or of you spinning?
    • Associated Symptoms: Is it accompanied by hearing loss, tinnitus (suggesting a peripheral cause like Ménière's disease), nausea/vomiting, or any neurological symptoms like weakness, slurred speech or visual changes (suggesting a central cause like a stroke)?
    • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: Is it triggered by head movements (suggesting BPPV) or is it spontaneous?
5. System-specific review of systems

Beyond the cardinal symptoms, a targeted review of other systems is essential to identify underlying causes, associated conditions, or systemic diseases that can manifest with ENT symptoms. This helps to complete the clinical picture.

  • General: Ask about fevers, night sweats, and unintentional weight loss. These are constitutional 'B symptoms' that can indicate malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, head and neck cancer) or chronic infections like tuberculosis.
  • Neurological: Enquire about headaches, facial numbness or weakness, visual changes, or changes to other cranial nerves. This is critical for assessing central causes of vertigo or identifying skull base tumours.
  • Gastrointestinal: Ask about symptoms of gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD), such as heartburn or an acidic taste in the mouth. Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR) is a common cause of chronic cough, throat clearing, and hoarseness.
  • Respiratory: Ask about cough, shortness of breath, and wheeze. Atopy (the predisposition to allergic diseases) often links rhinitis with asthma. A cough may also be a symptom of aspiration secondary to a swallowing disorder.
  • Dermatological: Ask about any skin lesions on the head, neck, or ears. Basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma are common on sun-exposed areas and can present as non-healing ulcers or lumps.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

The patient's background provides the context for their symptoms and is essential for risk stratification, particularly for head and neck cancer. This information helps establish the pre-test probability of a serious diagnosis and may reveal clues to the aetiology of the presenting complaint.

  • Past Medical History (PMHx):
    • Focus on atopic conditions (asthma, eczema, allergic rhinitis), GORD, and previous ENT procedures (e.g., tonsillectomy, grommets, septoplasty). Ask about autoimmune conditions (e.g., Wegener's granulomatosis) which can present with ENT manifestations.
  • Drug History (DHx):
    • Ask about specific medications like nasal sprays (steroids, decongestants), anticoagulants/antiplatelets (relevant for epistaxis), ACE inhibitors (can cause a chronic cough), and any ototoxic drugs (e.g., gentamicin, furosemide, certain chemotherapies).
  • Family History (FHx):
    • Ask about any family history of hereditary hearing loss, thyroid disease, or head and neck cancers. Also enquire about atopy in the family.
  • Social History (SHx):
    • Smoking & Alcohol: These are the two most critical modifiable risk factors for head and neck squamous cell carcinoma. Their effects are synergistic. Quantify smoking in pack-years ([cigarettes per day / 20] x years smoked) and alcohol in units per week.
    • Occupation: Ask about occupational noise exposure (risk for hearing loss) and exposure to dusts like hardwood dust (a risk factor for sinonasal adenocarcinoma).
    • Recreational Drugs: It is vital to ask about cocaine use, which can cause nasal septal perforation and epistaxis.
    • Travel: Recent travel history can be relevant for certain infections.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

The problem representation is a powerful clinical tool. It is a concise, one-sentence summary that captures the essence of the case, integrating the patient's demographics, key risk factors, and the primary symptom cluster. This summary bridges data collection and clinical reasoning, focusing the differential diagnosis. The structure is typically: [Age/sex] with [key risk factors] presenting with [symptom profile] suggestive of [most likely diagnostic category].

  • Example 1 (Malignancy): "A 68-year-old male, lifelong heavy smoker and drinker, presents with a six-week history of progressive hoarseness, dysphagia for solids, and an associated hard, fixed neck lump, highly concerning for a laryngeal carcinoma with regional metastasis."
  • Example 2 (Infection): "A 4-year-old child presents with a two-day history of acute-onset high fever, severe right-sided otalgia, and irritability, in the context of a preceding viral upper respiratory infection, suggestive of acute otitis media."
  • Example 3 (Inflammatory): "A 45-year-old woman with a history of asthma presents with a three-month history of episodic vertigo lasting hours, associated with fluctuating right-sided hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness, classic for Ménière's disease."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The specific pattern of symptoms, timeline, and risk factors derived from the history is the most effective tool for building a relevant and prioritised differential diagnosis.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Hoarseness >3 weeks, smoker/drinker, weight loss, referred otalgia, neck lump. Laryngeal/Hypopharyngeal Cancer
Sudden-onset, severe, spinning vertigo with nausea, lasting days, often following a viral illness. Hearing is unaffected. Vestibular Neuronitis (if hearing loss is present, consider Labyrinthitis)
Episodic vertigo (minutes-hours), fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness. Unilateral. Ménière's Disease
Unilateral, foul-smelling, purulent nasal discharge in a young child. Nasal Foreign Body
Facial pressure/pain worse on bending forward, purulent nasal discharge, fever, after a cold. Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis
Severe sore throat, fever, dysphagia, muffled "hot potato" voice, trismus (inability to open mouth fully). Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures all critical information is conveyed clearly and efficiently, which is vital for safe handovers, referrals to specialists, or discussions on ward rounds. The focus should be on the most pertinent positive and negative findings.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old [man/woman].

They presented with a [duration] history of [chief complaint, e.g., hoarseness].

The key features of the presenting complaint are [summarise SOCRATES, e.g., it has been progressive over 6 weeks, with no fluctuation]. Associated symptoms include [e.g., painful swallowing for solids, 5kg weight loss]. They deny [key negatives, e.g., any breathing difficulty or stridor].

Specifically regarding their ENT history, they report [other ENT symptoms, e.g., a painless lump on the right side of their neck]. They deny any [e.g., hearing loss or ear discharge].

Their past medical history is notable for [key conditions].
Current medications include [list key medications].
There is no significant family history.
Regarding their social history, they are a current smoker with a [number]-pack-year history and drink approximately [number] units of alcohol per week.

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old [man/woman] with significant smoking and alcohol history, presenting with [key symptom cluster e.g., progressive hoarseness and a neck lump], which is highly suspicious for [top differential, e.g., a laryngeal malignancy].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can dramatically improve the precision of the history, leading to a more accurate differential diagnosis and safer management plan.

  • Pitfall: Not clarifying "dizziness". This term is used by patients to describe vertigo, pre-syncope, and general imbalance.
  • Expert Tip: Ask specific questions to differentiate. "Does it feel like the room is spinning, or you are spinning?" (for vertigo). "Do you feel like you are about to pass out?" (for pre-syncope). "Do you just feel unsteady on your feet?" (for imbalance).
  • Pitfall: Failing to ask about unilateral symptoms. A patient might say "I have hearing loss" without specifying a side.
  • Expert Tip: Always ask "Is that in one ear or both?". Unilateral symptoms (hearing loss, tinnitus, blocked nose) are generally more concerning than symmetrical, bilateral symptoms and warrant a higher index of suspicion for serious pathology.
  • Pitfall: Forgetting to explicitly ask about the major risk factors for head and neck cancer.
  • Expert Tip: Frame smoking and alcohol questions as routine and non-judgemental. "I need to ask you some standard lifestyle questions as they can be relevant. Do you smoke? And do you drink alcohol?". Then quantify the responses accurately.
  • Pitfall: Not considering referred pain. Ear pain (otalgia) is a classic example.
  • Expert Tip: If a patient has severe ear pain but the ear canal and drum look completely normal on examination, the cause is almost certainly referred from elsewhere. Actively ask about dental problems, jaw clenching (TMJ dysfunction), and throat symptoms (a tumour in the throat can present with ear pain).
11. Illustrative history example

This is a brief, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with red flag throat symptoms.

  • Clinician: "Hello Mr. Jones, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've been sent up by your GP because of a change in your voice. Can you tell me about that?"
  • Patient: "Yes, for about two months now my voice has been really raspy. It just doesn't seem to be getting any better."
  • Clinician: "Okay, raspy for two months. Has it been getting worse over that time?"
  • Patient: "I think so, yes. My wife says I sound very gravelly now. I also find it's an effort to talk for too long."
  • Clinician: "Have you noticed any other symptoms along with the voice change? Any trouble with swallowing?"
  • Patient: "Funnily enough, yes. For the last few weeks, it's been painful to swallow my food. Bread and meat get stuck sometimes."
  • Clinician: "And have you had any pain anywhere else? Any earache?"
  • Patient: "Yes, a dull ache in my right ear that comes and goes."
  • Clinician: "Thank you. I need to ask a couple of routine lifestyle questions. Have you ever been a smoker?"
  • Patient: "I've smoked 20 a day since I was 16. I'm 66 now."
  • Clinician: "Okay. And can I ask about alcohol?"
  • Patient: "A pint or two down the pub most nights."
  • Clinician: "Thank you, Mr. Jones. One last thing, is there anything that you've been worried this might be?"
  • Patient: "Well, with the smoking... you worry about the worst, don't you? Cancer."

Problem Representation: A 66-year-old man with a 50-pack-year smoking history presents with a two-month history of progressive hoarseness, now associated with odynophagia and right-sided referred otalgia, which is highly suspicious for a laryngeal malignancy.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Unsure, but knows it is not normal.
  • Concerns: Explicitly worried about having cancer due to his smoking history.
  • Expectations: Wants to find out what is causing his symptoms and get it treated.
