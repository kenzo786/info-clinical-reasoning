Gynaecological System History
1. Overview & aims of this history

The primary aim of a gynaecological history is to build a detailed and chronological narrative of a patient's symptoms in the context of their reproductive life. This highly specific history is crucial for differentiating between gynaecological, urological, and gastrointestinal causes of pelvic symptoms, identifying red-flag conditions like ectopic pregnancy or malignancy, and understanding the impact of symptoms on the patient's quality of life. Unlike other histories, it delves into sensitive areas including menstrual, obstetric, and sexual health, requiring a high degree of trust and professionalism. A well-taken history is the most powerful tool for generating an accurate differential diagnosis and guiding appropriate investigations and management.

2. Core principles & communication

Given the sensitive nature of the topic, establishing a safe and confidential environment is paramount. It is essential to build rapport and trust, ensuring the patient feels able to share personal information without judgement. Offering a chaperone should be standard practice before taking the history or performing any examination. It is vital to use inclusive and neutral language, for example, using "partner" instead of "husband" and being mindful of gender identity by asking for preferred pronouns if unsure. Clearly explaining why certain questions are necessary can also help to alleviate patient anxiety and facilitate a more open conversation.

  • Establish Rapport & Privacy: Begin with a warm introduction, ensure the room is private, and confirm the patient is comfortable. "Hello, I'm one of the foundation doctors. Everything we discuss today is confidential. Before we start, would you be comfortable with a chaperone being present?"
  • Open-to-Closed Questioning: Start with broad, open questions ("Can you tell me what's brought you here today?") to let the patient narrate their story. Then, use focused, closed questions to clarify specifics ("Is the pain worse during your period?").
  • Active Listening & Signposting: Use verbal and non-verbal cues to show you are listening. Clearly signpost your transitions between topics, especially sensitive ones. "Thank you for sharing that. To get a full picture, I now need to ask some specific questions about your periods. Is that alright?"
  • Ideas, Concerns & Expectations (ICE): This is critically important for understanding the patient's perspective.
    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing the problem?"
    • Concerns: Directly ask, "Is there anything in particular that you have been worried this might be?" This often uncovers fears of cancer, infertility, or sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?" This clarifies the patient's goals for the consultation.
3. Key presenting complaints & cardinal symptoms

A focused exploration of cardinal gynaecological symptoms is essential. The priority is to characterise the symptom fully while actively screening for red flags that necessitate urgent referral or admission.

  • Abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB): This includes heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB), intermenstrual bleeding (IMB), and post-coital bleeding (PCB).
    • Red Flag: Post-menopausal bleeding (PMB), defined as any vaginal bleeding occurring more than 12 months after the last menstrual period, must be urgently investigated to rule out endometrial cancer.
    • Red Flag: Post-coital bleeding (PCB) is a key symptom of cervical cancer and requires urgent colposcopy referral.
  • Pelvic Pain: Can be acute or chronic. Its relationship to the menstrual cycle is a key diagnostic clue.
    • Red Flag: The combination of a missed period (amenorrhoea) and sudden-onset, severe, unilateral lower abdominal pain suggests a ruptured ectopic pregnancy, a life-threatening surgical emergency. Associated dizziness, shoulder tip pain, or collapse warrants immediate escalation.
  • Vaginal Discharge: Clarify colour, consistency, odour, and volume. Ask about associated symptoms like itching (pruritus), soreness, or pain.
    • Red Flag: A blood-stained or particularly offensive-smelling discharge, especially in a post-menopausal woman, can be a symptom of uterine or cervical cancer.
  • Subfertility: Defined as the inability to conceive after 12 months of regular, unprotected intercourse.
  • Symptoms of Prolapse: A sensation of a "lump" or "something coming down" in the vagina, or a dragging feeling. Often associated with urinary or bowel symptoms.
4. Deconstructing the presenting complaint

Each cardinal symptom must be broken down systematically. For pain, the SOCRATES mnemonic (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated Symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving Factors, Severity) is useful. For bleeding, a more tailored approach is required.

  • For Pelvic Pain (SOCRATES):
    • Site: Central, unilateral, or bilateral?
    • Onset: Sudden (e.g., ectopic rupture, ovarian torsion) or gradual (e.g., PID, endometriosis)?
    • Character: Sharp, dull, cramping, stabbing?
    • Radiation: To the back or down the legs? Shoulder tip pain suggests diaphragmatic irritation from intra-abdominal blood.
    • Associated Symptoms: Fever, bleeding, discharge, nausea, urinary/bowel symptoms.
    • Timing: Is the pain cyclical (related to periods)? Or is it constant? Is it related to intercourse (dyspareunia)?
    • Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: Does anything make it better or worse (e.g., movement, analgesia)?
    • Severity: Score out of 10. How does it impact daily activities?
  • For Abnormal Bleeding:
    • Quantify Blood Loss: "How many pads or tampons do you use on your heaviest day?" "Do you have to use double protection?" "Do you pass clots? If so, what size are they (e.g., smaller or larger than a 50p coin)?" "Have you ever bled through your clothes (flooding)?"
    • Pattern: Is the bleeding between periods (IMB), after sex (PCB), or after menopause (PMB)?
    • Associated Symptoms: Enquire about symptoms of anaemia such as fatigue, dizziness, and shortness of breath. Ask about pelvic pain or pressure.
5. System-specific review of systems

A targeted review of related systems is crucial to identify features of underlying systemic disease and rule out non-gynaecological causes for pelvic symptoms.

  • Endocrine: Ask about hot flushes, night sweats, or mood changes (suggesting perimenopause). Enquire about changes in weight, hair growth (hirsutism), or skin changes like acne (which can point towards Polycystic Ovary Syndrome - PCOS).
  • Gastrointestinal: Ask about bloating, changes in bowel habit (diarrhoea or constipation), rectal bleeding, or abdominal pain. These can overlap with conditions like endometriosis or ovarian cancer, but also suggest primary bowel pathology like Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) or Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD).
  • Urological: Ask about pain on passing urine (dysuria), frequency, urgency, blood in the urine (haematuria), and incontinence (stress or urge). Urinary symptoms can be caused by prolapse, fibroids pressing on the bladder, or a UTI.
  • General/Constitutional: Ask about fevers or rigors (suggesting infection, e.g., Pelvic Inflammatory Disease). Enquire about unintentional weight loss or persistent fatigue, which can be red flags for malignancy.
6. Contextual PMHx, DHx, FHx, & SHx

This section is fundamental in gynaecology. It provides the essential context for interpreting the presenting complaint and assessing risk. It is often structured to cover specific gynaecological, obstetric, contraceptive, and sexual history points in detail.

  • Gynaecological History:
    • Menstrual History: Note the age of menarche (first period) and menopause (if applicable). Establish the menstrual cycle pattern: length (e.g., 28 days), regularity, and duration of bleed. Crucially, always ask for the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP).
    • Cervical Screening History: Ask for the date and result of their last smear test, and if they have ever had an abnormal result or required treatment (e.g., LLETZ).
    • Previous Gynaecological Issues: Ask specifically about past diagnoses like endometriosis, fibroids, PCOS, pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), or ovarian cysts.
  • Obstetric History:
    • Record Gravidity (total number of pregnancies) and Parity (number of births beyond 24 weeks).
    • For each birth, ask about the year, mode of delivery (e.g., normal vaginal, forceps, C-section), gestation, and any complications like postpartum haemorrhage.
    • Ask about any history of miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, or termination of pregnancy.
  • Sexual and Contraceptive History:
    • Preface with: "To understand things fully, I need to ask a few personal questions about your sexual health. This is routine for this kind of problem."
    • Ask about current sexual activity (partners male, female, or both). Enquire about any pain during or after sex (dyspareunia). Ask about any past history of sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
    • Detail current and past contraceptive methods used.
  • Family History (FHx): Ask about family history of gynaecological cancers (ovarian, endometrial), breast cancer (BRCA gene link), or conditions like endometriosis.
  • Social History (SHx): Quantify smoking and alcohol intake. Discuss the impact of symptoms on the patient's work, relationships, and overall quality of life.
7. Synthesising the history: the problem representation

The problem representation is a one-sentence summary that synthesises the most pertinent information into a coherent clinical picture. It demonstrates your ability to extract the key features and initiates the diagnostic process. The structure is typically: [Age, gravidity/parity] with [key risk factors/background] presenting with a [symptom profile], with the top differential in mind.

  • Example 1 (Possible Ectopic): "A 26-year-old, gravida 1 para 0 woman with a history of pelvic inflammatory disease presents with 6 weeks of amenorrhoea and the acute onset of severe right iliac fossa pain and light vaginal spotting, highly concerning for an ectopic pregnancy."
  • Example 2 (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding): "A 44-year-old, para 2 woman presents with a one-year history of progressively heavy and painful periods, with flooding and large clots, now causing symptoms of iron deficiency anaemia."
  • Example 3 (Post-Menopausal Bleeding): "A 68-year-old woman, whose last period was 15 years ago, presents with a single episode of painless vaginal bleeding, requiring urgent investigation for endometrial malignancy."
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from the history

The history is the most valuable tool for constructing a relevant differential diagnosis, which can be categorised by the primary symptom.

Symptom Pattern / Historical Features Points Towards (Key Differentials)
Post-menopausal bleeding (any amount). Endometrial Cancer, Vaginal Atrophy, Endometrial Polyp, HRT effect.
Heavy, regular, painful periods in a woman aged 35-50. Uterine Fibroids, Adenomyosis, Endometrial Polyp.
Amenorrhoea, acute unilateral pelvic pain +/- spotting. Ectopic Pregnancy, Ruptured Ovarian Cyst, Ovarian Torsion.
Post-coital bleeding in a sexually active woman. Cervical Cancer, Cervical Ectropion, Cervicitis (e.g., chlamydia), Polyp.
Chronic cyclical pelvic pain, deep dyspareunia, and subfertility. Endometriosis.
Irregular, infrequent periods, with hirsutism or acne and difficulty conceiving. Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS).
Fever, bilateral lower abdominal pain, and purulent vaginal discharge. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID).
9. Presenting the history

A structured presentation ensures vital information is conveyed clearly and professionally, particularly during handovers or referrals to the gynaecology team.

This is [Patient Name], a [age]-year-old lady.

She has presented with [chief complaint], which started [timing, e.g., six weeks ago].

The key features of the presenting complaint are [summarise SOCRATES or bleeding details].

Her last menstrual period was on [date]. Her periods are usually [regular/irregular], lasting [number] days, with a [number]-day cycle.
Her menarche was at age [age].

Her obstetric history is Gravida [G], Para [P]. [Briefly summarise outcomes, e.g., "She has two children, both normal vaginal deliveries."].

Her last cervical smear was in [year] and the result was [result].
She is/is not currently sexually active. Her current contraception is [method].

Her past medical history is significant for [list key conditions].
Her current medications include [list key medications].
The family history is notable for [e.g., mother with breast cancer].
Socially, she [smokes/drinks/works as...].

In summary, this is a [age]-year-old lady presenting with [key symptom cluster and duration] on a background of [key PMHx/risk factors]. My main concern is [top differential, e.g., a possible ectopic pregnancy / post-menopausal bleeding for investigation].
10. Common pitfalls & expert tips

Avoiding common errors can significantly enhance the quality and diagnostic accuracy of the history.

  • Pitfall: Forgetting to ask for the Last Menstrual Period (LMP).
  • Expert Tip: Make the LMP one of your first questions in any woman of reproductive age. It is the single most important piece of data; it immediately frames the context for pelvic pain or abnormal bleeding and is essential for considering pregnancy.
  • Pitfall: Failing to quantify bleeding. A history of "heavy periods" is insufficient.
  • Expert Tip: Ask specific, objective questions: "How many pads/tampons on your heaviest day?", "Do you pass clots bigger than a 50p coin?", "Do you ever have to change your bedding at night?". This builds a much clearer picture of severity.
  • Pitfall: Shying away from the sexual history due to embarrassment.
  • Expert Tip: Normalise the questions and be professional. Use clear signposting: "I need to ask some personal questions now as they are important for working out the cause of your symptoms. This is something I ask everyone."
  • Pitfall: Not considering pregnancy in a woman of reproductive age with amenorrhoea, pain, or bleeding.
  • Expert Tip: Maintain a high index of suspicion for ectopic pregnancy. It is a diagnosis that cannot be missed. Always ask about the LMP and contraceptive use. A pregnancy test is mandatory.
11. Illustrative history example

This is a short, narrative example of a clinician taking a focused history from a patient with post-coital bleeding (PCB).

  • Clinician: "Hello Mrs. Jones, I'm one of the doctors. I understand you've come to see us about some bleeding. Could you tell me about that?"
  • Patient: "Yes. For the last couple of months, I've been noticing a bit of bleeding after I've been intimate with my husband."
  • Clinician: "Okay, bleeding after intercourse. Thank you for telling me. How much blood would you say you notice?"
  • Patient: "It's not a lot, just a streak on the toilet paper when I wipe afterwards. It's bright red."
  • Clinician: "And does this happen every time?"
  • Patient: "Pretty much, yes. It started about three months ago."
  • Clinician: "To get a full picture, I need to check a few things. Can I ask when your last period was?"
  • Patient: "About two weeks ago. My periods are regular as clockwork."
  • Clinician: "Thank you. And do you remember when you had your last smear test?"
  • Patient: "Oh, it must be years ago. I keep getting the letters but I just haven't got round to it. I'm probably overdue."
  • Clinician: "That's okay, thank you for being honest. Can I just check, is there anything specific you've been worried this could be?"
  • Patient: "Well, you hear stories, don't you? I'm worried it might be something serious, like cancer."

Problem Representation: A 42-year-old, para 2 woman presents with a three-month history of post-coital bleeding, on a background of being overdue for cervical screening.

Patient's ICE:

  • Ideas: Unsure of the cause.
  • Concerns: Worried it could be cancer.
  • Expectations: Wants to know what is causing the bleeding and to have an examination and tests to find out.
