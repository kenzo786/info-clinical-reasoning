1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary aim of the musculoskeletal (MSK) examination is to identify objective physical signs that corroborate the history and localise the pathology. The examination systematically assesses the structure and function of the joints, muscles, and nerves. Key goals are to detect signs of inflammation (warmth, swelling, redness), deformity, instability, muscle wasting, and neurological compromise. It is designed to differentiate between articular (joint-based) and peri-articular (soft-tissue) problems, and to distinguish between inflammatory arthritis, degenerative disease, and other pathologies. A crucial aim is to identify red flag signs, such as a hot, swollen joint suggesting infection.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A proficient examination requires a solid understanding of surface anatomy. While a full MSK examination covers all joints, the hands and wrists are crucial for detecting systemic inflammatory disease. Key landmarks include:

  • Joints of the Hand: It is essential to name the joints correctly.
    • DIP joints: Distal Interphalangeal joints (closest to the nail).
    • PIP joints: Proximal Interphalangeal joints (the middle knuckle).
    • MCP joints: Metacarpophalangeal joints (the main knuckles where fingers meet the hand).
  • Wrist Joint: Comprised of the radiocarpal, ulnocarpal, and intercarpal joints. Palpated as the crease between the forearm and hand.
  • Thenar and Hypothenar Eminences: The two fleshy mounds of muscle in the palm. The thenar eminence is at the base of the thumb (innervated by the median nerve), and the hypothenar eminence is at the base of the little finger.
  • Anatomical Snuffbox: A triangular depression on the radial, dorsal aspect of the hand, at the level of the carpal bones. Tenderness here can indicate a scaphoid fracture.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A structured and professional approach is essential for patient comfort and diagnostic accuracy.

  1. Equipment:
    • Examination couch
    • Pillow or cushion for the patient's hands
    • Tape measure (for muscle bulk or swelling)
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ideally, the patient should be sitting on the side of an examination couch.
    • Ask the patient to place their hands onto a pillow on their lap, which encourages them to relax their muscles.
    • Ensure adequate exposure. Ask the patient to remove any jewellery from their hands and wrists and to roll their sleeves up to their elbows.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Begin by washing your hands.
    • Introduce yourself with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, I'm [Name], a medical student").
    • Explain the examination and gain consent. A good phrase is: "I've been asked to examine your hands and wrists. This will involve looking at them, gently feeling the joints, and asking you to perform some movements. It shouldn't be painful, but please let me know if you feel any discomfort. Is that okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent "Look, Feel, Move" sequence, always comparing one side with the other, is the foundation of the MSK examination. The GALS (Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine) screen is often used as a quick general overview first.

  1. General Inspection: Observe the patient as they walk into the room, noting their gait and any obvious mobility aids. Look at the patient as a whole for any syndromic features.
  2. Look (Inspection of the Hands & Wrists):
    • Inspect the dorsal and palmar surfaces. Look for swelling, deformity (e.g., swan neck, ulnar deviation), muscle wasting (thenar/hypothenar, intrinsics), and skin changes (e.g., rashes, psoriatic plaques, thinning skin).
    • Look at the nails for pitting or onycholysis (associated with psoriatic arthritis).
  3. Feel (Palpation):
    • Assess the temperature over the wrists and MCP joints using the back of your hand, comparing sides.
    • Gently palpate the joints. Use your thumb and index finger to feel each wrist, MCP, PIP, and DIP joint in turn. Assess for swelling (is it boggy/soft synovitis or hard/bony?) and tenderness.
    • Squeeze gently across the MCP joints to elicit tenderness (a sensitive sign of early inflammatory arthritis).
    • Palpate the anatomical snuffbox for tenderness.
  4. Move (Range of Motion):
    • Assess active movements first: "Do what I do".
    • Finger movements: "Make a fist, now open your hand flat".
    • Wrist movements: Prayer sign (extension) and reverse prayer sign (flexion).
    • If active movement is reduced, assess passive movement gently to determine the full range and whether there is a mechanical block.
  5. Function:
    • Assess power grip: Ask the patient to squeeze two of your fingers.
    • Assess pincer grip: Ask the patient to pinch the tip of your finger.
    • Ask the patient to perform a functional task, such as picking up a coin or undoing a button.
  6. Special Tests:
    • Tinel's Test: Tap gently over the carpal tunnel. A positive test elicits paraesthesia in the median nerve distribution.
    • Phalen's Test: Ask the patient to hold their wrists in full flexion for 60 seconds. A positive test reproduces their symptoms.
5. Interpreting normal findings

A normal examination provides a crucial baseline. It is characterised by the absence of pathology.

  • Look: The hands and wrists are symmetrical with no visible swelling, deformities, muscle wasting, or skin changes. Nails are normal.
  • Feel: Normal and symmetrical temperature. No joint tenderness or swelling on palpation. The MCP squeeze test is negative.
  • Move: A full and pain-free range of active and passive movement in all joints of the fingers, thumb, and wrist.
  • Function: Normal and symmetrical power grip and pincer grip. Able to perform functional tasks without difficulty.
  • Special Tests: Tinel's and Phalen's tests are negative.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Identifying specific abnormal signs is key to forming a differential diagnosis.

Synovitis
  • The Sign: A "boggy" or "spongy" swelling of a joint, which may be accompanied by warmth and tenderness. It is the palpable thickening of the synovial membrane.
  • Pathology Link: This is the cardinal sign of active inflammatory arthritis (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis, Psoriatic Arthritis), caused by inflammation and proliferation of the synovium.
Joint deformity
  • The Sign: Fixed, abnormal shaping or positioning of a joint. Key examples in the hand include Swan Neck, Boutonnière's deformity, Z-thumb, and ulnar deviation.
  • Pathology Link: Results from chronic inflammation leading to erosion of cartilage, bone, and laxity or rupture of tendons and ligaments. A hallmark of long-standing, poorly controlled inflammatory arthritis, particularly RA.
Heberden's and Bouchard's nodes
  • The Sign: Hard, non-tender, bony swellings. Heberden's nodes are on the DIP joints, and Bouchard's nodes are on the PIP joints.
  • Pathology Link: These are classic signs of osteoarthritis, representing osteophyte (new bone) formation at the joint margins.
Muscle wasting
  • The Sign: Reduced bulk of the small muscles of the hand (intrinsics) or the thenar/hypothenar eminences.
  • Pathology Link: Can be due to disuse from chronic joint pain, damage to the nerve supply (e.g., median nerve compression causing thenar wasting in carpal tunnel syndrome), or be a feature of the systemic disease itself.
Hot, red, swollen joint
  • The Sign: A single joint that is visibly erythematous, hot to the touch, markedly swollen, and exquisitely tender to palpate or move.
  • Pathology Link: This is the classic presentation of septic arthritis (infection in the joint) or potentially a severe crystal arthropathy like gout.
  • Red Flag: A single hot joint is septic arthritis until proven otherwise and requires immediate hospital assessment and joint aspiration.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A structured summary should communicate the key positive and negative findings clearly, following the "Look, Feel, Move" sequence.

On general inspection, the patient appeared well.

On examination of the hands, there was/was not [symmetrical swelling of the MCP and PIP joints / list key deformity]. There was/was not [muscle wasting / skin changes / nail pitting].

On palpation, the joints were [warm / normal temperature]. There was tenderness of [list specific joints, e.g., the MCPs] and a positive MCP squeeze test. Swelling was [boggy and soft / hard and bony].

Range of movement was [globally reduced / full], with specific difficulty in [e.g., making a full fist]. Functional assessment revealed [reduced grip strength / normal function].

Special tests for carpal tunnel syndrome were [negative / positive].

To complete my examination, I would examine the joints above and below (the elbows and shoulders) and perform a full neurovascular assessment of the upper limbs.
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

A constellation of signs is more powerful than a single finding and allows for the construction of a targeted differential diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Symmetrical boggy synovitis of MCPs and PIPs, positive MCP squeeze, reduced grip strength. Maybe early deformities like ulnar deviation. Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
Hard, bony swelling of DIPs and PIPs (Heberden's/Bouchard's nodes), 'squaring' of the thumb base (CMC joint), minimal inflammation. Nodal Osteoarthritis (OA)
Asymmetrical arthritis, "sausage-like" swelling of a whole digit (dactylitis), and nail pitting or onycholysis. Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA)
Single, acutely red, hot, swollen, and exquisitely tender joint (e.g. wrist or MCP). Septic Arthritis vs. Crystal Arthritis (Gout/Pseudogout)
Thenar muscle wasting, weakness of thumb abduction, positive Tinel's and/or Phalen's test. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (Median Nerve Compression)
9. Relevant next-step investigations

Examination findings are used to justify and guide further investigation.

  • If you find signs of inflammatory arthritis (e.g., synovitis):
    • Logical Next Steps: Bloods (FBC, ESR, CRP), autoimmune screen (Rheumatoid Factor, Anti-CCP), and X-rays of the hands and feet to look for erosions.
  • If you suspect septic arthritis (hot, red, swollen joint):
    • Logical Next Steps: This requires immediate hospital admission. Key investigations are blood cultures, urgent bloods (FBC, CRP), and most importantly, urgent aspiration of the joint for gram stain, culture, and crystal microscopy.
  • If you find signs of osteoarthritis (e.g., bony nodes):
    • Logical Next Steps: Often a clinical diagnosis. An X-ray of the affected joints can confirm features like joint space narrowing and osteophytes. Blood tests are usually normal.
  • If signs suggest carpal tunnel syndrome:
    • Logical Next Steps: The definitive investigation is nerve conduction studies to confirm median nerve slowing across the wrist.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Performing well in a practical examination requires avoiding common mistakes and adopting a slick technique.

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Forgetting to ask about pain before starting.
    • Failing to expose the patient properly (jewellery on, sleeves not rolled up).
    • Not comparing one side with the other for every step.
    • Forgetting to assess function – this is often the most important aspect for the patient.
    • Hurting the patient by moving a joint too forcefully or squeezing an inflamed joint too hard.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always begin with "Wash hands, introduce yourself, gain consent."
    • Have the patient's hands on a pillow – it shows good technique and helps them relax.
    • Verbalise your steps logically using the "Look, Feel, Move" structure. This ensures you don't miss anything and shows the examiner your thought process.
    • When you find a positive sign (e.g., a swollen joint), describe it fully ("There is a soft, boggy swelling of the right 2nd MCP joint, which is tender on palpation but not warm or erythematous.").
    • Always finish by stating how you would complete your examination: "To complete my examination, I would examine the joints above and below (elbows and shoulders), conduct a full neurovascular assessment of the upper limbs, and perform a GALS screen."
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Applying the examination sequence to clinical scenarios helps consolidate learning.

  • Case 1: New-onset Rheumatoid Arthritis
    • Scenario: A 45-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of painful, stiff hands, worse in the morning.
    • Expected Findings: Symmetrical swelling of the MCP and PIP joints. On palpation, this swelling feels "boggy" (synovitis). The MCP squeeze test is likely to be positive. Grip strength will be reduced.
  • Case 2: Nodal Osteoarthritis
    • Scenario: A 72-year-old retired seamstress presents with lumpy, aching fingers that have developed over many years.
    • Expected Findings: Hard, bony, non-tender swellings of the DIP (Heberden's) and PIP (Bouchard's) joints. There may be "squaring" at the base of the thumb due to CMC joint OA. There should be no significant warmth or boggy swelling.
  • Case 3: Psoriatic Arthritis
    • Scenario: A 38-year-old man with a history of psoriasis presents with a single, very swollen and painful finger on his right hand.
    • Expected Findings: The entire digit is swollen and red, resembling a sausage (dactylitis). The nail on that finger may show pitting. Other joints might be affected asymmetrically. Skin plaques of psoriasis may be seen on the extensor surfaces of the elbows.
