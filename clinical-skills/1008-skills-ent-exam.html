1. Overview & aims of the examination

The primary goal of the Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) examination is to identify physical signs of pathology in these interconnected systems. It is a systematic process designed to localise symptoms presented in the history and detect evidence of conditions such as infection (e.g., otitis media, tonsillitis), hearing loss, inflammatory disease (e.g., rhinosinusitis), and head and neck malignancy. The examination aims to differentiate between benign and serious conditions, assess the impact on function (e.g., hearing, airway patency), and identify red flag signs, such as a suspicious neck lump or signs of airway obstruction, that require urgent referral or intervention.

2. Relevant anatomy & surface landmarks

A precise ENT examination requires a solid understanding of the underlying anatomy and their corresponding surface landmarks.

  • The Ear:
    • External Ear: The pinna and external auditory canal (EAC). The mastoid process is the bony prominence palpable just behind the pinna.
    • Tympanic Membrane (Eardrum): Visualised with an otoscope. Key landmarks include the handle of the malleus, the umbo (its most depressed part), and the cone of light (light reflex), typically seen in the anterior-inferior quadrant.
  • The Nose & Sinuses:
    • Nasal Cavity: The nasal septum divides the cavity. The inferior and middle turbinates are bony plates on the lateral walls, often visible during anterior rhinoscopy.
    • Paranasal Sinuses: The frontal sinuses are located superior to the eyebrows, and the maxillary sinuses are in the cheeks, lateral to the nose. These are the two most accessible sinuses for palpation.
  • The Throat & Neck:
    • Oral Cavity: Includes the tongue, buccal mucosa, hard and soft palate, and uvula.
    • Oropharynx: The visible part of the pharynx behind the mouth, containing the palatine tonsils within the tonsillar pillars.
    • Neck Triangles: The sternocleidomastoid muscle is the key landmark dividing the neck into the anterior and posterior triangles, which provides a framework for describing the location of lymph nodes and masses.
3. Preparation, equipment & communication

A professional and safe ENT examination requires careful preparation and clear communication.

  1. Equipment:
    • Personal protective equipment (gloves, apron).
    • Otoscope with various sizes of clean disposable specula.
    • Good light source (pen torch or headlamp).
    • Tongue depressors.
    • 512 Hz tuning fork.
    • Alcohol wipes for cleaning equipment.
  2. Positioning and Dignity:
    • Ask the patient to sit upright on a chair, ideally at a similar height to you.
    • Ensure the patient is comfortable throughout. Explain each step before you do it, as the examination can feel intrusive.
  3. Introduction and Consent:
    • Begin by washing your hands in front of the patient and donning gloves.
    • Introduce yourself clearly with your name and role (e.g., "Hello, I'm [Name], a foundation doctor").
    • Explain the examination in simple terms and gain consent. A suitable phrase is: "I've been asked to examine your ears, nose, and throat. This will involve me looking inside each with a light, feeling your neck, and performing some simple hearing tests. Would that be okay?"
4. The systematic sequence

A consistent sequence ensures all components of the examination are covered thoroughly and efficiently.

  1. General Inspection: From a conversational distance, note the patient's general wellbeing, any obvious hearing aids, facial asymmetry, scars, or audible stridor or hoarseness.
  2. The Ears:
    • Inspection: Inspect the pinna and mastoid area for scars, swelling, redness, or skin lesions.
    • Otoscopy: Gently pull the pinna up and back to straighten the canal. Perform otoscopy on both ears, assessing the external canal and the tympanic membrane for colour, position, and integrity.
    • Hearing Tests: Briefly assess hearing by whispering a number in each ear. Perform Weber's test (placing the tuning fork on the forehead) and Rinne's test (comparing air and bone conduction next to the ear).
  3. The Nose:
    • Inspection: Look at the external nose for any deformity or skin changes. Ask the patient to tilt their head back slightly.
    • Anterior Rhinoscopy: Use a pen torch or otoscope to inspect the nasal vestibule, septum (looking for deviation or perforation), and inferior turbinates (noting colour and size).
    • Palpation: Gently palpate the frontal and maxillary sinuses for tenderness.
  4. The Oral Cavity and Oropharynx:
    • Inspection: With a good light source and a tongue depressor, inspect the lips, buccal mucosa, teeth, gums, and floor of the mouth.
    • Inspect the dorsal and ventral surfaces of the tongue.
    • Visualise the oropharynx, including the soft palate, uvula, tonsils, and posterior pharyngeal wall. Ask the patient to say "ahhh" to check for symmetrical movement of the soft palate.
  5. The Neck:
    • Inspection: Look for asymmetry, scars, or obvious masses.
    • Palpation: Systematically palpate the lymph node chains of the head and neck. Feel for the position of the trachea. Palpate the thyroid gland from behind the patient if a thyroid abnormality is suspected.
  6. Completing the Examination: Briefly assess relevant cranial nerves (e.g., facial nerve power - CN VII) if indicated by the history.
5. Interpreting normal findings

Recognising normality is the foundation for identifying pathology. A normal examination provides a reassuring baseline.

  • Ears: Pinna and mastoid are non-tender with no lesions. Ear canal is clear. The tympanic membrane is pearly-grey and translucent, with the handle of malleus and cone of light clearly visible.
    • Significance: Suggests no active external or middle ear infection or significant pathology.
  • Hearing: Whispered voice heard normally. Weber's test does not lateralise. Rinne's test is positive (Air Conduction > Bone Conduction).
    • Significance: Indicates hearing is grossly intact with no evidence of significant conductive or sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Nose: Nasal passages are patent. Septum is midline. Mucosa is pink and moist, and sinuses are non-tender.
    • Significance: Suggests absence of significant inflammation, obstruction, or infection.
  • Throat: Moist, pink mucosa. Uvula is central and rises symmetrically. Tonsils are small and not inflamed.
    • Significance: No evidence of active infection or cranial nerve (IX, X) palsy.
  • Neck: Supple with no palpable lymphadenopathy, masses, or tracheal deviation.
    • Significance: Makes significant infection or malignancy in the head and neck less likely.
6. Interpreting pathological signs

Abnormal signs are objective evidence of disease. A systematic approach to their interpretation is key to forming a differential diagnosis.

Otitis media
  • The Sign: On otoscopy, the tympanic membrane is red, opaque, and may be bulging. The normal light reflex is lost.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates infection with fluid and/or pus in the middle ear space, causing inflammation and pressure on the eardrum.
Conductive vs. sensorineural hearing loss
  • The Sign (Conductive): Rinne's test is negative (Bone Conduction > Air Conduction). Weber's test lateralises to the affected ear.
  • The Sign (Sensorineural): Rinne's test is positive (AC > BC), but hearing is reduced. Weber's test lateralises to the *unaffected* ear.
  • Pathology Link: Conductive loss implies a problem in the outer/middle ear (e.g., wax, fluid). Sensorineural loss implies a problem in the inner ear (cochlea) or the auditory nerve.
Tonsillitis
  • The Sign: Large, red (erythematous) tonsils, often with visible white spots of pus (exudate). Associated tender cervical lymph nodes are common.
  • Pathology Link: Indicates acute infection and inflammation of the palatine tonsils, which can be viral or bacterial.
Septal deviation / perforation
  • The Sign: On anterior rhinoscopy, the nasal septum is clearly bent to one side (deviation) or has a visible hole (perforation).
  • Pathology Link: Deviation can be congenital or traumatic. Perforation is often linked to previous surgery, nasal cocaine use, or certain autoimmune conditions.
Malignant neck lump
  • The Sign: A single, hard, irregular, and non-tender lump, often fixed to underlying tissues, found on neck palpation.
  • Pathology Link: These features are highly characteristic of a metastatic lymph node from a primary head and neck squamous cell carcinoma or a primary lymphoma.
  • Red Flag: Any persistent, unexplained neck lump in an adult, especially with a history of smoking or alcohol use, requires urgent investigation to rule out cancer.
7. Putting it all together: summarising findings

A concise, structured summary is essential for communicating your findings clearly in notes and to senior colleagues. Report pertinent positives and negatives systematically.

On general inspection, the patient appeared well, with no facial asymmetry or hearing aids.

On examination of the ears, the external canals were clear and the tympanic membranes were intact and normal in appearance. Hearing was grossly intact to whispered voice. Weber's test did not lateralise and Rinne's test was positive bilaterally.

Examination of the nose revealed a midline septum and patent airways with no sinus tenderness.

The oral cavity and oropharynx were unremarkable, with symmetrical palatal movement and non-inflamed tonsils.

On neck examination, there was no palpable lymphadenopathy or other masses, and the trachea was central.
8. Forming a differential diagnosis from signs

Individual signs are useful clues, but a constellation of signs provides powerful evidence for a specific diagnosis.

Table linking common patterns of examination findings to likely diagnoses.
Constellation of Findings Points Towards Likely Diagnosis
Fever, red bulging tympanic membrane, negative Rinne's test on the affected side. Acute Otitis Media
Fever, erythematous tonsils with exudate, tender anterior cervical lymph nodes. Acute Tonsillitis
Hard, fixed, non-tender neck lump in an adult smoker with a hoarse voice. Head and Neck Malignancy (likely Laryngeal Cancer)
Pale, swollen nasal mucosa, bilateral grape-like growths on rhinoscopy, non-tender sinuses. Chronic Rhinosinusitis with Nasal Polyps
9. Relevant next-step investigations

Examination findings are used to guide the selection of appropriate investigations to confirm a diagnosis, assess severity, and plan management.

  • If you find evidence of hearing loss:
    • Logical Next Steps: A formal Pure Tone Audiogram (PTA) is the gold standard for quantifying and characterising hearing loss. Tympanometry may also be used to assess middle ear function.
  • If you find a suspicious neck lump or signs of malignancy:
    • Logical Next Steps: This requires urgent referral to an ENT specialist. Key investigations include Flexible Nasendoscopy to visualise the pharynx and larynx, and an Ultrasound with Fine-Needle Aspiration (FNA) of the lump.
  • If you find signs of middle ear disease (e.g., AOM, effusion):
    • Logical Next Steps: Usually a clinical diagnosis. If persistent, referral for an audiogram and tympanometry is appropriate.
  • If signs of chronic rhinosinusitis are present:
    • Logical Next Steps: Often managed clinically in primary care. A non-contrast CT scan of the sinuses is the imaging modality of choice if symptoms are severe, persistent, or if surgery is considered.
10. Common pitfalls & OSCE tips

Awareness of common errors and good examination technique can dramatically improve performance, especially in an Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE).

  • Common Pitfalls:
    • Hurting the patient with the otoscope by failing to hold it correctly or brace your hand against their face.
    • Using a tongue depressor too far back on the tongue, which induces a strong gag reflex.
    • Forgetting to inspect all areas of the oral cavity, particularly under the tongue and the buccal mucosa.
    • Palpating the neck unsystematically and missing lymph node groups.
    • Failing to adequately explain each step before performing it.
  • OSCE Tips:
    • Always begin by washing your hands, introducing yourself clearly, explaining the examination, and gaining explicit consent.
    • Verbalise what you are looking for as you go (e.g., "I am now inspecting the tympanic membrane for its colour and landmarks..."). This communicates your thought process to the examiner.
    • Be organised. Have your equipment ready and follow the same systematic sequence every time.
    • When using an otoscope, hold it like a pen and rest your little finger or the ulnar border of your hand on the patient's cheek. This protects them from injury if they suddenly move.
    • At the end, always state, "To complete my examination, I would..." and list relevant next steps, such as performing a full cranial nerve examination or formal audiometry.
11. Illustrative findings in a case context

Linking clinical scenarios to expected physical findings helps to build robust clinical reasoning skills.

  • Case 1: Acute Otitis Media
    • Scenario: A 4-year-old child presents with a two-day history of severe right ear pain and fever.
    • Expected Findings: The child may be distressed and unwilling to let you examine the ear. The pinna will be normal. Otoscopy of the right ear would show a red, bulging tympanic membrane with loss of the normal light reflex. The left ear would be normal. Weber's test would likely lateralise to the right ear.
  • Case 2: Laryngeal Cancer
    • Scenario: A 65-year-old man, a lifelong smoker, presents with three months of progressive hoarseness and a painless lump in his neck.
    • Expected Findings: His voice would be audibly hoarse. Examination of the oral cavity might be normal. The key finding would be a hard, non-tender, fixed mass in the anterior triangle of the neck (likely a metastatic lymph node). The trachea may be deviated away from the lump.
  • Case 3: Allergic Rhinitis
    • Scenario: A 25-year-old woman presents with a long history of a blocked, runny nose and sneezing, which is worse in the spring.
    • Expected Findings: She may have 'allergic shiners' (dark circles under the eyes). Anterior rhinoscopy would reveal pale, swollen (oedematous) nasal mucosa and turbinates, with clear watery secretions. The rest of the examination would be normal.
